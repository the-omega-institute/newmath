\chapter{Verification Reporting Policy}
\label{app:build-log}

This appendix records the reporting policy that a checked BEDC formalization report should follow. It is project-management material on the verification axis only, not part of the BEDC object theory; nothing in this appendix assigns a closure grade.

\section{Required fields for a checked report}
\label{sec:checked-report-fields}

A checked report should record:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item the exact proof-checker version;
  \item the exact command used;
  \item whether the implementation imports external libraries;
  \item the list of theorem names successfully checked;
  \item the list of admitted, postulated, or pending statements;
  \item whether public APIs expose host equality, host natural numbers, host functions, or host lists as BEDC internals.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Verification status}
\label{sec:appendix-verification-status}

The Lean library accompanying the paper contains the mathlib-free BEDC formalization; inline markers record whether each cited object is a checked theorem, a definition, or a structure-field reference. Lean target status, theorem-by-theorem, is rendered inline at each primary site via the \texttt{\textbackslash leanchecked}, \texttt{\textbackslash leansorryd}, \texttt{\textbackslash leanstmt}, and \texttt{\textbackslash leandef} markers. The summary block in the Lean Scaffold Contract chapter aggregates the five base-reflection targets at-a-glance. The axiom audit reports zero \texttt{axiom} declarations in \texttt{lean4/BEDC/}.

The external Rule 110 substrate witness is reported in \autoref{sec:appendix-rule110-substrate}. It is an independent witness for the verification state, not the canonical statement of BEDC theory.

\section{Rule 110 substrate witness}
\label{sec:appendix-rule110-substrate}

The substrate witness has two trust paths. Tier A is the cyclic-tag witness identified by tag \texttt{rule110-v2.0-fkernel-tier-a}; its trust base is an ANSI C compiler, approximately $250$ lines of cyclic-tag evaluator C, and the manifest text. Its ship standard is that the cyclic-tag evaluator accepts the manifest assertions for the FKernel and GroundCompiler surface, with green CI, $0$ axioms, and a checked cross-report. Tier B is the Rule 110 direct witness identified by tag \texttt{rule110-v3.0-fkernel-tier-b}; its trust base is an ANSI C compiler, approximately $50$ lines of Rule 110 cellular-automaton evaluator C, and direct-carrier manifest text. Its ship standard is that each FKernel \texttt{.enum.ct} direct-carrier row round-trips through Rule 110 evolution and decoding to the corresponding manifest input. The Tier B direct-carrier claim is deliberately narrower than a full Cook packet simulation of arbitrary cyclic-tag execution; the diagnostic Cook-packet layer is tracked as executable evidence, not as the direct-carrier ship gate. The cited substrate literature is Matthew Cook, ``Universality in Elementary Cellular Automata,'' \emph{Complex Systems} $15(1)$, $2004$; Martinez--McIntosh--Seck Tuoh Mora--Chapa-Vergara, ``Determining a regular language by glider-based structures called phases $f_{i\_1}$ in Rule 110,'' preprint $0706.3348$, $2007$; Martinez--Adamatzky--Chen--Chua, ``On Soliton Collisions between Localizations in Complex Elementary Cellular Automata: Rules 54 and 110 and Beyond,'' \emph{Complex Systems} $21.2.2$, $2012$; and Matthew Cook, ``A Concrete View of Rule 110 Computation,'' preprint $0906.3248$, $2009$.

The ontology is finite-witness ontology. A BEDC substrate claim is not a claim over an unbounded semantic universe supplied by the Lean kernel. It is the finite assertion family physically present under \texttt{rule110/manifests/<module>/*.\{enum,algo\}.ct}, together with the corresponding direct-carrier rows where Tier B supplies them. Thus a Lean formula written with a universal quantifier is read, at this substrate boundary, as a compact human-readable notation for all concrete instances listed in the manifest family. The binding substrate objects are the manifest assertions themselves: their module directory, assertion name, input field, expected field, and evaluator outcome.

The Lean formalization remains meaningful but sits outside the BEDC substrate trust path. The Lean kernel is used for paper bookkeeping, human-readable shorthand, and the verification-axis markers described in \autoref{app:build-log}. A marker such as \texttt{\textbackslash leanchecked\{X\}} still records a real audit obligation: \texttt{python3 lean4/scripts/bedc\_ci.py audit} checks that X resolves to an actual declaration under \texttt{lean4/BEDC/}. The truth of the BEDC substrate assertion, however, is carried by the manifest witness and its evaluator report, not by the Lean kernel.

This witness is another audit route on the verification axis. It does not create a closure grade and it does not alter the list of closure or verification levels. The manuscript's closure claim is still read from the relevant \texttt{closurestatus} block, as in \autoref{sec:per-chapter-closurestatus}; the substrate witness records an independently checkable verification report. The two axes compose only through $\VerifiedGate$ as defined in \autoref{def:verified-gate}.

The substrate witness covers $13$ FKernel modules and \GroundCompiler: Mark, Hist, Ext, Sig with SigRel and sameSig, Cont, Bundle, Unary, Ask, ExternalBinary, Gap, Package, NameCert, and Settled. It also carries the Beyond-FKernel appendix modules \texttt{circle\_up}, \texttt{fold\_up}, \texttt{meta\_cic}, and \texttt{topology\_up}. The manifest surface includes $22$ \texttt{.algo.ct} Cook-packet diagnostic files and at least $502$ counted manifest or semantic cases, with $0$ axioms and green CI at the cited tags. The Tier B direct-carrier subset covers $25$ FKernel \texttt{.r110.ct} manifests: Mark $4$, Hist $5$, Ext $1$, Sig $2$, Cont $1$, Bundle $2$, Unary $1$, Ask $1$, ExternalBinary $1$, Gap $1$, Package $1$, NameCert $1$, Settled $1$, and GroundCompiler $3$.

\section{Relation to theoretical closure}
\label{sec:appendix-relation-to-closure}

A checked report does not assign theoretical closure. It reports the formal verification grade of claims whose closure grade is supplied by the chapter's \texttt{closurestatus} block (\autoref{ch:closure-and-verification-discipline}). A closurestatus block at $\TheoryGate(N, \scopedClosure)$ does not require any verification report; conversely, a clean verification report cannot raise a closure grade. The two artefacts compose only via $\VerifiedGate$ of \autoref{def:verified-gate}, and their contents are interrogated separately.
