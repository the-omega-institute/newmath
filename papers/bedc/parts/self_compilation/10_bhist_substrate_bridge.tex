\section{BHist Substrate Bridge}
\label{sec:self-comp-bhist-substrate-bridge}

The binary-history substrate is the smallest data layer that BEDC carries
through its finite certificates. In the kernel layer it is not a derived
numeric notation, a string encoding, or a host-side serialization format. It
is the freely generated history type with one empty history and two visible
extension constructors.

\subsection{What BHist Is}
\label{subsec:bhist-substrate-what}

The core history type is defined in
\texttt{lean4/BEDC/FKernel/Hist.lean}. Its constructor shape is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{BHist}
  ::= \mathsf{Empty}
   \mid \mathsf{e0}(\mathsf{BHist})
   \mid \mathsf{e1}(\mathsf{BHist}) .
\end{aligned}
$$

The manuscript writes the visible constructors as $b0$ and $b1$ when the
emphasis is on binary digits, and as $\Ezero(-)$ and $\Eone(-)$ when the
emphasis is on generated history structure. The Lean constructors are
\texttt{BHist.Empty}, \texttt{BHist.e0}, and \texttt{BHist.e1}. The
important invariant is the same in every notation: a history is a finite
binary digit sequence, built by closing the empty sequence under the two
single-bit extensions.

This makes $\mathsf{BHist}$ a primitive carrier for BEDC's internal evidence.
The relation $\hsame$ on histories is definitional equality in the kernel
layer, and the visible no-confusion theorems for empty, $b0$, and
$b1$ are proved by induction over that generated type. Thus the object used
by package rows, continuations, signatures, and name certificates is not an
external byte array. It is an inductive value whose constructors are visible
to the proof language.

\subsection{Why Bridge BHist to Term}
\label{subsec:bhist-substrate-why-bridge}

The MetaCIC reconstruction has a separate object language,
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term}. That language represents the syntax of a small
dependent-function calculus: variables, applications, lambdas, Pi types, and
sort. Self-compilation needs a way for the raw BEDC carrier data to appear
inside that object language. Otherwise the substrate and the represented
calculus would remain adjacent artefacts rather than a single typed internal
surface.

The bridge supplied by \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate} serves exactly
this role. It embeds host-side histories into represented terms. The input is
a Lean value of the BEDC history type. The output is a MetaCIC term that can
be placed under the ordinary syntactic and metatheoretic predicates of the
represented calculus.

The point is not to claim that every MetaCIC term denotes a history. The point
is more precise: every BEDC history has a canonical represented value. This
turns the finite binary-history carrier into a Church-style data structure
inside the MetaCIC substrate, so that raw BEDC rows can be mentioned by the
same object calculus that carries the self-compilation surface.

\subsection{The Mechanized Embedding}
\label{subsec:bhist-substrate-embedding}

The embedding is the function

$$
\begin{aligned}
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm}
  : \mathsf{BHist} \to \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term}.
\end{aligned}
$$

\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm}

In Lean the local name of the source type is
\texttt{BHistIdx}, an abbreviation for
\texttt{BEDC.FKernel.Hist.BHist}. The abbreviation records that the substrate
module treats histories as the index values being encoded into MetaCIC terms;
it does not create a separate history type.

The encoding has the shape of a three-argument Church case analysis. The
outer term binds three represented arguments. The payload uses variable $2$
for the empty-history case, variable $1$ for the $b0$ branch, and variable $0$
for the $b1$ branch. A visible bit extension is represented by applying the
corresponding branch variable to the recursively encoded tail, protected
under the same three-binder interface.

For the empty history the payload is the represented variable for the empty
case:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{payload}(\mathsf{Empty}) = \mathrm{var}\ 2 .
\end{aligned}
$$

For a $b0$ extension the payload applies the $b0$ branch to the recursively
encoded tail:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{payload}(\mathsf{e0}(h))
  =
  \mathrm{app}\ (\mathrm{var}\ 1)\
    (\lambda\_0.\lambda\_1.\lambda\_2.\ \mathsf{payload}(h)).
\end{aligned}
$$

For a $b1$ extension the payload is analogous, using the other branch
variable:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathsf{payload}(\mathsf{e1}(h))
  =
  \mathrm{app}\ (\mathrm{var}\ 0)\
    (\lambda\_0.\lambda\_1.\lambda\_2.\ \mathsf{payload}(h)).
\end{aligned}
$$

The full embedding wraps this payload in the three lambdas that bind the
empty, $b0$, and $b1$ eliminator branches. In de Bruijn notation the branch
variables are local to that wrapper, so the represented value is closed at
depth $0$.

The mechanized closure theorem is

$$
\begin{aligned}
\texttt{bhistToTerm\_closed}\ (h : \mathsf{BHist}) :
  \texttt{ClosedAt}\ 0\
    (\texttt{bhistToTerm}\ h).
\end{aligned}
$$

\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm\_closed}

This is the primary checked fact for the bridge. It is the property needed by
the self-compilation surface: a raw finite history becomes a closed
represented term, hence it can enter the closed-term side of the MetaCIC
metatheory without free-variable debt.

The same module also proves injectivity of the encoding:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\texttt{bhistToTerm\_injective} :
  \texttt{bhistToTerm}\ h =
  \texttt{bhistToTerm}\ k \to h = k .
\end{aligned}
$$

\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm\_injective}

Injectivity is a useful shape fact: distinct generated histories do not
collapse under the represented encoding. It is not used here as the canonical
closure site, but it records that the Church-style representation preserves
the visible generated structure.

\subsection{What the Bridge Gives}
\label{subsec:bhist-substrate-gives}

The immediate result is a typed-substrate design point. BEDC's finite binary
histories can appear as ordinary represented data in the MetaCIC syntax layer.
The bridge does not need a host evaluator, a parser, or a binary-string
library. It is a structural map from the inductive history carrier into the
inductive term carrier.

Because the output is closed, all closed-term invariants apply to the image
of the bridge. A represented history can be placed in contexts where the
MetaCIC development reasons about closedness, beta-step preservation, finite
reduction traces, confluence statements, and bounded normalization behavior.
Those metatheorems remain theorems about \texttt{Term}; the bridge supplies a
large family of concrete closed \texttt{Term} inputs coming from the BEDC
history substrate.

This is the relevant self-compilation step. Host-side certificate rows are
generated as finite histories. The MetaCIC substrate needs closed represented
values for such rows. The embedding gives that interface without changing the
definition of $\mathsf{BHist}$ and without adding a new primitive constructor
to \texttt{Term}.

\subsection{Unproved Boundaries}
\label{subsec:bhist-substrate-unproved-boundaries}

Three boundaries remain outside the checked bridge.

First, no surjectivity theorem is claimed. The module does not characterize
which closed normal forms of \texttt{Term} are exactly in the image of
\texttt{bhistToTerm}. Such a theorem would need a normal-form predicate and a
recognizer theorem connecting the Church-style shape to the source history.

Second, no inverse decoding theorem is used by the paper. The module contains
partial decoding functions from represented terms back to optional histories,
but this section does not claim a full inverse theorem for all closed
represented terms, nor a completeness theorem over beta-equivalent normal
forms.

Third, no commutation theorem is mechanized between host-side history
operations and represented MetaCIC operations. The bridge preserves the
generated shape and closedness of histories, but it does not prove that every
host operation on histories has a corresponding represented program whose
execution commutes with \texttt{bhistToTerm}.

These boundaries keep the statement exact. The bridge establishes a closed,
injective, Church-style embedding from the BEDC history carrier into the
represented calculus. It does not identify the whole represented-history
fragment, does not certify total decoding, and does not internalize the
history-operation algebra as MetaCIC programs.
