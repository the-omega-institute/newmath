\section{MetaCIC Metatheory Roadmap}
\label{sec:self-comp-metatheory-roadmap}

This section gives a consolidated reading map for the MetaCIC metatheory
surface. The substrate is \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}: a first-principles
represented dependent-function fragment used by the self-compilation chapter
as its formal metatheory base. The Lean development for this surface is
mathlib-free, carries no project axioms, and contains no \texttt{sorry}
placeholders. The paper side reports only the properties that are represented
or proved for this object calculus; it does not import host-kernel metatheory
as a theorem about the represented terms.

\subsection{Setting}
\label{subsec:metatheory-roadmap-setting}

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} contains the syntax, typing judgment, substitution
operation, beta relation, beta-star closure, parallel beta relation, closure
predicate, and finite galleries used by the self-compilation discussion. The
objects under discussion are represented terms, not Lean elaborator syntax.
Their binders are de Bruijn indices, and binder transport is handled by the
explicit \texttt{shift} and \texttt{substitute} functions.

The surrounding siblings have different roles. The representational-adequacy
section explains how selected host-fragment terms are mirrored by checked
MetaCIC witnesses. The mechanization dashboard enumerates proved targets,
statement-level interfaces, and open textbook theorems. The substrate-design
section explains why the de Bruijn shift discipline is part of the claim. The
confluence and normalization sections isolate the checked fragments and the
remaining global metatheory obligations.

\subsection{Mechanized Surface}
\label{subsec:metatheory-roadmap-mechanized-surface}

The following items are available as checked Lean targets or checked finite
galleries for the represented calculus.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Closed atom diamond behavior for beta-star and parallel beta. Atom
    sources have forced targets, so forks out of \texttt{Term.sort} and
    \texttt{Term.var i} join at the source shape.
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_diamond\_sort}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_diamond\_var}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_sort\_diamond}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_var\_diamond}
  \item Closure preservation under one-step beta and beta-star. If a term is
    closed at a boundary, each represented beta step and each finite beta-star
    trace keeps it closed at the same boundary.
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_preserves\_closed}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}
  \item Bounded normalizer safety is a finite-trace safety statement: any
    concrete beta trace taken by a fuel-indexed run inherits the preceding
    closure-preservation theorem. It is an operational invariant, not a
    strong-normalization theorem.
  \item Representational adequacy is witnessed by checked typed examples for
    identity, projections, Pi-shaped identity, and function application in the
    represented dependent-function fragment.
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.carrier\_identity\_in\_sort\_ctx}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.first\_of\_two}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.second\_of\_two}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_on\_pi\_sort\_sort}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.apply\_function\_arg}
  \item The \texttt{BHist} substrate embedding has checked closedness and
    injectivity witnesses. The map packages a history object as a MetaCIC term
    while preserving enough structure to recover the original history from
    equality of encoded terms.
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm\_closed}
    \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate.bhistToTerm\_injective}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Why These Gaps Are Specific}
\label{subsec:metatheory-roadmap-gap-reasons}

The missing confluence cases are concentrated in the interaction between
application beta and congruence. A branch may keep an application wrapper while
another branch exposes a lambda and contracts. Joining those residuals requires
a statement saying that represented substitution commutes with parallel
residuals after binder descent. In the chosen substrate, binder descent changes
the cutoff and shifts the inserted term, so the needed theorem is not the
ordinary informal substitution slogan.

The same shift-aware issue explains the subject-reduction boundary. The three
stable interfaces used by the subject-reduction theorem are substitution-level
and domain-transport properties. They are stated explicitly because the
project has not collapsed them into a global substitution lemma for the whole
represented calculus.

Strong normalization has a different shape. Its standard proof route uses
logical relations or reducibility candidates. That requires an inductive
predicate of admissible represented types, a semantic interpretation of those
types, and closure of the interpretation under the represented constructors.
The MetaCIC layer has syntax, typing, reduction, and finite examples; it does
not yet carry that reducibility framework.

The false substitution-composition law in the substrate-design section is the
sanity check for this roadmap. It shows that the open algebra is not a missing
line of routine rewriting. The correct statement must mention the closure and
shift hypotheses that make the de Bruijn coordinates stable.

The dependency order is therefore:

\begin{itemize}
  \item adequacy witnesses depend on the represented syntax and typing rules;
  \item closure preservation depends on the shift-aware substitution substrate;
  \item bounded normalizer safety depends on closure preservation for finite
    beta traces;
  \item full subject reduction depends on the stable substitution and domain
    transport interfaces;
  \item full confluence depends on residual substitution commutation for
    application beta forks;
  \item strong normalization depends on a reducibility framework for the
    admissible represented type fragment.
\end{itemize}
