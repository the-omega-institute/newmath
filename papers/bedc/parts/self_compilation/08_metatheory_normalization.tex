\section{Normalization Status for the MetaCIC Fragment}
\label{sec:self-comp-metacic-normalization}

The normalization surface of \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} has two distinct layers.
The first layer is operational: reduction is represented by a first-principles
syntax, a beta-step relation, and finite reduction chains. This layer supports
bounded computation and checked closure preservation. The second layer is the
textbook strong-normalization theorem for the represented dependent-function
calculus. BEDC does not prove that theorem. The difference matters because the
bounded layer is an effective, fuel-indexed operational account, while strong
normalization is a global theorem over all well-typed reduction sequences.

\subsection{Bounded Normalizer}
\label{subsec:metacic-bounded-normalizer}

A bounded normalizer for the represented calculus has the shape

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.normalizeBounded} :
  \mathrm{Nat} \to \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term} \to
  \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Its intended reading is fuel driven. At fuel $0$ it returns the input term.
At fuel $k+1$ it attempts one head reduction step and then continues with
fuel $k$ on the reduct; if no head step is available, it returns the current
term. Termination of this procedure is inherited from structural recursion on
the natural-number fuel, not from any theorem that the represented calculus is
strongly normalizing.

This bounded interface is therefore not a strong-normalization certificate.
It says that every chosen run of the evaluator has a finite host computation
because the host recursion argument decreases. It does not say that every
well-typed represented term admits no infinite beta-reduction path, and it
does not identify the returned term as a unique normal form for arbitrary
fuel. A run may stop because a normal form has been reached, or because the
fuel budget has been exhausted.

The available checked metatheory around this operational layer is closure
preservation. If a fuel-driven run is decomposed into zero or more
\texttt{BetaStep} transitions, each transition preserves the closedness
predicate, and induction on the finite step trace preserves closedness at the
end of the run. The proof is not a termination argument. It is an invariant
argument over the finite trace that the bounded computation actually takes.

\subsection{Closure Preservation Under Reduction}
\label{subsec:metacic-normalization-closure}

The mechanized closure theorem for one-step beta reduction is

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \texttt{betaStep\_preserves\_closed} :
  \texttt{ClosedAt}\ n\ t \to
  \texttt{BetaStep}\ t\ t' \to
  \texttt{ClosedAt}\ n\ t' .
\end{aligned}
$$

The proof lives in
\texttt{lean4/BEDC/MetaCIC/ClosurePreservation.lean}. Its beta-contraction
case uses the closure-preserving substitution theorem. Its congruence cases
peel the corresponding \texttt{ClosedAt} constructor and apply the induction
hypothesis to the reduced component. Thus closedness is not treated as a
syntactic side condition on whole terms only; it is propagated through
application, lambda, and Pi constructors.

The finite-chain theorem is

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \texttt{betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed} :
  \texttt{ClosedAt}\ n\ t \to
  \texttt{BetaStarStep}\ t\ t' \to
  \texttt{ClosedAt}\ n\ t' .
\end{aligned}
$$

It is induction on the represented reflexive-transitive closure. The reflexive
case returns the original closedness proof. The step case applies
\texttt{betaStep\_preserves\_closed} to the first transition and then invokes
the induction hypothesis on the remaining chain.

\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_preserves\_closed}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}

These two checked targets are the normalizer-relevant safety results available
inside BEDC. They justify the following operational statement: for any finite
reduction trace produced by a bounded evaluator, closed input remains closed
at every subsequent represented term. They do not assert progress, uniqueness
of normal forms, confluence for all represented terms, or strong
normalization.

\begin{theorem}[Bounded normalization trace closure]
\label{thm:metacic-bounded-normalization-trace-closure}
Let $t$ be a represented MetaCIC term closed at index $n$. If a bounded evaluator run displays a finite beta trace
$$
t \to^{*} t'
$$
from $t$ to $t'$, interpreted as a \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStarStep} witness, then $t'$ is closed at the same index $n$. The statement does not assert progress, fuel sufficiency, unique normal form, confluence, or strong normalization.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the evaluator output only through the displayed finite \texttt{BetaStarStep} trace. The initial hypothesis supplies \texttt{ClosedAt} $n$ $t$, and the trace supplies the finite chain from $t$ to $t'$.

Apply \texttt{betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}. Its reflexive case returns the original closedness proof, and its step case uses \texttt{betaStep\_preserves\_closed} before applying the induction hypothesis to the remaining finite trace.

No claim about the evaluator's fuel budget or global reduction behavior enters the argument. The theorem records only preservation of closedness along the finite trace that is actually displayed.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Strong Normalization}
\label{subsec:metacic-strong-normalization-open}

Strong normalization for the represented calculus would state that every
well-typed term in the intended admissible fragment has no infinite
\texttt{BetaStep} chain. The standard proof route for a dependent-function
calculus is a logical-relations or reducibility-candidates argument. Such a
proof requires a predicate of admissible represented types and a semantic
interpretation that is stable under the type formers of the calculus.

BEDC does not currently carry that infrastructure. In particular, the current
substrate lacks an inductive predicate of admissible types strong enough to
support reducibility candidates. It also lacks preservation hypotheses in the
form needed under Pi, lambda, and application constructors. The available
subject-reduction surface is intentionally local: it records beta and
congruence preservation facts, plus hypothesis-based interfaces for domain
and argument stability. Those interfaces are useful for finite local
metatheory, but they are not a logical-relations model.

A strong-normalization proof would therefore need at least the following
ingredients:

\begin{itemize}
  \item an inductive definition of the admissible type fragment;
  \item a reducibility interpretation for sort, Pi, lambda, and application
    shapes;
  \item closure of the interpretation under beta expansion and beta
    contraction;
  \item preservation lemmas whose hypotheses are stable under the relevant
    binder constructors;
  \item an induction principle linking represented typing derivations to the
    reducibility interpretation.
\end{itemize}

None of these is a consequence of having a fuel-indexed evaluator. Fuel gives
an executable finite approximation. Strong normalization is a proof that no
unbounded reduction path exists for well-typed represented terms in the
chosen fragment.

\subsection{Practical Impact}
\label{subsec:metacic-normalization-practical-impact}

The practical BEDC examples sit far below the strong-normalization boundary.
The closed atom results prove that sort and closed variable atoms have no beta
successor, and the typed closed-atom theorem packages this as a normal-form
fact for the atomic cases.

\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_atom\_normal\_form}

The \texttt{TypedExamples} gallery is also operationally tame. Its witnesses
are built from sort, Pi, lambda, variable, and application constructors in
small closed shapes. The identity and projection examples have lambda or Pi
heads until they are applied; the sort-headed and variable-headed components
are already beta-inert; and the application examples expose the ordinary
single beta contraction at the represented head when the function is a lambda.
For these examples, a bounded evaluator gives an effective semantics: with
enough fuel, the finite computation reaches the expected beta-normal
representative and stops because there is no next reduction step along that
chosen strategy.

This is the operational content needed by the paper's examples. The examples
do not require a theorem that all well-typed represented terms strongly
normalize. They require a checked syntax, checked typing witnesses, checked
beta-step rules, and checked preservation of closedness along the finite
reduction traces under discussion.

\subsection{Comparison with the Host Calculus}
\label{subsec:metacic-normalization-host-cic}

Lean 4's host kernel is trusted as the checker for this development. Its
metatheory includes the normalization and consistency facts needed for the
host's own dependent type theory at the meta level of the project. BEDC does
not import those facts as axioms about \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}. The represented
calculus is a first-principles object language built with inductive syntax,
inductive typing, and inductive reduction relations. Its metatheory must be
proved over those represented objects.

This is the same boundary recorded in
\autoref{sec:self-comp-metacic-representational-adequacy}. Representational
adequacy for the finite gallery says that selected host-fragment terms have
corresponding represented terms and typing derivations. It does not transfer
the host kernel's global strong-normalization theorem into a checked theorem
about \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}. Such a transfer would require a formal bridge
whose statement and proof are not present here.

The normalization status is consequently precise. BEDC has a finite
operational reduction layer and mechanized closure preservation for one-step
and finite-chain beta reduction. It has atomic normal-form facts and concrete
closed examples whose reductions are finite and explicit. It does not claim a
strong-normalization theorem for the represented calculus.

entry is a theory-side boundary about the represented calculus as a whole.
The fuel-bounded operational layer is useful precisely because it gives finite
computation without pretending to close the global theorem.
