\section{Substrate Stack Summary}
\label{sec:self-comp-substrate-stack-summary}

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} is a first-principles substrate for the object calculus
used by the self-compilation surface. Its stack starts with a closed inductive
syntax, adds de Bruijn mechanics, reduction, typing, and local metatheory, and
then exposes concrete examples and BEDC-carrier embeddings. Each layer is a
Lean module family with a bounded responsibility. No layer imports mathlib,
adds an axiom, or treats a host-kernel theorem as a theorem about the
represented object calculus.

The arrows in this table point upward. Syntax is the object over which every
other layer recurses. Substrate mechanics define the admissible movement of
de Bruijn indices. Reduction consumes those mechanics in beta contraction.
Typing states the object judgment over the same terms and contexts.
Metatheory proves local preservation and joining facts over the represented
relations. Examples and embeddings give concrete closed inhabitants of the
same substrate.

\subsection{Layer 0: Term Syntax}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-term-syntax}

The base layer is \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Syntax}. It defines
\texttt{Term} as a closed inductive object with five constructors:
\texttt{sort}, \texttt{var}, \texttt{lam}, \texttt{pi}, and \texttt{app}.
Binders are de-named. Variable occurrence is represented by a natural-number
de Bruijn index, so alpha-equivalent presentations have the same constructor
shape once written in this substrate.

This layer deliberately contains no nominal freshness algebra, no quotient by
alpha-equivalence, and no host-level binder function. The cost is explicit
index arithmetic. The gain is that all binder movement is ordinary recursive
data processing over \texttt{Term}, visible to the Lean kernel and to the
paper-Lean audit.

\subsection{Layer 1: Substrate Mechanics}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-mechanics}

The mechanics layer is split across \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Substitution},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosedTerm}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosedTerm.Helpers}. The two central operations are
\texttt{shift} and \texttt{substitute}. Shift raises free indices at or above
a cutoff. Substitution replaces the variable at a selected depth, lowers
indices above the removed variable, and shifts the inserted term when the
operation crosses a binder.

\texttt{ClosedAt} records the boundary at which a term has no free variables.
The helper layer supplies the constructor-local facts that make this boundary
usable under application, lambda, and Pi shapes. These definitions are the
substrate's bookkeeping contract: every higher result about beta reduction or
typing inherits the same de Bruijn boundary discipline.

\subsection{Layer 2: Reduction}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-reduction}

Reduction lives in \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Beta}. The primitive relation
\texttt{BetaStep} has a root beta-contraction rule and congruence rules for
application heads, application arguments, lambda domains, lambda bodies, Pi
domains, and Pi codomains. \texttt{BetaStarStep} is the finite
reflexive-transitive closure used for finite traces. \texttt{BetaParallel} is
the parallel relation used by the confluence surface.

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosurePreservation} connects reduction back to the
mechanics layer. A beta contraction is implemented by substitution, so closure
preservation for reduction depends on the closure-preserving substitution
facts from Layer 1. This is the first place where the stack forces the algebra
of binders to match the operational semantics.

\subsection{Layer 3: Typing}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-typing}

Typing is centered on \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing.Core}. Its main predicate is
\texttt{HasType}, an inductive relation between contexts, represented terms,
and represented types. The formation rules cover sorts, variables, Pi types,
lambdas, and applications over the explicit-context de Bruijn substrate.

The guard surfaces live in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing.SubstitutionSurface} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing.ClosedBinderDomains}. They isolate the
substitution and binder-domain hypotheses needed by typing preservation
arguments. This matters because a domain introduced outside a binder must be
read one level deeper inside the body. The typing layer therefore uses the
same shift-aware interpretation as the syntax and substitution layers, rather
than treating binder domains as inert annotations.

\subsection{Layer 4: Metatheory}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-metatheory}

The confluence surface is in \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Confluence.*}. Atom
diamonds are mechanized for sort and variable sources, where no real beta fork
can occur. The full diamond over all compound beta forks is not asserted.
Application forks need residual reasoning that commutes parallel reduction
with shift-aware substitution, and that theorem is exactly where the
represented substrate remains restrictive.

The subject-reduction surface is in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReduction.*}. It is hypothesis-based: the beta
case and congruence cases are assembled under the substitution-preservation
and domain-stability assumptions stated by the typing surface. Thus subject
reduction is not obtained by importing a host theorem. It is a theorem over
the represented \texttt{HasType} and \texttt{BetaStep} relations, at the
strength supported by the current substrate hypotheses.

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Normalization} supplies a bounded normalizer and atomic
normal-form facts. The normalizer terminates because its fuel decreases in
the host recursion, not because the represented calculus has a checked strong
normalization theorem. The normalization layer is therefore operational and
finite-trace oriented.

\subsection{Layer 5: Examples and Embeddings}
\label{subsec:substrate-layer-examples-embeddings}

The example layer is the concrete gallery under
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.TypedExamples.*}. It contains sort, Pi, identity,
application, projection, constant-form, and polymorphic witnesses over the
same \texttt{HasType} relation used by the metatheory. These examples are not
a separate evaluator surface. They are ordinary represented terms with
ordinary typing derivations.

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BHistSubstrate} connects the finite BEDC history carrier
to the MetaCIC term language. Its embedding sends each generated binary
history to a closed represented term. This supplies a bridge from raw BEDC
certificate data into the object calculus without adding a primitive history
constructor to \texttt{Term}.

\subsection{Cross-Cutting Invariants}
\label{subsec:substrate-stack-cross-cutting-invariants}

The whole stack is mathlib-free. Its Lean files are built from the Lean 4
standard environment and project-local inductive definitions, functions, and
theorems. The project gate rejects \texttt{axiom} declarations and maintains
the zero-\texttt{sorry} discipline for public theorem bodies.

The stricter purity audit also checks transitive dependencies. It rejects
\texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound}, and \texttt{propext}. Thus the
checked MetaCIC theorems are CIC-derived in the relevant project sense:
constructive, quotient-free, and not backed by hidden classical or
extensional host principles.

The paper markers are an independent audit axis. A Lean-side theorem marker
must name an object that exists under \texttt{lean4/BEDC/}. Conversely, a
marker does not close an informal theorem by itself. The stack is reported on
two axes: what the paper claims about the represented calculus, and what Lean
has checked for the corresponding definitions and theorems.

\subsection{Open Seam}
\label{subsec:substrate-stack-open-seam}

The limiting algebraic point is substitution composition. In the naive
zero-depth form, composition is false for the represented operations; the
counterexample is recorded in
\autoref{subsec:substrate-open-algebraic-gap}. The failure is not a peripheral
notation issue. It shows that the substrate must account for closure,
shifting, and binder depth before a composition theorem can be used safely.

This same constraint reaches the upper layers. Full confluence needs residual
substitution lemmas for application beta forks. Subject reduction depends on
substitution preservation at the strength stated by the typing guard surface.
Strong normalization would need an admissible-type and reducibility
infrastructure stable under the same binder mechanics. The stack is therefore
coherent from bottom to top: the highest open metatheory is bounded by the
lowest shift-aware algebra that the substrate has made available.
