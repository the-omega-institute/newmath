\section{MetaCIC Confluence Status}\label{sec:self-comp-metatheory-confluence}

The confluence surface for \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} is intentionally split into
three layers. The first layer is the inductive reduction interface: one-step
beta reduction, its reflexive-transitive closure, and parallel beta reduction.
The second layer contains shape and join lemmas for terms whose source shape
prevents a real beta fork. The third layer records the component-wise diamond
principles available for application, lambda, and Pi terms, together with the
case distinctions that remain outside the checked surface.

This section records exactly what is represented in Lean and what is still a
mathematical obligation for a full Church-Rosser theorem over the represented
beta system. The statement is deliberately local to the object calculus in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}: it does not appeal to Lean kernel conversion and does
not import a confluence theorem from the host calculus.

\subsection{Setting}
\label{subsec:metacic-confluence-setting}

The primitive one-step relation is \texttt{BetaStep}, defined in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Beta}. Its constructors are the represented beta
contraction and the congruence rules under application, lambda body, lambda
domain, Pi codomain, and Pi domain:

$$
\begin{aligned}
(\lambda d.\,b)\ a &\longrightarrow_\beta b[0 := a].
\end{aligned}
$$

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStep} is the checked name for this interface.

The reflexive-transitive closure is \texttt{BetaStarStep}. It has only
reflexivity and left-consing by one \texttt{BetaStep}. The confluence predicate
\texttt{BetaStarConfluent} is the ordinary joinability condition for two
\texttt{BetaStarStep} paths with the same source:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{BetaStarConfluent}
\quad\equiv\quad
\forall t,u_1,u_2.\;
t \longrightarrow_\beta^\ast u_1
\to t \longrightarrow_\beta^\ast u_2
\to \exists v.\;
u_1 \longrightarrow_\beta^\ast v
\land
u_2 \longrightarrow_\beta^\ast v .
\end{aligned}
$$

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStarStep} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStarConfluent} are the checked names for these two
interfaces.

The parallel relation \texttt{BetaParallel} is the standard proof vehicle for
diamond arguments. It reduces independent subterms in one represented parallel
step and also contracts an application whose function side is already a lambda
after a parallel step. The associated predicate \texttt{BetaParallelDiamond}
asks for a one-step parallel join:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{BetaParallelDiamond}
\quad\equiv\quad
\forall t,u_1,u_2.\;
t \Rightarrow u_1
\to t \Rightarrow u_2
\to \exists v.\;
u_1 \Rightarrow v
\land
u_2 \Rightarrow v .
\end{aligned}
$$

\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaParallel} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaParallelDiamond} are the checked names for these two
interfaces.

The bridge between the two relations is already mechanized in the direction
needed for a Tait-Martin-Lof style proof: one-step beta embeds into parallel
beta, and a parallel step is simulated by beta-star.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_to\_parallel} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_to\_betaStar} are the checked bridge
lemmas.
Thus, a checked full parallel diamond would be enough substrate for a
beta-star Church-Rosser theorem, provided the standard multi-step lifting is
also assembled inside the same object calculus.

\subsection{Atom-Shape Diamond}
\label{subsec:metacic-atom-shape-diamond}

The atom fragment consists of \texttt{Term.sort} and variables. These terms
have no beta redex at the root and no subterm position in which a congruence
constructor can act. For one-step beta, this is recorded by impossibility
lemmas:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_sort\_absurd}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_var\_absurd}

Consequently, beta-star paths out of atoms are forced to be reflexive. The
target uniqueness lemmas state that a beta-star target of a sort is the same
sort, and a beta-star target of a variable is the same variable:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_sort\_unique\_target}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_var\_unique\_target}

The join lemmas are therefore immediate. Two beta-star paths out of
\texttt{Term.sort} join at \texttt{Term.sort}; two beta-star paths out of
\texttt{Term.var i} join at \texttt{Term.var i}.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_diamond\_sort} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_diamond\_var} are the checked join
theorems.

The same fact is also available on the parallel side. Parallel reduction from
an atom has a unique target, so the two branches of a parallel fork have the
source atom itself as their common reduct:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_sort\_diamond} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_var\_diamond} are the corresponding
parallel join theorems.

This is the precise sense in which atom confluence is closed in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}. There is no redex that can make the two branches choose
different residual structure. The proof is shape analysis over the constructors
of the represented reduction relation, not an appeal to normalization or
typing.

\subsection{Application, Lambda, and Pi Shapes}
\label{subsec:metacic-app-lam-pi-shape-diamond}

For compound terms, BEDC has mechanized important shape and component lemmas,
but not the full diamond theorem over all application, lambda, and Pi forks.
The checked component lemmas say that, if corresponding subcomponents already
have parallel joins, then the compound terms have a parallel join:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_app\_diamond\_of\_components},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_lam\_diamond\_of\_components}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_pi\_diamond\_of\_components}.

For lambda and Pi sources, the checked shape diamond lemmas reduce the
compound fork to diamond obligations for the domain and body or codomain:
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_lam\_shape\_diamond}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_pi\_shape\_diamond}
These lemmas do not prove the recursive premises. They are local assembly
principles: once the domain and body joins are supplied, the outer constructor
is joined by congruence.

Applications are where the complete proof becomes more delicate. The checked
surface contains a full shape inversion for parallel reduction from an
application:
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_app\_full\_inv}
It separates two target forms. Either the target is another application whose
function and argument are parallel reducts of the source components, or the
target is a beta contractum obtained after the function side reduces in
parallel to a lambda.

The full application diamond must account for four fork shapes.

\paragraph{Congruence against congruence.}
Both branches keep the application shape:

$$
\begin{aligned}
f\ a &\Rightarrow f_1\ a_1,\\
f\ a &\Rightarrow f_2\ a_2.
\end{aligned}
$$

This case is exactly the component-wise argument: join $f_1$ with $f_2$, join
$a_1$ with $a_2$, and rebuild the application.

\paragraph{Congruence against beta.}
One branch keeps application shape while the other branch contracts after the
function side exposes a lambda:

$$
\begin{aligned}
f\ a &\Rightarrow f_1\ a_1,\\
f\ a &\Rightarrow b_2[0 := a_2].
\end{aligned}
$$

The missing obligation is a residual statement: the application branch must be
shown to reduce toward a shape compatible with the contractum branch, and the
contracted body must commute with the residual reductions of the argument.

\paragraph{Beta against congruence.}
This is the symmetric case. The source branch that contracts first produces a
substitution instance, while the other branch keeps the application wrapper.
The join must show that the wrapper branch can expose a compatible lambda and
then contract to the same residual substitution target.

\paragraph{Beta against beta.}
Both branches expose lambda structure and contract:

$$
\begin{aligned}
f\ a &\Rightarrow b_1[0 := a_1],\\
f\ a &\Rightarrow b_2[0 := a_2].
\end{aligned}
$$

The join depends on a simultaneous residual argument for the two lambda bodies
and the two arguments, plus the substitution-commutation law that connects
parallel residuals before and after substituting the argument into the body.

Lambda and Pi terms avoid root beta contraction, but their complete diamond
proof still requires recursive joins for all binder subcomponents. For lambda,
the cases are:

\paragraph{Domain against domain.}
Both branches reduce the domain and keep the body branch compatible. The join
is rebuilt by the lambda congruence constructor.

\paragraph{Body against body.}
Both branches reduce the body. The join is rebuilt under the binder after the
body residuals are joined.

\paragraph{Cross branch.}
One branch reduces the domain and the other reduces the body. The diamond must
commute these independent moves and rebuild the lambda over the joined domain
and body. The checked \texttt{betaParallel\_lam\_shape\_diamond} lemma packages
this once the recursive component joins are supplied.

Pi terms have the same pattern with domain and codomain in place of lambda
domain and body. The checked \texttt{betaParallel\_pi\_shape\_diamond} lemma
again performs the outer constructor assembly, but it does not supply the
recursive induction principle for the subcomponents.

\subsection{Strategy Notes}
\label{subsec:metacic-confluence-strategy-notes}

The standard route to Church-Rosser for beta reduction is the
Tait-Martin-Lof parallel-reduction method. One proves a diamond property for
parallel reduction, shows that ordinary beta steps embed into parallel
reduction, shows that parallel steps are included in beta-star, and then lifts
the parallel diamond to confluence of beta-star.

BEDC has the first-order interface and many constructor-local lemmas needed by
that route. What is absent is the complete induction that covers every
constructor of \texttt{BetaParallel} with the correct residual term in the
application beta cases. In informal notation the residual target is natural:
parallel-reduce the body and argument, then substitute the residual argument
into the residual body. In the BEDC substrate, that sentence expands into
shift-aware de Bruijn algebra.

The hard point is the congruence-beta family of application cases. A branch
may first reduce the function position to a lambda while the other branch
contracts after a different exposure of lambda structure. Joining these
branches requires a theorem saying that substitution commutes with the
parallel residuals in exactly the represented form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
b \Rightarrow b'
\land
a \Rightarrow a'
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
b[0 := a] \Rightarrow^\ast b'[0 := a'].
\end{aligned}
$$

The displayed formula is schematic. In the actual substrate, descent under a
binder changes the cutoff and shifts the inserted term. The open algebraic gap
therefore is not merely the absence of a textbook substitution lemma; it is the
absence of the exact shift-aware commutation theorem that matches
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute}. \autoref{subsec:substrate-open-algebraic-gap}
shows why a naive zero-depth composition law is false for unclosed anchors, and
\autoref{sec:self-comp-substrate-shift-aware-design} explains the binder
transport invariant that any usable law must respect.

This is also why the atom fragment is not representative of the general case.
Atoms need no residual calculus, no binder descent, and no substitution
transport. Application beta cases need all three.

\subsection{BEDC Scope}
\label{subsec:metacic-confluence-bedc-scope}

Within the closed-atom fragment, BEDC already has the confluence behavior it
uses. Sorts and closed variables are normal forms for represented beta
reduction, and forks out of those sources are forced to join at the source
itself. This covers the atomic normal-form statements used in the
self-compilation discussion:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_atom\_normal\_form}.

The scope changes once reducible closed terms are admitted. A closed term can
contain an application redex, and then a fork may choose between reducing
inside subcomponents and contracting the exposed lambda. BEDC has checked
closure preservation for beta steps and beta-star, and it has checked
subject-reduction under the stated substitution-preservation interface, but
those facts are not a full diamond theorem:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_preserves\_closed},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.subject\_reduction}.

The resulting boundary is sharp. Atom sources are closed under the diamond
argument because their reduction relation is trivial. Reducible closed terms
remain within the represented closed world under reduction, but full
joinability for all their beta forks requires the application residual proof
and the shift-aware substitution commutation lemmas described above.
