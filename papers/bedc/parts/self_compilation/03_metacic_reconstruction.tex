\section{MetaCIC Reconstruction}\label{sec:self-comp-metacic}

The MetaCIC reconstruction surface asks how much of the host calculus can be
represented as BEDC data and predicates. It is a reconstruction analysis, not a
replacement of the host. The relevant namespace is `\verb|BEDC.MetaCIC|', whose
role is to name the current surface on which CIC syntax, typing, and reduction
can be discussed as first-class BEDC objects.

The structural reconstruction layer constructs syntax, contexts, terms,
universe annotations as needed, beta-reduction, and typing judgments as
inductive objects or inductive predicates over BEDC histories.
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term}
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStep}
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.HasType}
The result is not a claim that BEDC has reimplemented Lean. It is a claim that
the grammar and judgment forms of a CIC fragment can be represented directly
enough to support quantification and induction over them.

The difference between behavior and structure is central. A Turing-machine
simulation of Lean execution can describe observed runs:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\exists t,\ \mathrm{MachineTrace}(\mathrm{Lean},x,t)
  \wedge \mathrm{Accepts}(t).
\end{aligned}
$$

Such a statement is behavioral and trace-based. It predicts or records a
finite execution. A direct inductive predicate has a different form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma \vdash_{\mathrm{MetaCIC}} t : T.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is a first-class judgment. BEDC can quantify over contexts, terms, and
derivations, and can prove results by induction on the derivation. TM
simulation gives Sigma-one execution traces; direct reconstruction gives
typed judgment objects. The base sort judgment is inhabited in the empty
context. \leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.sort\_in\_empty\_ctx}

The same fragment carries concrete checked examples. A polymorphic identity
term over `Sort' is well typed, and its Pi type is itself a represented typing
judgment. \leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_sort\_well\_typed}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.pi\_sort\_sort\_well\_typed} Applying that identity
to `Sort' has the expected represented application typing, and the corresponding
beta step contracts to the argument. \leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_sort\_applied}


The construction surface covers syntax and judgments. It supports statements
of the form ``for every represented derivation with this constructor shape, the
following structural property holds.'' It also supports explicit boundary
claims: which syntax is represented, which conversion relation is present,
which typing rules are included, and which host-kernel features remain outside
the fragment.

The metatheory layer covers structural claims about represented derivations.
Subject reduction says that typing survives reduction:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\Gamma \vdash t : T \wedge t \to_{\beta} u
  \longrightarrow \Gamma \vdash u : T.
\end{aligned}
$$

Confluence says that divergent reductions can be joined. These theorems are
structural metatheorems about the represented calculus, and their checked
fragments must be stated over the explicit syntax, reduction, and typing
relations carried by the reconstruction.

The encoded confluence surface separates ordinary multi-step beta reduction
from parallel reduction. $\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$ is the reflexive-transitive
closure of one-step $\beta$; $\mathrm{BetaParallel}$ is a one-round parallel
relation with identity clauses for variables and sort, componentwise clauses
for application, lambda, and Pi, and a beta clause that contracts a lambda
redex after reducing the domain, body, and argument in parallel.
The checked diamond material is therefore shape-local. The sort and variable
sources have genuine joins because their parallel reductions can only return
the same atom, while application, lambda, and Pi constructors have join
assemblers when their component joins are supplied. At the ordinary multi-step
level, the atom cases are closed by showing that a beta-star path out of a
sort or variable has the same endpoint.
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStar\_var\_join}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStar\_sort\_join}

\subsection{Typing Rules}\label{subsec:reconstruction-typing-rules}

The represented typing judgment $\mathrm{HasType}\ \Gamma\ t\ A$ is generated
by five rules. They are the whole typing surface for the local MetaCIC
fragment:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{sortRule}:&\quad
  \Gamma \vdash \mathrm{sort} : \mathrm{sort}, \\
\mathrm{varRule}:&\quad
  \Gamma.\mathrm{lookup}\ i = \mathrm{some}\ A
  \Rightarrow \Gamma \vdash \mathrm{var}\ i : A, \\
\mathrm{piRule}:&\quad
  \Gamma \vdash \mathrm{dom} : \mathrm{sort},\
  (\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ \mathrm{dom}) :: \Gamma
  \vdash \mathrm{cod} : \mathrm{sort}
  \Rightarrow \Gamma \vdash
  \pi\,\mathrm{dom}\,\mathrm{cod} : \mathrm{sort}, \\
\mathrm{lamRule}:&\quad
  \Gamma \vdash \mathrm{dom} : \mathrm{sort},\
  (\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ \mathrm{dom}) :: \Gamma
  \vdash \mathrm{body} : \mathrm{cod}
  \Rightarrow \Gamma \vdash
  \lambda\,\mathrm{dom}\,\mathrm{body}
  : \pi\,\mathrm{dom}\,\mathrm{cod}, \\
\mathrm{appRule}:&\quad
  \Gamma \vdash f : \pi\,\mathrm{dom}\,\mathrm{cod},\
  \Gamma \vdash a : \mathrm{dom}
  \Rightarrow \Gamma \vdash f\,a :
  \mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ a\ \mathrm{cod}.
\end{aligned}
$$

\subsection{Reduction Layer Summary}\label{subsec:reconstruction-reduction-summary}

The reduction layer has three relations. $\mathrm{BetaStep}$ is single-step
$\beta$ reduction: root beta plus congruence under application heads and
arguments, lambda bodies and domains, and Pi codomains and domains.
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStep}

$\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$ is the reflexive-transitive closure, generated by
reflexive and one-step extension constructors, with checked transitivity for
finite path composition.
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStarStep}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStar\_trans}

$\mathrm{BetaParallel}$ is the Tait--Martin-L\"of one-round parallel relation:
variables and sort reduce to themselves; Pi, lambda, and application reduce
componentwise; beta reduces domain, body, and argument before contraction.
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaParallel}

The checked containment spine records reflexivity, $\mathrm{BetaStep}
\subseteq \mathrm{BetaParallel}$, and $\mathrm{BetaParallel} \subseteq
\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$.
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_refl}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_to\_parallel}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaParallel\_to\_betaStar}

The normalization facts are deliberately atomic. $\mathrm{sort\_no\_beta}$
and $\mathrm{closed\_var\_no\_beta}$ say that the sort constructor and a
variable constructor are not beta-redex heads; there is no one-step reduction
whose source has either shape. The typed closed-atom theorem packages this as
a normal-form statement: if a closed represented term typed at sort is either
the sort atom or a variable atom, then no beta successor exists. In this
bounded sense, closed atoms are already normal forms in the represented
calculus.
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_atom\_normal\_form}

Strong normalization has a different status. A global strong-normalization
theorem strong enough to recover consistency of the represented host calculus
meets the Godel-II boundary when the represented calculus is comparable in
strength to the host in which the proof is carried out. BEDC can study bounded
or fragment-specific termination claims, and it can state which hypotheses make
those claims available. It does not obtain an axiom-free global consistency
certificate for its own host by internal reconstruction.

The replacement reading would make BEDC the foundation instead of CIC. This is
not a claim of the paper. BEDC is formalized inside the host CIC. All Lean
theorems in the project are checked by that host. The MetaCIC surface may
represent syntax and prove fragment metatheory, but it does not move BEDC
outside the host or give BEDC a stronger foundational status than the kernel
that checks it.

This yields the governing inclusion:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{BEDC\ formalization} \subseteq \mathrm{host\ CIC}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The inclusion is methodological, not merely notational. A theorem about a
represented calculus is a theorem inside the host. It can be useful, precise,
and internally quantified, but it cannot serve as a host-independent foundation
for the host without crossing the Godel-Tarski boundary.

The MetaCIC reconstruction surface thus complements the Manifest. The Manifest
registers finite paper-anchored theorem entries as checked Lean data. MetaCIC
reconstruction studies the syntax and judgment structure of the calculus in
which such checking is meaningful. Together they explain why ``BEDC compiles
itself'' has a legitimate L4-mid reading and no legitimate L4a or replacement
reading.

\subsection{Host CIC Relation}\label{subsec:reconstruction-host-cic-relation}

MetaCIC is represented inside the host Lean calculus, not beside it. The
objects named \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Term}, \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.HasType}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStep} are implemented as inductive types or
inductive predicates in the host CIC. They are therefore first-order objects
for the host: Lean can quantify over represented terms, represented typing
derivations, and represented beta steps exactly as it quantifies over any
other inductively generated data.

The important boundary is axiomatic. The host Lean environment has available
constants such as \texttt{propext}, \texttt{Quot.sound}, and
\texttt{Classical.choice} when developments choose to depend on them.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} does not use those constants. Its syntax, reduction,
typing, and checked metatheory are built in the pure CIC fragment by
inductive definitions and structural proofs.

The verification relation is one-way. The host CIC kernel type-checks each
BEDC theorem. Within that checked development, a MetaCIC typing derivation is
an inhabitant of the inductive predicate \texttt{HasType}, and reasoning about
typing inference is ordinary induction over that predicate. There is no
circular dependence: the host checks BEDC, while BEDC encodes a CIC fragment
as data and judgments.

This also fixes the Godel-Tarski boundary. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} is not
outside the host and cannot prove the consistency of the host that checks it.
It can state and prove fragment metatheory for the represented calculus, but
it does not create a host-independent consistency proof.

\subsection{Encoding Choice}\label{subsec:reconstruction-encoding-choice}

The reconstruction uses a de Bruijn, shift-aware substrate for binders. Named
binders are convenient for informal prose, but a checked development must then
carry freshness side conditions through weakening, substitution, and binder
descent. Those side conditions are not part of the mathematical point of the
fragment, so the substrate uses numeric binder positions instead.

Naive de Bruijn encoding is still not sufficient. If a binder simply extends a
context by placing $\mathrm{dom}$ at the head, as $\mathrm{dom} :: \Gamma$,
then the body sees $\mathrm{var}\ 0$ both as the binder slot and as an index
sharing the outer context convention. Under substitution or descent below
another binder, this creates exactly the capture phenomenon the encoding is
meant to avoid.

The shift-aware substrate stores the binder slot type as
$\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ \mathrm{dom}$. Inside the body, $\mathrm{var}\ 0$
therefore points strictly to the binder, while the old context has been moved
under that new slot. This matches the host CIC's internal de Bruijn discipline:
crossing a binder changes the reference depth, and the represented context
records that change explicitly.

The price is that some attractive unconditional preservation statements are
not true at this level of representation. In particular, shift adjustment and
substitution do not commute freely under an open binder. Substitution
preservation therefore carries closure hypotheses in the binder cases; those
hypotheses make the shifted domain stable and let the represented typing
context line up without capture.

\subsection{Shift Mechanism Detail}\label{subsec:reconstruction-shift-detail}

The operation $\mathrm{shift}\ \mathrm{cutoff}\ \mathrm{amount}\ t$ takes a
cutoff threshold, an addition amount, and a represented term $t$. On a
variable $\mathrm{var}\ i$, it compares $i$ with the cutoff. If
$i \geq \mathrm{cutoff}$, the variable is mapped to
$\mathrm{var}\ (i+\mathrm{amount})$; if $i < \mathrm{cutoff}$, it is left
unchanged.

The operation is structural on applications and sorts. Under a lambda or Pi
binder, the domain is shifted at the same cutoff, while the body is shifted at
$\mathrm{cutoff}+1$. The extra unit records the binder slot that now occupies
one de Bruijn index position before the surrounding variables.

This is the mechanism behind capture-aware substitution. When
$\mathrm{substitute}$ descends below a binder, it raises the substitution
depth and shifts the incoming term by $\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1$, so the incoming
term passes around the binder slot instead of being captured by it.

The core lemmas state the bookkeeping equalities used downstream:
\texttt{shift\_closed} says shifting at a closed boundary leaves the term
unchanged; \texttt{substitute\_shift\_at\_eq} says substitution at depth $n$
undoes a same-depth one-step shift; and \texttt{substitute\_closed} says
substitution at a closed boundary leaves the term unchanged.

\subsection{Substitution Operation}\label{subsec:reconstruction-substitution-operation}

The operation $\mathrm{substitute}\ \mathrm{depth}\ v\ t$ takes a de Bruijn
replacement depth, an incoming replacement value $v$, and an original
represented term $t$. It is the capture-aware operation used by beta
contraction and by the substitution-preservation surface.

On a variable $\mathrm{var}\ i$, the operation compares $i$ with the
replacement depth. If $i = \mathrm{depth}$, the variable is replaced by $v$.
If $i > \mathrm{depth}$, the variable crosses the removed binder slot and
becomes $\mathrm{var}\ (i-1)$. If $i < \mathrm{depth}$, the variable is below
the active replacement boundary and is left unchanged.
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_var\_zero}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_var\_succ\_zero}

The operation is structural on application, sort, lambda, and Pi terms. Under
a binder, the body is processed at $\mathrm{depth}+1$, and the incoming value
is simultaneously raised to $\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ v$. This synchronized
shift makes $v$ pass the new binder slot without capture.

The syntax-level lemmas summarize the arithmetic used downstream:
\texttt{substitute\_var\_zero} gives replacement at depth zero;
\texttt{substitute\_var\_succ\_zero} gives the positive-variable decrement;
\texttt{substitute\_closed} says closed terms are unaffected by substitution;
and \texttt{substitute\_shift\_at\_eq} says substitution at depth $n$ cancels
a same-depth one-step shift.
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_closed}
\leanchecked{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_shift\_at\_eq}

\subsection{Glossary}\label{subsec:reconstruction-glossary}

The following terms name the local reconstruction conventions used in this
section:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A shift-aware binder context is a lambda or Pi binder slot whose stored
    domain is $\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ \mathrm{dom}$ rather than
    $\mathrm{dom}$; this choice prevents capture by $\mathrm{var}\ 0$.
  \item A closed binder domain is the domain of that lambda or Pi satisfying
    $\mathrm{ClosedAt}\ 0$.
  \item $\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$ is the reflexive-transitive closure of one-step
    $\mathrm{BetaStep}$.
  \item $\mathrm{BetaParallel}$ is Tait--Martin-L\"of style parallel reduction.
  \item An atom shape is a term whose shape is either $\mathrm{sort}$ or
    $\mathrm{var}\ i$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Construction Style}\label{subsec:reconstruction-construction-style}

The MetaCIC layer is built in the first instance from pure inductive
objects. $\mathrm{Term}$ is an inductive syntax type, while
$\mathrm{HasType}$, $\mathrm{BetaStep}$, $\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$,
$\mathrm{BetaParallel}$, and $\mathrm{ClosedAt}$ are inductive predicates.
No constructor in this surface depends on an additional axiom.

Binders are represented by de Bruijn indices. Variables have the form
$\mathrm{Term.var}\ i$ for an index $i$, and substitution is the explicit
operation $\mathrm{substitute}\ \mathrm{depth}\ v\ t$. Lambda and Pi binders
are handled by the same shift-aware discipline used by the typing judgment:
crossing a binder raises the depth and shifts the incoming term so that
capture is not introduced by representation.

The main substitution-preservation statements are conditioned by closure.
They work under hypotheses such as $\mathrm{ClosedAt}\ 0\ \mathrm{dom}$,
because a closed binder domain is stable under the binder shift:
$\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ \mathrm{dom} = \mathrm{dom}$. This is the local fact
that lets the represented binder context and the substituted context line up.

Many checked lemmas are intentionally statement-shape lemmas. Names such as
\texttt{betaSubstitutionPreservation\_sort\_var},
\texttt{betaSubstitutionPreservation\_pi\_body},
\texttt{betaSubstitutionPreservation\_lam\_body}, and
\texttt{betaSubstitutionPreservation\_app\_body} isolate the term shape under
which substitution preservation is proved. The full picture is assembled from
these shape-local facts rather than from an opaque global argument.

\subsection{Derivation Style}\label{subsec:reconstruction-derivation-style}

The MetaCIC proof layer follows a derivation style tied closely to the
inductive term and judgment constructors. Most local lemmas proceed by case
analysis on the represented term or typing witness. In the atom cases of
subject reduction and substitution preservation, a proof often reduces to
term-level inversion by \texttt{cases ht}: when the source term is
$\mathrm{sort}$ or $\mathrm{var}\ i$, the impossible typing constructors are
eliminated and the surviving constructor exposes exactly the required
premises.

Many theorems are therefore shape-restricted. A case-specific preservation
result may assume a syntactic side condition such as
$t = \mathrm{sort} \vee \exists i,\ t = \mathrm{var}\ i$, rather than
claiming the unrestricted preservation theorem directly. The global proof
surface is assembled by chaining such case lemmas, with separate entries for
sort/variable atoms, Pi bodies, lambda bodies, and application bodies.

The proofs are also closure-conditioned. Hypotheses such as
$\mathrm{ClosedAt}\ 0\ \mathrm{dom}$ keep binder-domain shifts from changing
the represented type, so the shifted context used by the typing rule agrees
with the context obtained after substitution. This is the point at which the
de Bruijn bookkeeping becomes a local equality instead of a global transport
problem.

Finally, the formalization separates statements from proof instances. Clean
targets such as $\mathrm{BetaSubstitutionPreservation}$ name the proposition
needed downstream, while the checked lemmas prove that target under specific
term shapes and closure hypotheses. The development does not hide the
remaining unrestricted theorem inside one monolithic proof; it exposes the
proof-relevant shapes that make each derivation go through.

\subsection{Namespace Rationale}\label{subsec:reconstruction-namespace-rationale}

The \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} namespace is divided by proof responsibility rather
than by presentation order. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Syntax} contains the
syntactic core: $\mathrm{Term}$, shift, substitution, and contexts.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Beta} contains one-step $\beta$ reduction, while
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing} contains the typing relation over that syntax.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReduction} names the subject-reduction theorem
suite, so that preservation claims remain downstream of both reduction and
typing. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Confluence} contains multi-step reduction and
parallel reduction, because join arguments use a different induction shape
from one-step contraction. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Normalization} records the
atom normal-form facts. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.AtomInfer} and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.TypedExamples} are demonstration and inference surfaces,
kept outside the metatheory namespaces so that examples do not determine the
core proof interfaces. Each sub-namespace is axiom-free under the strict
\texttt{bedc\_ci.py axiom-purity --strict} audit.

\subsection{Module Stack Overview}\label{subsec:metacic-module-stack}

The current MetaCIC stack is factored by proof responsibility.
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Syntax} provides the term grammar, de Bruijn shifting,
substitution, and contexts; \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosedTerm} defines
$\mathrm{ClosedAt}$ and the closed-term behavior of shift and substitution;
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Beta} gives the one-step $\mathrm{BetaStep}$ relation;
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Substitution}, together with
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Substitution.Core},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Substitution.Basic}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Substitution.Statements}, organizes the syntax-level

concrete typed terms; \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.PrettyPrint} gives a structural

\subsection{Internal Manifest Coverage}\label{subsec:internal-manifest-coverage}

\texttt{BEDC.Manifest} is the Lean-side registry for paper-facing theorem
claims. Its core record is \texttt{BEDC.Manifest.ClaimEntry}, with fields for
the paper label, fully qualified Lean name, proposition, and proof witness.
The field \texttt{proof : prop} is dependent: a manifest entry elaborates only
when Lean's kernel accepts the supplied proof term at the recorded proposition.
Thus manifest well-formedness is not a string audit; it is kernel type
checking of the \texttt{List ClaimEntry}.

The registry has 65 Lean-side entries in the unfiltered manifest-coverage
report. Its breadth includes paper Lean-checked theorems from base reflection,
ground compiler results, F-kernel gap transport, capstone fable-machine
claims, reflection claims, derived structures for holonomy, KKT, and spectral
measure packets, and the six primary MetaCIC examples in this section:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.sort\_in\_empty\_ctx},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_sort\_well\_typed},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.pi\_sort\_sort\_well\_typed},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_sort\_applied},
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.id\_sort\_beta}, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.var\_zero\_in\_sort\_ctx}.

The audit command \texttt{bedc\_ci.py manifest-coverage} compares two
sets: paper targets named by $\backslash\mathtt{leanchecked}\{X\}$ and Lean
targets registered in \texttt{BEDC.Manifest.manifest}. It reports the marker
count, manifest-entry count, coverage ratio, and a sample of paper markers
that are not represented by manifest entries. The unfiltered report gives
7831 paper markers and 65 manifest entries, for
\texttt{65/7831 = 0.83\%}. With the literal namespace filter
\texttt{--scope all}, the same command reports zero markers and zero entries,
because no Lean target has prefix \texttt{all}.

The gap is therefore marker-heavy rather than manifest-heavy. The manifest
contains a selected kernel-typed spine, while many paper markers remain outside
the internal registry. In this section, the six primary MetaCIC markers above
are manifest entries; the substitution, subject-reduction, and binder-transport
markers below are Lean-checked paper markers without manifest entries. The
audit makes this distinction mechanical: every paper-claimed theorem must be
visible either through the ordinary $\backslash\mathtt{leanchecked}$ marker
stream or through the stronger internal manifest entry stream.

\subsection{Current State of the Reconstruction}\label{subsec:current-state}

This subsection records the state of the MetaCIC reconstruction as encoded
in BEDC's Lean library.

\paragraph{Definitions in place.} The represented type theory exposes
$\mathrm{Term}$, $\mathrm{Ctx}$, $\mathrm{HasType}$,
$\mathrm{BetaStep}$, $\mathrm{shift}$, $\mathrm{substitute}$,
$\mathrm{WellFormedCtx}$, $\mathrm{infer}$, $\mathrm{check}$,
$\mathrm{Term.beq}$, $\mathrm{pp}$, $\mathrm{normalizeOneStep}$, and
$\mathrm{elaborate}$. Each definition is axiom-free under
\texttt{\#print axioms}.

\paragraph{Theorems with full proofs.} The library carries axiom-free proofs
for:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Sanity instances of $\mathrm{HasType}$: $\mathrm{sort}$ in the empty
    context, polymorphic identity at the $\mathrm{Sort}$ level,
    $\mathrm{Pi}$ types, and variable lookup in singleton contexts.
  \item A worked encoding of host single-sort polymorphic identity as a
    MetaCIC $\mathrm{Term}$ with its $\mathrm{HasType}$ derivation
    (\autoref{sec:self-comp-metacic}).
  \item Soundness of $\mathrm{Term.beq}$ as structural equality.
  \item Soundness of $\mathrm{infer}$ against $\mathrm{HasType}$.
  \item Soundness of $\mathrm{check}$ against $\mathrm{HasType}$.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Theorems with statements pending proof.} The library carries the
unrestricted target, $\mathrm{substitute\_preserves\_typing}$, guarded by a

$\mathrm{closed\_term\_substitute\_preserves\_typing}$ as a more tractable

The statement is registered as

  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_sort\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor}$ (sort case);
  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_var\_zero\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor}$ (variable, depth 0);
  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_var\_nonzero\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor}$ (variable, depth > 0);
  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_pi\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor}$ (Pi);
  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_lam\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor}$ (lambda, proved via term induction; the corresponding $\mathrm{HasType}$-induction path depends on binder-shift propagation);
  \item $\mathrm{substitute\_app\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_anchor\_with\_type\_eq}$ (application, conditional on a substitute composition equation).
\end{itemize}

The main theorem depends on two proof obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Prove the substitute composition equation
    $\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ s\ (\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ a\ \mathrm{cod})
    = \mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ (\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ s\ a)\
      (\mathrm{substitute}\ 1\ s\ \mathrm{cod})$, discharging the
    application conditional.
  \item Run the outer induction on the typing derivation, supplying each
    case lemma's recursive premises and assembling them into the main theorem.
\end{enumerate}

The closed-term restriction avoids the binder-shift propagation obstruction by
ensuring the substitution targets and source terms have no free variables that
would expose it.

\paragraph{Substitute composition.} The closure-restricted form of
the substitute composition equation
$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ s\ (\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ a\ \mathrm{cod}) =
  \mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ (\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ s\ a)\
    (\mathrm{substitute}\ 1\ s\ \mathrm{cod})
\end{aligned}
$$
is established for five of five inductive cases of $\texttt{cod}$:
sort, var, app, lam, and pi. The sort case is unconditional, the var
case assumes $\mathrm{ClosedAt}\ 0\ s$, the app case uses two recursive
premises, and the lam and pi cases are conditional on a
shift-substitute commutation lemma
$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ (\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ s\ a) =
  \mathrm{substitute}\ 1\ (\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ s)\
    (\mathrm{shift}\ 0\ 1\ a).
\end{aligned}
$$
The commutation lemma is the required premise for full unconditional
composition closure.

\paragraph{Host reflection bridge.}
$\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.HostBridge}.\mathrm{reflectExpr}$ covers eight
host Lean $\mathrm{Expr}$ constructors: $\mathrm{sort}$,
$\mathrm{bvar}$, $\mathrm{app}$, $\mathrm{lam}$,
$\mathrm{forallE}$, $\mathrm{const}$, $\mathrm{fvar}$ returning
$\mathrm{none}$, and $\mathrm{mdata}$ as a passthrough. The elaboration
commands $\#\mathrm{reflect\_metacic}\ e$ and
$\#\mathrm{metacic\_decide}\ e$ expose the bridge: the former prints
the reflected $\mathrm{Term}$, and the latter runs $\mathrm{infer}$ on
that reflected term and prints the inferred MetaCIC type.

\paragraph{Out of scope.} The reconstruction does not currently implement
strong normalization, confluence, full universe hierarchy, inductive
declarations within MetaCIC, or a verified embedding into the host Lean kernel.
These remain research targets as outlined in
\autoref{sec:self-comp-reflection-bridge}.

\paragraph{Audit guarantees.} The reconstruction's status is maintained by
four standing audits at PR merge time:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{check-axioms.py} rejects any \texttt{axiom} declaration.
  \item \texttt{bedc\_ci.py axiom-purity --strict} rejects theorems whose
    transitive axiom dependency includes \texttt{Classical.choice},
    \texttt{Quot.sound}, or \texttt{propext}.
  \item \texttt{bedc\_ci.py audit} rejects

  \item \texttt{bedc\_ci.py marker-existence-audit} reports the same

\end{itemize}
The current state is coherent. Formal targets without full proof terms are

\subsection{Open Targets}\label{subsec:reconstruction-open-targets}

The reconstruction does not yet close the following metatheoretic targets:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Substitute preservation for open binder domains. The checked theorem
    covers closed binder-domain hypotheses; open domains still require a
    general binder-shift and substitution transport argument.
  \item Subject reduction without body-shape restrictions. The closed body
    cases are represented, while arbitrary lambda and Pi bodies still require
    binder-drop handling that commutes with typing projections.
  \item Confluence as a full diamond property for parallel reduction across
    all term shapes. The atom cases are closed; Pi, lambda, and application
    cases still depend on general join assemblers for their components.
  \item Strong normalization for closed sort-typed terms.
  \item Decidable type checking for arbitrary Pi, lambda, and application
    terms. The checked $\mathrm{AtomInfer}$ surface covers only sort and
    variable atoms.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Results Overview}\label{subsec:reconstruction-results-overview}

The formalized MetaCIC result set covers the main closed reconstruction
claims needed by this chapter. Closed binder-domain substitute preservation is
proved by
$\mathrm{substitute\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_binder\_domains}$, giving the
complete closed theorem over the shift-aware typing substrate. Subject
reduction is represented by the main theorem
$\mathrm{subject\_reduction}$ together with the beta case, the application
congruence cases such as $\mathrm{subject\_reduction\_congApp1}$, and the
closed body cases for lambda and Pi constructors.

The confluence layer supplies the parallel reduction framework
$\mathrm{BetaParallel}$, beta-star transport lemmas
$\mathrm{betaStarStep\_*}$, and atom-shape diamond components for variables
and sorts. The normalization layer proves that atoms do not reduce:
$\mathrm{sort\_no\_beta}$, $\mathrm{closed\_var\_no\_beta}$, and
$\mathrm{closed\_atom\_normal\_form}$ close the represented normal-form facts
for the atomic fragment.

The executable demonstration layer contains the closed-atom inference
theorems $\mathrm{inferAtom\_sort\_sound}$ and
$\mathrm{inferAtom\_var\_sound}$, plus a concrete typed-term gallery with
roughly fifteen examples spanning variables, Pi terms, dependent identity
terms, and applications. All listed results are axiom-free under
\texttt{\#print axioms}; the audited dependency sets contain no
\texttt{propext}, \texttt{Classical.choice}, or \texttt{Quot.sound}.

\subsection{Reconstruction Summary}\label{subsec:reconstruction-summary}

The MetaCIC reconstruction has three foundation layers in Lean:
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Syntax} defines the de Bruijn term grammar, contexts,
lookup, shift, and substitution; \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Beta} defines
one-step beta reduction with congruence constructors; and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosedTerm} defines closure at a de Bruijn boundary
together with the closure lemmas for shift and substitution. On this base,
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing} exposes the shift-aware typing relation and its
substitution surface.

Substitution preservation is represented inside
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Typing}. The closed binder-domain form,
$\mathrm{substitute\_preserves\_typing\_closed\_binder\_domains}$,
gives the binder-domain complete closed variant: when the substituted
domain, source term, and binder-domain typing surface are closed at the
relevant boundary, substitution preserves the resulting typing judgment.
The same module family records the closedness, prefix-removal, and
$\mathrm{HasType}$ projection lemmas used by that theorem.

Subject reduction is represented by
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReduction}. It contains the beta case, the main
subject-reduction theorem parameterized by the substitution-preservation and
stability premises, and the congruence cases for application heads,
application arguments, lambda bodies, and Pi codomains. The confluence
surface in \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Confluence} supplies beta-star and parallel
beta relations, the embedding of one-step beta into parallel beta, and
diamond components for the variable, sort, application, lambda, and Pi
cases. The normalization surface in
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.Normalization} proves the atomic no-step cases for
sorts and closed variables, including the closed atomic normal-form theorem.

The demonstration layer consists of \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.AtomInfer}, which
infers and proves soundness for sort and variable atoms, and
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.TypedExamples}, which gives concrete typing derivations
for variables, Pi terms, lambda identity terms, and applications. The whole
MetaCIC reconstruction is maintained as axiom-free under
\texttt{\#print axioms}: the audited theorem dependencies do not include
\texttt{propext}, \texttt{Classical.choice}, or \texttt{Quot.sound}.
