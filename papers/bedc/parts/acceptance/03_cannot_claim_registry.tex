\chapter{Cannot-Claim Registry and Failure Witnesses}
\label{ch:acceptance-cannot-claim}
\origin{human}

The closure-and-verification discipline of \autoref{ch:closure-and-verification-discipline} states what the manuscript may export on the closure axis and what its Lean targets audit on the verification axis; the standard bridge protocol of \autoref{ch:acceptance-bridge} states the additional discipline that $\bridgedClosure$ export requires. Both chapters describe the positive face of the project's discipline. This chapter records the negative face: the typed failure witnesses that block a closure or verification claim and the explicit cannot-claim registry that records which export claims the manuscript refuses on principle. Read together with \autoref{rem:three-axioms-gate-meaning}, the cannot-claim registry plays at the export level the same role that axiom-purity plays at the kernel level: a refusal to admit an unwitnessed positive claim.

\section{The discipline of negative claims}
\label{sec:acceptance-cannot-claim-discipline}

\begin{remark}[Negative claims as part of the export surface]
\label{rem:acceptance-negative-claims-surface}
A research manuscript that exhibits only its positive theorems implicitly invites readers to infer the rest of mathematics is on its way. The BEDC discipline is the opposite: every claim outside the certified surface is presumed unsupported until a certificate is exhibited, and the manuscript states explicitly which downstream claims it refuses to make at the present closure grade. The cannot-claim registry of \autoref{def:acceptance-cannot-claim-entry} is the explicit form of that refusal.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Failure witnesses as negative duals of certificates]
\label{rem:acceptance-failure-witness-dual}
A certificate exhibits the structure that licenses an export. A failure witness exhibits the structure that blocks one. Both are typed: the certificate populates a fixed schema of fields, and the failure witness selects one of a fixed list of failure modes. The pairing is total: at any given moment an object either satisfies $\TheoryGate(N, c)$ and $\FormalStatus(N, v)$ at the grades it claims, in which case its paper witnesses and audit witnesses are exhibited; or it does not, in which case a failure witness names the missing field.
\end{remark}

\section{Failure witness type}
\label{sec:acceptance-failure-cert}

\begin{definition}[Failure certificate]
\label{def:acceptance-failure-cert}
The \emph{failure certificate} $\FailureCert(N, c, v)$ at the candidate closure grade $c$ and verification grade $v$ for an object $N$ is one of the thirteen typed branches:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \texttt{missing-source}: no source-layer assignment in the sense of \autoref{def:closure-cert-fields};
  \item \texttt{missing-classifier}: no classifier supplied for the cited source;
  \item \texttt{classifier-not-transitive}: classifier supplied but transitivity fails or is unproved;
  \item \texttt{exactness-fails}: classifier does not respect $\msame / \hsame / \psame$ on the cited source;
  \item \texttt{operation-does-not-descend}: a kernel operation fails to descend through the classifier;
  \item \texttt{gap-ledger-absent}: the ledger field is unspecified and no explicit absence statement is supplied;
  \item \texttt{completion-not-stable}: a completion construction fails the stability field;
  \item \texttt{scope-overstated}: the closure block claims a grade whose scope exceeds what the chapter actually closes (e.g.\ $\publicClosure$ asserted for a singleton certificate);
  \item \texttt{standard-bridge-fails}: $\StdBridge(N,T)$ is incomplete in at least one of its six fields when $\bridgedClosure$ is claimed;
  \item \texttt{host-primitive-leakage}: the no-host-leak field is violated, that is, host $\mathsf{Nat}$, $\mathsf{Quot}$, $\mathsf{propext}$, or $\mathsf{Classical.choice}$ appears in the BEDC public surface for $N$;
  \item \texttt{theorem-checker-failure}: a Lean target cited under $\verb|\leanchecked|$ is missing or fails to build (refutes $\theoremCheckedV$);
  \item \texttt{audit-not-clean}: \texttt{lean4/scripts/bedc\_ci.py audit} reports unresolved markers on the cited targets (refutes $\auditCleanV$);
  \item \texttt{axis-confusion}: the chapter's prose, table, or marker discipline assigns a closure grade as a function of a verification grade (or vice versa), in violation of \autoref{prin:verification-not-formation}.
\end{enumerate}
A claim that $\TheoryGate(N, c)$ holds is refuted by exhibiting any one of branches 1--10. A claim that $\FormalStatus(N, v)$ holds is refuted by branches 11--12. A claim that the discipline of \autoref{ch:closure-and-verification-discipline} is honoured is refuted by branch 13.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Failure witness blocks the gate]
\label{thm:acceptance-failure-blocks-gate}
If $\FailureCert(N, c, v)$ is exhibited against a claimed gate, then that claimed gate does not hold: branches 1--10 block $\TheoryGate(N, c)$, branches 11--12 block $\FormalStatus(N, v)$, and branch 13 blocks the two-axis discipline that would license $\VerifiedGate(N, c, v)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Each branch of \autoref{def:acceptance-failure-cert} negates a clause of \autoref{def:closure-cert-fields}, \autoref{def:acceptance-standard-bridge}, \autoref{def:marker-to-verification}, or \autoref{prin:verification-not-formation}. A field that the claimed gate requires is absent, invalid, or assigned on the wrong axis by exhibiting the branch; therefore the corresponding conjunction fails.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why thirteen branches and not fewer]
\label{rem:acceptance-thirteen-branches-rationale}
The thirteen branches of \autoref{def:acceptance-failure-cert} are not a partition into incomparable failure modes; some branches are special cases of others read at different closure or verification grades. The list is finite and explicit so that a given audit pass can refer to a single branch when refuting an export claim, and so that grade decisions can be local: a failure cited under one branch is the obligation that needs discharging before a stronger gate is claimed.
\end{remark}

\section{Cannot-claim registry}
\label{sec:acceptance-cannot-claim-registry}

\begin{definition}[Cannot-claim entry]
\label{def:acceptance-cannot-claim-entry}
A \emph{cannot-claim entry} is a quadruple
$$
  (\mathrm{ClaimText},\ \mathrm{Status},\ \mathrm{WhyNotClaimable},\ \mathrm{RequiredUpgrade})
$$
where $\mathrm{ClaimText}$ is a sentence the manuscript refuses to assert at the present time; $\mathrm{Status}$ is one of $\{\mathtt{forbidden}, \mathtt{deferred}, \mathtt{partial}\}$; $\mathrm{WhyNotClaimable}$ cites either an open obligation or a structural reason; and $\mathrm{RequiredUpgrade}$ names the certificate or bridge whose discharge would license the claim.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Cannot-claim registry]
\label{def:acceptance-cannot-claim-registry}
The \emph{cannot-claim registry} is the finite list of cannot-claim entries that the manuscript currently records. The registry is part of the manuscript's export surface in the sense of \autoref{rem:acceptance-negative-claims-surface}: removing an entry requires the corresponding upgrade discharged, and adding an entry records a new refusal.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Anchor entries of the registry]
\label{rem:acceptance-anchor-entries}
The four anchor entries below name claims that the manuscript explicitly refuses at the present closure and verification grades. They are the registry: principled refusals that this chapter commits to fixed phrasing. A claim's removal from the registry, or its addition, is a manuscript edit at the cadence of editorial review, not an automated state report.
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Anchor 1.} $\mathrm{ClaimText}$: ``BEDC derives all mathematics.'' $\mathrm{Status}$: \texttt{forbidden}. $\mathrm{WhyNotClaimable}$: the kernel admits only finite generative content; downstream theories enter through naming certificates and standard bridges, not as primitives. $\mathrm{RequiredUpgrade}$: there is no upgrade. The claim is refused on principle, not deferred.
  \item \textbf{Anchor 2.} $\mathrm{ClaimText}$: ``$\NatUp$ is the full Peano natural numbers.'' $\mathrm{Status}$: \texttt{partial}. $\mathrm{WhyNotClaimable}$: $\NatUp$ records unary repetition histories with $\TheoryGate(\NatUp, \scopedClosure)$ on the canonical NameCert scope; full Peano content (induction principle as a closed theorem schema, primitive recursion with computation rules, totality of arithmetic operations) requires the upgrade $\TheoryGate(\NatUp, \matureClosure)$. $\mathrm{RequiredUpgrade}$: $\NatUp$'s arithmetic test suite (induction, recursion, addition associativity and commutativity, multiplication distributivity, order totality) closed at $\matureClosure$ on the closure axis. The verification axis grade does not by itself close any further Peano content.
  \item \textbf{Anchor 3.} $\mathrm{ClaimText}$: ``A real-valued completion is exported.'' $\mathrm{Status}$: \texttt{deferred}. $\mathrm{WhyNotClaimable}$: the seal/completion module of \autoref{ch:core-thin-seal-interface} is a thin interface; Cauchy equivalence, completion existence, uniqueness of limits, and the field-operation descent are not discharged. $\mathrm{RequiredUpgrade}$: a Seal/Completion certificate satisfying \autoref{def:acceptance-closure-reflection-obligation} on Cauchy equivalence, with field operations descending to the completion.
  \item \textbf{Anchor 4.} $\mathrm{ClaimText}$: ``The Lean scaffold is verified end-to-end.'' $\mathrm{Status}$: \texttt{forbidden}. $\mathrm{WhyNotClaimable}$: the scaffold of \autoref{ch:proof-obligations-lean-scaffold-contract} is a statement-level surface; verification means each scaffold target stands at $\theoremCheckedV$ in \texttt{lean4/BEDC/} with a successful build report and at $\auditCleanV$ on \texttt{bedc\_ci.py audit}. $\mathrm{RequiredUpgrade}$: a complete checker report from \texttt{lake build} together with the audit of \texttt{lean4/scripts/bedc\_ci.py} and the axiom-purity gate, all exiting clean. This upgrade strengthens the verification axis only; it does not by itself close any object's theory.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The registry is finite by design]
\label{rem:acceptance-registry-finite}
The cannot-claim registry of \autoref{def:acceptance-cannot-claim-registry} is a finite list because the chapter's discipline is to commit only to refusals it can phrase precisely. A status report enumerating every object that does not currently meet $\TheoryGate(N, \scopedClosure)$ would mistake a derivation queue for a refusal: an object that is presently at $\seedClosure$ for lack of a classifier is not a refused claim but an unfinished one. The registry therefore lists principled refusals (Anchor 1, Anchor 4) and bounded deferred claims with explicit upgrade obligations (Anchor 2, Anchor 3); per-object derivation status is read from chapter closurestatus blocks and the audit script of \autoref{lem:audit-reflects-verification}, not from the registry.
\end{remark}

\section{Closure reading convention}
\label{sec:closure-reading-convention}

\begin{remark}[Reading a closure claim]
\label{rem:closure-reading-convention}
A theoretical closure claim and a formal verification claim are distinct. A line of the form $\TheoryGate(N, \scopedClosure)$ means that the paper supplies the source, classifier, exactness, ledger, and stability witnesses needed to close $N$ within the stated scope. A line of the form $\FormalStatus(N, \theoremCheckedV)$ means that the cited Lean target has been machine-checked. The second claim audits the first; it does not create the first. A theory may be closed without being machine-checked. Conversely, a checked scaffold or checked definition does not by itself close the corresponding mathematical object.
\end{remark}

\section{Anti-confusion register}
\label{sec:anti-confusion-register}

\begin{remark}[Confusions explicitly refused]
\label{rem:anti-confusion-list}
The following readings are refused. Citing any one of them is a discipline violation that the audit catches via the \texttt{axis-confusion} branch of \autoref{def:acceptance-failure-cert}.
\begin{enumerate}
  \item ``BEDC has re-derived all of mature mathematics.''
  \item ``$\RealUp$ is complete.''
  \item ``$\SOneUp$ is complete.''
  \item ``$\FoldUp$ is complete.''
  \item ``$\NatUp$ at $\scopedClosure$ equals full natural-number theory.''
  \item ``$\AddUp$ at $\scopedClosure$ equals full addition.''
  \item ``Paper certificates equal machine-checked certificates.''
  \item ``Lean scaffolds equal verified implementations.''
  \item ``External standard models replace BEDC internal proof obligations.''
  \item ``Host equality, host $\mathsf{Nat}$, or host functions are part of the BEDC public surface.''
  \item ``Machine verification is required for theoretical closure.''
  \item ``Without $\theoremCheckedV$ no closure grade above $\seedClosure$ may be claimed.''
  \item ``A checked theorem-shape scaffold closes the corresponding object theory.''
  \item ``A scoped certificate closure is a full traditional-object reconstruction.''
  \item ``A bridge is closed merely because the BEDC-side certificate is checked.''
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\section{Failure witness as audit input}
\label{sec:acceptance-cannot-claim-audit}

\begin{remark}[Audit policy when a failure is exhibited]
\label{rem:acceptance-audit-failure-policy}
When a failure certificate is exhibited for an object $N$ presently at $\TheoryGate(N, c)$ with $c \ge \scopedClosure$, or at $\FormalStatus(N, v)$ with $v \ge \theoremCheckedV$, the closurestatus block of the chapter is downgraded by the discipline of \autoref{def:marker-to-verification}: a $\verb|\leanchecked|$ becomes a $\verb|\leanstmt|$ if the source still survives but exactness no longer holds, or a $\verb|\leansorryd|$ if the proof has been retracted; the closure grade is lowered to the highest grade still licensed by the surviving witnesses. The audit script of \autoref{lem:audit-reflects-verification} reflects only the verification axis; the closure grade is reflected by the chapter's closurestatus block.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The cannot-claim registry is not a roadmap]
\label{rem:acceptance-not-roadmap}
A roadmap states what will be claimed when the present blockers are cleared. The cannot-claim registry of \autoref{def:acceptance-cannot-claim-registry} states what the manuscript currently refuses. The two artefacts are not the same: a refusal that is principled (Anchor 1) does not become a future claim regardless of progress; a refusal that is deferred (Anchor 3) becomes a future claim only when the named upgrade is discharged. The registry is the negative face of the manuscript, not the schedule of what the manuscript will eventually admit.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Composition with the axiom-purity gate]
\label{rem:acceptance-compose-axiom-purity}
The axiom-purity gate of \autoref{rem:three-axioms-gate-meaning} refuses kernel theorems whose transitive dependencies include the three meta-closure axioms. The cannot-claim registry refuses export claims whose certificates or bridges are absent. The two refusals operate at different layers and compose: a kernel theorem permitted by axiom-purity may still fail to license an export claim if its standard bridge is incomplete, and an export claim refused by the registry does not become licit by the kernel passing its purity gate.
\end{remark}
