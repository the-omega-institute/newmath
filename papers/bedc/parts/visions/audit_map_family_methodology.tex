\chapter{The audit-map family: a BEDC methodology}
\label{ch:audit-map-family-methodology}

The BEDC.MetaCIC material is not a single opaque theorem bundle. It is a
family of Lean substrate modules, paper audit maps, obstruction records,
assembly chapters, and discharge-route chapters whose purpose is to keep the
project's meta-theory readable at module granularity. This chapter names that
family as a methodology.

\concretizedIn{ch:concrete-instances-auditmapinterface-namecert}{Audit-map family methodology is realized as a finite BHist NameCert packet for positive, conditional, obstruction, frontier, and cross-map consumer rows.}

The audit-map family sits beside
\autoref{ch:visions-apophatic-naming-as-discipline} and
\autoref{ch:visions-certificate-closure-and-generator-induction}. Apophatic
naming says that an open dependency must have a stable name rather than a
hidden status. Generator-closure discipline says that a proof may range only
over the authorized generator whose closure is displayed. The audit-map family
is the third concrete application: every major MetaCIC substrate is required to
publish its established content, conditional content, obstruction content, and
frontier.

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-setup}

BEDC.MetaCIC is distributed across several formal modules: subject reduction,
confluence, normalisation, substitution, decidable checking, typed examples,
beta reduction, and closure preservation. The modules do not all have the same
closure status. Some nodes are unconditional. Some are conditional on an
explicit interface. Some expose an obstruction theorem. Some are examples or
boundary checks that prevent a nearby over-reading.

A paper chapter that merely announced "MetaCIC is partially formalised" would
lose the important information. The reader would not know which substrate
exports usable theorems, which substrate exports a parameterised route, which
substrate exports a no-go result, and which substrate is needed by a later
assembly. BEDC's apophatic naming discipline rejects that kind of blur. Each
open cell has to be visible.

The audit-map family is the systematic answer. Each major MetaCIC substrate
gets a paper map. The map says what the Lean substrate currently establishes,
what it establishes only under named parameters, what it blocks, and how its
frontier connects to the rest of the consistency path. The family is therefore
not a retrospective aside. It is part of the method by which BEDC prevents
partial closure from turning into implicit closure.

\section{What an audit map is}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-what-map-is}

\begin{definition}[Audit map]
\label{def:audit-map-family-audit-map}
An \emph{audit map} is a paper chapter focused on one Lean substrate
submodule. It records the checked inventory, the conditional inventory, the
obstruction inventory, and the cross-module frontier of that submodule without
claiming that the inventory has closed the neighbouring modules.
\end{definition}

The definition has several layers.

First, the unit is a paper chapter, not a footnote. The chapter format matters
because each substrate has enough structure to require its own setup, local
vocabulary, and boundary statement.

Second, the focus is one Lean substrate submodule or one tightly bounded
assembly. A confluence audit map is about confluence. A substitution audit map
is about closed syntax algebra. A consistency assembly map is about the
normal-form consistency branch and the subject-reduction transport boundary.
The point is module-level granularity.

Third, established theorems are listed by Lean-target references in the local
map. Those references are usually rendered as variants because the map is an
inventory site rather than the canonical primary theorem site. This keeps the
paper honest about what the Lean kernel actually checks while avoiding a second
primary claim for the same formal object.

Fourth, conditional or parameterised theorems are not weakened into prose. A
conditional diamond remains a conditional diamond. A subject-reduction route
that needs a discharge bundle remains a route that needs that bundle. A
normalisation theorem mediated by candidate evidence remains mediated by that
evidence.

Fifth, obstruction and no-go results are first-class entries. The subject
reduction obstruction chapter is not a failure appendix. It records the closed
application obstruction and the exact interface needed to move past it. That is
BEDC's apophatic naming discipline operating inside formal meta-theory.

Sixth, each map names its frontier and cross-module dependencies. The reader
can see which facts are consumed by synthesis, which facts are local sanity
checks, and which facts would be needed before a stronger assembly is allowed.

\section{The nine audit map chapters of BEDC.MetaCIC}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-nine-chapters}

The family has nine primary audit-map chapters.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction} records the subject
reduction obstruction. It names the dependent-codomain and closed-application
pressure point, records the parameterised preservation interface, and prevents
unparameterised closed subject reduction from being treated as available.
\item \autoref{ch:closed-metacic-consistency-assembly} records the
closed-MetaCIC consistency assembly. It separates closed-normal consistency
from full closed consistency and marks subject reduction as the transport
boundary between the two.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-confluence-audit-map} records the confluence
substrate. It distinguishes reduction algebra, atom fragments, closed-normal
fragments, and the substitution-sensitive diamond route.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-normalization-audit-map} records the normalisation
substrate. It shows the candidate-mediated strong-normalisation route and
keeps candidate-free global normalisation outside the present claim.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-substitution-audit-map} records the substitution
substrate. It supplies closed shift, closed substitution, and closed
composition fragments without converting them into beta typing preservation.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-decidable-audit-map} records the decidable-checking
substrate. It exposes executable equality and type-checking surfaces while
refusing to call them a global decision oracle for meta-theory.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-typedexamples-audit-map} records the typed-example
substrate. It treats examples as checked calibration surfaces rather than as
silent evidence for arbitrary closed terms.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-beta-audit-map} records the beta-reduction
substrate. It separates one-step beta structure, star closure, normality, and
transport interfaces needed by neighbouring maps.
\item \autoref{ch:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map} records the
closure-preservation substrate. It tracks which generators preserve closedness
and which preservation statements belong to other modules.
\end{enumerate}

The family also has four companion meta chapters.
\autoref{ch:metacic-audit-synthesis} reads the maps together as the
closed-CIC critical path. \autoref{ch:metacic-open-problems} consolidates the
open obligations so the same frontier is not scattered across prose.
\autoref{ch:closed-normal-consistency-main-result} elevates the strongest
available closed-normal result as a main-result node.
\autoref{ch:sr-discharge-routes} records the concrete routes by which the
subject-reduction boundary could be discharged.

Taken together, these chapters form a substrate-level inventory. The inventory
is deliberately redundant in one sense: the same boundary can appear in a local
map, in synthesis, and in open problems. That redundancy is controlled. It
keeps the boundary visible from the module view, the critical-path view, and
the obligation view.

\section{Common structure across audit maps}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-common-structure}

The maps use a shared writing structure.

\begin{description}
\item[Setup.] The map names the Lean files, relations, predicates, and
interfaces that form the local substrate.
\item[Established.] The map lists the checked results the paper may cite
directly.
\item[Conditional.] The map records parameterised theorems and the exact
inputs they require.
\item[Obstruction.] The map records counterexamples, impossibility results, or
shape restrictions that block a stronger reading.
\item[Frontier.] The map states the strongest nearby claim that remains
outside the present substrate.
\item[Cross-reference.] The map identifies the neighbouring maps that consume
or supply its facts.
\item[Boundary.] The map ends by stating what the chapter does not claim.
\end{description}

\begin{principle}[Audit-map template]
\label{prin:audit-map-family-template}
An audit map is complete only when it records positive content, conditional
content, obstruction content, frontier content, and cross-module use. Omitting
any one of these parts turns a partial-closure map into an ambiguous progress
summary.
\end{principle}

This template is intentionally stricter than an ordinary literature overview.
It is meant to be read against the Lean substrate and against the paper-Lean
audit. A result that is checked but local remains local. A parameter remains a
parameter. A blocked route remains blocked. A cross-module use must point to
the map that supplies the relevant node.

\section{Methodology versus other disciplines}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-versus-other-disciplines}

The audit-map family differs from a cataphatic results-first discipline. A
cataphatic presentation often foregrounds the main theorem and leaves adjacent
work implicit. BEDC cannot use that style for MetaCIC because the important
information is the shape of the partial closure: which generators are closed,
which transports are conditional, and which obstructions are theorem-level
facts.

The family also differs from silent proof holes. A hidden \texttt{sorry}, an
\texttt{admit}, or an unrecorded host assumption would make the map dishonest.
BEDC forbids that move. The paper-side analogue is equally strict: an open
obligation may be named, routed, and studied, but it may not be treated as
internal theorem content.

Finally, the family differs from a single high-level retrospective. A single
summary can help orientation, but it cannot replace module-level audit. The
BEDC pattern is module-level maps plus cross-module synthesis. The local maps
preserve granularity; the synthesis shows how the local nodes compose or fail
to compose.

\section{When to use this methodology}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-when-to-use}

The audit-map methodology is appropriate when a formalisation project has many
submodules, each with a different closure state. It is especially useful for
meta-theory, where a theorem in one module is often a premise, obstruction, or
calibration check for another module. Subject reduction, confluence,
normalisation, substitution, decidability, examples, beta reduction, closure
preservation, and consistency assembly are exactly that kind of landscape.

It is not the right tool for every theorem. A single self-contained result with
a direct proof does not need a family of audit maps. In that case the paper can
state the theorem, give the proof, and cite the Lean target at the primary
site. The audit-map family is for a different shape: distributed partial
closure with named obligations and cross-module dependencies.

\section{Audit-map family as apophatic naming in action}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-apophatic-application}

\autoref{ch:visions-apophatic-naming-as-discipline} gives the general rule:
when a dependency cannot be generated, defined, certified, or discharged inside
the displayed substrate, the dependency must be named. The audit-map family is
that rule applied to MetaCIC formalisation.

Each audit map is a collection of apophatic names at module scale. It names the
open obligations, obstruction points, conditional interfaces, and frontier
claims that would otherwise disappear into prose. The family as a whole is a
substrate-level inventory of those names. It tells the reader where the project
has generator-internal closure and where the project has an exposed socket.

This is why the methodology pairs with
\autoref{ch:visions-certificate-closure-and-generator-induction}. Generator
closure says that a certificate ranges over the objects produced by a displayed
generator. The audit-map family asks the same question at chapter scale: which
generator, relation, or interface licenses the local claim, and where does that
licence stop?

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:audit-map-family-boundary}

The audit-map family does not solve any substantive proof obligation by naming
it. It does not prove subject reduction. It does not turn conditional
confluence into unconditional confluence. It does not turn candidate-mediated
normalisation into a candidate-free theorem. It does not lift closed-normal
consistency to full closed consistency.

Its role is process discipline, not result discipline. It makes the proof
state explicit enough that later work can cite the right node, refuse the wrong
node, and see the exact dependency that would have to be supplied. That is a
BEDC methodology: not silence about open cells, not hidden assumptions, and not
blanket confidence, but a family of audit maps whose open names remain visible.
