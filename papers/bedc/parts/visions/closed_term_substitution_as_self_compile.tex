\chapter{Closed-term substitution as the self-compilation step}
\label{ch:closed-term-substitution-self-compile}

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-setup}

BEDC.MetaCIC represents a small CIC-like term language by the closed generator
\texttt{Term}.

The generator has exactly five surface forms:
\texttt{var},
\texttt{app},
\texttt{lam},
\texttt{pi},
and \texttt{sort}.

The closedness classifier for this generator is \texttt{ClosedAt}.

For a depth $d$ and a term $t$,
\texttt{ClosedAt} $d$ $t$ says that every free de Bruijn index visible in $t$
lies below the boundary $d$.

The boundary is not cosmetic.

It is the local budget by which the term may use names already present in the
ambient binder context.

At depth $0$ the classifier marks genuinely closed terms.

At larger depth it marks terms closed relative to the registered variables below
that depth.

Thus \texttt{ClosedAt} is not merely a syntactic predicate.

It is the classifier that tells the MetaCIC layer which terms remain inside the
registered binder ledger.

The two operations at issue are \texttt{shift} and \texttt{substitute}.

\texttt{shift} raises free indices at or above a cutoff.

\texttt{substitute} replaces the variable at a specified depth and adjusts
indices around the removed binder.

Both operations are ordinary generators over \texttt{Term}: they traverse the
closed constructor list and produce another \texttt{Term}.

But only some generator actions preserve a given classifier.

The self-compilation reading depends exactly on that distinction.

A compiler that compiles itself must feed compiler-shaped source terms through
the evaluator it implements.

Inside that evaluator, substitution is not optional bookkeeping.

It is the step that pushes an actual argument into a binder body.

If this step could cross the closedness boundary, the self-compilation chain
would silently acquire an unregistered free variable.

The chain would then cease to be a chain of BEDC-certified records.

It would be a chain whose later stage contains a name that no prior certificate
licensed.

BEDC therefore reads closed-term substitution as a certificate closure
condition.

The relevant claim is not that every substitution preserves every semantic
property.

The relevant claim is narrower and sharper: when a term is already closed at the
substitution depth, the attempted substitution at that boundary is syntactically
inert.

No hidden variable is consumed.

No variable is smuggled through the boundary.

No future binder budget is borrowed to make the operation typecheck.

In formula form, the MetaCIC witness says:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(d,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{substitute}(d,v,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the form of closure that self-compilation needs at the substitution
site.

The evaluator may attempt the syntactic operation, but the registered
closedness certificate proves that the operation cannot alter a term whose free
variables all sit below the boundary.

The source term remains inside the classifier.

The self-application step remains inside the same record discipline.

The compiler does not borrow an unrecorded name from the future of its own
compilation chain.

\section{The Lean witness}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-lean-witness}

Two theorems from \texttt{lean4/BEDC/MetaCIC/ClosedTerm.lean} carry the technical content:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{shift\_closed} -- \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.shift\_closed}
  \item \texttt{substitute\_closed} -- \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_closed}
  \item \texttt{closed\_under\_substitute\_classifier} -- \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_under\_substitute\_classifier} (instance of \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.GeneratorClosureClassifier})
\end{itemize}

\texttt{shift\_closed} states that shifting a term at its closedness boundary by
one position leaves the term unchanged.

Its statement is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{shift}(n,1,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

The proof is structural over the \texttt{ClosedAt} witness.

In the variable case, the inequality $i<n$ forces the cutoff test
$n \leq i$ to be false.

In the compound cases, the induction hypotheses pass through
\texttt{app},
\texttt{lam},
and \texttt{pi}.

The binder cases raise the boundary on the body or codomain, matching the
definition of \texttt{ClosedAt}.

The universe case is immediate.

The theorem says that a closed term already pays its depth bill.

Raising the external context boundary does not require rewriting the term,
because no free index reaches the cutoff.

This is the boundary budget law for self-compilation.

When a closed source fragment is moved into a deeper binder context, the chain
does not reach into a later context to justify the move.

The proof spends only the already recorded inequality inside
\texttt{ClosedAt}.

\texttt{substitute\_closed} states that substituting at the boundary of a closed
term leaves the term unchanged:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(d,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{substitute}(d,v,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

The value $v$ is arbitrary.

That arbitrariness matters.

The theorem does not inspect $v$ because the substitution site is absent.

For a variable index $i$ in a term closed at $d$,
the witness gives $i<d$.

Consequently $i=d$ is false, and $d<i$ is false.

The substitution branch cannot fire, and the decrement branch cannot fire.

The variable is returned as itself.

The compound cases again follow the \texttt{ClosedAt} constructor tree.

Under binders, the implementation shifts the substitution value while increasing
the depth, but the theorem proves that this shifted value is never observed in a
closed-at-boundary body.

That is the self-compilation content.

The evaluator may carry a substitution payload through the recursive traversal,
yet the classifier prevents the payload from becoming a hidden source of free
variables.

The accepted record is the equality of the syntactic output with the input term.

The certificate is the \texttt{ClosedAt} derivation.

The generator is \texttt{substitute}.

The closure law is that the generator action is inert on the classified slice.

\section{Why this is the self-compilation step}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-self-compilation-step}

Self-compilation is not a single mystical event.

In BEDC it is a chain of ordinary certified steps.

Each step takes a record already admitted by a classifier, applies an authorized
generator, and checks that the resulting record remains inside the promised
scope.

For MetaCIC, evaluation under binders inevitably uses substitution.

A compiler source term contains binder bodies.

The evaluator implemented by the compiler opens those binders when it applies
functions.

The self-compilation chain therefore cannot avoid the following local question:
when the compiler's own evaluator substitutes during evaluation, does the
operation preserve the closedness ledger of the source term?

\texttt{substitute\_closed} answers the question at the exact boundary where the
risk appears.

If the source fragment is closed relative to depth $d$, then a substitution at
$d$ has no target inside the fragment.

The operation returns the same term.

Hence the self-compilation chain does not create an unregistered free variable
at that step.

The compiler may feed its own term representation to its own evaluator without
turning closed source into boundary-leaking source at this operation.

The theorem is therefore a local non-escape certificate.

\concretizedIn{ch:concrete-instances-closedsubstitutionseal-namecert}{Closed substitution is realized as a finite seal whose inert boundary is certified by the MetaCIC closedness classifier.}

It is local because it speaks only about the \texttt{substitute} operation at a
single depth.

It is a certificate because it is a checked Lean theorem whose proof follows
only the closed constructor tree and elementary Nat tests.

It is a non-escape claim because every possible route for the substitution
payload to enter the term is blocked by the inequalities in the
\texttt{ClosedAt} witness.

The same reading applies to \texttt{shift\_closed}.

Self-compilation moves term fragments through binder contexts.

Moving through a binder context normally requires shifting free variables.

But a term closed below the relevant cutoff contains no free variable at that
cutoff.

Shifting it at the boundary is therefore inert.

The chain can deepen the surrounding context without borrowing a depth that has
not yet been registered.

This is why \texttt{shift\_closed} is the boundary budget law.

Depth behaves monotonically with respect to the certificate already held by the
term.

The term does not need a later binder to justify its present closedness.

The pair of theorems gives the self-compilation step its exact BEDC shape:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \text{registered closed source}
  &\xrightarrow{\ \mathsf{shift}\ }
  \text{same registered source},\\
  \text{registered closed source}
  &\xrightarrow{\ \mathsf{substitute}\ }
  \text{same registered source}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The equalities are stronger than mere preservation of a predicate.

They state that, at the closed boundary, the operational generator cannot alter
the term at all.

The self-compilation chain is therefore protected at the exact place where
capture, escape, or name leakage would otherwise enter.

\section{Generator-closure classifier reading}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-generator-closure-classifier}

The same facts can be read in the certificate-closure vocabulary.

\texttt{ClosedAt} is the classifier.

\texttt{Term} is the authorized generator carrier.

\texttt{shift} and \texttt{substitute} are authorized generator operations over
that carrier.

\texttt{shift\_closed} and \texttt{substitute\_closed} are generator-closure
laws for the classified slice.

This is not an analogy.

It is the literal BEDC structure.

A generator-closure classifier states which records remain inside the licensed
generator scope after an authorized operation is applied.

Here the records are MetaCIC terms.

The classifier is \texttt{ClosedAt}.

The authorized operations are the syntactic traversals in
\texttt{Syntax.lean}.

The closure law says that the operation cannot escape the classifier's boundary.

For \texttt{substitute}, the theorem is especially sharp.

The classifier does not merely say that the output is again closed.

It says the output is the input.

Thus every classified term is a fixed point of substitution at its boundary.

The name certificate reading is then immediate.

A naming certificate promotes a stable generated behaviour to a derived
interface.

The stable behaviour here is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  t \in \mathsf{ClosedAt}(d)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{substitute}(d,v,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

The certificate does not license arbitrary semantic substitution.

It licenses a boundary fact about closed terms.

It records that the generator action cannot access a variable whose existence
the classifier has ruled out.

The \NameCert{} surface therefore reads the theorem as a closure classifier over
the \texttt{Term} generator.

The theorem belongs to the records side of BEDC because its proof is mechanical
structural recursion over a closed inductive witness.

It does not ask for a free-floating universal principle over all possible
syntax.

It ranges over the closure of the declared \texttt{Term} and \texttt{ClosedAt}
generators.

Within that closure, it is fully internal.

Beyond that closure, BEDC does not pretend that the theorem has authority.

The \NameCert{} classifier says "this generator, this boundary, this closure
law".

It does not say "all imaginable binders in all possible metatheories".

That distinction is the exact difference between BEDC's generator-internal
induction and the forward-binding belief whose far end is the $\mathsf{T}$-socket.

\section{Audit gate as the executable form of the classifier}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-audit-gate}

The audit gate is the executable form of this classifier discipline.

The paper marker \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_closed}
points at a concrete Lean theorem.

The project audit verifies that the target exists.

The Lean build verifies that the proof elaborates.

The strict axiom-purity audit inspects the transitive dependency closure of the
accepted theorem.

If the proof of \texttt{substitute\_closed} depended on a hidden substrate
commitment such as \texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound}, or
\texttt{propext}, the audit would reject it.

This is not a separate quality check layered on top of the mathematics.

It is the operational classifier.

The classifier says that the generator action remains inside the authorized
closure.

The audit checks that the Lean witness to that statement is itself generated
inside the authorized proof discipline.

A proof that smuggled in a forbidden host axiom would be a generator escape at
the proof layer, even if its statement had the right shape.

The strict audit therefore enforces the same closure law one stratum higher.

The syntactic generator \texttt{substitute} must not create a free variable in a
closed term.

The proof generator used by Lean must not create a hidden axiom dependency in a
closed BEDC theorem.

The two checks are not merely parallel.

They are the same generator-closure classifier pattern applied at two strata.

At the term stratum, \texttt{ClosedAt} classifies admissible MetaCIC terms.

At the proof-record stratum, the axiom-purity audit classifies admissible Lean
proof dependencies.

The theorem is accepted only when both strata close.

Thus the audit gate is the executable manifestation of certificate closure.

It turns the prose promise "substitution does not leak names" into a checkable
record whose proof also does not leak forbidden host commitments.

\section{Boundary: what this lemma does not claim}
\label{sec:closed-term-substitution-boundary}

\texttt{substitute\_closed} does not claim that substitution is semantically
correct for arbitrary open terms.

It does not claim subject reduction for the whole evaluator.

It does not claim that every compiler pass preserves every classifier.

It does not claim that BEDC internalises the host CIC's truth predicate.

It does not claim that self-compilation is completed by a single syntactic
lemma.

Its claim is exact:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(d,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{substitute}(d,v,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

The exactness is the point.

\concretizedIn{ch:concrete-instances-closed-substitution-boundary-namecert}{Closed substitution is represented by a finite boundary packet whose substitute route is inert on the \texttt{ClosedAt} slice.}

At the boundary where a substitution payload could otherwise enter, the
closedness classifier proves that there is no target for it.

The operation returns the original term.

The generator remains inside the classifier.

The proof remains inside the axiom-pure audit gate.

The self-compilation chain receives one local certificate that no unregistered
free variable enters through this operation.

\texttt{shift\_closed} has the matching boundary:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{shift}(n,1,t)=t.
\end{aligned}
$$

This law does not say that every shift is harmless.

It says that the shift at the closed boundary spends no future depth budget.

Together the two theorems give the self-compilation story a precise local
socket:

the compiler may pass closed source through binder movement and boundary
substitution without converting closure into name leakage.

Where the story asks for more than this, it must cite further certificates.

Where it asks for a universal claim beyond any authorized generator, BEDC marks
the position as a $\mathsf{T}$-socket rather than internalising it.

That is why the theorem is small but load-bearing.

It is the records-side generator-closure classifier instance at the substitution
step of self-compilation.

It does not complete the entire compiler story.

It closes this boundary, with this classifier, for this authorized generator,
under the executable audit gate.
