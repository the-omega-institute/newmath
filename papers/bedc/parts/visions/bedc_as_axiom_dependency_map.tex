\chapter{BEDC.MetaCIC and external axiom dependency maps}
\label{ch:bedc-axiom-dependency-map}

\section{The framing}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-reframing}

This chapter is read under the framing principle of
\autoref{ch:bedc-is-its-own-theory}: BEDC.MetaCIC is its own complete type
theory, defined by its own first-principles commitments. An axiom dependency
map appears when an external reader compares BEDC.MetaCIC with classical CIC.
That map is a byproduct of comparison, not the primary identity of
BEDC.MetaCIC.

The primary identity is the self-contained substrate: closed generators,
literal AST equality, structural recursors, zero-axiom strict purity, and
visible external boundaries. Classical CIC is a neighbouring type-theoretic
family with different design commitments. When a universal claim familiar from
that family is brought into contact with the BEDC.MetaCIC substrate, the
contact can be recorded as dependency data.

The external analytical tuple has the form:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\text{Mode},\text{Witness},\text{Required-Supply}).
$$
Here $\phi$ is a claim selected for comparison with classical CIC. The output
records whether the claim is internally provable in BEDC.MetaCIC, internally
provable on a stated surface, or externally supplied through a mechanical
witness and named boundary.

The tuple is therefore not a definition of BEDC.MetaCIC. It is a diagnostic
reading of what happens when a non-internal proof expectation meets the
BEDC.MetaCIC substrate. The value of the map is that it exposes dependency
budgets without changing the substrate's own identity.

\section{Three modes of engagement}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-three-modes}

For a comparison claim $\phi$, BEDC.MetaCIC has three relevant outcomes.

\subsection{Mode A: completely provable}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-mode-a}

Mode A means that $\phi$ is unconditionally provable inside the zero-axiom
substrate. Its required supply is empty, and its witness is the checked proof
term itself:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\mathrm{A},\text{unconditional proof},\emptyset).
$$

The closed-syntax algebra gives representative examples. Theorems such as
\texttt{shift\_closed}, \texttt{substitute\_closed},
\texttt{betaStep\_preserves\_closed}, and
\texttt{no\_closed\_normal\_proof\_of\_false} are BEDC.MetaCIC theorems in
their own right. In an external comparison, they mark the part of the familiar
MetaCIC story whose proof already follows from the BEDC.MetaCIC substrate.

Mode A is therefore an internality result. It says exactly which claims are
settled by the closed generators, literal equality, recursors, and purity gate
already present in the theory. The important point is not only that the theorem
exists, but that the theorem survives the strict axiom-purity gate.

\subsection{Mode B: partial discharge}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-mode-b}

Mode B means that $\phi$ contains a surface that is internally provable, while
the broader reading asks for supply beyond that surface. Its witness is the
checked restricted theorem:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\mathrm{B},\text{restricted proof},\text{restricted-surface condition}).
$$

The subject-reduction application case supplies the sharp example. The theorem
\texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_independent\_codomain} proves the
closed application preservation statement when the codomain is closed
independently of the argument. On that surface, no conversion oracle is part of
the reasoning: closed substitution reads back the same codomain.

Mode B is more informative than a binary status. It says that a comparison
claim contains an internally clean subclaim. BEDC.MetaCIC separates the region
where literal syntax, closedness, and the represented reduction relation
already suffice from the region where an external proof route would have to
add supply.

\subsection{Mode C: counterexample plus named obligation}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-mode-c}

Mode C means that the unrestricted $\phi$ is not substrate-internal as stated.
The output is not silence and not an informal caveat. It is a counterexample,
together with named obligation sockets for the externally supplied content:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\mathrm{C},\text{counterexample plus named socket},\text{specific supply rows}).
$$

The same subject-reduction application case gives the canonical witness. The
theorem \texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_counterexample} exhibits a
closed function, a closed reducing argument, a closed application step, and an
original type that the reduct does not keep. The obstruction is not a vague
warning about dependent types. It is a checked dependent-codomain
counterexample in the BEDC.MetaCIC substrate.

The corresponding named sockets are the four subject-reduction obligations
\texttt{BetaSubstitutionPreservation}, \texttt{AppArgTypeStable},
\texttt{LamDomainSubjectReduction}, and \texttt{PiDomainSubjectReduction}.
They mark the precise pieces of externally supplied reasoning required before
the parameterised subject-reduction route can be read as an unparameterised
closed theorem.

Mode C is the place where boundary material becomes positive data. A
documented obstruction is a dependency witness. A named hypothesis is a
dependency socket. A counterexample is a mechanical certificate that the
universal comparison claim is not substrate-internal without explicit supply.

\section{The comparison map}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-deliverable}

The primary deliverable remains BEDC.MetaCIC itself: an audit-clean
closed-observational substrate with its checked theorems and named boundaries.
The dependency map is a secondary analytical product obtained by comparing
external claims with that substrate. For each selected claim $\phi$, the
comparison asks for the dependency tuple:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\text{Mode},\text{Witness},\text{Required-Supply}).
$$

The three modes give the interpretation.

\begin{description}
\item[Mode A.] The required supply is empty, and the witness is an
unconditional checked proof.
\item[Mode B.] The required supply is empty on a stated restricted surface,
and the witness is a checked restricted proof.
\item[Mode C.] The required supply is specific, and the witness is a
counterexample together with named hypothesis sockets.
\end{description}

This tuple is exactly what many classical CIC developments do not place at
the centre of presentation. They prove the theorem in their chosen proof
environment. The BEDC.MetaCIC comparison records what the theorem asks for
when the proof route is evaluated against the closed substrate.

The audit-map family of \autoref{ch:audit-map-family-methodology} is the
directory form of this comparison product. Each audit map records established
content, conditional content, obstruction content, frontier content, and
cross-module use. In the present framing, those columns can be read as an
axiom dependency ledger: which claims need no external supply, which claims
need only a stated surface, which claims expose an obstruction, and which named
socket would have to be filled to move the claim.

\section{How to read a dependency tuple}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-read-tuple}

A dependency tuple is not a semantic judgement about the intended mathematics
of $\phi$. It is a substrate judgement about the proof route available to the
present formal object. The tuple records what the kernel can check from the
displayed constructors, definitions, and named fields that are actually
present.

The three coordinates have separate jobs.

\begin{description}
\item[Mode.] The mode says how the claim meets the substrate: internal proof,
restricted internal proof, or obstruction with named supply.
\item[Witness.] The witness is the mechanical object that makes the mode
auditable: a proof term, a restricted theorem, a counterexample, a named
obligation, or a purity-gate refusal.
\item[Required-Supply.] The required supply names what would have to be added
or discharged before the broader comparison reading could be used without
qualification.
\end{description}

The tuple is deliberately local. A Mode A result in one module does not
upgrade a neighbouring Mode C claim. A Mode B result does not license the
unrestricted theorem. A Mode C counterexample does not say that the external
claim is meaningless; it says that the claim is not substrate-internal without
the named supply.

This locality is what makes the map useful. Classical presentations often
compress many local dependencies into one theorem. The comparison with
BEDC.MetaCIC expands the theorem back into its local dependency structure. The
result is a readable axiom budget rather than a yes-or-no theorem status.

\section{A worked entry: closed application subject reduction}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-worked-entry}

Closed application subject reduction gives a compact instance of the whole
method. The comparison claim is that if a closed application is typed and the
application reduces, then the reduct keeps the original displayed type.
BEDC.MetaCIC does not answer this external claim with a single status. It
splits the claim into the three dependency-map rows that the substrate can
actually justify.

First, the closed syntax and reduction infrastructure around the claim has
Mode A content. Closedness is preserved by the syntax operations that are
already in the substrate. The relevant shift, substitution, and beta-closure
facts are checked without conversion, extensionality, or classical choice.
This records the zero-supply syntactic core that BEDC.MetaCIC proves directly.

Second, the independent-codomain application surface has Mode B content. When
the codomain is closed apart from the argument, replacing the argument by its
reduct does not change the displayed result type. The restricted theorem
\texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_independent\_codomain} is the
mechanical witness for that row. It says exactly where closed substitution is
enough and where no conversion oracle is being used.

Third, the dependent-codomain surface has Mode C content. The theorem
\texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_counterexample} supplies the
mechanical counterexample: a closed application can step to a reduct that does
not retain the original displayed type. The obstruction is therefore a
positive dependency record. It points to the codomain-stability and
typing-preservation supply that an external subject-reduction proof route
would have to provide.

The tuple for the unrestricted closed application comparison claim is
therefore:
$$
\phi_{\mathrm{appSR}}
\mapsto
(\mathrm{C},
\text{dependent-codomain counterexample plus four sockets},
\text{subject-reduction supply}).
$$
The companion Mode B row records the strongest zero-supply fragment:
$$
\phi_{\mathrm{appSR}}^{\mathrm{independent}}
\mapsto
(\mathrm{B},
\text{independent-codomain proof},
\text{closed codomain condition}).
$$
Together these rows are more informative than either a bare theorem or a bare
failure. They show where the comparison claim is already internal, where it is
blocked, and which supply must enter to remove the block.

\section{Why the comparison is informative}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-more-informative}

Classical CIC formalisation projects prove many theorems inside environments
where conversion and library-level extensional principles are available. In
Lean-based developments, tools such as \texttt{\#print axioms} can inspect the
transitive constants of a theorem after the fact. That inspection is valuable,
but it is not necessarily the organising principle of the project.

BEDC.MetaCIC is organised differently. The substrate begins with no project
axioms in \texttt{lean4/BEDC/}, forbids \texttt{axiom} declarations, and
audits transitive dependencies through \texttt{axiom-purity --strict}. A
theorem that survives the gate records a zero-supply region. A theorem that
needs a named hypothesis records a socket. A counterexample records the
mechanical boundary of the comparison claim under the current substrate.

The difference is methodological. A classical development usually asks:
$$
\text{Can the theorem be proved in the chosen calculus?}
$$
The BEDC.MetaCIC comparison asks:
$$
\text{Which exact supply is needed for the theorem to pass this substrate?}
$$
The second question produces dependency information. It does not define
BEDC.MetaCIC by reference to the classical theorem; it explains the axiom
budget exposed by the comparison.

\section{The mapped axiom families}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-taxonomy}

The current map covers several families of supply. The point of the list is
not to repeat the detailed chapters, but to state how each family appears as
dependency-map data.

\subsection{Conversion and definitional equality}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-conversion}

Conversion is a central typing supply in many classical CIC presentations.
BEDC.MetaCIC makes a different first-principles commitment: type identity is
literal AST equality on the represented syntax, and conversion remains
external unless displayed through an explicit route.

The Mode A region consists of literal-AST and closedness facts where no
conversion is used. Closed shift, closed substitution, closure preservation
for beta steps, and closed-normal contradiction exclusion belong to this
region.

The Mode B region is visible in
\texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_independent\_codomain}. When the
codomain is closed independently of the argument, substitution after the
argument step reads back the same type by closedness alone.

The Mode C region is visible in
\texttt{subject\_reduction\_closed\_app\_counterexample}. A dependent codomain
can observe the argument strongly enough that the reduct does not keep the
original displayed type. The four named subject-reduction obligations in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction} are the corresponding
sockets.

\subsection{Extensional identification}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-extensional-identification}

Propositional extensionality, function extensionality, and quotient soundness
form an extensional identification family. \autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-supply-taxonomy}
classifies this as a supply type, and
\autoref{ch:visions-three-axioms-one-closure} reads \texttt{Quot.sound} and
\texttt{propext} as meta-closure moves at different strata.

BEDC.MetaCIC's strict purity gate refuses \texttt{propext} and
\texttt{Quot.sound} as transitive dependencies of checked substrate theorems.
The dependency-map reading is direct: whenever a comparison proof route
identifies propositions, functions, or quotient representatives by
extensional equality, the required supply is not part of the substrate. It
must appear as an explicit transport, classifier, or named socket.

\subsection{Choice and unwitnessed existence}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-choice}

\texttt{Classical.choice} supplies a selected witness from an existence claim
without exposing the witness as generated substrate data.
\autoref{ch:visions-three-axioms-one-closure} classifies this as term-stratum
meta closure.

The BEDC.MetaCIC comparison treats choice-dependent claims as claims with an
unwitnessed-selection dependency. Finite, countable, or explicitly recursive
witness routes may become Mode A or Mode B content when the witness is
generated. A free classical selection over an arbitrary family becomes Mode C
content unless the selection is replaced by displayed witness data.

\subsection{Mathematical induction as transcendental input}
\label{subsec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-induction}

\autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-input-and-induction} treats free-floating
universal induction over not-yet-observed cases as a transcendental input
rather than as internally generated finite observation. The dependency-map
reading is again explicit. A concrete finite trace is substrate data. A
universal claim over an open-ended domain requires a supply socket unless the
relevant induction principle is itself present as an authorised generator for
the carrier being used.

This does not make all induction unavailable. It distinguishes generated
recursors from untracked universal closure. The map records which generator
authorises the proof and which universal use would require supply beyond the
displayed substrate.

\section{Permanent and open-ended}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-permanent}

BEDC.MetaCIC has a fixed substrate discipline: closed CIC syntax,
first-principles definitions, no project axioms, and strict purity auditing.
The comparison space is open-ended because external proof traditions contain
open-ended families of universal claims. Each time such a claim touches the
BEDC.MetaCIC substrate, the project can record one more tuple:
$$
\phi \mapsto
(\text{Mode},\text{Witness},\text{Required-Supply}).
$$

This is not a theorem-count limitation. It is the point of the external
analytical product. BEDC.MetaCIC can keep extending its own audit-clean
theorem body, and comparison work can keep cataloguing the axiom budget of
claims from neighbouring proof traditions at fine granularity.

The map is also cumulative. Mode A entries expand the zero-supply fragment.
Mode B entries sharpen the surfaces where comparison claims become internal.
Mode C entries add mechanical witnesses for the exact place where external
supply would have to enter. Every entry improves the catalogue even when it
does not close the corresponding external theorem.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:bedc-axiom-dependency-map-boundary}

BEDC.MetaCIC does not claim to enumerate all dependency tuples for classical
CIC. The comparison map is permanent and open-ended because the space of
external universal claims is open-ended.

BEDC.MetaCIC also does not claim to replace classical CIC formalisation.
Classical developments prove theorems in their chosen systems. The comparison
with BEDC.MetaCIC complements them by exposing which parts of those theorems
are internal to the closed substrate, which parts live only on stated surfaces,
and which parts mechanically witness an axiom dependency.

The boundary is strict. The words \emph{axiom dependency} and
\emph{mechanical witness} are not metaphors. A documented obstruction,
counterexample, named hypothesis, purity-gate refusal, or audit-map frontier
is an inspectable dependency record. It is a record produced by external
comparison with BEDC.MetaCIC, not BEDC.MetaCIC's primary identity.
