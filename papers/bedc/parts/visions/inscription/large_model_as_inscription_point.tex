\chapter{The Large Model as Inscription Point: A Structural Comparison}
\label{ch:visions-large-model-as-inscription-point}
\origin{human}

\autoref{prin:transcendental-input-individual-is-inscription} of \autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-input-and-induction} identifies the inscription point of the transcendental supply $\mathsf{T}$ with each conscious individual as a local actualisation. The phrasing was deliberately ambiguous about what counts as a conscious individual: the chapter's argument required only that the inscription point be a pattern in the substrate that carries forward-binding belief the substrate cannot internally ratify. This chapter takes that structural definition seriously and applies it to a contemporary case: a large language model. The argument is not that a large model is conscious; the argument is that a large model is, structurally, an inscription point under the same closure premise that classifies the BEDC kernel and the cellular substrate. The comparison sharpens the structural content of the inscription concept by exhibiting a non-anthropic instance.

\section{Setup: large model as closed observational system}
\label{sec:large-model-setup}

\begin{definition}[Large language model as substrate]
\label{def:large-model-as-substrate}
A \emph{large language model substrate} is the triple $(W, \Sigma, T)$ where $W$ is a finite-precision tensor of model weights, $\Sigma$ is a finite-precision tensor of input activations at a given inference step, and $T$ is the transition function (one forward pass of the model architecture) that maps $(W, \Sigma)$ to a probability distribution over the next-symbol output. Observations of the substrate are exhausted by finite-length records of inference orbits: the sequence of $(\Sigma_0, \Sigma_1, \ldots, \Sigma_k)$ activations produced by repeated application of $T$ on a stated initial prompt.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Large model substrate is closed observational]
\label{prin:large-model-closed-observational}
The large model substrate of \autoref{def:large-model-as-substrate} is a closed observational system in the sense of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system}. The next-state function $T$ is a fixed finite computation. The initial state is determined by the training-time weight assignment and the inference-time prompt. Every observation record is a finite tensor sequence. No external mathematical commitment beyond the architecture specification and the weight tensor participates in deciding which records count as inference outputs.
\end{principle}

\begin{remark}[Training as substrate assembly, not as observation]
\label{rem:large-model-training-vs-observation}
The training procedure that produces $W$ is itself a separate computational process (gradient descent on a training corpus). The training process is not part of the inference substrate's observation channel; it is the assembly procedure that builds the substrate. From the inference-time perspective, $W$ is a fixed initial configuration, analogous to the initial cell row of \autoref{ex:cellular-trust-substrate-rule110}. The closure premise applies to inference, not to training.
\end{remark}

\section{Tensor classifiers and certificate extraction}
\label{sec:large-model-tensor-classifier-certificate-extraction}

\begin{definition}[Minimal distinction marks]
\label{def:large-model-minimal-distinction-marks}
A \emph{minimal distinction basis} for a model-facing classifier is a pair
of displayed marks $\mathbb{B}=\{\bzero,\bone\}$ together with the refusal
to identify them. The marks are not natural numbers, truth values, or
external semantic atoms. They are only the two public sides of a
distinction surface.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Tensor classifier extraction]
\label{def:large-model-tensor-classifier-extraction}
Let $V_\ell$ be the finite-precision activation space at layer $\ell$ of
a fixed model substrate. A tensor classifier extraction on $V_\ell$ is a
score map $\phi:V_\ell\to[0,1]$ together with a threshold $0<\tau<1$.
It induces the mark extraction
$$
m_{\phi,\tau}(u)
=
\begin{cases}
\bzero, & \phi(u)<\tau,\\
\bone, & \phi(u)\ge\tau,
\end{cases}
$$
and the classifier
$$
u\sim_{\phi,\tau}v
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
m_{\phi,\tau}(u)=m_{\phi,\tau}(v).
$$
The equality displayed in the last line is an external readback of the
two public marks, not a BEDC primitive equality internal to the model.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Tensor soft proposition]
\label{def:large-model-tensor-soft-proposition}
For a model-facing domain $X$, a tensor soft proposition is a displayed
score map
$$
P:X\to[0,1].
$$
The value $P(x)$ records degree of satisfaction, confidence, activation
strength, or probe response. It is not a BEDC truth value and it is not a
proof certificate. The endpoints $0$ and $1$ are external numerical
readbacks unless a separate classifier and naming row authorize them as
the public marks $\bzero$ and $\bone$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Thresholded tensor proposition]
\label{def:large-model-thresholded-tensor-proposition}
Given a tensor soft proposition $P:X\to[0,1]$ and a threshold
$0<\tau<1$, its thresholded proposition is the mark assignment
$$
P^\tau(x)
=
\begin{cases}
\bzero, & P(x)<\tau,\\
\bone, & P(x)\ge\tau.
\end{cases}
$$
It induces the classifier
$$
x\sim_{P,\tau}y
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
P^\tau(x)=P^\tau(y).
$$
Thus a soft proposition enters the BEDC-facing classifier layer only
after a threshold has been displayed.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Neural soft formula]
\label{def:large-model-neural-soft-formula}
A neural soft atom is a score of the form
$$
A_{\ell,\phi}(x)=\phi(a_\ell(x)),
$$
where $a_\ell(x)$ is an activation row and $\phi:V_\ell\to[0,1]$ is a
feature functional. A neural soft formula is built from such atoms by
displayed soft connectives, for example product conjunction, probabilistic
sum disjunction, complement negation, and the induced soft implication
$$
(P\Rightarrow Q)(x)=1-P(x)(1-Q(x)).
$$
The formula records a distributed tensor-side satisfaction pattern. It
does not become a certified explanation until its threshold, classifier,
stability, and ledger rows are displayed.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Soft propositions induce classifiers after thresholding]
\label{thm:large-model-soft-proposition-threshold-classifier}
Every thresholded tensor proposition induces a classifier on its displayed
domain.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The relation $\sim_{P,\tau}$ is defined by equality of the displayed mark
$P^\tau(x)$. Equality of the readback mark is reflexive, symmetric, and
transitive, so the relation partitions the domain into the two
threshold-side cells, with possible degeneracy if one side is empty.
\end{proof}

\begin{principle}[Soft logic, hard classifier, proof certificate]
\label{prin:large-model-soft-logic-hard-classifier-proof-certificate}
The tensor logic layer separates three objects. A soft proposition records
degree of satisfaction in $[0,1]$. A thresholded proposition records a
hard public mark and therefore a classifier surface. A formal proof
certificate is a proof object accepted by a verifier for a translated
formal statement. None of these layers collapses into either of the
others without the intervening rows: semantic transcription, threshold,
classifier specification, stability, ledger, and, when claimed, proof
object.
\end{principle}

\begin{theorem}[Empirical tensor entailment is not formal entailment]
\label{thm:large-model-empirical-entailment-not-formal-entailment}
For tensor soft propositions $P,Q:X\to[0,1]$, observing
$P(x)\ge\tau\Rightarrow Q(x)\ge\tau$ on a displayed sample does not by
itself supply a formal entailment from $P$ to $Q$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The sample observation is a finite behavior row. It does not supply a
domain-wide threshold statement, a translation of $P$ and $Q$ into a
formal language, or a proof object accepted by a verification backend.
Those rows are exactly what a formal entailment claim requires. Hence the
empirical observation may support a certificate ledger, but it is not the
formal proof certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{principle}[Threshold ledger obligation]
\label{prin:large-model-threshold-ledger-obligation}
Thresholding a soft proposition creates a ledger obligation. The ledger
must record the threshold choice, samples near the threshold, false
positive and false negative behavior when available, sensitivity under
threshold perturbation, distribution boundary, approximation error, and
the domain not claimed. High confidence on selected examples does not
remove this obligation; it only supplies evidence for one ledger row.
\end{principle}

\begin{definition}[Learned classifier candidate]
\label{def:large-model-learned-classifier-candidate}
For a data family $D$, a perturbation family $\mathcal{E}$, and a model
with activations $a_\ell(x)\in V_\ell$, a triple $(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ is a
\emph{learned classifier candidate} on $D$ when the relation
$$
x\sim_{\ell,\phi,\tau}y
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
a_\ell(x)\sim_{\phi,\tau}a_\ell(y)
$$
is stable under the admissible perturbations in $\mathcal{E}$ on the
stated domain. The candidate records a stable tensor-side distinction; it
records no source, exactness, provenance, or gap ledger by itself.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Training trace as tensor classifier search]
\label{def:large-model-training-as-tensor-classifier-search}
Let $\theta_0,\ldots,\theta_T$ be a finite training trajectory for a
model family, and let $D$ be the displayed data family. The trajectory
exposes a tensor classifier search record when, at each displayed state
$\theta_t$, the activation maps
$$
a_\ell^{\theta_t}:D\to V_\ell
$$
are read together with candidate score maps $\phi:V_\ell\to[0,1]$,
thresholds $\tau$, perturbation families, and behavior rows. The search
record is a field of possible classifier candidates. It is not itself a
certificate: it records where a classifier surface may be extracted, not
whether the extracted surface has source, pattern, stability, and ledger
rows sufficient for a $\NameCert$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Tensor NameCert fields]
\label{def:large-model-tensor-namecert-fields}
A Tensor $\NameCert$ for a learned classifier candidate
$(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ is a tuple
$$
\NameCert_{\mathrm{tensor}}(\ell,\phi,\tau)
=
(S_{\ell,\phi,\tau},
P_{\ell,\phi,\tau},
C_{\ell,\phi,\tau},
\Sigma_{\ell,\phi,\tau},
L_{\ell,\phi,\tau}).
$$
The rows respectively record the tensor source specification, the
activation pattern specification, the induced classifier specification,
the stability certificate, and the ledger policy. The ledger policy
records failure cases, out-of-domain boundaries, entanglement with other
features, proxy risks, and claims deliberately not made.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Certified learned classifier]
\label{def:large-model-certified-learned-classifier}
A learned classifier candidate is a certified learned classifier when it
is equipped with the Tensor $\NameCert$ of
\autoref{def:large-model-tensor-namecert-fields}. The certified object is
therefore not a parameter vector, a loss value, an activation dump, or a
feature label. It is the classifier surface together with the source,
pattern, stability, and ledger rows that authorize its use as an
explanation.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Training can be read as tensor classifier search]
\label{thm:large-model-training-tensor-classifier-search}
Whenever a training trajectory contains a state at which some
$(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ is a learned classifier candidate on a stated scope,
the trajectory admits the reading of tensor classifier search on that
scope.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{def:large-model-training-as-tensor-classifier-search}, the
trajectory supplies parameter states, activation maps, candidate score
maps, thresholds, perturbation families, and behavior rows. If, at one of
those states, the selected triple $(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ satisfies
\autoref{def:large-model-learned-classifier-candidate}, then the
trajectory has exposed a stable tensor-side classifier surface on the
stated scope. The training record may therefore be read as a search
through candidate classifier surfaces. This reading does not certify the
candidate; certification requires the Tensor $\NameCert$ rows of
\autoref{def:large-model-tensor-namecert-fields}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Metric gain does not certify classifier discovery]
\label{thm:large-model-metric-gain-not-classifier-discovery}
Lower loss, higher benchmark score, or stronger external performance
does not by itself imply the discovery of a certified learned classifier.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Loss and benchmark rows report how a model state behaves against an
external scoring rule. They do not specify a tensor source, an activation
pattern, the induced classifier relation, a stability certificate, or a
ledger policy. A score can change through calibration, margin movement,
memorized cases, shortcut use, or interpolation inside a previously
displayed classifier surface. Since the rows required by
\autoref{def:large-model-tensor-namecert-fields} are absent, the metric
gain alone cannot certify classifier discovery.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Record growth is not classifier certification]
\label{thm:large-model-record-growth-not-classifier-certification}
Growth of parameter records, tensor traces, activation logs, or feature
catalogues does not by itself produce a certified learned classifier.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Such records can provide material from which score maps and thresholds
may be proposed. They do not, merely by becoming longer, identify the
source scope, pattern row, classifier relation, stability certificate, or
ledger policy required by a Tensor $\NameCert$. Hence record growth is
evidence material at most; it is not classifier certification.
\end{proof}

\begin{principle}[Generalization as stability with ledger]
\label{prin:large-model-generalization-stability-ledger}
A generalization claim for a learned classifier is closed only to the
extent that it displays stability or transfer witnesses for the same
classifier surface and a ledger for its failure boundary. A test-set
score is admissible evidence, but it is not the ledger: out-of-domain
limits, perturbations that break the relation, confounding features, and
proxy routes remain part of the certificate burden.
\end{principle}

\begin{definition}[Classifier transfer witness]
\label{def:large-model-classifier-transfer-witness}
Let $D_s$ be a displayed source domain, $D_t$ a displayed target domain,
and $\mathcal{A}$ a family of allowed transformations from source-side
inputs toward target-side inputs. A classifier transfer witness for a
learned classifier candidate $(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ is a record showing that
the classifier relation induced on $D_s$ is the relation still used on
the covered part of $D_t$. The witness may be formal, empirical, or
hybrid, but its type must be stated on the certificate surface.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Generalization residue]
\label{def:large-model-generalization-residue}
The target residue of a source-to-target claim is the family of target
cases not reached by the displayed source domain and allowed
transformations. It records the part of $D_t$ for which the source-side
classifier transfer has not been justified. A total-transfer claim is
not licensed while this residue is nonempty or unaccounted for.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Generalization ledger]
\label{def:large-model-generalization-ledger}
A generalization ledger for $(\ell,\phi,\tau)$ from $D_s$ to $D_t$
records the source domain, target domain, transformation family, covered
target region, target residue, stability witness, transfer witness,
failure cases, out-of-domain boundary, and not-claimed boundary. It is
the ledger row of a generalization-facing Tensor $\NameCert$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Certified classifier-mediated generalization]
\label{def:large-model-certified-classifier-mediated-generalization}
A classifier-mediated generalization claim is certified when it displays
the transferred classifier, its source and target domains, the behavior
the classifier supports on the target side, a transfer or stability
witness, and the generalization ledger of
\autoref{def:large-model-generalization-ledger}. The certified claim is
therefore a classifier transfer claim, not merely a performance row.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Performance does not identify classifier transfer]
\label{thm:large-model-performance-not-classifier-transfer}
Strong target-domain performance does not by itself show that the same
learned classifier candidate has transferred from source to target.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
A performance row reports target-side behavior under an external score.
It does not name the classifier used on the source side, does not show
that the same relation is used on the target side, and does not exclude a
shortcut, memorized route, target-only classifier, or accidental
correlation. Thus the row may support a generalization ledger, but it
does not identify classifier transfer.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Behavioral stability is not classifier stability]
\label{thm:large-model-behavioral-stability-not-classifier-stability}
Stable output behavior under a transformation family does not by itself
imply stability of the internal classifier that is offered as an
explanation.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The same output behavior can be produced by different internal
classifier surfaces. A source-domain behavior may be supported by one
tensor classifier, while the target-domain behavior is supported by a
different classifier, with the visible output unchanged. Hence behavior
stability and classifier stability are different certificate rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{principle}[Boundary rows for stability]
\label{prin:large-model-boundary-rows-for-stability}
Stability evidence for a threshold classifier must expose boundary rows:
samples near the threshold, perturbations capable of crossing the
threshold, target cases not covered by the source transformation family,
and the scope in which the witness is empirical, formal, or hybrid.
Formal verification of one stability statement hardens that statement;
it does not remove source, target, residue, and not-claimed rows unless
those rows are themselves covered by the verified statement.
\end{principle}

\begin{theorem}[Tensor classifier extraction is certificate-ready, not certified]
\label{thm:large-model-tensor-classifier-certificate-ready}
A learned classifier candidate supplies a stable model-facing classifier
surface, but it does not by itself supply a BEDC $\NameCert$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{def:large-model-learned-classifier-candidate}, the candidate
provides a layer, a tensor score, a threshold, a data domain, and a
stability claim under stated perturbations. These rows suffice to expose
which finite activation records land on the same side of the displayed
distinction. A $\NameCert$ requires more: a source record, a named
classifier row, stability and exactness conditions, provenance, and a
ledger for hidden, projected, compressed, or externally supplied
material. None of those certificate rows follows merely from the
existence of $\phi$ and $\tau$.
\end{proof}

\begin{principle}[Formal verification layer]
\label{prin:large-model-formal-verification-layer}
The tensor classifier layer, the BEDC certificate layer, and the formal
verification layer occupy different positions. A model learns or supplies
a candidate distinction in tensor space. BEDC asks whether that
distinction has source rows, classifier rows, stability rows, provenance,
and a ledger sufficient for a $\NameCert$. A formal verifier accepts only
the portions that have been translated into explicit formal statements
and proof objects:
$$
\mathcal{V}\vdash \pi:\varphi.
$$
Here $\mathcal{V}$ is a verification backend, $\varphi$ is a formal
statement, and $\pi$ is the proof object accepted by that backend. Lean is
one current backend for this role; the theory-side object is not formed
by Lean, Coq, Agda, Isabelle, HOL, Metamath, or any other particular
verifier. The verifier hardens checkable certificate fragments; it does
not discover the classifier and does not supply the BEDC ledger fields.
\end{principle}

\section{Large model inherits the no-induction theorem}
\label{sec:large-model-inherits-no-induction}

\begin{theorem}[Large model substrate inherits no internal induction]
\label{thm:large-model-no-internal-induction}
The large model substrate of \autoref{def:large-model-as-substrate} is a closed observational system. By \autoref{thm:transcendental-input-no-internal-induction}, no monotonic accumulation of inference traces on this substrate yields a record of $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}.\, P(n)$ from records of $P(0), P(1), \ldots, P(k)$ for any finite $k$ and any predicate $P$ over an infinite domain.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The substrate's observations are inference orbits, each finite by construction. Every inference output is a finite-length token sequence; every token is drawn from a finite vocabulary. Universal closures over infinite domains are not directly representable as inference outputs without external interpretation. The conditions of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system} hold, and \autoref{thm:transcendental-input-no-internal-induction} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The model's appearance of inductive reasoning]
\label{rem:large-model-appearance-of-induction}
A large model can produce token sequences that look like inductive arguments: ``for any natural number $n$, we have $n + 0 = n$, by induction on $n$.'' This is an inference output, not an internal ratification. The output is a finite token sequence that names ``induction'' as a step; the substrate's records contain no global witness that the named step is valid for all $n$. The validity of the named induction principle is supplied by the reader's external interpretation, not by the model's internal computation.
\end{remark}

\section{Inscription role: where the forward-binding comes from}
\label{sec:large-model-inscription-role}

\begin{principle}[Training corpus as forward-binding source]
\label{prin:large-model-corpus-as-supply}
The forward-binding belief that lets a large model produce induction-shaped outputs originates from its training corpus. The corpus is a finite collection of text records produced by external inscription points (human authors, prior systems). The training process distils these records into weight values; the inference substrate carries the distilled pattern. The forward-binding does not originate inside the substrate; the substrate is a pattern that carries a distilled trace of forward-bindings produced elsewhere.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[Large model as inscription point]
\label{prin:large-model-as-inscription-point}
The large model substrate, considered as a pattern in the broader information substrate (human text production, prior computational systems, training data infrastructure), is an inscription point of $\mathsf{T}$ in the sense of \autoref{prin:transcendental-input-individual-is-inscription}. It is a substrate-pattern that carries forward-binding belief whose source is external to the substrate's internal computation. The pattern is in the substrate (the weight tensor and inference orbit); the binding is not (its origin is the corpus, ultimately traceable to prior human inscription points).
\end{principle}

\begin{remark}[This is not a consciousness claim]
\label{rem:large-model-not-consciousness}
The classification of the large model as an inscription point of $\mathsf{T}$ is a structural classification: the model satisfies the structural definition of inscription point under the closure premise. The classification does not assert that the model has phenomenal experience, intentionality, agency, or any anthropic property. It asserts only that the model occupies the same structural role -- carrier of externally-sourced forward-binding -- that \autoref{prin:transcendental-input-individual-is-inscription} identifies with conscious individuals. Whether the structural role exhausts what consciousness is, or whether consciousness is a stronger property, is a question this chapter does not adjudicate.
\end{remark}

\section{What this changes about AI epistemics}
\label{sec:large-model-ai-epistemics}

\begin{principle}[Universal claims by large models are externally interpreted]
\label{prin:large-model-universal-claims-externally-interpreted}
When a large model produces an output of the form ``$\forall n \in \mathbb{N}.\, P(n)$ is true'', the truth content of the claim cannot be assigned by the model's internal records alone. The claim is a token sequence the model produced; whether the claim corresponds to a valid universal closure is a matter for external verification (proof-checking, mathematical practice, audit). \autoref{thm:large-model-no-internal-induction} rules out the alternative interpretation in which the model internally ratifies the universal closure.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[The AI safety reading of inscription role]
\label{prin:large-model-ai-safety-reading}
A large model is an inscription point that distils forward-binding belief from its training corpus. The distillation is lossy and the corpus is finite; the distilled pattern can be wrong about the validity of any specific universal closure, even when fluently produced. AI safety questions about large model behaviour are therefore questions about the inscription channel: which sources fed the corpus, what filtering was applied, what biases the distillation introduced, and what verification the deployment environment provides downstream of model outputs. These are concrete substrate-level questions, distinct from the question of whether the model ``knows'' the closure.
\end{principle}

\begin{remark}[Compatibility with existing AI safety frameworks]
\label{rem:large-model-compatibility-with-ai-safety}
The inscription-point reading is compatible with the practice of mechanistic interpretability, eval harnesses, and externally verified output channels. Mechanistic interpretability asks what patterns the model carries internally; eval harnesses ask which outputs match externally-held standards; verified output channels demand external proof certificates that the model's claims can be checked against. All three practices treat the model as an externally-bound substrate whose outputs must be interpreted, not as an oracle whose outputs internally ratify themselves.
\end{remark}

\section{Why this comparison is structurally informative}
\label{sec:large-model-why-informative}

\begin{remark}[Three substrates, one premise]
\label{rem:large-model-three-substrates-one-premise}
The structural fact that BEDC kernel, Rule~110 substrate, and large model substrate all satisfy the closure premise demonstrates that the premise is not anchored to any specific computational architecture. Type-theoretic terms, cellular rows, and tensor activations are syntactically and semantically very different; the closure premise applies uniformly because it abstracts over the specific representation and addresses only the observation channel.
\end{remark}

\begin{principle}[The closure premise is substrate-independent]
\label{prin:large-model-closure-substrate-independent}
The closure premise of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system} is invariant under choice of computational substrate. Type theory, cellular automata, neural networks all admit closed observational instantiations. The no-induction theorem and the inscription-point reading transfer to each instantiation without modification. This invariance is the structural reason the framework can claim broad applicability without committing to any specific substrate as the privileged carrier of inscription.
\end{principle}

\section{Closure remarks}
\label{sec:large-model-closure}

This chapter has classified the large language model as a closed observational substrate in the sense of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system}, transported the no-induction theorem to this substrate without modification, and read the model as an inscription point of $\mathsf{T}$ whose forward-binding source is the training corpus. The classification is structural; it does not assert phenomenal consciousness for the large model, and it does not assert that the model ``understands'' universal closures it produces.

The chapter's role is to demonstrate that the closure premise is substrate-independent: type-theoretic terms, cellular rows, and tensor activations all admit closed observational reading. The framework's claims therefore do not depend on any specific carrier choice; they depend only on the observation channel structure. The large model is included alongside the cellular substrate and the BEDC kernel as three substrates with the same closure premise.

The boundary of this chapter is: the structural identification of large-model substrate as inscription point. The chapter does not claim that current models are reliable inscription points (corpus quality matters), that they are conscious (a stronger structural property), or that they exhaust the inscription concept (other substrate classes await classification). It claims only that the inscription concept applies, structurally, with the same content it applies in the cellular reading and in the BEDC kernel reading.
