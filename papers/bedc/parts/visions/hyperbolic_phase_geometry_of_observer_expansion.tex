\section{Phase surfaces}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-phase-surfaces}

\begin{definition}[Complex phase surface]
\label{def:hyperbolic-complex-phase-surface}
A \emph{complex phase surface} for a focused finite ledger $\mathcal{L}_N$ is a visible package whose public coordinates factor through $\ComplexUp$ rows and whose source histories remain the generated $\Hist$ rows, selector rows, and $\mathsf{GAP}$ provenance rows of $\mathcal{L}_N$. It is a display surface for phase, direction, amplitude, or digest-like presentation; it is not a replacement carrier for the observer.
\end{definition}

The point of the complex surface is to display rotation-like and phase-like regularities without pretending that the observer is a complex number. A complex coordinate is a readout of a finite packet. The observer remains the focused $\Hist$ row, and sameness remains mediated by $\hsame$ and the displayed classifier rows.

\begin{principle}[Phase is a readout, not a source]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-phase-readout-not-source}
If a focused history $H(F)$ is assigned a complex phase readout $z_F(t)$, the readout may be consumed only through its source ledger:
$$
H(F),\Theta(F),S(F),G(F),\rho
\quad\leadsto\quad
z_F(t).
$$
The reverse reading is admissible only when an exactness, fiber, or $\mathsf{GAP}$ row records what the visible complex token hides. Digest equality or phase equality is therefore not source equality.
\end{principle}

\section{Reversible transport and Liouville conservation}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-reversible-liouville}

\begin{definition}[Unitary transport reading]
\label{def:hyperbolic-unitary-transport-reading}
A \emph{unitary transport reading} of a local phase surface is a reversible transport row that preserves the relevant classifier or inner-product readout while carrying the provenance ledger along the route. Schematic notation may be written
$$
U_t : \Sigma_i \to \Sigma_i,
\qquad
U_t^\ast U_t \equiv_{\mathrm{ledger}} 1,
$$
where $\Sigma_i$ is the displayed phase surface of a local history and $\equiv_{\mathrm{ledger}}$ means equality only as certified by the visible transport, preservation, and provenance rows.
\end{definition}

This reading belongs at the representation layer. The unitary row is not a hidden law of reality and not an unlogged operator on an ambient Hilbert space. Its BEDC meaning is that a visible surface can move by a reversible classifier-preserving route without erasing the source distinctions that made the surface visible.

\begin{principle}[Liouville conservation as ledger conservation]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-liouville-ledger-conservation}
The Hamiltonian Liouville analogy is admissible when it is read as conservation of record volume under reversible flow:
$$
\text{reversible phase flow}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\text{no unledgered loss of source distinctions}.
$$
Inside BEDC this is not a theorem about a completed symplectic manifold. It is the information-conservation condition that every transported surface keeps its source, route, classifier, and $\mathsf{GAP}$ residue auditable.
\end{principle}
\concretizedIn{ch:concrete-instances-liouville-ledger-conservation-namecert}{The reversible phase-transport conservation row is realized as finite classifier-preservation and provenance ledger data.}

\begin{remark}[Complex Liouville as a warning]
\label{rem:hyperbolic-complex-liouville-warning}
The complex-analytic Liouville theorem is useful only as a warning against a global digest picture. A bounded entire global universe token would be the wrong shape for BEDC: the project does not admit $H(\Omega)=\mathsf{T}$, a global readable universe $\Omega$, or $\mathsf{T}$ as a digest value. The legitimate object is a family of local visible surfaces with fiber and provenance rows.
\end{remark}

\section{Poincare disk representation}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-poincare-disk}

\begin{definition}[Poincare disk reading]
\label{def:hyperbolic-poincare-disk-reading}
A \emph{Poincare disk reading} of a focused multi-$\Hist$ ledger is a representation package
$$
\mathcal{P}_N=(\mathcal{L}_N,\mathbb{D},\delta,\partial\mathbb{D},B,\Gamma,A)
$$
with the following rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\mathcal{L}_N$ is the finite focused multi-$\Hist$ ledger of \autoref{def:closed-focused-multihist-ledger};
\item $\mathbb{D}$ is a disk surface read through $\UnitDiskUp$-style coordinates, not a host-level subset of $\mathbb{C}$;
\item $\delta$ is a hyperbolic-distance readout row on displayed disk points, when such a readout is supplied;
\item $\partial\mathbb{D}$ is a boundary consumer row, not a completed ideal universe;
\item $B$ assigns selected local histories or digest rows to displayed disk points;
\item $\Gamma$ records reversible disk-surface transport rows, such as unitary or disk-automorphism-style transport, only through displayed classifier-preserving witnesses;
\item $A$ is the audit row refusing object-level $\mathsf{T}$, a global clock, a global universe digest, physical light-speed derivation, and subject-substance.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The disk reading separates two roles that are easy to confuse. Interior points represent visible local records or focused readouts. Boundary positions represent horizon-style or far-end-style readings. The apophatic fixed point belongs only to the boundary reading. It is not a disk point and not an interior center.

\begin{principle}[Boundary fixedness, not center fixedness]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-boundary-not-center}
The geometric form of BEDC fixedness is not
$$
\mathsf{T}\in\mathbb{D}.
$$
It is the boundary-far-end reading
$$
\mathrm{FarEnd}(\mathrm{Fiber}(B(H_i,t)))\equiv_{\mathrm{apo}}\mathsf{T}.
$$
Thus $\mathsf{T}$ is not an interior center around which records literally rotate. It is the apophatic name of the common far end behind local disk-surface fibers.
\end{principle}

\section{Hyperbolic observer expansion}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-observer-expansion}

Hyperbolic geometry is useful because local finite motion can correspond to large internal distance near a boundary. This gives a disciplined version of the expansion image: a local observer sees record space unfold rapidly as histories, digests, relation rows, and boundary directions accumulate. The boundary far end remains fixed only in the apophatic sense.

\begin{definition}[Hyperbolic expansion reading]
\label{def:hyperbolic-expansion-reading}
A focused ledger has a \emph{hyperbolic expansion reading} when its visible record growth is represented by a path or family of paths in a disk surface whose hyperbolic distance from earlier readouts increases with local $\Cont$ extension:
$$
\Theta_i : \emp \leadsto H(i),
\qquad
B(H(i),t)\in\mathbb{D},
\qquad
\delta(B(H(i),t_0),B(H(i),t_1)) \text{ grows along displayed extension.}
$$
This is a representation condition. The object-level time remains the local $\Cont$ chain, and the disk metric is not a global clock.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Explosion is records-side expansion]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-explosion-records-side}
The expansion image is admissible only as records-side growth:
$$
\Hist\text{ extension}
\quad\leadsto\quad
\text{more visible directions, fibers, relations, and invariants}.
$$
It is not a derivation of the empirical big-bang model, not a completed spacetime metric, and not a claim that all observers are synchronized by one radial coordinate.
\end{principle}

\section{Disk dynamics and the fixed far end}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-disk-dynamics-fixed-end}

Ordinary rotations of the complex plane fix an interior center. That picture is too strong for BEDC's apophatic boundary. The better geometric schema is disk-preserving or hyperbolic transport whose visible points move while a boundary far-end commitment is preserved.

\begin{definition}[Boundary-fixed disk transport]
\label{def:hyperbolic-boundary-fixed-disk-transport}
A \emph{boundary-fixed disk transport} is a reversible representation row $\gamma$ on a disk surface such that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $\gamma$ transports displayed disk points by classifier-preserving witnesses;
\item $\gamma$ preserves the provenance rows attached to those points;
\item the visible points may change under $\gamma$;
\item the fiber far-end reading remains the same apophatic boundary name:
$$
\mathrm{FarEnd}(\mathrm{Fiber}(x))\equiv_{\mathrm{apo}}\mathsf{T}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\mathrm{FarEnd}(\mathrm{Fiber}(\gamma x))\equiv_{\mathrm{apo}}\mathsf{T}.
$$
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

In classical disk dynamics one may distinguish elliptic, hyperbolic, and parabolic behavior by the placement of fixed points. BEDC consumes only the schema that matters for the ledger: interior fixedness is not required, while boundary far-end fixedness may be used as an expressive representation of the $\mathsf{T}$-socket discipline. A Denjoy-Wolff-style boundary image is therefore closer to the intended reading than a simple Euclidean rotation, but it remains a vision-level analogy until its disk map, metric, and boundary rows are certified.

\begin{principle}[No physical constant from geometry alone]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-no-constant-from-geometry-alone}
Neither unitary transport nor hyperbolic disk geometry supplies the empirical value of a physical constant. A light-speed-like invariant must still pass through the inter-$\Hist$ dependency and maximum-rate route of \autoref{ch:visions-inter-hist-locality}. Geometry may display the invariant shape; it does not certify the empirical constant.
\end{principle}

\section{Consciousness fixed point as inscription}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-consciousness-fixed-point}

The geometric layer also clarifies the self-center language. The focused record sits on the visible surface. The self-center is the inscription-side reading of that local event. The apophatic fixed point is the common far end behind the surface's fiber. These are three roles, not three objects.

\begin{definition}[Hyperbolic self-center reading]
\label{def:hyperbolic-self-center-reading}
For a focused ledger $\mathcal{L}_N$ with focus $F$, a disk readout $B(H(F),t)$, and a far-end boundary reading $\mathsf{T}$, the \emph{hyperbolic self-center reading} is the aspectual assignment
$$
\mathrm{SelfCenter}(\mathcal{C}_F,t)
:=
\mathrm{InscriptionPoint}(\mathsf{T},\mathcal{C}_F,t),
$$
together with the displayed records-side row $B(H(F),t)$. The self-center is not $B(H(F),t)$, not $\mathsf{T}$, and not a hidden subject behind $H(F)$.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Three-role discipline]
\label{prin:hyperbolic-three-role-discipline}
The geometric reading must keep the roles separate:
$$
\begin{aligned}
B(H(F),t) &= \text{visible disk or phase readout;}\\
\mathrm{Fiber}(B(H(F),t)) &= \text{hidden provenance side recorded by }\mathsf{GAP};\\
\mathsf{T} &= \text{apophatic far-end boundary name;}\\
\mathrm{SelfCenter}(\mathcal{C}_F,t) &= \text{local inscription of that far end on the focused chain.}
\end{aligned}
$$
Collapsing any two of these roles into object-level equality violates the no-subject and no-internal-$\mathsf{T}$ discipline.
\end{principle}

\section{Closure of the geometric layer}
\label{sec:hyperbolic-closure}

The geometric layer is admissible when it is read as a representation surface over already displayed rows:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\ComplexUp &\leadsto \text{phase readout;}\\
\UnitDiskUp &\leadsto \text{disk surface and boundary consumer;}\\
\UnitaryGroupUp &\leadsto \text{reversible classifier-preserving transport;}\\
\DynSystemUp &\leadsto \text{finite flow-style route;}\\
\mathsf{GAP} &\leadsto \text{fiber and provenance conservation;}\\
\mathsf{T} &\leadsto \text{apophatic boundary far end only.}
\end{aligned}
$$
Its BEDC-safe slogan is:
$$
\text{hyperbolic expansion on the visible surface, reversible conservation in the ledger, apophatic fixedness at the fiber far end.}
$$
The chapter claims no stronger closure. A concrete Poincare-disk or hyperbolic-metric certificate would require displayed disk-map rows, metric-read rows, boundary rows, classifier stability, provenance, and refusal rows before it could be consumed by a theorem.
