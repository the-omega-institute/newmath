\chapter{Closed-normal consistency: BEDC's first unconditional main result}
\label{ch:closed-normal-consistency-main-result}

\section{The result}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-result}

The BEDC project uses the closed-CIC substrate as its internal metatheoretic
base. Within that substrate, the strongest presently established unconditional
consistency result is closed-normal consistency. If $t$ is a closed term, $t$
has the internal false encoding as its type, and $t$ is beta-strong, then $t$
is impossible:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_normal\_consistency\_assembly}.

The checked statement has the following shape:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{HasType}([],t,\mathsf{falseEncoding})
  \wedge
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(0,t)
  \wedge
  \mathsf{BetaStrong}(t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \bot.
\end{aligned}
$$

The three hypotheses are not interchangeable. The typing row says that the
term is displayed at the internal false encoding. The closedness row says that
the term is not depending on an external context entry. The beta-strong row
says that no beta step can still be taken from the displayed term. The
conclusion is contradiction inside the substrate's Lean formalisation.

Equivalently, the substrate has no closed beta-normal proof of its internal
false type:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.no\_closed\_normal\_proof\_of\_false}. The target is
not merely a line in the assembly map of
\autoref{ch:closed-metacic-consistency-assembly}; following the operational
priority in \autoref{ch:metacic-open-problems}, this chapter treats that node
as a first-class BEDC main result.

The proposition that full closed consistency would target is separately named:
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.no\_closed\_proof\_of\_false}. That definition removes
the beta-strong input. The distinction between the two statements is the
central boundary of this chapter. Closed-normal consistency is checked as a
theorem. Full closed consistency is a stronger proposition whose missing
transport step is documented elsewhere.

\section{Why this is a first-class main result}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-first-class}

The result is non-trivial for exactly the reason that full closed consistency
is not claimed. It is not a corollary of closed subject reduction. Closed
subject reduction is documented as unavailable on the present substrate in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}. The dependent-codomain
obstruction prevents an arbitrary closed proof term from being transported
through beta reduction while preserving the displayed false type.

Closed-normal consistency bypasses that blocked lift. It works directly at the
normal-form boundary. Its input already contains beta-strong normality, so the
proof does not consume the four subject-reduction discharge hypotheses, does
not require dependent-codomain inversion, and does not import a quotient or
Church--Rosser theorem to repair the missing transport step.

This is why the result deserves main-result status rather than being treated as
a minor helper lemma. It is the largest consistency statement that the present
closed-CIC substrate supports without additional setup fields. The theorem
touches the object of interest, the internal false encoding, and it eliminates
that object under exactly the normal-form hypotheses that the substrate can
audit.

The result also has the right failure behaviour. If a reader asks for arbitrary
closed consistency, the chapter can point to the missing subject-reduction
transport. If a reader asks what is already established without that transport,
the chapter can point to a single Lean theorem. The paper therefore has a
central positive statement and a central boundary statement, instead of an
ambiguous promise that depends on informal CIC intuition.

This makes the theorem an unconditional positive result of the current
BEDC.MetaCIC substrate. The substrate cannot yet say that every closed proof of
false is impossible. It can say, without any additional logical commitment,
that a closed proof already in beta-normal form cannot inhabit false.

\section{Constructive content}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-constructive}

The result has a concrete audit reading. Given a represented closed term $t$,
the decidability layer supplies the mechanical typing surface described in
\autoref{ch:metacic-decidable-audit-map}. In particular, the
\texttt{inferTypeCtxClosed} interface is sound and complete for empty-context
typing at the stated \texttt{HasType} relation.

The checker contribution is intentionally only a typing contribution. A
successful checker run exposes a type judgement for the represented relation,
and the completeness theorem says that represented typing judgements are seen
by the checker. This is enough for the first row of the main result. It does
not decide every semantic question about the term, and it does not decide the
full conversion theory of the substrate.

Given a well-typed closed term $t$, the normalisation layer supplies
candidate-mediated strong normalisation when the relevant candidate evidence is
present:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.closed\_typed\_term\_sn\_from\_candidate}. The audit
map in \autoref{ch:metacic-normalization-audit-map} keeps that evidence
visible. It is not erased into a global theorem over every closed typed term.

The normalisation contribution is likewise exact. Candidate evidence gives a
strong-normalisation witness. A beta-strong endpoint is already at the
normal-form boundary required by the consistency theorem. In the cases where
the audit chain supplies candidate evidence and a beta-strong endpoint, the
theorem closes the false-inhabitation question at once.

Given a closed beta-strong well-typed term $t$, the present main result rules
out inhabitation of the false encoding. The pipeline is therefore:

\begin{enumerate}
\item use the checker layer to expose the empty-context typing judgement;
\item use candidate-mediated normalisation when the candidate witness is part
  of the audited input;
\item apply the closed-normal consistency theorem at the beta-strong boundary;
\item keep every Lean marker tied to a kernel-checked BEDC.MetaCIC declaration.
\end{enumerate}

This constructive content is deliberately narrow. It is Lean-kernel verified,
and the BEDC audit discipline requires it to remain independent of forbidden
stdlib logical dependencies. It is also explicit about its inputs: typing,
closedness, and beta-strong normality are separate rows, not prose assumptions
hidden inside a single word such as consistency.

This separation is important for reproducibility. A consumer can inspect which
row is being supplied by a checker theorem, which row is supplied by closedness
evidence, which row is supplied by normal-form evidence, and which row is
discharged by the consistency theorem. No row is silently converted into
another. The proof is therefore useful even when the surrounding metatheory is
still being audited, because it tells the reader exactly what data reaches the
contradiction.

\section{Relation to classical results}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-classical}

Classical CIC consistency is usually read through a three-part route: strong
normalisation, Church--Rosser or subject reduction, and a no-false-inhabitant
argument for normal forms. That route eliminates arbitrary proof terms by
reducing them to a normal endpoint while preserving the relevant type.

BEDC's closed-normal statement is the normal-form version of that route. It
contains the no-false-inhabitant endpoint, and it can be paired with the
candidate-mediated normalisation facts where their evidence is available. It
does not contain the subject-reduction transport from arbitrary closed proofs
to typed normal endpoints.

The comparison can be read as a factorisation:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \text{arbitrary closed proof}
  &\longrightarrow
  \text{typed normal endpoint}
  \longrightarrow
  \bot.
\end{aligned}
$$

Classical presentations often package the first arrow through subject
reduction, confluence, and normalisation. BEDC's theorem establishes the
second arrow for the closed beta-strong endpoint. The first arrow is not
available unconditionally in the present substrate.

The result is therefore weaker than full classical closed consistency, but it
is strictly stronger than having no consistency theorem. The subject-reduction
counterexample prevents the paper from elevating arbitrary closed consistency
on the current substrate. The closed-normal theorem is the maximal positive
consistency claim available without crossing that obstruction.

The word ``maximal'' is used here only relative to the stated substrate and
the stated obstruction. It does not mean that stronger metatheorems are
mathematically impossible. It means that the paper has a checked theorem at
the normal boundary and a checked reason not to erase the reduction boundary.
That is exactly the main-result shape that BEDC can honestly export.

\section{What it does not claim}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-not-claim}

The theorem does not claim full closed consistency:
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.no\_closed\_proof\_of\_false}. That proposition is the
stronger statement excluding every closed proof of false, whether or not it is
already beta-strong. The present chapter does not mark it as checked.

It does not claim that every closed proof term has a displayed false type
preserved along every beta path. Such a statement would be a subject-reduction
or typed-normal-endpoint theorem. The current closed-normal theorem starts
after that transport problem has already been resolved in the input.

It also does not claim a global theorem that all closed typed terms reduce to
normal form without candidate-mediated evidence. The normalisation audit map
records checked projections from candidate evidence, closed-candidate evidence,
and candidate-set membership. Those projections are central to the positive
pipeline, but they are not a blanket closed-term strong-normalisation theorem.

It does not claim unrestricted closed beta-conversion decidability. The
bounded normal-equality checker described in
\autoref{ch:metacic-decidable-audit-map} has its own finished-normalisation
boundary. That checker is part of the audit surface, not a replacement for the
missing closed-consistency transport.

Finally, the result does not solve the dependent-codomain obstruction. The
four subject-reduction discharge hypotheses remain exactly where
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction} and
\autoref{ch:metacic-open-problems} place them. Closed-normal consistency is a
main result because it is clean at its boundary, not because it hides the
boundary.

These non-claims are not qualifications that weaken the theorem. They are the
conditions that make the theorem readable. A full consistency claim would need
different hypotheses and different Lean evidence. The closed-normal claim has
the evidence it needs and stops there.

\section{The role of the BEDC project}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-project-role}

BEDC's current mature metatheoretic deliverable is this closed-normal
consistency theorem. It is produced by the MetaCIC substrate, the normal-form
audit chain, the explicit decidability surface for represented typing, and
the apophatic discipline that refuses to blur open obligations into finished
proofs.

The project role is therefore not merely documentary. BEDC turns a blocked
classical route into a precise theorem plus a precise boundary. The theorem is
not an announcement that all conventional consistency infrastructure has been
internalised. It is the part of that infrastructure that the present substrate
can state, check, and maintain as a public result.

This also gives the surrounding audit chapters a common endpoint. The
subject-reduction chapter explains why arbitrary closed consistency is blocked.
The normalisation chapter explains what evidence supplies termination. The
decidability chapter explains the typing-checker surface. The assembly chapter
shows the route. The present chapter names the resulting theorem as a main
BEDC deliverable.

The role of \autoref{ch:visions-apophatic-naming-as-discipline} is positive
here. By naming the missing subject-reduction lift and the candidate-mediated
normalisation boundary, the paper can elevate the unconditional theorem
without overstating it. The negative names make room for the positive result:
closed-normal consistency is visible precisely because full closed consistency
is not silently claimed.

In that sense, closed-normal consistency is the first main result with the
right BEDC shape. It is mathematically specific, Lean-linked, substrate-local,
and bounded by named non-claims. A reader can cite it without inheriting an
unstated subject-reduction promise.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:closed-normal-consistency-main-result-boundary}

This chapter elevates no statement through a proof hole. It does not pretend
that the subject-reduction obstruction has been discharged. It does not
identify normal-form consistency with full closed consistency. It records
BEDC's first-class main consistency result at its exact checked strength:
closed, typed, beta-strong terms do not inhabit the internal false encoding.

The boundary can be summarised as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \text{closed} + \text{typed at false} + \text{beta-strong}
  &\Rightarrow
  \bot, \\
  \text{closed} + \text{typed at false}
  &\nRightarrow_{\text{present substrate}}
  \bot.
\end{aligned}
$$

The first line is the main result of this chapter. The second line is the
refused overclaim. Keeping both lines visible is the point of the
closed-normal consistency elevation.
