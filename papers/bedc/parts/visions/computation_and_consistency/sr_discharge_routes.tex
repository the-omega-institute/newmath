\chapter{Subject reduction discharge routes: explicit bundle}
\label{ch:sr-discharge-routes}
\origin{human}

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:sr-discharge-routes-setup}

BEDC.MetaCIC subject reduction for the closed CIC substrate is not
an unparameterised theorem of the substrate itself.  As recorded in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}, the checked theorem
is parameterised by four named discharge hypotheses.  A downstream
carrier may supply those hypotheses explicitly, but the present raw
relation does not supply a global discharge packet.

The public route is the explicit bundle route.  It packages the four
hypotheses as finite evidence and passes that packet to the
subject-reduction theorem.  The current substrate also exports checked
obstruction facts showing that the application-argument, lambda-domain,
and Pi-domain fields cannot be filled for the raw relation, and therefore
the full bundle is not inhabited at this boundary.

\section{Bundle route}
\label{sec:sr-discharge-routes-bundle-route}

\subsection{Definition}
\label{subsec:sr-discharge-routes-bundle-definition}

The bundle route is centred on
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}.
This Lean object is a structure of four propositions.  Its fields are the
beta-substitution preservation obligation, the application-argument
stability obligation, the lambda-domain subject-reduction obligation, and
the Pi-domain subject-reduction obligation.

The structure is carrier-agnostic: it does not say where the four
propositions come from, which substrate implements them, or how an
implementation proves them.  Any caller that can provide the four fields
can build the packet.  No ambient instance route is part of the public
MetaCIC audit surface.

\subsection{Subject reduction from bundle}
\label{subsec:sr-discharge-routes-subject-reduction-from-bundle}

The theorem
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.subject\_reduction\_from\_bundle}
consumes a
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}
and yields the subject-reduction conclusion for the represented reduction
step.  The theorem does not prove the four hypotheses.  It records that
once those four hypotheses are explicitly supplied, the parameterised
subject-reduction theorem has no remaining open argument.

This route is direct at a call site.  Code constructs a bundle, passes it
to the theorem, and receives the subject-reduction result.  The discharge
evidence is visible in the term that invokes the theorem.

\section{Checked obstruction ledger}
\label{sec:sr-discharge-routes-obstruction-ledger}

The raw relation supplies checked negative facts for three fields:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.not\_appArgTypeStable},
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.not\_lamDomainSubjectReduction}, and
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.not\_piDomainSubjectReduction}.  These facts
are kernel-checked consequences of the closed counterexamples in the
MetaCIC subject-reduction audit.

The bundle obstruction
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.not\_subjectReductionDischargeBundle}
then follows by projecting the application-argument field from any
purported bundle.  Thus the current raw relation has a checked no-bundle
ledger.  This is a negative theorem about the present substrate, not a
replacement for the beta-substitution obligation in future carriers.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:sr-discharge-routes-boundary}

This chapter does not solve beta-substitution preservation and does not
construct a
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}
for the present substrate.  It records one public discharge route and the
checked obstruction ledger explaining why that route is not inhabited by
the raw relation.

Future downstream carriers can still consume
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.subject\_reduction\_from\_bundle}
by providing an explicit
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}.
At this boundary, however, the canonical published surface is explicit:
bundle consumption plus checked no-bundle obstruction.
