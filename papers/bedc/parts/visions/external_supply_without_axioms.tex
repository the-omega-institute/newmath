\chapter{External Supply Without Axioms}
\label{ch:visions-external-supply-without-axioms}

BEDC repeatedly reaches boundary positions where a proposed inference, comparison, continuation, or universal claim requires supply that the displayed substrate has not generated. The wrong response is to turn that supply into an axiom. An axiom makes an unsupported commitment available inside the formal environment as if it had been produced by the substrate. BEDC's response is different: the supply position is named, socketed, ledgered, and audited, while the supplied content remains external unless a later certificate reconstructs it from displayed generators. This chapter records that discipline as a manuscript-level governance rule. It introduces no new primitive, no new object-level constructor, and no new logical axiom. Its purpose is to prevent a recurring confusion: a boundary that BEDC names is not a datum that BEDC has assumed. A named socket is weaker than an axiom because it does not grant internal content; it is stronger as an audit device because it prevents the content from disappearing into prose, tactic ancestry, quotient collapse, or hidden selection.

The chapter sits beside \autoref{ch:visions-apophatic-naming-as-discipline}, the GAP-as-ledger discipline of \autoref{ch:visions-certificate-closure-and-generator-induction}, the audit-gate chapters of \autoref{ch:visions-audit-gates-as-transcendental-boundary}, and the anti-internalisation roadmap of \autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-input-and-induction}. Its active sentence is: external dependence is admissible only when it remains visible as boundary, not when it becomes axiom.

\section{The boundary problem}
\label{sec:external-supply-boundary-problem}

A closed observational substrate interface may be admitted by a naming certificate. A parametric input may be exposed as a setup field. A compression may be accompanied by a $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger. A claim that exceeds all of these may be marked as an apophatic socket.

The dangerous move is to replace one of these visible forms by an axiom. Once a missing selector, quotient representative, proposition equality, universal induction principle, synchronization map, or causal law is stated as an axiom, the substrate can consume it as internal truth. The original source-channel disappears. From the audit point of view, this is not a harmless shortcut. It is source erasure.

\begin{principle}[No internalization of unsupported supply]
\label{prin:external-supply-no-internalization}
When a BEDC argument requires content not generated, defined, certified, or displayed as an interface input, the content may not be introduced by axiom. The dependency must remain one of: a setup field, a named carrier, a classifier row, a provenance row, a $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, a bridge slot, or an apophatic socket.
\end{principle}

This principle is not a new substrate axiom. It is a discipline on how claims enter the manuscript and the Lean surface. Its enforcement is external and mechanical where possible: the axiom scan rejects Lean axioms, the strict axiom-purity gate rejects forbidden host dependencies, the conservativity audit rejects source-channel pollution, and the paper-Lean audit rejects name drift (\autoref{ch:visions-audit-gates-as-transcendental-boundary}).

\section{Axiom, setup field, and socket}
\label{sec:external-supply-axiom-setup-socket}

Three forms must be kept separate.

An \emph{axiom} asserts content as available inside the formal environment without deriving it from an authorized source. In Lean, an axiom declaration gives the kernel a constant with a type and no construction. In the BEDC reading, this collapses the boundary between generated content and unsupported supply.

A \emph{setup field} exposes a required input at an interface boundary. It says that the caller of the interface must supply a carrier, classifier, operation, proof row, or stability witness. The substrate has not proved the field; it has only made the requested input visible and typed. This preserves the source-channel.

A \emph{socket} records that even a setup field or certificate row does not internalize the requested supply. The socket is a stable boundary handle. It marks the site where a proposed claim would require a supply whose internal nature the substrate does not claim to characterize.

\begin{definition}[Non-axiom boundary form]
\label{def:external-supply-non-axiom-boundary-form}
A non-axiom boundary form is any public interface form that exposes a dependency without converting the dependency into substrate truth. The canonical forms are:
$$
\begin{aligned}
&\texttt{inductive} && \text{closed generator;} \\
&\texttt{def} && \text{computable or definitional compression;} \\
&\text{structure/class field} && \text{parametric setup input;} \\
&\mathsf{NameCert}\text{ row} && \text{licensed public name with source, classifier, stability, ledger;} \\
&\mathsf{GAP}\text{ ledger} && \text{compression residue or missing conservation row;} \\
&\text{bridge slot} && \text{external interpretation kept separate from internal proof;} \\
&\text{apophatic socket} && \text{named external supply position not internalized by the substrate.}
\end{aligned}
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Externalized, not assumed]
\label{prin:external-supply-externalized-not-assumed}
A dependency that remains in a non-axiom boundary form is externalized. A dependency introduced by axiom is assumed. BEDC permits externalization and rejects assumption.
\end{principle}

The difference is not rhetorical. A setup field can be inspected. A $\mathsf{NameCert}$ row can be audited. A $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger can be followed back to the compressed material. A bridge slot can be restricted or refused. A socket can be marked as open. An axiom hides all of those distinctions behind immediate usability.

\section{The admission decision}
\label{sec:external-supply-admission-decision}

Every proposed BEDC addition must pass through a replacement decision before it can enter the public surface.

\begin{definition}[Admission decision]
\label{def:external-supply-admission-decision}
Given a proposed object, operation, theorem, or interface component $X$, the BEDC admission decision asks for the strongest non-axiom form that can carry $X$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
X \text{ finite grammar or closed syntax} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \texttt{inductive}; \\
X \text{ computed from earlier rows} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \texttt{def}; \\
X \text{ parametric interface input} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{setup field}; \\
X \text{ mature public name} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \mathsf{NameCert}; \\
X \text{ compression residue} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \mathsf{GAP}\text{ ledger}; \\
X \text{ external semantic correspondence} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{bridge slot}; \\
X \text{ unsupported forward supply} &\;\Longrightarrow\; \text{apophatic socket}.
\end{aligned}
$$
\end{definition}

If none of these forms is adequate, the boundary has not been identified clearly enough, and the interface must be redesigned rather than accepted as an axiom.

\begin{principle}[Redesign before axiom]
\label{prin:external-supply-redesign-before-axiom}
Failure to fit a proposed dependency into a non-axiom boundary form is evidence of an unclear interface, not license to add an axiom.
\end{principle}

The decision preserves BEDC's anti-primitive principle. Mature mathematics may be reconstructed, bridged, or socketed, but it is not smuggled in as primitive. The same rule applies to philosophical pressure points. Consciousness, free will, mathematical induction beyond authorized generators, global time, light-speed constancy, and universal semantic truth are not admitted by declaration. At most they receive boundary names, certificate obligations, or schema-only horizons.

\section{GAP as the memory of boundary pressure}
\label{sec:external-supply-gap-memory}

$\mathsf{GAP}$ has two simultaneous roles. The first is internal and engineering-facing: it records the residue of compression, packaging, projection, sealing, normalization, or identification. Whenever a visible token replaces richer source material, the $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger keeps the memory of what the surface form no longer displays.

The second role is boundary-facing: $\mathsf{GAP}$ marks a supply position whose far end is not generated by the substrate. When a generator-internal claim is pushed toward a free-floating universal claim, the pressure cannot be discharged by the generator itself. $\mathsf{GAP}$ records that the claim has reached a $\mathsf{T}$-socket rather than pretending the universal extension is already internal.

\begin{principle}[Dual role of GAP]
\label{prin:external-supply-gap-dual-role}
Every $\mathsf{GAP}$ instance must be read under both possible loads:
\begin{enumerate}
\item as an information-conservation ledger for compression residue; and
\item as a socket marker for forward-binding supply that the substrate does not internalize.
\end{enumerate}
\end{principle}

Reading $\mathsf{GAP}$ only as bookkeeping hides the externality role. Reading it only as transcendental socket hides the conservation role. BEDC requires both readings to remain available.

\begin{remark}[Compression requires memory]
\label{rem:external-supply-compression-memory}
A compressed BEDC object may be easier to use than its source records, but it may not erase the source-channel. The ledger is the memory of the compression. If the memory is absent, the compression has become an unledgered quotient. Such a quotient is exactly the kind of source-erasing step the axiom-purity gate is designed to reject.
\end{remark}

This is why information conservation in BEDC is not merely a statement about bit-count. It is provenance conservation. A visible package must retain a route back to the histories, signatures, classifiers, or certificates that generated it. A public name must retain the rows by which it was licensed. A derived interface must retain the $\mathsf{GAP}$ by which hidden residue remains auditable.

\section{Generator-local induction and the refusal of free-floating induction}
\label{sec:external-supply-generator-induction}

BEDC does not reject induction. It rejects unbounded induction detached from an authorized generator.

A closed inductive generator carries its own structural induction principle. $\mathsf{Hist}$ induction is licensed by the constructor structure of $\mathsf{Hist}$. A $\mathsf{Term}$ generator licenses structural induction over terms produced by that generator. A proof over a closed list, finite tree, syntax grammar, or certified carrier may use the eliminator attached to that generator. Such induction is internal because its domain is exactly the closure of displayed constructors.

What BEDC refuses is the move from generator-local induction to a universal statement over all conceivable cases. The phrase ``all conceivable'' names a domain that has not been generated, certified, or bounded by a displayed eliminator. To use it as if it were a closed carrier would be to import forward-binding belief (\autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-input-and-induction}).

\begin{definition}[Generator-local induction]
\label{def:external-supply-generator-local-induction}
Generator-local induction is induction whose quantifier ranges only over the closure of a displayed generator $G$, and whose step cases are exactly the constructor cases of $G$. Its eliminator is part of the generator's public structure.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Free-floating induction]
\label{def:external-supply-free-floating-induction}
Free-floating induction is an induction claim whose domain is not the closure of any displayed generator and whose universal quantifier ranges over an unspecified semantic totality.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Induction boundary]
\label{prin:external-supply-induction-boundary}
Generator-local induction is an internal method. Free-floating induction is a boundary pressure. The former may be discharged by an eliminator; the latter must be marked by a $\mathsf{GAP}$/$\mathsf{T}$-socket or replaced by a certified generator.
\end{principle}

This distinction answers the recurring question about mathematical induction. BEDC may prove structural claims about $\mathsf{Hist}$, $\mathsf{Term}$, $\mathsf{List}$, $\mathbb{N}$-like unary histories, and other authorized carriers. But if a claim requires induction over a domain not yet reconstructed as a BEDC carrier, the missing domain cannot be installed by axiom. It must be given as a $\mathsf{NameCert}$, setup field, bridge slot, or socketed obligation.

\section{Choice, quotient, and proposition equality}
\label{sec:external-supply-three-host-axioms}

The forbidden trio --- \texttt{Classical.choice}, \texttt{Quot.sound}, and \texttt{propext} --- are not three unrelated restrictions. They are three ways to accept an unwitnessed existential as usable content at different strata; see \autoref{ch:visions-three-axioms-one-closure} for the meta-closure unification.

At the term stratum, choice supplies a selector without constructing the selected values. At the type stratum, quotient soundness permits a collapsed representative discipline without preserving the source-level representatives as public data. At the proposition stratum, propositional extensionality identifies propositions from bidirectional implication without keeping the transport route explicit.

BEDC replaces these by witnessed forms:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{choice} &\;\leadsto\; \text{finite, countable, or dependent selection with explicit witness functions;} \\
\text{quotient soundness} &\;\leadsto\; \mathsf{NameCert}\text{ classifier transport and }\mathsf{psame}/\mathsf{hsame}\text{ reflection;} \\
\text{propext} &\;\leadsto\; \text{explicit directional transport between proof consumers.}
\end{aligned}
$$

\begin{principle}[No unwitnessed existential as input]
\label{prin:external-supply-no-unwitnessed-existential}
No BEDC proof may consume an existential, representative, equality, selector, or equivalence as input unless the witness route is displayed at the term, classifier, or ledger level.
\end{principle}

This principle is stronger than saying ``avoid classical axioms.'' It says what the classical axioms do that BEDC refuses: they turn missing witnesses into available inputs. The replacement discipline keeps the use-site and the supply-site connected.

\section{Observer growth and selector ledgers}
\label{sec:external-supply-observer-selectors}

The same boundary rule governs observer growth. A local $\mathsf{Hist}$ may grow by an enacted mark. But if a branch is selected, the selected mark must appear in the history or its selector ledger. There is no hidden chooser behind the observer (\autoref{ch:visions-observer-hist-identity}).

\begin{definition}[Selector ledger]
\label{def:external-supply-selector-ledger}
A selector ledger for a history transition is a displayed record containing the chosen mark, the local step witness, and the certificate that the step is admissible in the current interface:
$$
\mathrm{SelStep}(h,r) := (m,\, \mathrm{step},\, \mathrm{cert}).
$$
\end{definition}

The next history is not merely asserted to exist; it is reached through the displayed mark and step witness.

\begin{principle}[No observer-choice axiom]
\label{prin:external-supply-no-observer-choice}
When several local continuations are admissible, BEDC may not introduce a global chooser selecting among them. A realized branch must be recorded as a selector ledger. An unrealized family of possible branches remains a local possibility space, not an internal selection function.
\end{principle}

This protects both axiom purity and the observer-$\mathsf{Hist}$ identity. If an observer were equipped with a hidden selection function, the observer would no longer be only the generated history --- it would carry an extra subject-like substance behind the trace. BEDC's no-subject discipline forbids that move. The trace is the observer; the selector event is part of the trace; no separate chooser is admitted.

\section{Multi-Hist physics without global axioms}
\label{sec:external-supply-multihist-physics}

The boundary rule also governs the physical readings of BEDC (\autoref{ch:visions-inter-hist-locality}). Multi-$\mathsf{Hist}$ configurations do not license a global clock, a global frame, a privileged observer, or a universal spacetime container. Such objects may be useful as external models, but they cannot be placed into the BEDC kernel by axiom.

If a multi-observer structure is to enter BEDC, it must enter by ledger-local relations and inter-$\mathsf{Hist}$ invariants. A comparison between histories must be witnessed by finite continuation data, emitted marks, classifier comparisons, package rows, or naming certificates. A proposed maximum cross-$\mathsf{Hist}$ causal rate is not asserted as a physical axiom; it is a $\mathsf{NameCert}$ target or schema-only horizon whose closure obligations remain visible.

\begin{principle}[No global-frame axiom]
\label{prin:external-supply-no-global-frame}
No global time, global frame, global observer, or global synchronization map may be added as a kernel-level axiom. Any synchronization must be an extension carrier above the kernel and must expose its source, classifier, stability, and ledger rows.
\end{principle}

\begin{principle}[Physical constants as invariant targets]
\label{prin:external-supply-physical-constants-invariant}
A physical constant in the BEDC reading is not a primitive scalar inserted into the kernel. It is an invariant target of an admissible inter-$\mathsf{Hist}$ relation. If the maximum causal rate is reconstructed, its constancy must be shown as observer-symmetric preservation under the licensed transformations, not postulated as a global axiom.
\end{principle}

This preserves the earlier insight that information conservation and locality should not force a God's-eye object into the theory. The universe is not admitted as a single global $\mathsf{Hist}$. It is represented, where BEDC can represent it, by local histories, ledger-local relations, and invariants whose witnesses remain finite and auditable.

\section{Audit gates as the enforcement layer}
\label{sec:external-supply-audit-gates}

A named boundary is only useful if a gate can test whether it has remained visible. BEDC's audit gates give the operational form of the non-axiom discipline (\autoref{ch:visions-audit-gates-as-transcendental-boundary}).

\begin{definition}[Boundary gate]
\label{def:external-supply-boundary-gate}
A boundary gate is a finite check whose surface condition enforces a source-channel rule. Its input may be a Lean declaration, a dependency graph, an import edge, a paper marker, or a certificate row. Its output is not a metaphysical theorem; it is an audit judgment that a specified boundary has or has not been crossed.
\end{definition}

The main gates are:
$$
\begin{aligned}
&\text{axiom scan} && \text{rejects substrate-internal axiom declarations;} \\
&\text{strict axiom-purity} && \text{rejects transitive dependence on Classical.choice, Quot.sound, propext;} \\
&\text{conservativity audit} && \text{rejects source-channel pollution across surfaces;} \\
&\text{paper-Lean audit} && \text{rejects claims whose Lean names do not resolve;} \\
&\text{axis-confusion gate} && \text{rejects collapse of theory closure into verification status;} \\
&\text{tactic discipline} && \text{rejects tactics that silently import forbidden host commitments.}
\end{aligned}
$$

\begin{principle}[Gate correspondence]
\label{prin:external-supply-gate-correspondence}
Every boundary form should determine a gate question. If a dependency is named as a socket, the gate asks whether it was later consumed as internal theorem content. If a compression is named as a $\mathsf{GAP}$, the gate asks whether the source residue remained recorded. If a proof claims axiom purity, the gate asks whether the transitive proof ancestry is free of forbidden axioms.
\end{principle}

The gate does not prove the full philosophical reading. It protects one face of the boundary. BEDC's discipline is compositional: many small gates together prevent many small source-erasing moves from becoming a hidden global assumption.

\section{The non-axiom theorem schema}
\label{sec:external-supply-theorem-schema}

The following schema records the main theorem shape of this chapter. It is stated as a paper-side governance theorem because the exact implementation depends on the surrounding interface.

\begin{theorem}[Non-axiom boundary preservation schema]
\label{thm:external-supply-non-axiom-preservation}
Let $X$ be a proposed BEDC dependency. Suppose $X$ is consumed by a public theorem, certificate, or interface. If every use of $X$ factors through one of the non-axiom boundary forms of \autoref{def:external-supply-non-axiom-boundary-form}, and every corresponding gate reports that the boundary form remains visible, then $X$ has not been internalized as a substrate axiom.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Proceed by cases on the boundary form. An inductive generator supplies constructors and eliminators, so the source is displayed. A \texttt{def} unfolds to earlier material, so the source is computationally traceable. A setup field remains a field of the interface, so the caller must supply it. A $\mathsf{NameCert}$ exposes source, pattern, classifier, stability, and ledger rows. A $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger records compression residue or socket pressure. A bridge slot is interpretive and does not replace the internal proof obligation. An apophatic socket names an external dependency without providing content as theorem. In each case, the use of $X$ is source-channel visible. The audit gates rule out the opposite cases: axiom declaration, forbidden transitive host axiom, unledgered quotient, unresolved paper marker, and axis collapse. Therefore the dependency remains externalized rather than assumed.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Axiom-free does not mean dependence-free]
\label{cor:external-supply-axiomfree-not-dependencefree}
A BEDC development may be axiom-free while still exposing external dependencies. Axiom-free means that such dependencies do not become substrate-internal sources of truth. It does not mean that no boundary, bridge, socket, or setup field exists.
\end{corollary}

\begin{corollary}[Socketed content is not a theorem]
\label{cor:external-supply-socket-not-theorem}
A socketed dependency is not an internally proved theorem. It is a named site of non-internal supply. The manuscript may reason about the socket's role, audit surface, and required supply shape, but it may not consume the socket as if the supplied content had been generated.
\end{corollary}

\section{Boundary of this chapter}
\label{sec:external-supply-boundary-of-this-chapter}

This chapter does not claim that every external dependency has already been reconstructed inside BEDC. It does not claim that every socket can be discharged. It does not claim that host Lean's runtime, CIC truth, mathematical completeness, physical spacetime, or free-floating induction has been internalized. It also does not prove that no hidden assumption can ever appear in prose.

Its claim is narrower: when BEDC sees external pressure, it must expose the pressure rather than axiomize it.

The chapter is therefore a governance layer, not a new foundation. It strengthens the manuscript by making explicit how to proceed when the theory reaches a boundary: name the boundary, state the supply shape, record the ledger, attach the gate, and refuse to call the result an internal theorem until a certificate actually reconstructs it.

\section{Closure}
\label{sec:external-supply-closure}

BEDC is not a system with no externality. It is a system that refuses anonymous externality. This is the correct interpretation of its axiom-purity discipline. External supply may appear as setup field, $\mathsf{NameCert}$ row, $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, bridge slot, audit boundary, or apophatic socket. It may not appear as axiom.

The distinction is load-bearing. Axioms internalize unsupported commitments. Sockets externalize unsupported dependencies. $\mathsf{GAP}$ records the memory of compression and the pressure of forward-binding supply. Audit gates enforce that the source-channel remains visible. Generator-local induction is internal; free-floating universal induction is socketed. Local observer growth is selector-ledgered; hidden observer choice is refused. Multi-$\mathsf{Hist}$ physics proceeds by invariants; global frame axioms are refused.

The final rule is simple: do not add an axiom; expose the boundary.

Concretized in a future implementation: a finite $\mathsf{ExternalSupplyBoundary}$ packet should expose boundary name, requested supply shape, non-internalization status, accepted boundary form, associated $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, and gate question. The packet is not a theorem that the supply exists. It is the audit-visible record that the supply has not been smuggled into BEDC as a hidden source of truth.
