\section{Ontic state as stable signature source}
\label{sec:ontic-state-stable-signature-source}

The distinction between mathematical approximation and physical adequacy has an ontological counterpart. A physical object is not identified with an arbitrary mathematical representation, nor with the final visible idealization produced by an approximation tower. It is approached as the stable source of reality-facing signatures. Mathematical representations remain indispensable, but they become physically meaningful only when tied to the signatures through which the system manifests itself under admissible observation bundles.

\begin{definition}[Ontic state]
\label{def:reality-constrained-ontic-state}
For a physical system $R$ and an admissible family of observation bundles $\mathcal P$, the ontic state of $R$ is the stable source of the system's reality-facing signatures:
$$
\operatorname{Ont}(R)
:=
\operatorname{StableSource}
\bigl(
\{\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(R)\}_{\Pi\in\mathcal P}
\bigr).
$$
This notation does not identify $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ with a single representation. A representation $M$ expresses an aspect of $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ only relative to specified bundles, classifiers, stability data, and ledgers.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ontic adequacy]
\label{def:ontic-adequacy}
A mathematical representation $M$ is ontically adequate for $R$ relative to $\Pi$ when it preserves the physical signatures generated by the ontic state of $R$:
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M)
\sim_{\Pi}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(\operatorname{Ont}(R)).
$$
Ontic adequacy is stronger than formal consistency and stronger than mathematical approximation. It requires preservation of the reality-facing properties through which the system's state is physically manifested.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ontic underdetermination]
\label{def:ontic-underdetermination}
Representations $M_1$ and $M_2$ are ontically underdetermined by $\Pi$ when
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M_1)
\sim_{\Pi}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M_2),
$$
but there is a refined observation bundle $\Pi'\supseteq\Pi$ such that
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi'}(M_1)
\not\sim_{\Pi'}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi'}(M_2).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ontic residue]
\label{def:ontic-residue}
Let $M$ be a representation of $R$ relative to $\Pi$. The ontic residue of $M$ relative to $R$ and $\Pi$ is the reality-facing content of $R$ not preserved, represented, or queried by $M$ under $\Pi$. Schematically,
$$
\operatorname{Res}_{\Pi}(R,M)
:=
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\mathcal P}(R)
\setminus
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M),
$$
where $\mathcal P$ denotes a broader family of admissible observation bundles. The notation names hidden or unqueried physical content; it need not be an ordinary set difference.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Representation ledger]
\label{def:representation-ledger}
A representation ledger for $M$ as a model of $R$ records
$$
\operatorname{RepLedger}(M,R;\Pi)
=
(H_n,\Pi,\operatorname{Classifier},\operatorname{StabilityCert},
\operatorname{Res}_{\Pi}(R,M)).
$$
It records the finite observational source, observation bundle, comparison classifier, stability certificate, and residue hidden by the representation.
\end{definition}

This is the physical counterpart of BEDC's $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger requirement. Compression is permitted, but the source route and hidden material must remain traceable; when compressions compose, the resulting ledger must retain the source map through the intermediate visible interfaces.

\begin{theorem}[Syntax does not exhaust ontic state]
\label{thm:syntax-does-not-exhaust-ontic-state}
Let $M$ be a mathematical representation of a physical system $R$. The syntactic existence of $M$ does not imply that $M$ exhausts $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
A mathematical representation supplies equations, spaces, functions, parameters, diagrams, or computational rules. By \autoref{def:reality-constrained-ontic-state}, $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ is the stable source of reality-facing signatures across admissible bundles. To exhaust that source, $M$ would have to preserve every relevant physical signature of $R$ across those bundles. The mere existence of the syntax of $M$ supplies no observation bundle, classifier, stability condition, or residue ledger. Therefore syntax alone cannot establish ontic exhaustion.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Model as ontic interface]
\label{cor:model-as-ontic-interface}
A model is a certified interface to $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$, not the ontic state itself.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{thm:syntax-does-not-exhaust-ontic-state}, a representation does not exhaust the ontic state by syntactic existence. By \autoref{def:ontic-adequacy}, it becomes ontically adequate only relative to a signature-preservation condition. It therefore functions as an interface to the ontic state under specified conditions.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Realism requires representation ledgers]
\label{cor:realism-requires-representation-ledgers}
A realist interpretation of a model requires a certificate specifying which physical signatures are preserved and which remain as residue.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If correspondence is read as ontic adequacy, then \autoref{def:ontic-adequacy} requires signature preservation. By \autoref{def:representation-ledger}, such preservation must be accompanied by source, classifier, stability, and residue data. Bare representation is therefore insufficient.
\end{proof}

\section{Signature priority and exactness}
\label{sec:signature-priority-exactness}

\begin{definition}[Signature-realization map]
\label{def:signature-realization-map}
A signature-realization map for a representation $M$ under $\Pi$ is a map
$$
\sigma_{M,\Pi}:M\longrightarrow\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M)
$$
that extracts the physical signature predicted by $M$ under the observation bundle $\Pi$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Signature completeness and soundness]
\label{def:signature-completeness-soundness}
A representation $M$ is signature-complete for $R$ relative to $\Pi$ when every reality-facing distinction of $R$ exposed by $\Pi$ is matched by a model distinction:
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(R)
\preceq
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M).
$$
It is signature-sound when every distinction predicted by $M$ under $\Pi$ is physically realized by $R$:
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M)
\preceq
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(R).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Signature exactness]
\label{def:signature-exactness}
A representation $M$ is signature-exact for $R$ relative to $\Pi$ when it is both signature-complete and signature-sound, equivalently when
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(M)
\sim_{\Pi}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(R).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Physical adequacy is signature exactness]
\label{thm:physical-adequacy-is-signature-exactness}
A representation $M$ is physically adequate for $R$ relative to $\Pi$ if and only if it is signature-exact for $R$ relative to $\Pi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Physical adequacy is the preservation of the reality-facing signature under the relevant observation bundle. This is exactly the equivalence displayed in \autoref{def:signature-exactness}. Completeness says the model misses no relevant distinction; soundness says it invents no unsupported distinction. Together these are signature-level adequacy.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Adequacy does not collapse representation into source]
\label{cor:adequacy-does-not-collapse-representation}
A physically adequate model may be mathematically non-identical to the system it represents.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{thm:physical-adequacy-is-signature-exactness}, adequacy requires matching signatures under $\Pi$, not identity of representation and source. The model remains a representation and the system remains the source of the signatures.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Mathematical exactness may be physically inadequate]
\label{cor:mathematical-exactness-physically-inadequate}
A representation may be mathematically exact within its formal system while failing to be physically adequate for $R$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Mathematical exactness concerns internal validity relative to formal definitions. Physical adequacy concerns signature exactness relative to reality-facing observation. If the mathematically exact model fails to preserve the observed signatures, it is physically inadequate despite its internal exactness.
\end{proof}

\section{Residue and refinement}
\label{sec:ontic-residue-refinement}

\begin{theorem}[Coarse bundles induce ontic underdetermination]
\label{thm:coarse-bundles-induce-ontic-underdetermination}
Let $\Pi$ expose only a proper subset of the reality-facing properties relevant to $R$. Then there may exist distinct representations $M_1$ and $M_2$ such that
$$
M_1\equiv_{\mathrm{phys},\Pi}M_2
$$
while $M_1$ and $M_2$ are separated by a refined bundle $\Pi'\supseteq\Pi$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Because $\Pi$ exposes only a subset of the relevant properties, it ignores distinctions that may be exposed by a refinement. Choose representations that agree on all distinctions visible to $\Pi$ but differ on a distinction visible under $\Pi'$. They are physically fit-equivalent under $\Pi$ and separated under $\Pi'$, which is exactly ontic underdetermination relative to the coarse bundle.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Underdetermination is bundle-relative]
\label{cor:underdetermination-bundle-relative}
Underdetermination is indexed by an observation bundle, not absolute.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The same pair of representations can be equivalent under $\Pi$ and inequivalent under $\Pi'$. Therefore the underdetermination judgment depends on the bundle.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Refinement can make ambiguity empirical]
\label{cor:refinement-makes-ambiguity-empirical}
A distinction that appears metaphysical under a coarse bundle may become empirical under a refined bundle.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If $\Pi$ cannot expose a distinction, the difference is not empirically separated at that level. If a refined bundle $\Pi'$ exposes it, \autoref{thm:coarse-bundles-induce-ontic-underdetermination} turns the difference into a signature separation.
\end{proof}

\begin{definition}[Physically active residue]
\label{def:physically-active-residue}
Residue $\operatorname{Res}_{\Pi}(R,M)$ is inactive relative to $\Pi$ when it does not separate $M$ from $R$ under $\Pi$. It becomes physically active under a refined bundle $\Pi'$ when
$$
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi'}(M)
\not\sim_{\Pi'}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi'}(R).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Hidden residue can become active]
\label{thm:hidden-residue-can-become-active}
If $M$ is physically adequate for $R$ under $\Pi$, and a refined bundle $\Pi'\supseteq\Pi$ exposes residue not preserved by $M$, then $M$ is not physically adequate under $\Pi'$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Adequacy under $\Pi$ gives signature exactness at the coarse level. If $\Pi'$ exposes residue not preserved by $M$, then the model-generated and reality-generated signatures differ under $\Pi'$. By \autoref{thm:physical-adequacy-is-signature-exactness}, physical adequacy fails at the refined level.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Idealization is scale-relative]
\label{cor:idealization-scale-relative}
An idealization may be physically adequate at one scale and inadequate at another.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Observation scale is part of the bundle. A coarse bundle may leave residue inactive, while a refined-scale bundle may expose it. \autoref{thm:hidden-residue-can-become-active} then separates adequacy at the two scales.
\end{proof}

\section{Observer-internal access}
\label{sec:observer-internal-ontic-access}

\begin{definition}[Observer-internal ontic access]
\label{def:observer-internal-ontic-access}
Observer-internal access to $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ is access in which every claim about the ontic state factors through
$$
H_n(R)
\longrightarrow
\operatorname{ObsSig}_{\Pi}(R\mid H_n)
\longrightarrow
\operatorname{Classifier}_{\Pi}
\longrightarrow
\operatorname{FitCert}_n(M,R;\Pi).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ontic humility]
\label{def:ontic-humility}
Ontic humility is the methodological constraint that no model may be asserted to exhaust $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ unless the relevant signature bundles, stability certificates, and ledgers have been specified. It is a discipline on representation, not skepticism about reality.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Observer-internal access does not eliminate ontic reality]
\label{thm:observer-internal-access-not-subjectivism}
If access to $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ is observer-internal, it does not follow that $\operatorname{Ont}(R)$ is merely subjective.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Observer-internal access means that claims about $R$ pass through finite traces, signatures, classifiers, and certificates. It does not say that signatures are freely invented. Finite fit requires comparison between model-generated and reality-generated signatures, and failed fit occurs when reality generates a signature not matched by the model. Reality therefore constrains the observer-internal process.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Objectivity as invariant signature stability]
\label{cor:objectivity-invariant-signature-stability}
Objectivity is the stability of signatures across admissible observers, bundles, and continuations, not access to reality from nowhere.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{thm:observer-internal-access-not-subjectivism}, reality constrains observer-internal access through signatures. If those signatures remain stable across admissible changes of observer, bundle, and continuation, the content is not private to one trace. It is invariant across the observational structure.
\end{proof}

\section{Certified ontic towers}
\label{sec:certified-ontic-towers}

The expression
$$
\operatorname{Ont}(R)
\sim
\lim_{\Pi\in\mathcal P}
\operatorname{PhysSig}_{\Pi}(R)
$$
must be read as schematic. The limit is not a primitive completion imported into the theory; it names a research target: the reconstruction of ontic access as a certified tower of increasingly refined signatures.

\begin{definition}[Certified ontic tower]
\label{def:certified-ontic-tower}
A certified ontic tower for $R$ is a chain
$$
\Pi_0\preceq\Pi_1\preceq\Pi_2\preceq\cdots
$$
of increasingly refined observation bundles, together with fit certificates $\operatorname{FitCert}_i(M_i,R;\Pi_i)$ and transition certificates $\operatorname{TransCert}_{i\to j}$ showing how signatures at level $i$ are preserved, refined, or corrected at level $j$. The tower is ledger-complete when each transition records what was hidden, preserved, and newly exposed.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Ontic convergence]
\label{def:ontic-convergence}
A certified ontic tower converges ontically relative to a family of physical questions $\mathcal Q$ when, for every $q\in\mathcal Q$, there is a stage $i$ such that all later stages preserve the answer to $q$ up to the relevant physical classifier:
$$
\forall q\in\mathcal Q,\;
\exists i,\;
\forall j\geq i,\;
\operatorname{Ans}_{\Pi_j}(q)
\sim_q
\operatorname{Ans}_{\Pi_i}(q).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Ontic convergence yields stable content, not final totality]
\label{thm:ontic-convergence-stable-not-total}
If a certified ontic tower converges relative to $\mathcal Q$, then it yields stable physical content for questions in $\mathcal Q$, but it does not yield a final total description of $R$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{def:ontic-convergence}, every question in $\mathcal Q$ eventually stabilizes across the tower, so the tower yields stable content for those questions. The family $\mathcal Q$ need not contain every admissible physical question. A new question, refined bundle, or new scale of interaction may expose previously inactive residue. Therefore convergence over $\mathcal Q$ does not imply final totality.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Ontic knowledge is expandable and constrained]
\label{cor:ontic-knowledge-expandable-constrained}
A theory can become more ontically informative by expanding its certified tower, but the expansion is constrained by signature preservation and ledger continuity.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Adding a bundle or question expands the tower. If the expansion is certified, it must preserve, refine, or explicitly correct earlier signatures while recording the relevant ledger. The growth is therefore real but not arbitrary.
\end{proof}
