\chapter{MetaCIC ClosurePreservation module audit map}
\label{ch:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map}

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-setup}

\texttt{ClosurePreservation.lean} is a single-file module inside
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC}. It records the closedness-preservation layer for the
core syntax operations that later appear in beta reduction and its finite
closures. The file is deliberately narrower than subject reduction: its
classifier is \texttt{ClosedAt}, not \texttt{HasType}.

The public surface is a five-theorem chain. It starts with shift at one
successor depth, passes through variable substitution and full term
substitution at an arbitrary closedness boundary, and then lifts the result to
one-step beta reduction and finite beta-step chains. Each theorem is an
ordinary structural fact about MetaCIC terms and the closedness predicate.

This gives a useful contrast with the subject-reduction obstruction in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}. Closure preservation is
available without extra typing hypotheses because no dependent codomain is
being transported. Subject reduction is blocked at the typing layer, where
application arguments, binder domains, and beta substitution must preserve
displayed types.

\section{Established theorems}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-established-theorems}

\subsection{\texttt{shift\_one\_preserves\_closed\_succ}}
\label{subsec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-shift-one}

The shift theorem is
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.shift\_one\_preserves\_closed\_succ}.
It says that shifting by one at depth $n$ preserves closedness at the
successor boundary. In schematic form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n+1,\mathsf{shift}(n,1,t)).
\end{aligned}
$$

The theorem is the local shift witness needed by substitution and beta
closure. Under binders, the closedness boundary advances in the same way as the
term recursion. The proof obligation is therefore structural: every free index
that was already below the source boundary remains below the target boundary
after the one-step lift.

\subsection{\texttt{substitute\_var\_preserves\_closed\_at}}
\label{subsec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-substitute-var}

The variable-level substitution theorem is
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_var\_preserves\_closed\_at}.
It is the pointwise closedness fact for the variable branch of substitution.
The theorem separates the arithmetic cases that full substitution later uses:
the replaced variable, indices below the replacement depth, and indices above
the replacement depth.

This theorem is not a typing statement. It does not assign a type to a
replacement payload and does not read back a substituted codomain. It only says
that the variable operation used by substitution respects the relevant
closedness boundary when its closedness inputs are present.

\subsection{\texttt{substitute\_preserves\_closed\_at}}
\label{subsec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-substitute}

The full substitution theorem is
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.substitute\_preserves\_closed\_at}.
It lifts the variable theorem through the full \texttt{Term} recursion. In
schematic form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n+1,t)
  \wedge
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,v)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,\mathsf{substitute}(n,v,t)).
\end{aligned}
$$

The displayed form records the usual binder budget: the substituted term is
closed one level deeper, and the payload is closed at the receiving boundary.
After substitution removes the selected variable, the result is closed at the
receiving boundary.

This is the main closure-preservation lemma for syntactic substitution. It is
strong enough to support beta-redex closure, because beta contraction is
implemented by \texttt{substitute}. It is not strong enough to give beta
subject reduction, because subject reduction also requires the type assigned to
the contractum to be the substituted codomain.

\subsection{\texttt{betaStep\_preserves\_closed}}
\label{subsec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-beta-step}

The one-step beta theorem is
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStep\_preserves\_closed}.
It states that a single beta step preserves \texttt{ClosedAt} at any depth:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{BetaStep}(t,t')
  \wedge
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t').
\end{aligned}
$$

The beta-redex constructor uses
\texttt{substitute\_preserves\_closed\_at}. The congruence constructors use
the corresponding recursive closedness components for applications, lambdas,
and Pi types. No typing context is inspected, and no conversion rule is
introduced.

This theorem is a genuine building block for any typed route through beta
reduction. It guarantees that beta movement does not leave the closed-term
slice. Its scope stops exactly there: preserving the closedness classifier is
not preserving the \texttt{HasType} relation.

\subsection{\texttt{betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}}
\label{subsec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-beta-star-step}

The finite beta-chain theorem is
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed}.
It lifts one-step closedness preservation from \texttt{BetaStep} to
\texttt{BetaStarStep}. In schematic form:

$$
\begin{aligned}
  \mathsf{BetaStarStep}(t,t')
  \wedge
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t)
  &\Rightarrow
  \mathsf{ClosedAt}(n,t').
\end{aligned}
$$

The theorem is the finite-iteration closure fact for beta reduction. A
zero-step chain is immediate, and a nonzero chain composes the one-step theorem
with the induction hypothesis for the remaining chain.

This is the exact closure fact consumed by closed-normal and conversion-facing
arguments: a closed term remains closed along any finite beta path. It does
not say that the term remains typed, normalizing, confluent, or convertible
under an unrestricted typing rule.

\section{Why ClosurePreservation is unconditional while SR is not}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-unconditional-sr}

The distinction is the classifier. ClosurePreservation works over
\texttt{ClosedAt}, a structural predicate on the syntax tree. The proof cases
follow the constructors of terms, beta steps, and finite beta chains. When the
operation descends under a binder, the closedness index advances. When beta
contracts a redex, the substitution theorem supplies the closedness budget for
the body and argument.

Subject reduction works over \texttt{HasType}. Its input is not just a term but
a typing derivation in a context with a displayed type. The dependent-codomain
cases record information that closedness cannot see. Reducing an argument can
change the codomain read through that argument. Reducing a displayed binder
domain can change the type annotation that a typing derivation depends on.
Contracting a beta redex requires the body type and codomain to be transported
through substitution.

Therefore the closure layer is unconditional, while the subject-reduction
layer remains conditional at the interface named in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}. The two statements do not
compete. They live over different classifiers.

\section{Role within MetaCIC}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-role}

ClosurePreservation supplies the closed-term invariant used around subject
reduction, confluence, normalization, beta conversion, and closed consistency.
It says that the syntax operations used by beta reduction do not escape the
closed slice once their inputs are closed at the required boundary.

For subject-reduction routes, this removes one possible source of failure. A
typed proof term that is already closed does not become open merely because a
beta step or a finite beta path is taken. The remaining obstruction is not
free-variable escape. It is the dependent typing transport described by the
subject-reduction map.

For confluence and normalization routes, the same invariant lets the paper
keep closed-term hypotheses stable while it discusses common reducts, normal
forms, and beta-strong fragments. A finite beta path may be followed without
rechecking the closedness boundary from scratch at each endpoint.

\section{Connection to GeneratorClosureClassifier}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-generator-classifier}

The substitution and shift theorems instantiate the generator-closure reading
of \autoref{def:certificate-closure-generator-closure-classifier}. A generator
is authorized only relative to a classifier, and its closure field records
that admitted inputs remain inside the classifier after the generator acts.

For MetaCIC closedness, the relevant classifier is \texttt{ClosedAt}. The
concrete generator witnesses are
\texttt{closed\_under\_shift\_classifier} and
\texttt{closed\_under\_substitute\_classifier}. The shift
and substitution preservation theorems are exactly the local facts that make
those witnesses meaningful.

Beta preservation then reads as a composed generator story. The beta redex
case uses substitution, the congruence cases recurse through term
constructors, and the beta-star theorem applies finite iteration. The
classifier stays closed under those authorized moves, but the classifier is
still closedness rather than typing.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:metacic-closure-preservation-audit-map-boundary}

This chapter records the closure state of
\texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC.ClosurePreservation}. It may be used to cite the five
checked preservation theorems named above and the concrete
GeneratorClosureClassifier witnesses for shift and substitution.

It does not claim that closure preservation implies typing preservation. It
does not claim that \texttt{betaStarStep\_preserves\_closed} implies typed
subject reduction for closed terms. It does not supply a conversion rule for
typing or a discharge of the dependent-codomain, binder-domain, or beta
substitution obligations isolated in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}.
