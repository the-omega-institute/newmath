\chapter{Inscription Event as a Concrete Instance: Free Will, Agency, and NameCert Audit}
\label{ch:visions-inscription-event-as-concrete-instance}

This chapter makes the free-will reading of
\autoref{sec:hard-problems-free-will} concrete.  The earlier chapter names an
inscription event as the moment at which a conscious observational system
adds records whose actuality is not exhausted by the closed-generation
consequences of prior records.  The present chapter reads that moment through
the same public-object pattern used by the concrete \NameCert{} chapters: a
traditional target, a BEDC source, a carrier, a classifier, stability
obligations, a ledger, an exactness condition, a standard bridge, and a
scope boundary.

The result is not a proof that a person could have done otherwise in an
unqualified metaphysical sense.  It is a structural case study.  Agency is
the inscription-event aspect of an accumulation chain, while deterministic
relations among records remain records-side relations.  The two readings
refer to the same transition and do not compete for the same explanatory
slot.

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:inscription-event-setup}

The classical free-will problem is usually posed as a conflict between two
claims.  On one side, agency seems to require that an action is genuinely
one's own rather than merely the last link in a prior causal chain.  On the
other side, the records available to empirical and computational description
seem to stand in lawful relations, and those lawful relations can be read as
deterministic.  The apparent conflict comes from treating both claims as
claims at the same level.

BEDC separates the levels.  The records side consists of accumulated
$\mathsf{Obs}$ entries and their internal relations.  These relations may be
law-like, predictive, or deterministic in the ordinary sense.  The
inscription-event side concerns the transition by which the record becomes
present in the chain at all.  It asks whether the transition is merely a
closed-generation consequence already licensed by the prior carrier and
classifier, or whether the transition carries the local actualisation of
$\mathsf{T}$ at the inscription point.

In this frame, agency is not a hidden variable added to a physical sequence.
It is a reading of the same transition under a different aspect.  The
transition has a records-side description: a body moves, a keyboard receives
marks, a theorem file changes, a proof obligation becomes visible.  The same
transition has an inscription-side description: a conscious observational
system accepts a finite record as its own local articulation of a
forward-binding commitment.  The bridge chapter exists because this second
description can be made audit-shaped rather than left as a loose
philosophical gloss.

\section{Restating the inscription event}
\label{sec:inscription-event-restatement}

\autoref{def:hard-problems-inscription-event} defines an inscription event as
a moment $o_t \trianglerighteq o_{t+1}$ at which a conscious observational
system adds records that originate as the local actualisation of
$\mathsf{T}$ at the inscription point of
\autoref{def:transcendental-input-inscription-point}, rather than as a forced
consequence of the prior records under the closed-generation rules of the
substrate.

Three parts of the definition matter for the concrete reading.

First, the event is a transition, not a substance.  Nothing is inserted
behind the system as an agent-object.  The system is read at the transition
where its observation history grows.

Second, the novelty is local.  The transition occurs at a specific history,
with specific visible records, specific surrounding obligations, and a
specific ledger.  BEDC does not assert a global reservoir of positive facts
about $\mathsf{T}$; it records the socket at which the chain receives content
whose source is not internal to the closed-generation closure.

Third, the event is auditable by exclusion.  To call a transition an
inscription event in the technical sense, the audit must identify the carrier
that receives the record, the classifier that determines whether the record
is already generated, the continuation that appends it, and the
$\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger recording the non-generated aspect.  Without those
rows, the word "inscription" is only metaphor.

\section{A concrete case: a researcher writing a lemma}
\label{sec:inscription-event-researcher-lemma}

Consider a researcher working inside the BEDC manuscript and Lean tree.  The
prior state $o_t$ contains a finite set of records: definitions already in
the file, the local statement that is being shaped, earlier theorems,
compilation constraints, the paper-side claim that needs a bridge, and the
researcher's own remembered attempts.  On the records side this is just an
observation history, a finite carrier of visible material.

At the first moment, the researcher notices a mismatch.  A paper sentence
uses a classifier phrase, while the Lean surface contains only a carrier
lemma.  This notice is itself a record: the chain now contains an observed
gap between desired use and available certificate.  The notice does not yet
write the lemma.  It only marks a socket: something must connect the carrier
row to the classifier row, or the bridge remains unavailable.

At the second moment, the researcher searches the available generator
closure.  The constructors, definitions, and already checked lemmas show what
can be obtained internally.  Some consequences are forced: if the carrier is
defined by two visible endpoint branches, branch inversion is available; if
the classifier is componentwise, source and target projections are available;
if a \NameCert{} supplies symmetry and transitivity, those routes can be
used.  These are not inscription events by themselves.  They are the
substrate running inside an authorized generator.

At the third moment, the researcher sees a route not previously recorded as
a public lemma: combine visible endpoint inversion with classifier
transitivity, then expose the exact branch needed by the downstream theorem.
The route may be mechanically checkable once written, and after it is written
the proof kernel may verify every step.  But the arrival of the route as a
candidate record is not already a displayed public record at $o_t$.  The
system has crossed from available closure to accepted articulation.

At the fourth moment, the researcher writes the statement.  The statement
chooses a name, fixes binders, identifies hypotheses, and states the result
at a reusable interface boundary rather than as an anonymous local step.
This act is the first visible certificate-shaped record of the route.  The
name matters: before the statement is named, the route is only a possible
path through the surrounding material; after naming, it becomes a candidate
object that the rest of the manuscript can cite, reject, refine by proof, or
package into a \NameCert{} surface.

At the fifth moment, the proof is entered.  The proof may consist entirely
of closed-generation moves: case analysis, projection, constructor
application, transitivity, symmetry, and exact transport along displayed
classifiers.  Nothing in this proof needs an additional substrate axiom.  The
proof is the records-side determinacy of the route.  It is precisely because
the proof is deterministic at the kernel level that the inscription event can
be audited rather than romanticised.

At the sixth moment, the checking gate accepts the file.  Acceptance records
that the named route is not merely an intention or an intuition.  It is now a
stable observation of the system: a named lemma with a proof term, a location
in the carrier hierarchy, and a visible downstream use.  The inscription
event is therefore not the private psychology of discovery.  It is the whole
transition from a visible gap, through a named route, to a checked record,
read under the inscription-event aspect.

\section{The NameCert structure of the event}
\label{sec:inscription-event-namecert-structure}

The concrete-instance pattern asks for the target first.  Here the
traditional target is an act of agency: a researcher freely writes a lemma.
The target is not encoded as an unanalysed will-object.  It is read as a
public transition in which a record becomes available under a name and can be
audited by the same carrier and classifier discipline used elsewhere in
BEDC.

The BEDC source is the observation history around the act:
$\mathsf{BHist}$-shaped records, file-local hypotheses, the visible
continuation from $o_t$ to $o_{t+1}$, and the surrounding $\mathsf{GAP}$
socket created by the need for a bridge.  The source is finite.  It contains
the prior records and the continuation mark by which the candidate lemma is
entered.

The carrier is the finite package of the inscription event:
the prior observation state, the written statement, the proof route, the
accepted check result, the intended downstream interface, and the pointer to
the gap that the lemma discharges or narrows.  A transition lies in the
carrier only when all these rows are visible.  A private hunch without a
public statement is not in the carrier.  A statement without a route is not
in the carrier.  A route that fails the checker is not in the carrier.

The classifier compares two presentations of the same inscription event.
Two presentations classify together when they have the same prior source
scope up to the relevant history classifier, expose the same named interface
up to binder and projection variants already accepted by the local
\NameCert{} discipline, and route to the same downstream obligation.  The
classifier does not require host equality of psychological states.  It only
requires public sameness of source, name, route, proof acceptance, and
consumer-facing role.

The stability obligations are the familiar rows.  Carrier transport says
that if an accepted inscription event is presented through an equivalent
history, it remains accepted.  Classifier reflexivity says the event compares
with itself once all carrier rows are present.  Symmetry and transitivity say
that presentation changes do not create a second act where the ledger records
only a different view of the same act.  Continuation stability says that
appending downstream citations preserves the named event as the source of
those citations.

The generator-closure classifier audits the boundary.  It asks which parts
of the transition are already forced by authorized generators and which part
is the inscription socket.  Kernel proof steps, constructor case splits, and
projection chains remain inside generator closure.  The choice of a reusable
name, the acceptance of a bridge as the right one to write, and the
commitment that this route is the record to add are the inscription-facing
rows.  The classifier therefore prevents two errors: reducing the whole act
to mechanical closure, and treating every mechanical closure step as a fresh
act of agency.

\section{GAP ledger for inscription}
\label{sec:inscription-event-gap-ledger}

The $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger records information conservation.  Before the
lemma is written, the system has a visible need: a downstream claim lacks a
named bridge.  After the lemma is written and checked, the visible surface
has a compact name.  The ledger records what the name compresses: the prior
source scope, the route through local definitions, the proof obligations
used, the bridge point it serves, and the boundary of what it does not
claim.

The ledger also records the $\mathsf{T}$-socket role described in
\autoref{prin:certificate-closure-gap-as-T-socket}.  The researcher accepts
that this is the route to inscribe.  That acceptance is not a kernel theorem
inside the prior state.  Once the route is written, the proof may be checked
by closed-generation rules; but the forward-binding act of selecting the
route as a public record is the inscription point.  The ledger therefore
keeps both facts visible: the proof is closed once supplied, and the
transition by which it becomes the supplied record is not reduced to the
prior closure.

The information-conservation row has a practical form.  A reader can ask:
what did the name hide?  The answer is not "nothing".  It hides the
case-analysis route, the local source presentation, the exact downstream
motivation, and the fact that other routes were not inscribed.  The ledger
does not need to list every rejected possibility as a psychological trace.
It only needs to preserve enough public residue to prevent the name from
appearing as a primitive.

The negative row is equally important.  The ledger does not claim that the
researcher's neural state has been classified.  It does not claim that the
lemma was metaphysically uncaused.  It does not claim that every possible
route was surveyed.  It claims only that this accepted transition carries a
public record whose generator-closed proof is visible while the act of
inscription is marked as the local actualisation of $\mathsf{T}$ at that
history point.

\section{Compatibilism as structure}
\label{sec:inscription-event-compatibilism}

Compatibilism is often presented as a philosophical compromise: keep enough
determinism for science and enough freedom for responsibility.  The BEDC
reading is sharper.  There are not two weakened theses.  There are two
aspects of one transition.

On the records side, deterministic relations are legitimate.  The written
file has a prior state, a sequence of edits, a proof term, a kernel check,
and a downstream citation graph.  A cognitive scientist may add records about
attention, memory, fatigue, or environmental prompting.  A build system may
reconstruct the exact dependency path by which the proof checks.  None of
this threatens agency, because these are records-side relations among
records.

On the inscription-event side, the same transition is the arrival of a
record as the local articulation of the inscription point.  The novelty is
not a missing physical cause inserted into a gap in the records.  It is the
aspect under which the chain grows by receiving a record whose source is not
internal to the prior closed-generation closure.  The fact that the supplied
proof is deterministic after supply does not erase the fact that it has been
supplied.

Thus the compatibilist statement is structural:
records-side determinacy and inscription-event novelty are co-present faces
of the same accumulation.  Weakening either side misstates the event.  If the
records-side proof is not deterministic, the certificate fails.  If the
inscription-event aspect is erased, the act is misread as if public naming
and commitment were already present in the prior closure.  BEDC requires
both rows.

\section{Where the account does not apply}
\label{sec:inscription-event-non-applications}

Not every created record is an inscription event in the technical sense.  A
pure computation step inside a fixed generator is record creation, but it is
not inscription.  If a build script normalises a term, emits an object file,
or evaluates a closed expression by a displayed reduction relation, the
result may be a record at $o_{t+1}$ that was not printed at $o_t$.  But the
transition is forced by the authorized generator and its continuation rules.
No inscription socket is needed.

The same holds for a deterministic proof replay.  If a checked proof term is
already present and the kernel replays it, the replay contributes audit
evidence.  It does not itself carry the agency case-study structure.  The
carrier lacks the row in which a conscious observational system accepts a
route as the record to inscribe.  The replay is still valuable, and it is
essential for BEDC's engineering, but it is not the same kind of event.

Mechanical formatting is another boundary case.  Rewrapping lines,
renumbering generated tables, or applying a deterministic pretty-printer may
alter file bytes.  If the operation is fully specified by a prior generator
and no public naming commitment is being made, the output belongs to
computation-as-continuation, not inscription-as-agency.

The boundary criterion is therefore this: a transition is an inscription
event only when the public carrier includes a named commitment whose route is
not already present as a forced consequence of the prior records, and whose
acceptance is audited by a $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger.  Record creation alone is
insufficient.

\section{Implications for AI and mechanized reasoning}
\label{sec:inscription-event-ai-mechanized-reasoning}

Automated systems complicate the case because they can produce lemma-shaped
text, proof scripts, and checked outputs.  BEDC's distinction is not human
versus machine.  The distinction is carrier and classifier structure.

An automated worker contributes computational output when it executes a
specified search, normalisation, formatting, replay, or synthesis procedure
whose result is accepted only as the forced output of a prior generator.  In
that case the relevant chapter is \autoref{ch:visions-computation-as-continuation}:
the worker is part of the continuation ledger.  Its output may be checked,
stored, and reused, but the technical inscription-event carrier is absent.

An automated worker participates in an inscription event when its output is
taken up by a conscious observational system as a named commitment in the
public carrier.  The event is then not "the machine has free will" as a
positive predicate.  The event is the transition in which the system that
owns the observation history accepts the generated candidate as the record to
inscribe, exposes the generator-closure audit, and records the
$\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger.  The automated worker may supply the candidate route;
the inscription event is the accepted public transition.

There is also a possible stronger case.  If a mechanized system itself
carries a closed observational history, a self-recognising carrier, and the
ability to issue public named commitments under a $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, then
the same criterion applies to it directly.  BEDC does not need a special
biological exception.  It asks for the rows: closed observational system,
inscription point, carrier, classifier, generator-closure audit, continuation
stability, and ledger.

The boundary criterion for AI is therefore public and structural.  A machine
step is computational output when it is exhausted by a prior generator.  It
is part of an inscription event when it enters a named, audited commitment
whose acceptance is not already a forced consequence of the prior records.
The question is not whether the surface text looks creative.  The question is
whether the transition carries the NameCert-shaped inscription rows.
