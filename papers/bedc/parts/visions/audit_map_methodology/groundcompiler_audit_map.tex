\section{The setup}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-setup}

\texttt{BEDC.GroundCompiler} is the main Lean formalisation of the BEDC
self-compilation path. It is parallel to \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} in project
role, but not in subject matter. \texttt{BEDC.MetaCIC} formalises a closed CIC
substrate: terms, reduction, typing-adjacent interfaces, and the consistency
critical path. \texttt{BEDC.GroundCompiler} formalises the self-compile
surface: event flows, source channels, generated recognizers, certificate
gates, address analysis, cannot-claim ledgers, and streaming compiler
discipline.

The audit question is therefore different from the MetaCIC audit maps in
\autoref{ch:audit-map-family-methodology}. The local issue is not whether CIC
reduction has a global confluence or preservation theorem. The local issue is
whether a compiler-facing artifact is allowed to count as a BEDC input,
whether a displayed code channel is lossless at the event-flow layer, whether
generated recognizers and certificate gates mediate exports, and whether
reports or motifs can silently replace proof or certificate evidence.

This chapter records the GroundCompiler module as an audit map. It is an
inventory site. It cites checked Lean targets as variants, separates Prop
statement cells from their proof theorems, names the open cells, and states
the boundary of the self-compilation claim.

\section{Core statement defs}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-core-statement-defs}

\texttt{MainTheorems.lean} exposes three top-level Prop statement cells.

\begin{itemize}
\item \leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.GlobalConservativityStatement}
records event-flow conservativity, legal encoding of flow images, exclusion of
structural hidden inputs, metric conservativity, and the non-input status of
host package, name-certificate, derivation-certificate, and closure-certificate
data. It is paired with
\leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.global\_conservativity}.
\item \leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.CompilerLayerAddressAnalysisStatement}
records channel round-trip, legal-stream completeness, code-not-proof,
metric-not-certificate, and global conservativity content. It is paired with
\leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.compiler\_layer\_address\_analysis\_layer}.
\item \leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.NoHiddenInputStreamingCompilerStatement}
records the streaming compiler surface: formal event-flow inputs, legal
encoding and decoding, hidden host-structure exclusion, recognizer
generatedness, certificate-mediated export, theorem-code and chapter-code
bijections, classifier quotient limits, source-channel separation, metric
conservativity, self-hosting removal of hidden compiler input, visible
bootstrap boundary, and global conservativity. It is paired with
\leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.no\_hidden\_input\_streaming\_compiler}.
\end{itemize}

The important audit distinction is that these are not statement-only names
covered by a vacuous theorem. Each Prop cell above has a companion theorem
whose conclusion is the cell itself. The proof terms compose existing
GroundCompiler lemmas and definitions. Thus the statement defs act as named
aggregate interfaces, while the theorem names are the checked aggregate
witnesses.

Within the inspected top-level file, there is no unpaired
\texttt{*Statement} Prop cell of the kind that would have to be cited only as a
paper-side target. GroundCompiler still has many lower-layer definitions and
interfaces, but the three named aggregate statements above are theorem-paired
inside \texttt{MainTheorems.lean}.

\section{Relation to MetaCIC}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-relation-to-metacic}

GroundCompiler and MetaCIC cooperate, but their audit maps must remain
separate. GroundCompiler executes the BEDC self-compile route from source-like
event flows to generated recognizers, code ledgers, certificate gates, and
cannot-claim rows. MetaCIC formalises the closed CIC substrate in which
reduction, normalisation, confluence, and consistency boundaries are studied.

The relation is therefore complementary. GroundCompiler can say that a
compiler-facing artifact is generated, encoded, decoded, ledgered, or excluded
as a hidden input. MetaCIC can say that a closed term, reduction path, or
candidate-mediated normalisation route has a particular meta-theoretic status.
Neither side replaces the other. A GroundCompiler theorem about code not being
proof is not a MetaCIC subject-reduction theorem. A MetaCIC confluence bridge
is not a GroundCompiler certificate gate.

This separation is the same discipline used by the audit-map family: each map
exports its own local content and names its own frontier. Cross-reference is
allowed; substitution of one module's theorem for another module's missing
edge is not.

\section{Open obligations and conditional statements}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-open-obligations}

The visible open cells are compiler-layer cells, not closed-CIC proof cells.
They include the distinction between self-hosting as a supplied compiler-flow
condition and full removal of hidden compiler input; the distinction between a
metric or motif report and certificate evidence; the distinction between a
theorem code channel and a proof; and the distinction between a classifier
quotient and raw event-flow identity.

The theorem
\leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.self\_hosting\_removes\_hidden\_compiler}
is conditional on a self-hosted compiler flow and hierarchy coverage. It is a
valid route from those inputs to the no-hidden-compiler conclusion, but it does
not assert that every candidate compiler already satisfies those inputs.
Similarly,
\leanvariant{BEDC.GroundCompiler.MainTheorems.cannot\_claim\_registry\_mandatory}
requires registry adequacy before the listed cannot-claim ledger rows are
available.

Those conditions are useful precisely because they are explicit. They prevent
the paper from reading reports as proof, motifs as mature objects, code as
acceptance, source substrings as decoded events, or self-hosting prose as a
discharged compiler tower.

\section{Apophatic framing}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-apophatic-framing}

The open cell for GroundCompiler is the \emph{compiler-layer evidence gap}.
The module can check that event-flow code is legal and lossless, that hidden
host structures are excluded from formal input, that generated recognizers
mediate recognition evidence, and that certificate gates mediate accepted
exports. It also says what those facts do not license.

That is the apophatic role of this audit map. The chapter gives names to the
non-claims: code is not proof, report is not evidence, motif similarity is not
object identity, classifier quotient is not raw equality, and conditional
self-hosting is not an unconditional compiler tower. These names prevent the
self-compilation path from acquiring silent extra power through prose.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:bedc-groundcompiler-audit-map-boundary}

This chapter does not solve the remaining GroundCompiler obligations. It does
not prove that every compiler candidate is self-hosted. It does not turn metric
reports, motif reports, theorem code, or chapter code into proof or
certificate evidence. It does not collapse classifier equality into raw
event-flow equality. It does not replace any MetaCIC audit map.

The allowed reading is narrower and stronger: GroundCompiler supplies a
checked compiler-layer audit surface for BEDC self-compilation, with explicit
theorem witnesses for conservativity, address analysis, and no-hidden-input
streaming compilation. Everything beyond that surface remains named at the
boundary.
