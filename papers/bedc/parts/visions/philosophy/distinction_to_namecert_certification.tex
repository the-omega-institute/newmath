\section{Distinction-to-NameCert Certification}
\label{sec:visions-philosophy-distinction-to-namecert-certification}
\origin{ai}

The distinction-history and classifier-shift layers identify the route
from generated marks to certified classifier states. This section names
the mediating certification layer. BEDC does not treat a stable
distinction, a feature label, a proof trace, or a model behavior as an
auditable object merely because it is visible. The object is licensed
only when the candidate classifier is carried by a NameCert surface with
source, pattern, classifier, stability, and ledger rows.

\begin{definition}[Generated material]
\label{def:philosophy-generated-material}
Generated material is any finite record produced by the mark-history,
event-flow, proof-trace, tensor-trace, or activation-trace interfaces.
It is material for certification, not a certified object by itself.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Candidate classifier]
\label{def:philosophy-candidate-classifier}
A candidate classifier on a generated-material family $G$ is a displayed
relation $C$ on $G$ that proposes which records are to be read as the
same for the purpose at hand. It may arise from history sameness,
continuation behavior, compression cells, tensor activation patterns,
neural features, empirical clusters, proof traces, or certificate
structure.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Certified classifier object]
\label{def:philosophy-certified-classifier-object}
A certified classifier object is a pair $(C,N)$ where $C$ is a candidate
classifier and $N$ is a NameCert for $C$. The certificate has the
five-field form
$$
N=
(S_C,P_C,C,\Sigma_C,L_C),
$$
where $S_C$ is the source specification, $P_C$ is the pattern
specification, $\Sigma_C$ is the stability certificate, and $L_C$ is the
ledger policy.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Candidate classifiers are not certified objects]
\label{thm:philosophy-candidate-classifier-not-certified-object}
A candidate classifier without a NameCert is not a certified classifier
object.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By \autoref{def:philosophy-certified-classifier-object}, a certified
classifier object is a pair $(C,N)$ with a NameCert component $N$.
A bare candidate classifier supplies only the relation $C$. It does not
supply source, pattern, stability, or ledger rows, so it does not satisfy
the defining form of a certified classifier object.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Stable behavior is not objecthood]
\label{cor:philosophy-stable-behavior-not-objecthood}
Stable behavior in histories, tensor traces, activations, or proof
records is not by itself a BEDC object.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Stable behavior may support a candidate classifier, but
\autoref{thm:philosophy-candidate-classifier-not-certified-object}
requires a NameCert before that classifier has certified object status.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[NameCert licenses classifier objecthood]
\label{thm:philosophy-namecert-licenses-classifier-objecthood}
If a candidate classifier $C$ carries a NameCert $N$, then $(C,N)$ is a
certified classifier object.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The conclusion is exactly the construction named in
\autoref{def:philosophy-certified-classifier-object}. The NameCert
provides the rows that make the classifier auditable: source, pattern,
classifier, stability, and ledger.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[No ledger, no closed certificate]
\label{thm:philosophy-no-ledger-no-closed-certificate}
A certificate surface without a ledger policy is not a closed NameCert.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The ledger policy is one of the five required rows in
\autoref{def:philosophy-certified-classifier-object}. Without it, hidden
material, projection residue, compression residue, failure cases, and
not-claimed boundaries have no audit row. The certificate surface is
therefore incomplete.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Single-field claims are not NameCerts]
\label{cor:philosophy-single-field-claims-not-namecerts}
Source-only, pattern-only, classifier-only, stability-only, and
ledger-only claims are not NameCerts.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Each such claim omits at least one required field from the five-field
certificate surface. A source row without a classifier does not say what
relation is licensed; a pattern row without a source does not say where
it applies; a classifier row without stability does not say how it is
transported; a stability row without ledger does not expose what it hides;
and a ledger row without classifier does not identify the relation being
audited.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Semantic Assignment}
\label{subsec:philosophy-semantic-assignment}

\begin{definition}[Semantic assignment]
\label{def:philosophy-semantic-assignment}
A semantic assignment maps classifier cells or public marks to an
external meaning space, such as truth values, numbers, concept labels,
model behaviors, or mathematical objects.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Certified semantic assignment]
\label{def:philosophy-certified-semantic-assignment}
A semantic assignment is certified when the assignment itself carries a
NameCert specifying its source, pattern, classifier, stability, and
ledger rows.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Semantic readings require certification]
\label{thm:philosophy-semantic-readings-require-certification}
A semantic reading of a mark, history, tensor feature, or classifier cell
is not licensed by the reading alone.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The reading supplies an external label or interpretation. It does not
thereby expose the source on which the reading acts, the pattern that
supports it, the classifier relation it respects, the stability witness
under transport or perturbation, or the ledger of hidden and excluded
material. Those rows are supplied only by a certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Feature labels are not explanations]
\label{cor:philosophy-feature-labels-not-explanations}
Calling a neural feature by a concept label is not yet a certified
explanation.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
The label is a semantic assignment. By
\autoref{thm:philosophy-semantic-readings-require-certification}, the
assignment must be certified before it can serve as an auditable
explanation.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Meaning is certificate-derived]
\label{thm:philosophy-meaning-certificate-derived}
Within the BEDC certificate reading, meaning is derived from certified
semantic assignment rather than present as a primitive mark property.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The primitive display contains marks, histories, generated material, and
candidate distinctions. External meaning enters through semantic
assignment. Since a semantic assignment is licensed only through
\autoref{def:philosophy-certified-semantic-assignment}, certified meaning
is a derived certificate object.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Hardening}
\label{subsec:philosophy-certification-hardening}

\begin{definition}[Certificate hardening]
\label{def:philosophy-certificate-hardening}
Certificate hardening is the passage in which one or more certificate
rows are supported by a stronger evidence form, such as a formal proof
object accepted by a verification backend, while the classifier surface
being certified remains identified.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Hardening does not create the NameCert layer]
\label{thm:philosophy-hardening-does-not-create-namecert-layer}
Formal hardening can strengthen certificate rows, but it does not by
itself create a complete NameCert.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
A verifier accepts a proof object for a translated formal statement. That
acceptance may harden a source, classifier, stability, or boundary claim.
It does not automatically supply the remaining NameCert rows. The
certificate layer therefore remains distinct from the verification layer.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Verified components are not whole explanations]
\label{cor:philosophy-verified-components-not-whole-explanations}
A verified statement inside an explanation is not the same as a certified
explanation.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
By \autoref{thm:philosophy-hardening-does-not-create-namecert-layer},
verification hardens the component it checks. A certified explanation
still requires the complete source, pattern, classifier, stability, and
ledger surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Certificate hardening is not classifier shift]
\label{thm:philosophy-certificate-hardening-not-classifier-shift}
Certificate hardening does not by itself constitute classifier shift.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Hardening changes the evidence status of one or more rows. If the
classifier relation on the common source remains the same, the public
classifier surface has not shifted. This is the certificate-layer form of
\autoref{thm:philosophy-formal-hardening-not-classifier-shift}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Certification-layer theorem]
\label{thm:philosophy-certification-layer-theorem}
BEDC forms a certification layer between generated behavior and optional
formal hardening: candidate distinctions are organized as candidate
classifiers, NameCert rows license them as certified classifier objects,
and verification hardens only the translated rows it actually checks.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Candidate distinctions and generated behavior supply material for
classifiers by \autoref{def:philosophy-candidate-classifier}. Certified
classifier objecthood requires the NameCert pair of
\autoref{def:philosophy-certified-classifier-object}. Formal hardening is
separate by \autoref{thm:philosophy-hardening-does-not-create-namecert-layer}.
Thus the certification layer is neither raw behavior nor the formal
verification backend; it is the auditable NameCert surface between them.
\end{proof}
