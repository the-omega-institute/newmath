\section{The setup}
\label{sec:metacic-beta-audit-map-setup}

BEDC.MetaCIC's beta layer is the driving reduction relation used by the
subject-reduction, confluence, normalisation, evaluation, and closed-consistency
interfaces. Its public surface is deliberately small. The single-step relation
is declared in \texttt{Beta.lean}, while conversion by common beta reduct is
declared in \texttt{Beta/Conversion.lean}.

The two files should be read together. \texttt{Beta.lean} gives the raw
generator: a one-step relation $\mathrm{BetaStep}$ over MetaCIC terms, with
one direct beta-redex constructor and congruence constructors for applications,
lambdas, and Pi types. \texttt{Beta/Conversion.lean} builds the conversion
interface $\mathrm{BetaConv}$ from the multi-step relation
$\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$ and records the checked conversion lemmas that later
modules may cite.

This chapter is an audit map, not another proof layer. It records which beta
objects are constructors, which conversion facts are checked, and where the
relation meets the subject-reduction obstruction isolated in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}.

\section{Core inductive definition}
\label{sec:metacic-beta-audit-map-core-inductive}

\subsection{BetaStep}
\label{subsec:metacic-beta-audit-map-betastep}

The core declaration is the inductive relation
\leandef{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaStep}. It has seven constructors.

\begin{itemize}
\item \texttt{beta} contracts an application whose function is a lambda:
  $\mathrm{app}(\mathrm{lam}\ dom\ body)\ arg$ steps to
  $\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ arg\ body$.
\item \texttt{congApp1} reduces the function position of an application while
  leaving the argument fixed.
\item \texttt{congApp2} reduces the argument position of an application while
  leaving the function fixed.
\item \texttt{congLam} reduces the body of a lambda while leaving the displayed
  domain fixed.
\item \texttt{congPiCod} reduces the codomain of a Pi type while leaving the
  displayed domain fixed.
\item \texttt{congPiDom} reduces the displayed domain of a Pi type while
  leaving the codomain syntactically fixed.
\item \texttt{congLamDom} reduces the displayed domain of a lambda while
  leaving the body syntactically fixed.
\end{itemize}

The constructor list is intentionally syntax-directed. A beta step is not a
typing judgement, not a semantic equivalence, and not a closure certificate. It
only says that the represented term syntax admits one local reduction at a
displayed site.

\subsection{File-level reading}
\label{subsec:metacic-beta-audit-map-file-reading}

\texttt{Beta.lean} is therefore the root reduction file, not a theorem bundle.
Its exported object is the closed inductive grammar of one-step beta movement.
Every later theorem that mentions beta reduction receives one of these
constructors, or a finite chain generated from them, as its reduction witness.

\texttt{Beta/Conversion.lean} is the first theorem bundle over that grammar. It
does not add a new reduction rule. It changes the reading mode from one-step
movement to common-reduct comparability, and then proves the small algebra of
that comparison relation that is already available in the substrate.

Two features matter for later audits. First, beta contraction uses the concrete
MetaCIC substitution operation, so all preservation claims that read across the
redex must account for substitution through binders. Second, the domain
congruence constructors for lambda and Pi terms change displayed binder
annotations without reducing the body or codomain at the same time. These are
valid reduction steps, but their typing behaviour requires separate evidence.

\section{Established results}
\label{sec:metacic-beta-audit-map-established-results}

The conversion file exposes $\mathrm{BetaConv}$ as common-reduct conversion:
there is a term $w$ such that both sides reduce to $w$ by
$\mathrm{BetaStarStep}$.
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv}

The elementary conversion facts are checked directly:
\begin{itemize}
\item reflexivity and symmetry:
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.refl}
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.symm};
\item conversion from a multi-step beta reduction on either side:
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.of\_betaStar\_left}
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.of\_betaStar\_right};
\item conversion from a one-step beta reduction on either side:
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.of\_betaStep\_left}
  \leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.of\_betaStep\_right}.
\end{itemize}

The file also records the conditional transitivity shape. Full transitivity is
obtained from an explicit confluence input, while a normal middle term gives a
smaller unconditional route:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.trans\_of\_confluence}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.trans\_normal\_middle}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv.trans\_closed\_normal\_middle}

Closed normal forms are rigid under beta conversion when both sides are closed
and beta-strong:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv\_normal\_forms\_equal\_closed}

The same conversion file also names a typing-side boundary. The closed
beta-conversion typing invariance statement is represented as a proposition,
and the file proves a concrete closed lambda-domain obstruction against it:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.HasTypeBetaConvInvariantClosedStatement}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.closedLamDomainTarget\_closed}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.BetaConv\_closed\_lam\_domain\_type\_obstruction}
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.not\_HasType\_BetaConv\_invariant\_closed}

These facts are conversion facts and obstruction facts. They are not a blanket
permission to replace displayed types by beta-convertible types inside typing
derivations. Any citation must preserve the exact input shape: common reduct,
normal middle term, closed beta-strong normal forms, or the displayed negative
typing boundary.

\section{Connection to the SR obstruction}
\label{sec:metacic-beta-audit-map-sr-connection}

\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction} names four discharge
obligations for the parameterised subject-reduction theorem. The beta module is
where the reduction triggers behind those obligations are born.

The direct \texttt{beta} constructor is the trigger for
$\mathrm{BetaSubstitutionPreservation}$. A term
$\mathrm{app}(\mathrm{lam}\ dom\ body)\ arg$ can reduce unconditionally to
$\mathrm{substitute}\ 0\ arg\ body$. To turn that syntactic step into typing
preservation, the substrate must show that substituting the argument into the
body also transports the displayed codomain in the expected way. That is a
typing statement about substitution, not a consequence of the beta constructor
alone.

The \texttt{congApp2} constructor is the trigger for the dependent-codomain
application obstruction. It reduces the argument while leaving the function and
the originally displayed application type fixed. In an independent codomain
regime this can be harmless, and the subject-reduction map records the checked
independent-codomain fragment. In a dependent codomain regime the codomain can
observe the argument, so reducing the argument may change the readback type.
That is the reason $\mathrm{AppArgTypeStable}$ is a separate obligation.

The \texttt{congLamDom} and \texttt{congPiDom} constructors are the binder-domain
triggers. They reduce displayed binder annotations. A lambda or Pi term can
therefore take a syntactic beta step even when the typing derivation for the
old annotation does not transfer to the new annotation at the same displayed
external type. These are the sources of
$\mathrm{LamDomainSubjectReduction}$ and
$\mathrm{PiDomainSubjectReduction}$.

Thus the beta relation itself is unconditional, but subject reduction under that
relation is not unconditional in the present MetaCIC interface. The distinction
is structural: $\mathrm{BetaStep}$ authorizes one syntax step, while subject
reduction must also preserve typing data across the dependent codomain and
binder interfaces touched by that step.

\section{Apophatic framing and boundary}
\label{sec:metacic-beta-audit-map-boundary}

The beta audit map gives BEDC a precise negative reading discipline. The paper
may cite \texttt{BetaStep} as the checked single-step reduction relation. It may
cite the conversion lemmas listed above. It may cite the closed normal-form
rigidity theorem and the checked obstruction to closed typing invariance under
beta conversion.

The paper may not infer a hidden conversion rule for typing, an unconditional
closed subject-reduction theorem, or a full closed consistency proof from the
existence of $\mathrm{BetaStep}$. A beta step is a syntactic generator. Its
typing consequences require the exact preservation interfaces named in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction}.

This is the apophatic role of the module boundary. The beta layer says exactly
what can reduce. The conversion layer says exactly which common-reduct facts are
available. The subject-reduction map says exactly which typing transports are
not supplied by those reduction facts alone. Keeping the three readings
separate prevents the manuscript from treating reduction, conversion, and typed
preservation as one unrecorded operation.
