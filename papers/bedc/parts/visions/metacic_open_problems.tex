\chapter{Closed-MetaCIC open problems: consolidated obligation list}
\label{ch:metacic-open-problems}

\section{The setup}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-setup}

The MetaCIC audit maps deliberately separate positive checked content from the
places where the closed-term consistency route is still blocked. The subject
reduction map, confluence map, normalisation map, substitution map,
decidability map, typed-example map, and consistency assembly record those
boundaries in their own local vocabularies:
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction},
\autoref{ch:metacic-confluence-audit-map},
\autoref{ch:metacic-normalization-audit-map},
\autoref{ch:metacic-substitution-audit-map},
\autoref{ch:metacic-decidable-audit-map},
\autoref{ch:metacic-typedexamples-audit-map}, and
\autoref{ch:closed-metacic-consistency-assembly}.

This chapter is the companion inventory. The synthesis view gives the reader
the critical path; the present chapter gives the concrete list of outstanding
obligations, obstructions, partial-closure boundaries, and frontier questions.
It does not introduce a new proof principle, a new Lean target, or a stronger
closure claim. It is a single reading surface for the work that remains open
after the existing audit chapters are read together.

The consolidated list has four layers. First, the subject-reduction route has
four named discharge hypotheses and a checked obstruction to replacing them by
an unconditional closed subject-reduction theorem. Second, confluence and
substitution have algebraic boundaries around residual substitution and
unrestricted commutation. Third, normalisation and decidability have
candidate-mediated or bounded-computation interfaces that should not be erased
by prose. Fourth, the worked examples are gallery-level witnesses, not a
coverage theorem over all dependent or polymorphic programs.

\section{Subject reduction obligations (4)}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-subject-reduction}

The subject-reduction frontier is the sharpest obstruction in the closed
MetaCIC path. The parameterised theorem exists, but it consumes four discharge
hypotheses. The checked obstruction in
\autoref{ch:metacic-subject-reduction-obstruction} prevents the paper from
replacing that four-input interface by a blanket closed subject-reduction
claim for the present substrate.

\begin{enumerate}
\item $\mathrm{BetaSubstitutionPreservation}$. The beta-redex case must show
  that substituting a typed argument into a typed body preserves the substituted
  codomain. The obstruction blocks an unconditional proof because the dependent
  codomain can observe the argument strongly enough for the displayed target
  type to change after reduction.
\item $\mathrm{AppArgTypeStable}$. The application-argument congruence case
  must show that replacing an argument by its reduct leaves the codomain
  readback stable. The independent-codomain case is checked, but the dependent
  codomain case is exactly where the counterexample lives.
\item $\mathrm{LamDomainSubjectReduction}$. The lambda-domain congruence case
  must preserve the type of a lambda when its displayed domain annotation
  reduces. This is not a direct consequence of syntactic beta stepping, because
  the binder annotation participates in the typing judgement being preserved.
\item $\mathrm{PiDomainSubjectReduction}$. The Pi-domain congruence case must
  preserve the type of a Pi term when the displayed domain reduces. This is
  separate from the lambda case because the Pi codomain is checked under the
  binder induced by the domain, so domain reduction changes the context-facing
  data.
\end{enumerate}

All four obligations are simultaneous. Supplying only the beta redex row, only
argument stability, or only binder-domain preservation leaves the parameterised
subject-reduction theorem unavailable as an unconditional closed theorem. The
first-class bundle records exactly the simultaneous discharge interface:
\leanvariant{BEDC.MetaCIC.SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}.

\section{Confluence remaining questions}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-confluence}

The confluence map in \autoref{ch:metacic-confluence-audit-map} has a different
shape from the subject-reduction map. It is not blocked by dependent typing.
It is blocked at the residual-substitution algebra needed for the full
parallel diamond and then for arbitrary closed star confluence.

The unconditional confluence content is shape-limited. Closed normal sources,
sort sources, closed variable impossibility, atom joins, non-lambda application
shape facts, componentwise diamonds, and parallel-to-star transport are
available at the strengths stated in the confluence audit map. Those results
are real checked infrastructure and may be cited at that scope.

The remaining confluence questions are the stronger endpoints. Does the present
substrate support the needed
$\mathrm{BetaParallelSubstitutionCompatibility}$ interface without importing
unclosed substitution assumptions? Can the closed parallel diamond be supplied
for arbitrary closed terms rather than consumed as a premise? Can the resulting
closed multi-step Church--Rosser theorem be stated without hiding the
substitution boundary? Until those inputs exist, arbitrary closed-term
confluence remains conditional rather than unconditional.

\section{Normalization remaining questions}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-normalization}

The normalisation map in \autoref{ch:metacic-normalization-audit-map} is
candidate-mediated. Candidate evidence, closed-candidate evidence, and
candidate-set membership imply strong normalisation; the application and Pi
adequacy layers explain how that evidence behaves over the displayed
fragments.

The remaining question is not whether the candidate projections are checked.
They are checked. The question is whether every empty-context typed closed
MetaCIC term can be placed into the relevant candidate interface without adding
an unrecorded semantic assumption. The present normalisation layer does not
export a blanket theorem saying that all closed typed terms are strongly
normalising independent of candidate evidence.

This boundary matters for the consistency route. Closed-normal consistency can
use beta-strong normality as an input, and candidate evidence can supply such
normality for terms inside the candidate interface. Full closed consistency
would still require a bridge from arbitrary closed proofs to a typed normal
endpoint with the displayed false type preserved, which returns the problem to
the subject-reduction frontier.

\section{Decidability remaining questions}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-decidability}

The decidability map in \autoref{ch:metacic-decidable-audit-map} has a positive
typing layer and a narrower conversion layer. Structural term equality,
same-term checking, and option-valued type inference are sound and complete for
the represented relations at the interface stated by the checker theorems.

The remaining decidability questions sit around closedness and beta
conversion. The submodule does not export a theorem named
\texttt{Decidable (ClosedAt n t)}. It also does not export an unrestricted
closed-term beta-conversion decider. The bounded normal-equality checker is
sound for equality of bounded normalizer outputs and for finished closed normal
forms when the finished-normalisation witnesses are supplied.

Thus the decidability frontier is the endpoint bridge: how should bounded
normal computation be connected to finished normal forms for the class of
closed typed terms under discussion? A global conversion decider would require
that bridge explicitly. Gallery success and checker completeness for typing do
not supply it by themselves.

\section{TypedExamples coverage questions}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-typedexamples}

The typed-examples map in \autoref{ch:metacic-typedexamples-audit-map} supplies
a worked gallery: Church booleans, naturals, pairs, options, lists, sums,
Leibniz equality, dependent identity, polymorphic identity, applications,
beta-redexes, constants, Pi forms, projections, and sort-indexed context
examples. These examples are Lean-side witnesses for concrete represented
terms.

The open question is coverage, not existence. The gallery demonstrates many
typical dependent, polymorphic, Church-encoded, and application-shaped terms,
but it does not claim exhaustive coverage of all dependent-program forms or all
expected algebraic laws of every Church encoding inside MetaCIC. The examples
therefore remain a regression and audit surface rather than a general
metatheorem.

The practical frontier is to decide which example families should be treated
as representative enough for the paper's public narrative. Dependent
codomains, polymorphic instantiation, eliminator computation, nested binder
movement, and conversion-sensitive examples are the highest-value families
because they touch the same boundaries as subject reduction, normalisation, and
decidable conversion.

\section{Cross-cutting frontier questions}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-cross-cutting}

\subsection{Dependent-codomain barrier}
\label{subsec:metacic-open-problems-dependent-codomain}

Subject reduction is not unconditionally available in the dependent-codomain
case. The independent-codomain application case is checked, while the dependent
case has a counterexample in the present substrate. This is the obstruction
that prevents the closed-normal consistency result from automatically becoming
full closed consistency.

There are three coherent routes through the barrier. One can change the
substrate so that a context-reduction lemma is available. One can weaken the
subject-reduction statement so that it accepts the typing reorganisation
forced by dependent codomains. One can also expose dependent-codomain inversion
as a setup field or axiom-set-shaped interface rather than as an internal
theorem. Each route changes the public meaning of the substrate, and none is
taken here.

\subsection{Bundle discharge route}
\label{subsec:metacic-open-problems-bundle}

The discharge-bundle route is conceptually clean. A value of
$\mathrm{SubjectReductionDischargeBundle}$ packages the four subject-reduction
inputs, and the corresponding wrapper lifts the parameterised theorem to an
unconditional theorem relative to that bundle. This makes the discharge
interface first-class without spreading the four hypotheses through every
downstream citation.

The same route also states the obstruction sharply. On the present closed-term
substrate, inhabiting the bundle at the required strength is incompatible with
the documented obstruction. The bundle is therefore a marker for a
paper-side discharge path or for a substrate extension, not evidence that the
closed-term substrate already contains the missing proof.

\subsection{Normal-form-only consistency}
\label{subsec:metacic-open-problems-normal-form-consistency}

\autoref{ch:closed-metacic-consistency-assembly} establishes the positive
closed-normal node. A closed proof term that is already beta-strong cannot
inhabit the internal false encoding. This result is unconditional at its
stated input shape and does not depend on discharging subject reduction.

The open judgement is presentational and strategic. The paper can treat
normal-form consistency as a first-class BEDC deliverable, because it is a real
closed theorem at the normal-form boundary. Doing so would avoid overstating
full closed consistency while still making the checked positive result visible
as a central endpoint.

\subsection{Substrate extension for dependent-codomain closure}
\label{subsec:metacic-open-problems-substrate-extension}

The strongest design decision is whether to extend the closed-CIC substrate so
that dependent-codomain reduction can be closed internally. Such an extension
could make the conventional subject-reduction route possible, but it would also
change the substrate interface and the audit obligations that downstream
chapters must respect.

The conservative alternative is to keep the present substrate and report the
closed-normal theorem as the main internal result. The expansive alternative is
to add the exact context-reduction or setup-class field needed by the
dependent-codomain cases, then re-audit subject reduction, confluence,
normalisation, decidability, and examples against that enlarged interface.

\section{Operational priorities}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-operational-priorities}

The apophatic naming discipline of
\autoref{ch:visions-apophatic-naming-as-discipline} is binding here. The paper
should not pretend that the obligations above are solved. They are named open
problems with exact interfaces.

The reader-facing priority order is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Treat closed-normal consistency as a first-class deliverable. It is the
  strongest presently assembled consistency node and it does not require
  subject-reduction discharge.
\item Explore a setup-class field route for dependent-codomain inversion. This
  keeps the additional input explicit and avoids converting the obstruction
  into an untracked theorem claim.
\item Keep the subject-reduction obstruction documented. It should not be
  hidden behind a proof placeholder, an informal appeal to CIC intuition, or an
  overbroad citation of the parameterised theorem.
\end{enumerate}

This priority order also constrains citations. The paper may cite the checked
normal-form result, the parameterised subject-reduction theorem, the
shape-limited confluence fragments, the candidate-mediated normalisation
projections, the typing checker, the bounded normal-equality facts, and the
typed-example gallery. It may not cite any of those as full closed consistency,
arbitrary closed subject reduction, arbitrary closed Church--Rosser, global
closed-term strong normalisation, or unrestricted beta-conversion
decidability.

\section{Boundary}
\label{sec:metacic-open-problems-boundary}

This chapter is an inventory, not a discharge. It consolidates the open
MetaCIC work exposed by the existing audit maps and the consistency assembly.
It does not prove any of the four subject-reduction obligations. It does not
prove residual substitution compatibility for full confluence. It does not
erase candidate evidence from normalisation, finished-normalisation evidence
from bounded conversion, or gallery scope from typed examples.

The boundary is therefore simple: the chapter names the remaining problems and
the available design routes, while leaving the proof state exactly where the
Lean and paper audit maps place it.

\begin{proposition}[Lambda-domain setup row is mandatory]\label{prop:metacic-lam-domain-setup-row-mandatory}
Consider a MetaCIC subject-reduction discharge route at either public interface of \autoref{ch:sr-discharge-routes}: the explicit bundle route $\SubjectReductionDischargeBundle$ or the typeclass route $\SubjectReductionSetup$. If that route is used to make the parameterised subject-reduction theorem available for the represented reduction relation, then its finite discharge packet contains a distinct row $LD$ satisfying $\mathrm{LamDomainSubjectReduction}$. This row is the lambda-domain congruence obligation of \autoref{def:metacic-lam-domain-subject-reduction}; it cannot be supplied by the beta-substitution row, the application-argument row, the Pi-domain row, transport, continuation replay, package provenance, or local naming.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The four-obligation audit of \autoref{sec:metacic-subject-reduction-four-obligations} separates the reduction shapes consumed by the parameterised subject-reduction theorem. The lambda-domain shape is named by \autoref{def:metacic-lam-domain-subject-reduction}: if $\lambda d.\,b$ has type $A$ in $\Gamma$ and $d$ steps to $d'$, the target lambda $\lambda d'.\,b$ must still have type $A$ in the same context. This is a binder-annotation preservation requirement, not a beta-redex substitution requirement and not an application-codomain stability requirement.

Read the explicit bundle route from \autoref{subsec:sr-discharge-routes-bundle-definition}. The bundle has four discharge fields, and one of them is the lambda-domain field. Therefore any use of the bundle route carries the lambda-domain row as a named component of the finite packet.

Read the typeclass route from \autoref{subsec:sr-discharge-routes-typeclass-definition} and its conversion in \autoref{subsec:sr-discharge-routes-conversion-to-bundle}. A $\SubjectReductionSetup$ instance has the same four fields, including the lambda-domain field, and $\mathsf{SubjectReductionSetup.toBundle}$ copies those fields into the bundle shape. Thus the setup route does not remove the lambda-domain obligation; it exposes the same row through instance fields and then through the bundle projection.

The finite ledger form gives the same conclusion at the BEDC packet surface. In \autoref{def:subject-reduction-discharge-ledger-carrier} an accepted $\SubjectReductionDischargeLedgerUp$ carrier is displayed as
$$
L=(B,A,LD,PD,R,H,C,P,N),
$$
where $LD$ is the lambda-domain subject-reduction row and $R$ is the route that consumes exactly $B,A,LD,PD$. The handoff theorem \autoref{thm:subject-reduction-discharge-ledger-subjectreduction-handoff} confines subject-reduction consumption to those four obligation rows through $R$, and the non-escape theorem \autoref{thm:subject-reduction-discharge-ledger-nonescape} confines downstream reads to the displayed packet. Hence $H,C,P,N$ can transport, replay, authenticate, and name the route, but none of them can create or replace the missing lambda-domain preservation row.

Consequently, whenever the MetaCIC subject-reduction discharge interface is used, $\mathrm{LamDomainSubjectReduction}$ is a required finite setup obligation row. Supplying the other rows without $LD$ leaves the discharge interface incomplete for the lambda-domain congruence case.
\end{proof}
