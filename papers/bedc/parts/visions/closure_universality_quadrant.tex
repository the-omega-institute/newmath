\autoref{prin:cellular-automaton-universality-vs-closure} of \autoref{ch:visions-cellular-automaton-substrate-reading} established that two facts about Rule~110 are logically independent: it is Turing-universal in the sense of Cook~2004, and it is closed observational in the sense of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system}. This chapter takes that independence seriously and reads it as an organising principle. The two axes -- universality and closure -- generate a $2 \times 2$ quadrant of substrate types. The project currently exhibits one quadrant in concrete detail (universal-and-closed, the Rule~110 reading); the other three quadrants are populated by recognisable substrate classes, each of which sharpens the structural claim about Rule~110 by contrast. The chapter populates the quadrant, locates known substrates within it, and identifies what each quadrant says about the closed-observational framework.

\section{The two axes, formally}
\label{sec:quadrant-axes}

\begin{definition}[Universality axis]
\label{def:quadrant-universality-axis}
A substrate $S$ is \emph{Turing-universal} if every partial recursive function on natural numbers is realised as a computation of $S$ on some encoded input. The universality axis assigns each substrate a value $\mathsf{Univ}(S) \in \{\text{yes}, \text{no}\}$. The Church-Turing thesis identifies $\mathsf{Univ}(S) = \text{yes}$ with the existence of a polynomial-time compilation from any Turing machine into $S$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Closure axis]
\label{def:quadrant-closure-axis}
A substrate $S$ is \emph{closed observational} if its observations are exhausted by finite-length records of orbits of $S$'s next-state function, with no external commitments participating in deciding which records count as observations (\autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system}). The closure axis assigns each substrate a value $\mathsf{Closed}(S) \in \{\text{yes}, \text{no}\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Axes are independent]
\label{prin:quadrant-axes-independent}
The universality axis and the closure axis are logically independent. There is no derivation from $\mathsf{Univ}(S)$ to $\mathsf{Closed}(S)$ or vice versa; each axis is fixed by separate structural features of $S$. \autoref{prin:cellular-automaton-universality-vs-closure} is the special case of this independence for Rule~110. The quadrant generated by the two axes has four cells, each of which is structurally meaningful.
\end{principle}

\section{Quadrant I: universal and closed}
\label{sec:quadrant-universal-closed}

\begin{example}[Rule~110 substrate]
\label{ex:quadrant-rule110-as-uc}
The cellular substrate of \autoref{ex:cellular-trust-substrate-rule110} inhabits the universal-and-closed quadrant. Cook~2004 proves $\mathsf{Univ}(\mathsf{R110}) = \text{yes}$; \autoref{thm:rule110-no-internal-induction} proves $\mathsf{Closed}(\mathsf{R110}) = \text{yes}$. The project's \texttt{rule110/} sub-system is the engineering exhibit of this quadrant at minimum 220-line scale.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Other CA at this quadrant]
\label{ex:quadrant-other-ca-at-uc}
Elementary cellular automaton Rule~30 and Rule~54 are conjectured (not yet proved) to be Turing-universal. Game of Life is provably universal (Conway, Berlekamp, Guy 1982). Each of these CA, when used as a closed observational substrate in the sense of \autoref{def:cellular-trust-substrate}, is a candidate inhabitant of the universal-and-closed quadrant. Rule~110 is the project's choice because it has the smallest mechanically-verified universality construction.
\end{example}

\begin{principle}[This quadrant is the project's anchor]
\label{prin:quadrant-uc-is-anchor}
The universal-and-closed quadrant is the substrate position from which the closed-observational framework derives its principal claim: even at maximum computational capacity in the substrate, internal observation cannot ratify universal closures over an infinite domain (\autoref{thm:transcendental-input-no-internal-induction}, transported to the cellular reading in \autoref{thm:rule110-no-internal-induction}). The project anchors at this quadrant because it is the strongest version of the closure argument: universality buys no leverage against closure.
\end{principle}

\section{Quadrant II: universal but not closed}
\label{sec:quadrant-universal-open}

\begin{definition}[Oracle-augmented substrate]
\label{def:quadrant-oracle-substrate}
An \emph{oracle-augmented substrate} is a Turing-universal substrate equipped with one or more oracles for problems strictly above the recursive level: a halting oracle, an arithmetic-hierarchy oracle, or a hypercomputation channel. The presence of an oracle violates the closure premise because the substrate's observations include records produced by consulting the oracle, and the oracle is not a substrate-internal process.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Hypercomputation models]
\label{ex:quadrant-hypercomputation-models}
Several mathematical models occupy the universal-but-not-closed quadrant: Turing's o-machines (with halting oracles), infinite-time Turing machines (Hamkins-Lewis), real-number machines (Blum-Shub-Smale), and accelerating Turing machines. Each of these models is at least Turing-universal; each violates closure because each admits a step that is not a finite local rule on a finite state.
\end{example}

\begin{principle}[Open universal substrates lose the no-induction theorem]
\label{prin:quadrant-open-universal-lose-no-induction}
The no-induction theorem of \autoref{thm:transcendental-input-no-internal-induction} fails for oracle-augmented substrates. Specifically, a halting oracle can ratify universal closures on Turing-decidable predicates by direct query; a $\Sigma^0_2$ oracle can ratify closures over $\Pi^0_1$ predicates. The forward-binding source $\mathsf{T}$ of \autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-input-and-induction} is therefore consulted via the oracle, rather than via the inscription point of \autoref{prin:transcendental-input-individual-is-inscription}. The project does not currently exhibit an oracle-augmented substrate; the universal-but-not-closed quadrant is sketched here as the structural complement of the project's anchor quadrant.
\end{principle}

\begin{remark}[Why this quadrant matters even if empty in practice]
\label{rem:quadrant-open-universal-matters}
A reader might argue that hypercomputation is physically unrealised and therefore the universal-but-not-closed quadrant is theoretically vacant. The project does not take a position on the physical realisability of oracles. The structural relevance of this quadrant is that it shows what the closure premise is doing work to exclude: closure rules out exactly the substrates whose observations would otherwise bypass the no-induction theorem. The premise is therefore not vacuous; it carries information.
\end{remark}

\section{Quadrant III: not universal but closed}
\label{sec:quadrant-subuniversal-closed}

\begin{definition}[Sub-universal closed substrate]
\label{def:quadrant-subuniversal-closed-substrate}
A substrate is \emph{sub-universal closed} if it is closed observational (\autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system}) and admits a decision procedure for its computational power that places it strictly below Turing universality. Examples include linear-bounded automata, pushdown automata, finite-state machines, and decidable arithmetic fragments such as Presburger arithmetic and the theory of real closed fields.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Presburger arithmetic as substrate]
\label{ex:quadrant-presburger-as-substrate}
The first-order theory of $(\mathbb{N}, 0, 1, +)$ is decidable (Presburger 1929). Used as a closed observational substrate, every Presburger statement has a decision procedure that terminates in a finite, computable number of steps; the substrate is therefore closed observational. It is not Turing-universal; multiplication is not definable in the theory. This is a clean inhabitant of the sub-universal-closed quadrant.
\end{example}

\begin{principle}[Closure plus sub-universality keeps no-induction]
\label{prin:quadrant-closure-plus-subuniversality-keeps-no-induction}
The no-induction theorem transports to the sub-universal-closed quadrant without modification. Even a decidable closed substrate cannot ratify universal closures over an infinite domain from inside; decidability of a fragment is not internal ratification of universal statements over that fragment. The universal-but-decidable-by-external-procedure case is consistent with the substrate's records being finite. The closure argument is therefore stronger in this quadrant: even when an external decision procedure exists, the substrate's own records cannot internally produce $\forall n \in \mathbb{N}.\, P(n)$ without external supply.
\end{principle}

\begin{remark}[BEDC kernel at this quadrant on the host side]
\label{rem:quadrant-bedc-kernel-as-subuniversal-closed}
On the host side, BEDC itself is a sub-universal-closed substrate: total functional, so not Turing-universal as a programming language (\autoref{rem:strong-normalization-cannot-express}); closed observational, in that every kernel-level observation is a finite typed term. The host therefore sits in this quadrant; the cellular substrate sits in the universal-and-closed quadrant. The project displays both quadrants simultaneously as host plus substrate, with the closure premise transferred uniformly across both.
\end{remark}

\section{Quadrant IV: neither universal nor closed}
\label{sec:quadrant-degenerate}

\begin{definition}[Degenerate quadrant substrate]
\label{def:quadrant-degenerate-substrate}
A substrate in the neither-universal-nor-closed quadrant is sub-universal and admits external commitments in its observation premise. This includes substrates whose observations are physical signals from environments outside the substrate (sensors, human input, network traffic), and whose computational expressiveness is bounded below Turing universality.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}[Embedded controller with sensor input]
\label{ex:quadrant-embedded-controller}
An embedded microcontroller running a finite-state program that reads from external sensors inhabits this quadrant. The program's computation is finite-state, hence sub-universal. The observations include sensor readings, whose values are not produced by the controller's internal computation; the closure premise fails because observation values originate from outside the substrate.
\end{example}

\begin{remark}[The framework does not anchor here]
\label{rem:quadrant-degenerate-not-anchor}
The degenerate quadrant is structurally the weakest position for the closed-observational framework; the framework's conclusions transfer to this quadrant only after the external observation channel is itself given a closure analysis. The project's substrates do not anchor here. The quadrant is included in the map for completeness, not as a target.
\end{remark}

\begin{principle}[The map sharpens the closure premise]
\label{prin:quadrant-map-sharpens-closure}
The four-quadrant map sharpens the closure premise of \autoref{def:transcendental-input-closed-system} by showing what the premise is not: it is not a statement about computational expressiveness (Quadrant~I and Quadrant~III both satisfy it); it is not a statement about cellular structure (Presburger satisfies it without being cellular); it is a statement specifically about the absence of external commitments in the observation channel. The premise is therefore identifiable independently of universality, and the no-induction theorem is the conclusion of the closure premise alone.
\end{principle}

\section{Closure remarks}
\label{sec:quadrant-closure}

This chapter has read the universality and closure axes as independent and generated the $2 \times 2$ quadrant they span. The project's two anchors (\texttt{rule110/} as Quadrant~I substrate and BEDC host as Quadrant~III system) jointly demonstrate that the closure premise applies uniformly across computational expressiveness levels. Quadrant~II and Quadrant~IV are populated by recognised substrate classes that the project does not anchor in; their structural role is to identify what the closure premise rules out (external observation channels) versus what it tolerates (any computational power, from finite-state to Turing-universal).

The boundary of this chapter is: the closure premise is independent of universality; the no-induction theorem applies in any closed quadrant; the project anchors in two of the four quadrants and identifies the other two without claiming substrates there. No new theorem is introduced; the chapter generalises \autoref{prin:cellular-automaton-universality-vs-closure} into a full map and reads the map as a check on the closure premise's information content.
