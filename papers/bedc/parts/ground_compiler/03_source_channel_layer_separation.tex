\chapter{Source-Level Events and Channel-Level Bits}
\label{ch:ground-compiler-source-channel-layer-separation}

\autoref{ch:ground-compiler-event-flow} fixes the source-layer input language $\mathsf{EventFlow} = (\Sigma^{*})^{*}$, and \autoref{ch:ground-compiler-zeckendorf-safe-channel-encoding} fixes the channel-layer encoder
$$
  \mathsf{Compile} \colon \mathsf{EventFlow} \longrightarrow \mathsf{LegalZStream}
  \;=\; \big(\, (0 \mid 10)^{*}\,11 \,\big)^{*},
$$
together with its decoder $\mathsf{Decode}$ and the bijection $\mathsf{EventFlow} \cong \mathsf{LegalZStream}$ of \autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}.

The two layers share the glyph set $\Sigma = \{0,1\}$, but the role of each glyph differs by layer:
\begin{itemize}
  \item in the source layer, $0$ and $1$ display the raw marks $\bzero$ and $\bone$, and the pair $11$ is a permissible substring of a raw event;
  \item in the channel layer, $0$ and $1$ are encoded glyphs, and $11$ is the unique event terminator.
\end{itemize}
This chapter records the discipline that follows from this difference. The central principle is that source-level normalization, classification, and ledger-recording act on decoded event flows in $\mathsf{EventFlow}$, never on compiled glyph strings in $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$. The Zeckendorf carry classifier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zeckendorf-carry-classifier} and the Zeckendorf normal-form classifier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zeckendorf-normal-namecert} are source-layer relations on recognized history flows; they may not be applied to channel glyph strings as substring rewrites. The chapter records the mistake explicitly, supplies the correct decode-normalize-compile pipeline, and proves a layer-separation theorem with seven independent components.

\section{Source layer and channel layer}
\label{sec:source-channel-source-and-channel-layers}

\begin{definition}[Source layer]
\label{def:source-channel-source-layer}
The \emph{source layer} consists of raw events and event flows of \autoref{ch:ground-compiler-event-flow}:
$$
  \mathsf{RawEvent} \;=\; \Sigma^{*},
  \qquad
  \mathsf{EventFlow} \;=\; (\Sigma^{*})^{*}.
$$
A glyph string in the source layer, e.g.\ $011$, denotes the raw event $011 \in \mathsf{RawEvent}$. The pair $11$ is a permissible substring of a source raw event; the raw events $11$, $011$, and $0011$ are all elements of $\mathsf{RawEvent}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Channel layer]
\label{def:source-channel-channel-layer}
The \emph{channel layer} consists of compiled glyph strings:
$$
  \mathsf{LegalZStream} \;=\; \big(\, (0 \mid 10)^{*}\,11 \,\big)^{*}.
$$
In the channel layer, the pair $11$ has a single fixed role: it marks the end of the encoding of the current event (\autoref{def:channel-encoding-event-terminator}). A channel-layer $11$ is therefore not a source-layer raw substring, not a Zeckendorf carry source, not a completion witness, not a theorem seal, and not an $\hsame$ judgment.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Same display, different layers]
\label{rem:source-channel-same-display-different-layers}
A glyph string written on the page may be read at either layer, but never at both simultaneously. The source-layer reading places the string in $\mathsf{RawEvent}$ or $\mathsf{EventFlow}$; the channel-layer reading places the string in $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$. The reading is fixed by the surrounding context---typically by which side of $\mathsf{Compile}$ or $\mathsf{Decode}$ the string is generated by---and the chapter's discipline insists that the reading not silently change.
\end{remark}

\begin{definition}[Source-level relation]
\label{def:source-channel-source-level-relation}
\leandef{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.SourceLevelRelation}
A \emph{source-level relation} is a binary relation
$$
  (\leadsto) \;\subseteq\; \mathsf{RawEvent} \times \mathsf{RawEvent}
$$
on raw events. By extension, a source-level relation may be lifted pointwise or via context to a relation on event flows. A relation $w \leadsto v$ asserts only a source-layer fact between two raw events; it asserts neither $\hsame$ between underlying histories nor channel-layer glyph-string rewriting.

A representative source-level relation is the Zeckendorf carry $\leadsto_{Z}$, whose canonical instance is
$$
  011 \;\leadsto_{Z}\; 100,
$$
read as: ``$011$ is a pre-normal source event whose Zeckendorf-normal source event is $100$.'' The classifier-level reading of this relation is the Zeckendorf carry classifier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zeckendorf-carry-classifier}, which is a structural relation between recognized normal-form histories, not glyph-string rewriting.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Source carry ledger]
\label{def:source-channel-carry-ledger}
\leandef{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.SourceCarryLedger}
Given a source-level relation $w \leadsto v$, the corresponding \emph{ledger flow} is the event flow
$$
  (w, v) \;\in\; \mathsf{EventFlow}.
$$
Displayed, the flow is written $w \mid v$ and read as ``pre-normal event $w$ followed by normal event $v$.''

For the canonical Zeckendorf instance $011 \mid 100$, the channel encoding is computed event-by-event using \autoref{def:channel-encoding-event-encoding}: $\mathsf{Ev}(011) = 0101011$ and $\mathsf{Ev}(100) = 100011$, so
$$
  \mathsf{Compile}(011, 100) \;=\; 0101011\,100011.
$$
The compiled stream records both events; it does not erase the pre-normal event $011$.
\end{definition}

\section{No channel-level source rewrite}
\label{sec:source-channel-no-channel-rewrite}

\begin{principle}[No channel-level source rewrite]
\label{prin:source-channel-no-channel-rewrite}
For every source-level relation $w \leadsto v$, it is not admissible to rewrite the displayed substring $w$ to $v$ inside the channel string $\mathsf{Ev}(w)$ or inside the compiled stream $\mathsf{Compile}(S)$. In particular, the source-level pair $011 \leadsto_{Z} 100$ is not a channel-layer string-rewriting rule.
\end{principle}

\begin{example}[Channel-level $011 \to 100$ rewrite breaks legality]
\label{ex:source-channel-rewrite-breaks-legality}
The source raw event $011$ has channel encoding
$$
  \mathsf{Ev}(011)
  \;=\; \zeta(0)\,\zeta(1)\,\zeta(1)\,11
  \;=\; 0\,10\,10\,11
  \;=\; 0101011.
$$
Suppose the displayed substring $011$ at the right end of $0101011$ is mistakenly rewritten to $100$:
$$
  0101011 \;\longrightarrow\; 0101100.
$$
Decoding the result of this rewrite glyph-by-glyph, per \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-event}: $0$ emits raw $0$; $10$ emits raw $1$; $11$ marks event end; the remaining glyphs are $00$, which contains no subsequent terminator. By \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-stream}, the stream is rejected. Thus the channel-level rewrite has destroyed legality.
\end{example}

\begin{proposition}[Channel-level source rewrite may destroy legality]
\label{prop:source-channel-rewrite-may-destroy-legality}
There exists a legal channel stream $c \in \mathsf{LegalZStream}$ and a displayed source-level rewrite $w \leadsto v$ such that the result of replacing the displayed substring $w$ in $c$ by $v$ is not a legal channel stream.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Take $c = \mathsf{Ev}(011) = 0101011$, which lies in $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$ by \autoref{def:channel-encoding-legal-event}. Apply the source-level rewrite $011 \leadsto_{Z} 100$ to the displayed substring $011$ at the right end of $c$. The result is $c' = 0101100$. By \autoref{ex:source-channel-rewrite-breaks-legality}, the stream-decoder of \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-stream} rejects $c'$, so $c' \notin \mathsf{LegalZStream}$. Hence channel-level application of a source-level rewrite is not legality-preserving in general.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[A source normalizer is not a channel transducer]
\label{cor:source-channel-normalizer-not-channel-transducer}
A source-level normalizer
$$
  N \colon \mathsf{EventFlow} \longrightarrow \mathsf{EventFlow}
$$
does not lift to a glyph-level transducer $\Sigma^{*} \to \Sigma^{*}$ obtained by rewriting displayed substrings of channel streams. To apply $N$ to an already-compiled channel stream, the only admissible procedure is to decode first, normalize on the resulting event flow, and recompile.
\end{corollary}

\section{Decode-normalize-compile pipeline}
\label{sec:source-channel-pipeline}

\begin{definition}[Decode-normalize-compile pipeline]
\label{def:source-channel-decode-normalize-compile}
\leandef{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.NormalizeChannel}
Given a source-level normalizer $N \colon \mathsf{EventFlow} \to \mathsf{EventFlow}$, the \emph{decode-normalize-compile pipeline} is the composition
$$
  \mathsf{NormalizeChannel}_{N}
  \;:=\;
  \mathsf{Compile} \circ N \circ \mathsf{Decode}
  \;\colon\; \mathsf{LegalZStream} \longrightarrow \mathsf{LegalZStream},
$$
read as
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{l}
  channel stream \\
  $\xrightarrow{\mathsf{Decode}}$ source event flow \\
  $\xrightarrow{N}$ normalized source event flow \\
  $\xrightarrow{\mathsf{Compile}}$ channel stream.
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
On an arbitrary channel glyph string the same operation is partial: it rejects malformed input when $\mathsf{Decode}$ rejects, and otherwise returns $\mathsf{Compile}(N(S))$ for the decoded event flow $S$. Restricted to $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$, it is the displayed map above. By construction the pipeline forbids any direct rewrite on channel glyphs.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[The pipeline preserves channel legality]
\label{thm:source-channel-pipeline-preserves-legality}
\leanchecked{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.normalize\_channel\_preserves\_legality}
For every channel stream $c \in \mathsf{LegalZStream}$ and every total source-level normalizer $N \colon \mathsf{EventFlow} \to \mathsf{EventFlow}$,
$$
  \mathsf{NormalizeChannel}_{N}(c) \;\in\; \mathsf{LegalZStream}.
$$
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By \autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}, $\mathsf{Decode}(c) \in \mathsf{EventFlow}$. By the totality of $N$, $N(\mathsf{Decode}(c)) \in \mathsf{EventFlow}$. By \autoref{def:channel-encoding-flow-encoding}, $\mathsf{Compile}$ sends every event flow into $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$. The composite output is therefore in $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$.
\end{proof}

\section{Ledger preservation and classifier quotient}
\label{sec:source-channel-ledger-and-quotient}

\begin{definition}[Ledger-preserving source relation]
\label{def:source-channel-ledger-preserving}
A source-level relation $w \leadsto v$ is \emph{ledger-preserving} when its action on event flows records the pair $(w, v)$ rather than deletes the pre-normal event. The ledger-preserving form of $w \leadsto v$ is the event flow $w \mid v$ of \autoref{def:source-channel-carry-ledger}, not the singleton flow $v$ alone.
\end{definition}

\begin{principle}[Normal address does not erase the raw event]
\label{prin:source-channel-no-raw-erasure}
For any source-level relation $w \leadsto v$, the right-hand event $v$ may be displayed as a normal address, but the left-hand event $w$ must remain present in the recorded ledger flow. The flow $w \mid v$ is admissible; deleting $w$ in favor of the singleton flow $v$ is not. This discipline is the source-layer specialization of \autoref{prin:compression-requires-memory}: any compression of generated material requires a ledger that tracks the hidden material; composite ledgers must keep the source map rather than only the visible interface.
\end{principle}

\begin{proposition}[Ledger records distinguish ordered pairs]
\label{prop:source-channel-ledger-injective}
\leanchecked{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.ledger\_records\_distinguish\_ordered\_pairs}
The ledger-record map $w, v \mapsto (w, v)$ is injective on its arguments: if $(w, v) = (w', v')$, then $w = w'$ and $v = v'$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equality of ordered pairs is componentwise. The conclusion is immediate.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Quotient may be many-to-one; ledger flow is lossless]
\label{cor:source-channel-quotient-vs-ledger}
A source-level classifier may identify multiple raw events as one classified object, but the ledger flow recording the carry events remains lossless. In particular, even when a classifier asserts $w \sim v$, the ledger flow $w \mid v$ continues to distinguish the pre-normal event $w$ from the normal event $v$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{definition}[Source-level classified object]
\label{def:source-channel-classified-object}
For a source-level equivalence relation $\sim$ on raw events or event flows, a \emph{classified object} is an equivalence class $[w]_{\sim}$. For the Zeckendorf carry classifier $\sim_{Z}$ of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zeckendorf-carry-classifier}, $011 \sim_{Z} 100$ identifies $[011]_{\sim_{Z}} = [100]_{\sim_{Z}}$, while at the raw-event layer $011 \neq 100$ remains.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Classified-object map is generally not injective]
\label{prop:source-channel-classified-not-injective}
The natural map
$$
  q_{\sim} \colon \mathsf{RawEvent} \longrightarrow \mathsf{RawEvent}/\!\sim,
  \qquad
  q_{\sim}(w) = [w]_{\sim},
$$
is generally not injective.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If there exist distinct raw events $w \neq v$ with $w \sim v$, then $q_{\sim}(w) = [w]_{\sim} = [v]_{\sim} = q_{\sim}(v)$, so $q_{\sim}$ identifies $w$ and $v$. The Zeckendorf instance is $011 \neq 100$ but $011 \sim_{Z} 100$, hence $q_{\sim_{Z}}(011) = q_{\sim_{Z}}(100)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Bijection is unconditional only at the raw-flow layer]
\label{cor:source-channel-bijection-only-at-raw-layer}
The unconditional bijection supplied by channel encoding is between raw event flows and legal channel streams,
$$
  \mathsf{EventFlow} \;\cong\; \mathsf{LegalZStream}
  \quad\text{(\autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}).}
$$
The composite map $\mathsf{EventFlow} \to \mathsf{EventFlow}/\!\sim$ to a classifier quotient is generally not injective. Therefore, when a downstream argument speaks of a bijection between ``theory'' and ``code,'' the theory side must be the canonical raw-event flow, not an abstract classified proposition.
\end{corollary}

\section{Encoding and decoding examples for ledger flows}
\label{sec:source-channel-examples}

\begin{example}[Carry ledger encoding]
\label{ex:source-channel-carry-ledger-encoding}
The carry-ledger event flow $011 \mid 100$ encodes event-by-event as
$$
  \mathsf{Ev}(011) \;=\; 0101011,
  \qquad
  \mathsf{Ev}(100) \;=\; 100011,
$$
hence
$$
  \mathsf{Compile}(011, 100) \;=\; 0101011\,100011.
$$
The stream decoder of \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-stream} segments the result at the leftmost terminator, recovering $0101011 \mid 100011$ and decoding to $(011, 100)$. The pre-normal event $011$ is preserved by the channel round trip.
\end{example}

\begin{example}[Completion skeleton with carry ledger]
\label{ex:source-channel-completion-with-carry}
The event flow $0 \mid 00 \mid 000 \mid 01 \mid 011 \mid 100$ of \autoref{ex:ground-event-flow-completion-skeleton} encodes event-by-event as
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
  $\mathsf{Ev}(0)$    & $= 011$ \\
  $\mathsf{Ev}(00)$   & $= 0011$ \\
  $\mathsf{Ev}(000)$  & $= 00011$ \\
  $\mathsf{Ev}(01)$   & $= 01011$ \\
  $\mathsf{Ev}(011)$  & $= 0101011$ \\
  $\mathsf{Ev}(100)$  & $= 100011$.
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The complete channel stream is therefore the concatenation
$$
  011\,0011\,00011\,01011\,0101011\,100011,
$$
which is in $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$ by \autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}. If a downstream classifier asserts $011 \sim_{Z} 100$, the assertion is read against the decoded source events, not against any displayed substring of the channel stream.
\end{example}

\begin{theorem}[Source-level normalization is conservative over channel decoding]
\label{thm:source-channel-conservative-over-decoding}
For every source-level normalizer $N \colon \mathsf{EventFlow} \to \mathsf{EventFlow}$, the diagram
$$
  \mathsf{LegalZStream}
  \xrightarrow{\;\mathsf{Decode}\;}
  \mathsf{EventFlow}
  \xrightarrow{\;N\;}
  \mathsf{EventFlow}
  \xrightarrow{\;\mathsf{Compile}\;}
  \mathsf{LegalZStream}
$$
is well-defined. Source-level normalization therefore acts on channel streams indirectly through the decode-normalize-compile pipeline of \autoref{def:source-channel-decode-normalize-compile}; it does not act on channel streams as a direct glyph-level rewrite.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The first arrow is well-defined by \autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}; the second arrow is well-defined by the totality of $N$; the third arrow is well-defined by \autoref{def:channel-encoding-flow-encoding}. The composite is therefore a well-defined function from $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$ to itself. Direct glyph-level channel rewriting is excluded from this diagram and is forbidden by \autoref{prin:source-channel-no-channel-rewrite}.
\end{proof}

\section{Channel substrings versus source events}
\label{sec:source-channel-substrings-vs-events}

\begin{proposition}[Channel terminator is not the source event $11$]
\label{prop:source-channel-terminator-not-source-event-11}
\leanchecked{BEDC.GroundCompiler.SourceChannel.channel\_terminator\_not\_source\_event\_11}
The channel-layer terminator $11$ of \autoref{def:channel-encoding-event-terminator} is not the source raw event $11 \in \mathsf{RawEvent}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The source raw event $11$ has channel encoding
$$
  \mathsf{Ev}(11)
  \;=\; \zeta(1)\,\zeta(1)\,11
  \;=\; 10\,10\,11
  \;=\; 101011,
$$
whereas the channel terminator is the literal glyph pair $11$. If the channel terminator were identified with the source raw event $11$, the encoding of the source raw event $11$ would be $11$ rather than $101011$. The two strings are distinct, so the identification fails.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[The source raw event $11$ encodes unambiguously]
\label{cor:source-channel-source-event-11-unambiguous}
The source raw event $11$ encodes as the channel string $\mathsf{Ev}(11) = 101011$. The decoder of \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-event} reads this as $10 \to \mathrm{raw}\ 1$, $10 \to \mathrm{raw}\ 1$, $11 \to \mathrm{event\ end}$, recovering $11 \in \mathsf{RawEvent}$ without ambiguity.
\end{corollary}

\begin{principle}[No channel proof by substring]
\label{prin:source-channel-no-proof-by-substring}
The appearance of a displayed substring $u$ inside a channel stream $c \in \mathsf{LegalZStream}$ does not entail that the source event $u$ appears in $\mathsf{Decode}(c)$. Source-event analysis is admissible only on the output of $\mathsf{Decode}$.
\end{principle}

\begin{proposition}[Channel substrings are not source events]
\label{prop:source-channel-substring-not-source-event}
There exists a channel stream $c \in \mathsf{LegalZStream}$ and a displayed substring $u$ of $c$ such that $u$ is not any source event of $\mathsf{Decode}(c)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Take $c = \mathsf{Ev}(1) = 1011$. The displayed substring $u = 011$ occurs at positions $2$--$4$ of $c$. By \autoref{thm:channel-encoding-event-round-trip}, $\mathsf{Decode}(c) = (1)$. The only source event of $\mathsf{Decode}(c)$ is $1$, not $011$. Hence the displayed substring $011$ is not a source event of the decoded flow.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Source analysis must begin after decoding]
\label{cor:source-channel-analysis-after-decoding}
Any source-level analysis---finite repetition, completion witness, Zeckendorf carry, $\NameCert$ source pattern, theorem event, ledger event---is admissible only after $\mathsf{Decode}$ has been applied to the channel stream. Source-level analyses applied directly to channel glyphs are not admissible.
\end{corollary}

\section{Layer separation theorem and closing observations}
\label{sec:source-channel-layer-separation-theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Layer separation theorem]
\label{thm:source-channel-layer-separation}
The source layer and the channel layer satisfy the following seven separation conditions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item source events are elements of $\mathsf{RawEvent}$;
  \item channel streams are elements of $\mathsf{LegalZStream}$;
  \item $\mathsf{Compile}$ encodes source event flows into channel streams;
  \item $\mathsf{Decode}$ recovers source event flows from channel streams;
  \item source-level normalization acts on $\mathsf{EventFlow}$ only;
  \item the channel-layer event terminator $11$ is not the source raw event $11$;
  \item displayed channel substrings are not, in general, source events of the decoded flow.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Items (1) and (2) are \autoref{def:source-channel-source-layer} and \autoref{def:source-channel-channel-layer}. Items (3) and (4) are \autoref{def:channel-encoding-flow-encoding}, \autoref{def:channel-encoding-decoder-stream}, and \autoref{cor:channel-encoding-bijection}. Item (5) is \autoref{def:source-channel-source-level-relation} and \autoref{thm:source-channel-conservative-over-decoding}. Item (6) is \autoref{prop:source-channel-terminator-not-source-event-11}. Item (7) is \autoref{prop:source-channel-substring-not-source-event}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Outlook]
\label{rem:source-channel-outlook}
The next chapter records how recognized package, $\NameCert$, $\ClosureCert$, theorem, and ledger structures are read inside a decoded event flow. By \autoref{thm:source-channel-layer-separation}, every such recognition begins from $\mathsf{Decode}(c)$, never from $c$ as a glyph string. The recognizer flows of \autoref{def:ground-event-flow-generated-recognizer}--\autoref{def:ground-event-flow-recognized-compiler-flow} are therefore source-layer relations on event flows, in line with \autoref{prin:ground-event-flow-no-hidden-input}; their composition with $\mathsf{Compile}$ and $\mathsf{Decode}$ is the discipline by which structural readings reach the channel layer without appearing as primitive compiler inputs.
\end{remark}
