It sits beside the kernel acceptance witness of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-kernelacceptancewitness-namecert}, the finite reflection tuple of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-finitereflectiontuple-namecert}, and the GroundCompiler recognizer flow of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-groundcompilerrecognizerflow-namecert}. \begin{definition}[Standard bridge audit packet carrier]
\label{def:standard-bridge-audit-packet-carrier}
A $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
S=(N,T,E,D,R,U,P,L,H,C,Q)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $N$ is the accepted BEDC object row, read through the named $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ surface for the object being bridged;
\item $T$ is the host-theory row, recording the host theory only as the right-hand side of the bridge and not as a BEDC primitive;
\item $E$ is the encoding row from the BEDC carrier into the host theory;
\item $D$ is the decoding row from the host theory back to the BEDC carrier;
\item $R$ is the paired round-trip row, containing both decode-after-encode and encode-after-decode witnesses under the licensed classifier;
\item $U$ is the finite theorem-preservation row, pairing each preserved host theorem with a BEDC counterpart and a displayed translation;
\item $P$ is the no-host-leak row, recording the host primitives, quotient principles, semantic equality assumptions, proof objects, and host compiler readings that do not appear on the BEDC public surface;
\item $L$ is the bridge ledger row, recording unsupported fields, refused translations, theorem-preservation gaps, and bridge scope boundaries;
\item $H$ stores componentwise $\hsame$ transport for the accepted object, host theory, encoding, decoding, round-trip, theorem-preservation, no-host-leak, ledger, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows;
\item $C$ stores displayed $\Cont$ replay for bridge checking, theorem-pair lookup, no-host-leak inspection, and downstream audit reads;
\item $Q$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance and local $\NameCert_{\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp}$ row over $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$, $\FiniteReflectionTupleUp$, $\GroundCompilerRecognizerFlowUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for a host primitive promoted to BEDC input, an infinite theorem schema, an unpaired host theorem, a hidden decoder, a private compiler route, or a bridge assertion detached from the displayed no-host-leak row.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Standard bridge audit packet non-host-leak]
\label{thm:standard-bridge-audit-packet-nonhost-leak}
For every accepted $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$ carrier
$$
S=(N,T,E,D,R,U,P,L,H,C,Q),
$$
the public bridge read factors through the displayed no-host-leak row $P$. A bridge route that omits $P$ cannot export a BEDC-recognized standard bridge, even when $E,D,R,U$ are present.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:standard-bridge-audit-packet-carrier}. The host theory row $T$, encoding row $E$, decoding row $D$, round-trip row $R$, and theorem-preservation row $U$ are not enough to define a public BEDC bridge because each of them may mention host-side structure.

The no-host-leak row $P$ records exactly which host primitives, quotient principles, semantic equalities, proof objects, and host compiler readings are barred from the BEDC public surface. It is therefore the row that separates bridge recognition from host import.

The structural rows $L,H,C,Q$ preserve and replay the same displayed packet. Since none of them supplies a replacement for $P$, any route that omits $P$ exits the accepted $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$ carrier rather than exporting a standard bridge.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Standard bridge audit packet independence]
\label{thm:standard-bridge-audit-packet-independence}
The carrier of \autoref{def:standard-bridge-audit-packet-carrier} is not bijective, row for row, with $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$, $\FiniteReflectionTupleUp$, $\GroundCompilerRecognizerFlowUp$, finite BHist injection data, or $\ProofTermErasureTraceUp$. A row-preserving bijection would have to preserve accepted-object evidence, reflection-image readback, generated-recognizer admission, finite history injection, proof-term erasure visibility, encoding, decoding, two round-trip witnesses, finite theorem preservation, no-host-leak, bridge ledger, transport, replay, provenance, and local naming at once; no listed sibling carries that whole surface.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First compare with $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$. Kernel acceptance witnesses expose candidate, accepted declaration, environment, purity-query, refusal, transport, replay, provenance, and local naming rows, but they do not contain host encoding, decoding, round trips, theorem-preservation pairs, or a no-host-leak row for a host theory.

Next compare with $\FiniteReflectionTupleUp$ and $\GroundCompilerRecognizerFlowUp$. The reflection tuple supplies displayed image and readback rows, while the recognizer flow supplies source-channel and generated-recognizer admission. Neither records the six standard-bridge fields as one finite audit packet.

Finally compare with finite BHist injection data and $\ProofTermErasureTraceUp$. Injection data concerns how finite histories embed into a displayed history surface, and proof-term erasure traces separate proof evidence from erased public rows. They do not carry the host-relative bridge ledger $E,D,R,U,P,L$. Thus no row-preserving bijection can identify any listed sibling with $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Standard bridge audit packet round-trip exactness]
\label{thm:standard-bridge-audit-packet-round-trip-exactness}
For every accepted $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$ carrier
$$
S=(N,T,E,D,R,U,P,L,H,C,Q),
$$
the two public round-trip reads are exactly the displayed reads through $R,H,C,Q$. Decode-after-encode may consume $E$ and $D$ only after the paired round-trip row $R$ names the witness, the componentwise transport row $H$ preserves the accepted object and host-theory coordinates, the $\Cont$ row $C$ replays the bridge-check request, and the provenance-and-naming row $Q$ records the local packet. Encode-after-decode is governed by the same four rows in the opposite read direction. No hidden encoder, hidden decoder, host equality, or bridge assertion outside $R,H,C,Q$ can supply either exactness witness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:standard-bridge-audit-packet-carrier}. The rows $E$ and $D$ store the two bridge maps, but the carrier gives them no public exactness role by themselves; the paired round-trip row $R$ is the only coordinate assigned to decode-after-encode and encode-after-decode witnesses.

Transport and replay are then forced through the structural rows. The row $H$ moves the accepted object, host theory, encoding, decoding, and round-trip data componentwise, while $C$ replays the concrete bridge-check request that asks for one of the two round-trip reads.

Finally $Q$ records the $\Pkg$ provenance and local $\NameCert_{\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp}$ row. Since the carrier has no coordinate for a hidden map, host equality import, or detached bridge assertion, both exactness directions are precisely the reads through $R,H,C,Q$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Standard bridge audit packet theorem-preservation ledger]
\label{thm:standard-bridge-audit-packet-theorem-preservation-ledger}
For every accepted $\StandardBridgeAuditPacketUp$ carrier
$$

A host theorem that has no paired BEDC counterpart in $U$, or whose required host primitive is refused by $P$ or recorded as unsupported in $L$, is not exported as a preserved BEDC theorem.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The theorem-preservation coordinate of the carrier is $U$. By definition it contains finite rows pairing preserved host theorems with BEDC counterparts and displayed translations, so an unpaired host theorem has no preservation row to project.

The ledger row $L$ records unsupported fields, refused translations, theorem-preservation gaps, and bridge scope boundaries. Thus a preservation claim must also be compatible with the finite ledger entry that says the translation is supported inside the packet's bridge scope.

The no-host-leak row $P$ supplies the public admissibility check. If the proposed preservation uses a host primitive, quotient principle, semantic equality assumption, proof object, or compiler reading barred by $P$, then the claim remains a host-side statement rather than a BEDC-preserved theorem. Hence theorem preservation is exactly the finite $U,L,P$ ledger surface.
