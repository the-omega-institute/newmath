\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-kernelacceptancewitness-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ surface turns the Lean-side acceptance boundary into a finite BEDC certificate packet. It records the generated candidate declaration, the accepted declaration row, the environment ledger entry, the axiom-query result, refusal evidence for unaccepted candidates, transport and continuation rows, package provenance, and the local naming row. The packet is not a global trust principle for Lean; it is the visible witness that a specific candidate crossed the kernel boundary and that the accepted result has a readable purity ledger.

The $\KernelAcceptanceAuditWitnessUp$ packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-kernelacceptanceauditwitness-namecert} consumes the candidate, accepted-declaration, environment-ledger, axiom-query, refusal, transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows of this packet and adds build replay plus audit-gate specialization for a single acceptance event.

\begin{definition}[KernelAcceptanceWitness carrier]
\label{def:kernel-acceptance-witness-carrier}
A $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
K=(G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $G$ is the generated-candidate row, naming the proposed Lean declaration, its source module, its type surface, and the finite generator context that produced it;
\item $A$ is the accepted-declaration row, present exactly when the kernel has accepted the declaration named by $G$;
\item $E$ is the environment-ledger row, recording the module position, imported BEDC declarations, and replay boundary at which $A$ is entered into the environment;
\item $Q$ is the axiom-query row, recording the transitive dependency query for $A$ and the reported absence or presence of forbidden dependency names;
\item $R$ is the refusal-boundary row, recording failed candidates, unresolved names, rejected recursion, forbidden axioms, or dependency reports that prevent a candidate from being exported as accepted;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transports for generated candidate, accepted declaration, environment ledger, axiom-query, refusal-boundary, continuation, package, and local naming rows;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes by which consumers read acceptance, environment replay, purity query, and refusal evidence;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\BHist$, generated candidates, accepted declarations, environment ledger rows, axiom-query rows, refusal rows, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and local naming;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier exposes no host-kernel completeness theorem, no hidden proof-producing channel, no acceptance for a candidate absent from $A$, and no purity claim outside the displayed query row $Q$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[KernelAcceptanceWitness NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp}$ surface for the carrier of \autoref{def:kernel-acceptance-witness-carrier} consists of six finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier exposure for the packet $K=(G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N)$;
\item candidate-to-acceptance matching, stating that the accepted row $A$ names the declaration proposed by $G$ and no unlisted candidate;
\item environment replay, stating that $E$ records the module boundary and imported BEDC rows used when $A$ is entered into the environment;
\item purity query visibility, stating that $Q$ is the dependency ledger attached to $A$ and records the outcome of the transitive axiom query;
\item refusal separation, stating that rows recorded in $R$ are not exported as accepted declarations;
\item exactness of the displayed $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and local $\NameCert$ rows for the acceptance and refusal surfaces.
\end{enumerate}
These obligations bind kernel acceptance as finite certificate data rather than as an ambient trust principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier exposure is projection from \autoref{def:kernel-acceptance-witness-carrier}. The rows $G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N$ are the only coordinates available to consumers.

Candidate-to-acceptance matching and environment replay are read from $G,A,E$. The accepted row is attached to the generated candidate named by $G$, and the environment ledger records where that accepted declaration is replayed against the visible BEDC imports.

Purity query visibility is read from $Q$. The dependency report belongs to the accepted declaration row and is carried as a finite ledger entry, so the consumer sees the query result rather than an unstated promise about the environment.

Refusal separation is read from $R$. Failed candidates and forbidden dependency reports are retained as boundary data but have no coordinate that exports them as accepted declarations.

The transport, continuation, package, and local naming obligations are componentwise over the displayed rows. They can move or read the packet, but they do not add a kernel-completeness theorem or a hidden proof-producing channel.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[KernelAcceptanceWitness ledger purity]
\label{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-ledger-purity}
For an accepted $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ packet, every purity-facing consumer route factors through the accepted-declaration row $A$, the environment-ledger row $E$, the axiom-query row $Q$, the displayed $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ provenance, and the refusal boundary $R$ for candidates that do not cross the acceptance gate. The route exports no declaration as BEDC-internal when its query row reports $\mathsf{Classical.choice}$, $\mathsf{Quot.sound}$, $\mathsf{propext}$, an unaccepted candidate, or an unresolved declaration name.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the NameCert obligations of \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-namecert-obligations}. They attach the accepted declaration to its generated candidate, environment replay row, axiom-query row, and local naming row.

Next read the purity route through $Q$. The consumer may inspect the transitive dependency report only as the ledger entry paired with $A$ and $E$, so purity is a displayed row of the packet rather than a separate meta-level assertion.

Finally use the refusal row $R$. Any candidate with a forbidden dependency report, unresolved name, failed kernel replay, or absent accepted-declaration row remains on the refusal side of the packet and is not exported by the local certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[KernelAcceptanceWitness refusal separation]
\label{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-refusal-separation}
In an accepted $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ packet
$$
K=(G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N),
$$
any candidate row recorded in $R$ because of unresolved names, forbidden dependency reports, failed kernel replay, or an absent accepted declaration cannot be exported through $A$, $E$, or $Q$ as accepted BEDC content.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:kernel-acceptance-witness-carrier}. The accepted-declaration, environment-ledger, axiom-query, and refusal-boundary rows are separate coordinates of the packet.

Use the NameCert obligations of \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-namecert-obligations}. Candidate-to-acceptance matching places accepted content in $A$, environment replay places its accepted module boundary in $E$, purity-query visibility places its dependency report in $Q$, and refusal separation keeps unresolved names, forbidden dependency reports, failed kernel replay, and absent accepted declarations in $R$.

Finally apply ledger purity from \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-ledger-purity}. Purity-facing consumers read accepted BEDC content only through $A,E,Q$, while failed candidates remain on the refusal side of the same packet, so no route exports an $R$ row as accepted content.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[KernelAcceptanceWitness public export package]
\label{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-public-export-package}
For every accepted $\KernelAcceptanceWitnessUp$ packet
$$
K=(G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N)
$$
satisfying the local NameCert obligations of \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-namecert-obligations}, the public BEDC acceptance-witness export consists exactly of the generated-candidate row $G$, accepted-declaration row $A$, environment-ledger row $E$, axiom-query row $Q$, refusal-boundary row $R$, componentwise transport row $H$, displayed continuation routes $C$, package provenance row $P$, and local naming row $N$. The row $R$ is exported only as refusal-boundary evidence: no failed candidate, unresolved name, forbidden dependency report, failed replay row, or absent accepted declaration recorded on the refusal side can be exported through $A$, $E$, or $Q$ as accepted BEDC content.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:kernel-acceptance-witness-carrier}. Its displayed packet has exactly the nine coordinates $G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N$. The first four content rows expose the generated candidate, accepted declaration, environment replay, and dependency query; the row $R$ exposes the refusal boundary; and the rows $H,C,P,N$ provide componentwise transport, displayed routes, provenance, and local naming for that same finite packet.

Apply the NameCert obligations of \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-namecert-obligations}. Candidate-to-acceptance matching places accepted content in $A$ only for the candidate named by $G$. Environment replay places the accepted declaration at the boundary recorded by $E$. Purity-query visibility makes $Q$ the dependency ledger attached to $A$. Refusal separation keeps failed candidates, unresolved names, rejected recursion, forbidden axioms, and dependency reports on the $R$ side rather than in the accepted-declaration row.

Now use ledger purity from \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-ledger-purity}. Every purity-facing route factors through $A,E,Q$ for accepted content and through $R$ for candidates that do not cross the acceptance gate. Thus a route that tries to read a forbidden dependency report, unresolved declaration name, or unaccepted candidate as BEDC-internal accepted content has no exported coordinate through which to do so.

Finally apply refusal separation from \autoref{thm:kernel-acceptance-witness-refusal-separation}. Rows recorded in $R$ because of unresolved names, forbidden dependency reports, failed kernel replay, or absent accepted declarations cannot be exported through $A$, $E$, or $Q$ as accepted content. The structural rows $H,C,P,N$ preserve, route, source, and name the displayed packet; by the exactness obligation they add no second acceptance channel. Therefore the public export is precisely the nine-row packet $G,A,E,Q,R,H,C,P,N$, with $R$ visible only as boundary evidence and never as accepted BEDC content.
\end{proof}
