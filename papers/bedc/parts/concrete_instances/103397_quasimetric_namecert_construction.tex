\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\QuasiMetricUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-quasi-metric-namecert}

Following Lawvere, Nachbin, and Kelley, the $\QuasiMetricUp$ packet records the finite directed-distance surface that sits beside the symmetric metric certificate of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert}, the directed entourage carrier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-quasi-uniform-space-namecert}, the filter route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-cauchyfilter-namecert}, the net route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-cauchynet-namecert}, and the completion consumer of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-uniformcompletion-namecert}. Its Real-valued distance rows read the terminal seal of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert} through the dyadic tolerance source of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicratcore-namecert}, so completion-facing consumers see an oriented distance ledger before any uniform-entourage handoff is exposed. The chapter names only that directed NameCert surface; it does not assert symmetry, separatedness, metric completeness, open-cover compactness, quotient distance, selected limits, or a standard bridge.

\begin{definition}[QuasiMetric carrier surface]
\label{def:quasi-metric-carrier}
A $\QuasiMetricUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
Q=(S,X,D,Z,T,B,F,R,E,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $S$ is the source carrier row. It names the traditional quasi-metric source and the finite observation context in which directed distance requests are admitted.
\item $X$ is the point carrier row. It records the admitted point names and their classifier, but it does not identify points by zero distance.
\item $D$ is the directed $\RealUp$-valued distance row. It records oriented requests $d(x,y)$ and keeps the reverse request $d(y,x)$ as a separate row when that reverse request is present.
\item $Z$ is the zero-on-diagonal row. It records $d(x,x)=0$ as a displayed boundary condition on directed distance requests.
\item $T$ is the forward triangle ledger. It records the directed comparison from $d(x,z)$ through the ordered pair of rows $d(x,y)$ and $d(y,z)$.
\item $B$ is the directed-ball row. It records finite requests of the form $B^+(x,\varepsilon)$ by displaying the center, radius, and incoming point through $D,Z,T$.
\item $F$ is the $\CauchyFilterUp$ row. It records filter-base Cauchy reads whose directed balls are supplied by $B$ and whose comparisons remain ordered.
\item $R$ is the $\CauchyNetUp$ row. It records net-tail Cauchy reads whose eventual directed-ball comparisons are supplied by $B$.
\item $E$ is the uniform-reflection row. It records the finite handoff from directed balls to $\QuasiUniformSpaceUp$, $\UniformSpaceUp$, and completion-facing entourage requests without installing symmetry as primitive data.
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for source, point-carrier, directed distance, zero diagonal, triangle, directed-ball, Cauchy filter, Cauchy net, uniform reflection, replay, provenance, and local naming rows.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay from a consumer request through point admission, directed distance readback, zero diagonal or triangle evidence, directed-ball inspection, Cauchy filter or net readback, uniform reflection, provenance, and local naming.
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\QuasiMetricUp$, $\MetricSpaceUp$, $\QuasiUniformSpaceUp$, $\UniformSpaceUp$, $\CauchyFilterUp$, $\CauchyNetUp$, $\UniformCompletionUp$, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\RealUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\QuasiMetricUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The classifier compares accepted packets only through $S,X,D,Z,T,B,F,R,E,H,C,P,N$. It has no coordinate for symmetry, separated point identity, metric completeness, open-cover compactness, quotient distance, selected limits, quotient completion, or bridge checking.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[QuasiMetric NameCert obligation surface]
\label{thm:quasi-metric-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\QuasiMetricUp$ carrier
$$
Q=(S,X,D,Z,T,B,F,R,E,H,C,P,N),
$$
the local $\NameCert_{\QuasiMetricUp}$ surface consists exactly of the following obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission for the source, point carrier, directed $\RealUp$-valued distance row, zero diagonal row, forward triangle row, directed-ball row, Cauchy filter row, Cauchy net row, uniform-reflection row, componentwise transport, displayed replay, provenance, and local name;
\item asymmetric distance readback, requiring every exported distance request to keep the ordered pair of point rows visible before the $\RealUp$ distance seal is read;
\item zero diagonal stability, requiring self-distance to be stored in $Z$ rather than imported from a host metric law;
\item oriented triangle stability, requiring each triangle comparison to be displayed in $T$ with its ordered endpoints;
\item directed-ball handoff, requiring every ball consumer to pass through $B$ after $D,Z,T$ have been displayed;
\item Cauchy filter and Cauchy net routing, requiring filter-base and net-tail comparisons to read $F,R$ through the directed-ball row;
\item classifier transport, requiring accepted packets to move only by the componentwise $\hsame$ row $H$ and the replay row $C$;
\item uniform-reflection handoff, requiring every quasi-uniform, uniform, or completion-facing request to pass through $E$ after the directed balls and Cauchy rows have been displayed;
\item ledger non-escape, excluding symmetry, separatedness, metric completeness, open-cover compactness, quotient distance, quotient completion, selected limits, and any standard bridge.
\end{enumerate}
No quasi-metric NameCert obligation is stored outside these rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:quasi-metric-carrier}. The tuple lists exactly the source, point carrier, directed distance, zero diagonal, forward triangle, directed-ball, Cauchy filter, Cauchy net, uniform-reflection, transport, replay, provenance, and local naming rows.

Asymmetric distance readback is forced by $D$. A request for $d(x,y)$ keeps $x$ and $y$ in that order, and a request for $d(y,x)$ is a distinct displayed row unless the carrier separately records a comparison between them.

The zero diagonal and triangle obligations are the displayed rows $Z$ and $T$. Thus reflexive zero and forward triangle comparison are local quasi-metric data, not imported symmetry or host metric structure.

Directed-ball reads use $B$ only after $D,Z,T$ have been displayed. The Cauchy filter and net rows $F,R$ read those directed balls rather than a quotient completion object.

Classifier transport is componentwise through $H$, while $C$ replays the same ordered route for consumers. The uniform-reflection handoff uses $E$ only after the directed distance, ball, and Cauchy rows have been read.

The remaining exclusions follow from the carrier inventory. Since no coordinate names symmetry, separated identity, metric completeness, open-cover compactness, quotient distance, quotient completion, selected limits, or a standard bridge, none of those data can be exported by the local certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuasiMetric directed ball handoff]
\label{thm:quasi-metric-directed-ball-handoff}
Let
$$
Q=(S,X,D,Z,T,B,F,R,E,H,C,P,N)
$$
be an accepted $\QuasiMetricUp$ packet. Every $\QuasiUniformSpaceUp$, $\UniformSpaceUp$, $\CauchyFilterUp$, $\CauchyNetUp$, or $\UniformCompletionUp$ consumer that asks for a distance-presented directed ball reaches it only through the finite route
$$
S,X,D,Z,T \longmapsto B \longmapsto F,R,E \longmapsto H,C,P,N .
$$
The handoff may reflect to quasi-uniform, uniform, Cauchy-filter, Cauchy-net, or completion-facing rows, but it receives no symmetry theorem, no separated point identity, no metric completeness, no open-cover compactness, no quotient distance, no quotient completion, and no standard bridge.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the accepted carrier. The consumer first reads the source and point rows $S,X$, then the directed distance ledger $D$.

The diagonal and triangle rows $Z,T$ are read before any ball-facing handoff. They supply the quasi-metric boundary conditions needed to form directed balls without turning the directed distance into a symmetric metric.

Only after those rows are displayed does the consumer reach $B$. Filter and net consumers then read $F,R$, while quasi-uniform, uniform, and completion-facing consumers read the reflection row $E$.

The support rows $H,C,P,N$ transport, replay, package, and name the same finite route. Therefore the handoff exports finite directed-distance and directed-ball data, not symmetry, separatedness, metric completeness, open-cover compactness, quotient distance, quotient completion, or bridge evidence.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuasiMetric non-escape boundary]
\label{thm:quasi-metric-nonescape}
Every accepted $\QuasiMetricUp$ read used by $\MetricSpaceUp$, $\QuasiUniformSpaceUp$, $\UniformSpaceUp$, $\CauchyFilterUp$, $\CauchyNetUp$, or $\UniformCompletionUp$ factors through the displayed rows of \autoref{def:quasi-metric-carrier}. In particular, a metric or completion consumer cannot bypass the directed-distance row $D$, the forward triangle ledger $T$, the directed-ball row $B$, the Cauchy rows $F,R$, and the uniform-reflection row $E$ by importing symmetry, quotient completion, selected limits, host metric equality, \texttt{Classical.choice}, or \texttt{Quot.sound}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Apply \autoref{thm:quasi-metric-namecert-obligations}. The NameCert surface stores all quasi-metric obligations in $S,X,D,Z,T,B,F,R,E,H,C,P,N$ and stores none outside those rows.

Apply \autoref{thm:quasi-metric-directed-ball-handoff}. Every directed-ball, Cauchy, quasi-uniform, uniform, and completion-facing consumer reaches the public route only after $D,Z,T,B,F,R,E$ have been displayed.

A metric-facing consumer may read the sibling metric surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert} only as a reflected reader named by $E$ or $P$. Since the carrier has no primitive coordinate for symmetry, quotient completion, selected limits, host equality, \texttt{Classical.choice}, or \texttt{Quot.sound}, those exits remain unavailable.
\end{proof}
