\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\ApophaticNameUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticname-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\ApophaticNameUp$ packet is the concrete NameCert surface for a named externality. It records a socket-name handle, the requested supply shape, the refusal gate that keeps the supply outside the generated substrate, the ledger row that makes the boundary auditable, and the provenance rows through which a downstream chapter may cite the socket without turning it into an internal object.

\begin{definition}[ApophaticName carrier]
\label{def:apophatic-name-carrier}
An $\ApophaticNameUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ socket-name packet
$$
A=(S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $S$ is the socket-name row, recording the named boundary position, the local history window, and the chapter site at which the external supply is requested;
\item $R$ is the requested supply-shape row, recording the finite interface that a caller would have to provide without asserting that the supply is generated by the substrate;
\item $G$ is the refusal-gate row, recording that the requested supply is not admitted as an axiom, quotient representative, hidden theorem, host object, or substrate constructor;
\item $L$ is the refusal-ledger row, recording each permitted consumer read and each barred conversion from socket to internal content;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the socket name, requested supply shape, refusal gate, ledger row, continuation route, package provenance, and local naming row;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes by which consumers read the socket, the request, the refusal decision, and the ledger entry;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\BHist$, socket names, requested supply shapes, refusal gates, refusal ledgers, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\ApophaticNameUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier contains no supplied object behind the socket, no theorem asserting that the requested supply exists, no quotient equality for external positions, and no host-side realization of the named dependency.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[ApophaticName refusal ledger]
\label{thm:apophatic-name-refusal-ledger}
For an accepted $\ApophaticNameUp$ carrier
$$
A=(S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N),
$$
the local refusal ledger exposes exactly the socket-name row $S$, the requested supply-shape row $R$, the refusal-gate row $G$, the ledger row $L$, the componentwise $\hsame$ transport row $H$, the displayed $\Cont$ routes $C$, the $\Pkg$ provenance $P$, and the local $\NameCert_{\ApophaticNameUp}$ row $N$. It records that consumers may cite the named external dependency only as a visible boundary request, and it supplies no internal axiom, quotient, hidden theorem, or host object.
\end{theorem}

\leanvariant{BEDC.Derived.ApophaticNameUp.ApophaticNameCarrier\_refusal\_ledger}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier of \autoref{def:apophatic-name-carrier}. The only rows available to a consumer are $S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N$.

Read the socket and request through $S$ and $R$. These rows make the external dependency referable and describe the finite supply shape, but they do not provide the supply itself.

Use the refusal gate $G$ and ledger row $L$. They record that an attempted conversion from named socket to internal content is refused, with separate entries for axioms, quotient representatives, hidden theorems, and host objects.

Finally route the read through $H,C,P,N$. Transport, continuation, package provenance, and local naming preserve the same boundary packet, so the ledger exposes the dependency without creating a substrate-internal witness.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[ApophaticName socket carrier]
\label{thm:apophatic-name-socket-carrier}
Every accepted $\ApophaticNameUp$ packet exposes its socket row, boundary-request row, refusal row, $\hsame$ transport row, $\Cont$ route, $\Pkg$ provenance, and local $\NameCert_{\ApophaticNameUp}$ row as finite $\BHist$ data. For
$$
A=(S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N),
$$
the displayed surface is exactly $S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N$: $S$ names the socket, $R$ gives the boundary request, $G$ and $L$ record the refusal decision and ledger, $H$ transports the components, $C$ gives the consumer route, $P$ records provenance, and $N$ is the local naming row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier through \autoref{def:apophatic-name-carrier}. Its tuple has only the eight displayed coordinates $S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N$, and each coordinate is introduced there as finite $\BHist$ data.

Apply the refusal-ledger surface of \autoref{thm:apophatic-name-refusal-ledger}. It identifies the same coordinates as the complete consumer ledger for the named boundary request.

The socket-facing side is compatible with the sibling socket surface of \autoref{thm:apophatic-socket-consumer-surface}. The gate-facing side is compatible with the visible refusal boundary of \autoref{thm:apophatic-gate-question-visible-socket-nonescape}. Thus the accepted packet exposes the socket and refusal rows as finite boundary data and not as a hidden supply.
\end{proof}

Use the local classifier stability theorem \autoref{thm:apophatic-name-classifier-stability}. It states that transported packets still read only the displayed coordinates $S,R,G,L,H,C,P,N$.

Finally compare the refusal gate with \autoref{thm:apophatic-socket-gate-correspondence} and the gate-question boundary \autoref{thm:apophatic-gate-question-visible-socket-nonescape}.
