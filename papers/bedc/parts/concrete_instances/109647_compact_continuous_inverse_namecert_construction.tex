\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$}

Following Kelley and Bishop--Bridges on compact Hausdorff inverse-continuity arguments, the $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$ packet records the finite BEDC surface by which a continuous bijection from a compact finite-net source into a separated target receives a displayed inverse-continuity handoff. It sits over the compact carrier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-compact-namecert}, the continuous-map graph of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-continuousmap-namecert}, the separated target boundary of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-separatedmetric-namecert}, and the uniform-homeomorphism consumer of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-uniformhomeomorphism-namecert}. The packet names only finite-net inverse readback; it does not import open-cover compactness, quotient equality of maps, host inverse functions, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness, countable choice, or a global topological inverse theorem.

\begin{definition}[Compact continuous inverse carrier]
\label{def:compact-continuous-inverse-carrier}
A $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
I=(K,F,B,S,G,M,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $K$ is the $\CompactUp$ finite-net source row, exposing the compact packet only through named finite covers and source-window witnesses.
\item $F$ is the $\ContinuousMapUp$ graph row, recording the forward graph and its local continuity data over the displayed compact source.
\item $B$ is the bijection ledger, recording graph-totality, graph-single-valuedness, target coverage, and source uniqueness for the same displayed map row.
\item $S$ is the $\SeparatedMetricUp$ target-separation row, or the corresponding Hausdorff separation read when the target is supplied through $\HausdorffSpaceUp$.
\item $G$ is the inverse graph readback row: a target observation reads a unique source window only through $B$ and then returns to the forward graph row $F$.
\item $M$ is the finite-net modulus extraction row, turning the compact source row $K$, graph row $F$, bijection ledger $B$, and separation row $S$ into a named inverse-continuity tolerance window.
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for the compact, graph, bijection, separation, inverse-readback, and modulus rows.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay from a target-window request through $G$ and $M$ back to the same finite source-net route.
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$, $\CompactUp$, $\ContinuousMapUp$, $\SeparatedMetricUp$, $\HausdorffSpaceUp$, $\UniformHomeomorphismUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\CompactContinuousInverseUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for an ambient host inverse function, quotient equality of maps, open-cover compactness, selected representatives, countable choice, or ambient $\RealUp$ completeness.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Compact continuous inverse NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:compact-continuous-inverse-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$ carrier $I=(K,F,B,S,G,M,H,C,P,N)$, the local $\NameCert_{\CompactContinuousInverseUp}$ surface consists exactly of carrier admission for the displayed compact source, continuous graph, bijection ledger, separated target, inverse graph readback, finite-net modulus, hsame transport, Cont replay, provenance, and local naming rows. Every inverse-continuity read must inspect $K,F,B,S$ before $G$ and $M$ are available, and every transport or replay read must preserve the same ordered route through $H,C,P,N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:compact-continuous-inverse-carrier}. The rows $K,F,B,S$ display the compact source, forward graph, bijection data, and target separation before any inverse read is admitted.

The inverse graph row $G$ is therefore a readback of the same graph through the bijection ledger, not a host inverse function. The finite-net modulus row $M$ consumes the compact finite-net row and the separated target row after the graph has been fixed.

Finally $H,C,P,N$ transport, replay, source, and name exactly that route. Since the carrier has no additional inverse, quotient-map equality, selected representative, choice, open-cover, or ambient-completeness coordinate, the stated rows exhaust the local NameCert obligations.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Compact continuous inverse uniform handoff]
\label{thm:compact-continuous-inverse-uniform-handoff}
Any $\UniformHomeomorphismUp$ or inverse-continuity consumer that reads an accepted $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$ packet must factor its read through
$$
\CompactUp,\ \ContinuousMapUp,\ \SeparatedMetricUp,\ \BHist,\ \hsame,\ \Cont,\ \Pkg,\ \NameCert .
$$
The exported handoff is the finite-net modulus row $M$ together with the inverse graph readback row $G$; it is not an open-cover compactness theorem, a quotient equality of functions, a selected host inverse, a countable-choice construction, or an ambient $\RealUp$ completeness claim.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with an accepted consumer read. The carrier admits only $K,F,B,S,G,M,H,C,P,N$, so the source compactness and forward graph data must be visible before the inverse graph is read.

The row $G$ turns target observations back into source windows through the bijection ledger. The row $M$ then extracts the finite-net modulus from the compact source and separated target route.

Transport and replay preserve that same route through $H$ and $C$, while $P$ and $N$ attach provenance and local naming. The consumer may use the resulting modulus as a uniform-homeomorphism handoff, but it receives no extra host inverse, quotient equality, open-cover compactness, choice, or real-completeness principle.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Compact continuous inverse finite-net readback]
\label{thm:compact-continuous-inverse-finite-net-readback}
Let $I=(K,F,B,S,G,M,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\CompactContinuousInverseUp$ carrier. Every target-neighbourhood request for inverse continuity is answered by first reading the inverse graph row $G$, then the finite-net modulus row $M$, and then replaying the answer through $C$ over the same $K,F,B,S$ dependency route.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The request enters through the separated target row $S$ and the inverse graph readback row $G$. The graph readback uses the bijection ledger $B$ and the forward graph row $F$, so it cannot select a host inverse independently of the displayed map.

The finite-net modulus row $M$ then uses the compact source row $K$ to extract a finite source-window response. This is the only modulus coordinate in the packet.

The replay row $C$ returns the answer along the same ordered rows, and $H,P,N$ preserve the route under transport, provenance, and local naming. No open-cover, quotient-equality, selected-representative, choice, or ambient-completeness read is available.
\end{proof}
