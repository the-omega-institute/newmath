\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\TreeUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-tree-namecert}

The tree interface $\TreeUp$ packages a connected acyclic $\NameCert_{\GraphUp}$ (\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-graph-namecert}) together with a designated root or root-free witness. It is the constructive substrate for inductive carriers and for the syntactic representation of derivations.

\section{Carrier definition}
\label{sec:tree-carrier}

\begin{theorem}[TreeUp graph-source carrier obligation]
\label{thm:tree-graph-source-carrier-obligation}
The carrier for $\TreeUp$ is a $\mathsf{BHist}$ predicate read through the
existing $\GraphUp$ source. An accepted tree history exposes a carried
$\GraphUp$ source row, a finite vertex-and-edge incidence ledger over
$\mathsf{BHist}$ endpoints, and a classifier comparison inherited from
$\hsame$ and the $\GraphUp$ classifier rows. The carrier does not identify
host graphs by equality; every comparison is recorded as a displayed
classifier row over the accepted histories and their incidence ledger.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack an accepted tree history as a graph-source presentation. The first
projection is the $\GraphUp$ carrier row, so each visible vertex and edge is
read as a carried $\mathsf{BHist}$ endpoint rather than as a host-level graph
object.

The second projection is the finite incidence ledger. It lists the displayed
vertex endpoints, displayed edge endpoints, and the source-target incidence
rows that attach each edge to carried vertices. Finiteness belongs to this
ledger, so no quotient over ambient graphs is introduced.

The classifier projection is inherited rowwise. Endpoint movement is
componentwise $\hsame$, and graph-source movement is the comparison supplied
by $\GraphUp$. Transporting the finite incidence ledger along those rows gives
the same accepted tree carrier predicate at the target history, with no
appeal to equality of host graphs.
\end{proof}

\section{The certificate}
\label{sec:tree-certificate}

\begin{theorem}[TreeUp connectedness obligation]
\label{thm:tree-connectedness-obligation}
For every accepted $\TreeUp$ history and every carried endpoint displayed by
its $\GraphUp$ source, the certificate supplies a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ path
row through the $\GraphUp$ edge ledger from that endpoint to the selected root
component. The path row records each intermediate endpoint, each traversed
edge incidence, and the classifier comparison that carries the path under
$\hsame$ transport.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the endpoint from the graph-source carrier row supplied by
\autoref{thm:tree-graph-source-carrier-obligation}. The finite incidence
ledger gives the only admissible edges and their source-target endpoint rows.

The connectedness witness is a finite path ledger indexed by those admissible
edges. Its first endpoint is the displayed carried endpoint, its last endpoint
lies in the selected root component, and every adjacent step is justified by
one incidence row from the $\GraphUp$ ledger.

Transport the path by applying componentwise $\hsame$ to the endpoint rows and
the inherited $\GraphUp$ classifier to the edge rows. The transported ledger
has the same finite shape, so it remains a path from the transported endpoint
to the transported root component.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[TreeUp acyclicity obligation]
\label{thm:tree-acyclicity-obligation}
For an accepted $\TreeUp$ history, every closed finite edge walk in the
$\GraphUp$ edge ledger collapses under the $\TreeUp$ classifier to the
trivial endpoint row. Equivalently, the certificate records that the only
closed walk recognized by the tree classifier is the displayed stationary
walk at its endpoint, and this recognition is stable under $\hsame$ transport.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with a closed finite edge walk in the incidence ledger of
\autoref{thm:tree-graph-source-carrier-obligation}. Each step is a
$\GraphUp$ edge row whose endpoints are carried $\mathsf{BHist}$ histories,
and the closing condition is itself a displayed classifier row.

Apply the acyclicity witness carried by the $\TreeUp$ certificate. It reduces
the closed walk ledger to the stationary endpoint row in the $\TreeUp$
classifier, so the edge sequence contributes no nontrivial cycle to the
accepted carrier.

Stability follows by transporting the whole walk ledger componentwise through
$\hsame$ and through the inherited $\GraphUp$ classifier. The acyclicity
witness reads the transported closed walk and again returns the stationary
endpoint row at the transported endpoint.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[TreeUp root-or-root-free witness obligation]
\label{thm:tree-root-or-root-free-witness-obligation}
The $\TreeUp$ certificate supplies either a displayed root endpoint carried by
the $\GraphUp$ source or a root-free witness whose classifier is stable under
$\hsame$. In the rooted case the root endpoint is one of the carried
vertices used by the connectedness path rows. In the root-free case the
witness records that connectedness is read componentwise without selecting a
distinguished endpoint, and this record transports along the same
$\TreeUp$ classifier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Inspect the root row of the certificate. If the row is rooted, it displays a
$\mathsf{BHist}$ endpoint and a proof that this endpoint is carried by the
$\GraphUp$ source ledger. The connectedness paths then target the component
containing that displayed root.

If the row is root-free, the certificate instead displays a witness that no
distinguished endpoint is part of the public tree data. Connectedness is then
read by component rows, and the witness itself is attached to the
$\TreeUp$ classifier rather than to a host graph equality.

In both cases, transport is rowwise. The rooted endpoint moves by $\hsame$
and the inherited graph classifier, while the root-free witness moves by the
stability field attached to the $\TreeUp$ classifier. Thus the certificate
retains the same rooted or root-free form after transport.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[TreeUp NameCert obligation surface]
\label{thm:tree-namecert-obligation-surface}
The carrier row of
\autoref{thm:tree-graph-source-carrier-obligation}, the connectedness row of
\autoref{thm:tree-connectedness-obligation}, the acyclicity row of
\autoref{thm:tree-acyclicity-obligation}, and the root-or-root-free row of
\autoref{thm:tree-root-or-root-free-witness-obligation} assemble the
paper-side $\NameCert_{\TreeUp}$ obligation surface. The surface names the
stability, ledger, exactness, and dependency fields required for the certified
tree interface: stability is $\hsame$ and inherited $\GraphUp$ transport;
ledger data are finite vertex, edge, incidence, path, closed-walk, and root
rows; exactness says every public tree row is read from those ledgers; and
dependencies are only the $\mathsf{BHist}$ substrate, $\hsame$, and the
$\GraphUp$ source certificate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the graph-source carrier obligation. It supplies the accepted
$\mathsf{BHist}$ predicate, the inherited $\GraphUp$ classifier, and the
finite incidence ledger for visible vertices and edges.

Add the connectedness obligation. Its finite path rows show that every
carried endpoint reaches the selected root component through the same edge
ledger, and its transport clause supplies the corresponding stability row.

Add the acyclicity and root-or-root-free obligations. Acyclicity gives the
closed-walk exactness clause, while the final witness records whether the
certificate is rooted or root-free and how that row transports under
$\hsame$.

These projections cover the named carrier, classifier, stability, ledger,
exactness, and dependency rows. Since every row is read from
$\mathsf{BHist}$, $\hsame$, and the $\GraphUp$ source certificate, the
assembled surface does not require host graph equality or any additional
bridge field.
\end{proof}
