\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\AuditMapInterfaceUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-auditmapinterface-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\AuditMapInterfaceUp$ packet realizes the audit-map family methodology of \autoref{ch:audit-map-family-methodology} as a finite interface rather than as a prose convention. It sits beside the kernel acceptance trace of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-kernelacceptancetrace-namecert}, whose rows expose accepted theorem inventory, and the substitution audit map of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-substitutionauditmap-namecert}, whose rows show how a local MetaCIC substrate is kept separate from stronger typing claims. The present packet names the common interface consumed by audit-map ledgers, synthesis packets, and cross-map chapters.

\begin{definition}[Audit-map interface carrier]
\label{def:audit-map-interface-carrier}
An $\AuditMapInterfaceUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
I=(E,K,O,F,X,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $E$ is the established theorem inventory row, listing the positive results that the local audit map may cite directly;
\item $K$ is the conditional theorem inventory row, listing results available only through named discharge, setup, or route data;
\item $O$ is the obstruction row, recording no-go, boundary, or shape facts that block a stronger local reading;
\item $F$ is the frontier row, recording the strongest adjacent theorem or package that remains outside the local map;
\item $X$ is the cross-map consumer row, naming neighbouring audit maps, synthesis packets, and route chapters that may consume the visible rows;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for the established, conditional, obstruction, frontier, cross-map, provenance, and naming rows;
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ routes by which consumers read the positive inventory, conditional inventory, obstruction boundary, frontier handoff, cross-map use, provenance, and local name;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over \autoref{ch:audit-map-family-methodology}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-kernelacceptancetrace-namecert}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-substitutionauditmap-namecert}, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\AuditMapInterfaceUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for turning conditional rows into unconditional theorems, obstruction rows into solved obligations, frontier rows into closed packages, or cross-map consumer rows into hidden module imports.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Audit-map interface NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:audit-map-interface-namecert-obligations}
For the carrier of \autoref{def:audit-map-interface-carrier}, the local $\NameCert_{\AuditMapInterfaceUp}$ surface consists of five obligations: carrier admission for
$$
I=(E,K,O,F,X,H,C,P,N),
$$
inventory exactness for the established, conditional, obstruction, frontier, and cross-map rows $E,K,O,F,X$, componentwise transport stability through $H$, displayed route readback through $C$, and package provenance with local naming through $P,N$.
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.AuditMapInterfaceUp.AuditMapInterfaceCarrier\_namecert\_obligations}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is the displayed finite packet. Its first five coordinates are the only audit-map row classes, so inventory exactness is a projection of $E,K,O,F,X$ from the carrier.

Transport stability is componentwise. The row $H$ may compare an established row with an established row, a conditional row with a conditional row, and similarly for obstruction, frontier, cross-map, provenance, and naming data; it supplies no cross-class coercion.

Route readback is then forced through $C$. A consumer must choose one of the displayed routes before using a row, and the route returns to the same inventory coordinate that it reads.

Finally, $P$ and $N$ bind provenance and local naming. Since provenance cites only the visible methodology and sibling concrete packets, the five obligations exhaust the NameCert surface without adding a stronger theorem coordinate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Audit-map interface cross-map consumer boundary]
\label{thm:audit-map-interface-cross-map-consumer-boundary}
Every cross-map consumer of an accepted $\AuditMapInterfaceUp$ carrier
$$
I=(E,K,O,F,X,H,C,P,N)
$$
must read the positive inventory $E$, conditional inventory $K$, obstruction row $O$, frontier row $F$, and cross-map consumer row $X$ through the displayed transport, continuation, provenance, and naming rows $H,C,P,N$. Such a consumer cannot turn the interface into an unconditional theorem, a discharged obstruction, a closed frontier package, a hidden import of a neighbouring map, or a host semantic equality.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the NameCert obligations. They identify the accepted interface with the finite rows $E,K,O,F,X,H,C,P,N$ and force each consumer read through the displayed route row $C$.

For a positive read, the consumer obtains exactly the established inventory row $E$. For a conditional read, it obtains the conditional row $K$ together with its visible route requirement. For an obstruction read, it obtains the boundary row $O$ rather than a proof of the blocked claim.

For a frontier or cross-map read, the consumer follows $F$ or $X$ through the same transport and provenance rows. Those rows name neighbouring maps and handoffs; they do not import their hidden contents or solve their obligations.

The carrier has no coordinate for unconditional discharge, solved obstruction, closed frontier package, hidden module import, or host semantic equality. Therefore every cross-map use remains an interface read and cannot become a stronger theorem.
\end{proof}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.AuditMapInterfaceUp.AuditMapInterfaceCarrier\_cross\_map\_consumer\_boundary}
