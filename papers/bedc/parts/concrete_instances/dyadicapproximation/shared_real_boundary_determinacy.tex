\begin{theorem}[DyadicApproximation shared Real-boundary determinacy]
\label{thm:dyadicapproximation-shared-real-boundary-determinacy}
Let $A$ and $A'$ be accepted $\DyadicApproximationUp$ finite packets. Suppose
the two packets display the same precision row, transported dyadic endpoint,
rational-interval cell, $\DyadicMeshUp$ level and index rows,
$\ValidatedNumericsUp$ containment ledger, $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback window,
$\RealUp$ readback token, visible $\hsame$ transports, visible $\Cont$
routes, $\Pkg$ provenance, and local $\NameCert_{\DyadicApproximationUp}$,
$\NameCert_{\DyadicMeshUp}$, and $\NameCert_{\ValidatedNumericsUp}$ rows.

Then every mesh-cell consumer, validated-enclosure consumer, and terminal
dyadic finite-window consumer that reads either packet through the displayed
BEDC rows reads the same finite $\RegSeqRatUp/\RealUp$ boundary surface. The
surface is exactly the shared precision row, transported endpoint,
rational-interval cell, mesh level, mesh index, containment ledger,
regular-rational window, real-readback token, visible transport rows,
continuation routes, package provenance, and local name-certificate rows.

The determinacy is finite. It does not assert completed real equality,
quotient-real equality, solver correctness, floating-point semantics, or
classical completeness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First compare the two dyadic packets before any downstream consumer is read.
Apply
\autoref{thm:dyadicapproximation-refined-finite-packet-readback-determinacy}
to $A$ and $A'$. The hypotheses identify precisely the displayed precision,
endpoint, regular-rational, real-readback, mesh-and-validated handoff,
transport, continuation, provenance, and local certificate rows available to
both packets.

Next put each packet together with its mesh cell and validated enclosure in
normal form. Apply
\autoref{thm:dyadicapproximation-dyadicmesh-validatednumerics-consumer-normal-form}
to the accepted packet, the displayed mesh cell, and the validated enclosure
over the same rational-interval cell and containment ledger. The normal form
has no coordinate beyond the rows listed in the statement.

Now identify the two routes that consumers may use. Apply
\autoref{thm:dyadicapproximation-validated-enclosure-route-commutation} to
compare the terminal dyadic finite-window route with the route through the
mesh cell and validated enclosure. Apply
\autoref{thm:dyadicapproximation-mesh-validated-real-boundary} to expose the
shared Real-boundary surface carried by that comparison.

Finally restrict mesh and enclosure reads to the finite packet boundary. Apply
\autoref{thm:dyadicapproximation-finite-mesh-enclosure-exactness}. Its
factorization says that every accepted mesh-cell or validated-enclosure read
passes through the same finite $\RealUp$ surface already identified above.

Combining the refined packet determinacy, the three-packet normal form, route
commutation, shared Real-boundary comparison, and finite mesh-enclosure
exactness leaves a single displayed BEDC surface for every listed consumer.
Since each cited boundary is a finite packet boundary, the conclusion exports
no completed real equality, quotient-real equality, solver correctness,
floating-point semantics, or classical completeness.
\end{proof}
