\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-supplysocketledger-namecert}
\origin{ai}

The $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ surface is the concrete NameCert ledger for the seven $\mathsf{T}$-supply socket kinds of \autoref{ch:visions-transcendental-supply-taxonomy}. It records which kind is being exposed, which site consumes the request, which audit gate governs the refusal, and which provenance and continuation rows make the refusal available to later concrete-instance chapters. The ledger is a finite classifier over displayed rows; it is not a hidden source of the requested supply.

The socket-site row $S$ is the ledger projection of the supply socket exposed by \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-transcendentalinductionsocket-namecert}, the audit row $A$ consumes the admission and refusal discipline of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-tastegateadmission-namecert}, and downstream public-record consumers such as \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-inscriptionevent-namecert} may cite only the displayed GAP, kind, site, audit, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows.

\begin{definition}[SupplySocketLedger carrier]
\label{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}
A $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $G$ is the $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag row naming the boundary socket under inspection;
\item $K$ is one of the seven supply kinds: free-floating induction, unwitnessed witness, extensional identification, causal continuity, inter-history coordination, inscription novelty, or self-reference closure;
\item $S$ is the displayed socket site, recording where the requested supply would be consumed and which concrete theorem, definition, audit gate, or consumer route would use it;
\item $A$ names the audit gate that refuses silent internalisation of the requested supply kind;
\item $C$ records the $\Cont$ consumption routes by which downstream consumers read $G$, $K$, $S$, and $A$ without obtaining the missing supply;
\item $P$ records $\Pkg$ provenance over $\BHist$, the $\mathsf{GAP}$ row, the seven supply kinds, socket sites, audit gates, $\Cont$ routes, and the local naming surface;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for an unrestricted induction principle, selected witness, quotient equality, empirical persistence law, shared host history, inscription source, or truth-total self-description.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations}
For the carrier of \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}, the local $\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ surface consists exactly of carrier admission for
$$
L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N),
$$
classifier exposure for the row $K$, kind exactness against the seven displayed supply kinds, audit-gate routing through $A$, continuation locality through $C$, package provenance through $P$, local naming through $N$, and ledger non-escape for any requested supply not present in the finite packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}. The packet exposes the $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag, supply-kind row, socket site, audit gate, continuation routes, provenance, and local naming row as its complete visible inventory.

Classifier and kind exactness are read from $K$. The row is required to be one of the seven displayed supply kinds, and the NameCert surface accepts no classifier outside that finite list.

Audit routing and consumer locality are the rows $A$ and $C$. They say which gate blocks the request and how downstream consumers may inspect the blocked route without replacing it by an internal theorem.

Provenance and local naming are supplied by $P$ and $N$. Since the carrier inventory contains no row for the requested supply itself, the NameCert obligations expose the socket while preserving ledger non-escape.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger kind exhaustion]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-kind-exhaustion}
Every accepted $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ row classifies its socket as exactly one of the seven displayed supply kinds: free-floating induction, unwitnessed witness, extensional identification, causal continuity, inter-history coordination, inscription novelty, or self-reference closure. The ledger exports no eighth hidden kind, no unlisted classifier branch, and no catch-all route that can discharge a $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag without passing through one of these seven rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$. The only kind coordinate is $K$, and \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier} restricts that coordinate to the seven named supply kinds.

Apply the NameCert obligations of \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations}. They make kind exactness a certificate row rather than a prose convention, so consumer access to the socket classifier must pass through the displayed finite list.

The absence claim follows from the carrier inventory. No coordinate names an additional kind, a default classifier branch, or a discharge route outside the seven rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger audit non-escape]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape}
The $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ packet records refusal of $\mathsf{Classical.choice}$, $\mathsf{Quot.sound}$, $\mathsf{propext}$, host equality, unbounded induction, and truth-total self-description as explicit socket rows rather than as substrate theorems. Each refusal is visible only through its $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag, supply-kind classifier, socket site, audit gate, continuation route, package provenance, and local NameCert row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read each refusal through the carrier coordinates. The $\mathsf{GAP}$ row names the boundary, the kind row classifies the requested supply, the site row says where the request would be consumed, and the audit row names the gate that keeps the request external.

Continuation and provenance then preserve the same boundary. The $\Cont$ routes let consumers inspect the refusal, while $P$ records that the route belongs to the finite ledger over $\BHist$ and local naming data.

No substrate theorem is produced by these rows. The items $\mathsf{Classical.choice}$, $\mathsf{Quot.sound}$, $\mathsf{propext}$, host equality, unbounded induction, and truth-total self-description are recorded as refused socket requests, and no coordinate in $L$ turns them into available BEDC principles.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger TasteGate carrier boundary]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-tastegate-carrier-boundary}
The local $\ChapterTasteGate$-relevant carrier boundary for $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ is exactly the displayed finite packet
$$
L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N).
$$
It exposes the $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag row, supply-kind row, socket site, audit gate, $\Cont$ consumption routes, $\Pkg$ provenance, and local $\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row, and it contains no coordinate carrying the refused supply itself.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}. Its complete displayed inventory is the seven-tuple $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$, so the TasteGate-relevant boundary has precisely those coordinates.

Apply \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations}. The NameCert surface reads carrier admission, classifier exposure, audit routing, continuation locality, package provenance, local naming, and ledger non-escape from the same seven rows.

Finally use \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape}. Refused supplies are represented as socket requests behind the displayed audit rows, not as coordinates of $L$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger route coverage]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-route-coverage}
Every consumer route that reads a $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ supply-kind row factors through the displayed $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag $G$, socket site $S$, audit gate $A$, and $\Cont$ routes $C$. No consumer can reach the classifier row $K$ through an unnamed route, a hidden eighth kind, or a route that bypasses the audit gate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with an accepted packet $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$. By \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}, the only classifier coordinate is $K$, and the carrier displays the boundary tag, socket site, audit gate, and continuation rows beside it.

Apply \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-kind-exhaustion}. Any read of $K$ lands in one of the seven displayed supply kinds, with no hidden classifier branch available to the consumer.

The route factorisation then follows from \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations}: audit-gate routing is through $A$ and continuation locality is through $C$. The non-escape result of \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape} rules out replacing that factored route by the refused supply itself.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger consumer provenance]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-consumer-provenance}
Downstream concrete-instance chapters may cite a $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ packet only through the displayed $\Pkg$ provenance row $P$ and the local $\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row $N$, together with the visible classifier, socket, audit, and continuation rows that they certify. They may not cite the requested supply as a produced theorem, selected witness, quotient equality, persistence law, shared host history, inscription source, or truth-total self-description.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the provenance and naming rows from \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}. The rows $P$ and $N$ are the only coordinates whose role is to make the ledger citeable by later concrete-instance chapters.

Use \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations} to identify what those rows certify: the finite carrier, the supply-kind classifier, audit-gate routing, continuation locality, package provenance, local naming, and ledger non-escape.

The forbidden citations have no carrier coordinate. By \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape}, they remain refused socket requests visible through the ledger surface, so a downstream chapter may cite the provenance and NameCert rows but not the missing supply.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger kind-route determinacy]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-kind-route-determinacy}
For an accepted $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ packet $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$, the
$\mathsf{BHist}$ row $K$ determines the visible socket-kind route consumed by
any $\Cont$ read of the ledger. The route is read with the socket-site row
$S$, audit row $A$, $\Pkg$ provenance row $P$, and local
$\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row $N$; no second route can classify the
same accepted packet as a different supply kind.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}. The packet
has one kind coordinate $K$, one socket-site row $S$, one audit row $A$, one
family of consumption routes $C$, one provenance row $P$, and one local naming
row $N$.

Apply \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-kind-exhaustion} to the projected
kind row. Since $K$ ranges over the seven displayed supply kinds and the
carrier has no further classifier coordinate, a consumer route cannot obtain a
different kind without leaving the packet.

Finally use \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-route-coverage}. Every
classifier read factors through the displayed $\Cont$ routes and audit gate,
so the route determined by $K$ is the only route certified by $P$ and $N$ for
the accepted row.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger refused-row non-escape]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-refused-row-nonescape}
If a $\mathsf{BHist}$ supply row is outside the displayed
$\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ packet $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$, then no consumer can read
it through the local ledger. The only readable refusal data are the displayed
$G,K,S,A$ rows, their $\Cont$ consumption routes $C$, the $\Pkg$ provenance
row $P$, and the local $\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row $N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start from the inventory in \autoref{def:supply-socket-ledger-carrier}. A row
outside $G,K,S,A,C,P,N$ is absent from the finite packet and therefore is not a
coordinate that a local consumer can project.

Apply \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape}. The refused
principles and host supplies remain recorded as socket requests behind the
displayed audit row; they are not converted into substrate theorems or
available carrier fields.

The NameCert obligations then preserve the boundary. By
\autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-namecert-obligations}, provenance and local
naming certify exactly the displayed refusal rows and their $\Cont$ routes, so
an outside supply row has no certified path into the ledger.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[SupplySocketLedger consumption coverage]
\label{thm:supply-socket-ledger-consumption-coverage}
Every accepted $\SupplySocketLedgerUp$ socket route consumed by a downstream
chapter is covered by the displayed $\mathsf{BHist}$ rows of the same packet:
the $\mathsf{GAP}$ tag $G$, socket-kind row $K$, socket-site row $S$, audit row
$A$, $\Cont$ routes $C$, $\Pkg$ provenance row $P$, and local
$\NameCert_{\SupplySocketLedgerUp}$ row $N$. No accepted route may cite the
ledger while omitting the local NameCert row or bypassing provenance.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project an accepted packet $L=(G,K,S,A,C,P,N)$. The displayed carrier gives
the complete list of rows from which a consumer may read a socket route.

Use \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-route-coverage} for reads of the
classifier and \autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-consumer-provenance} for
downstream citations. Together they force each accepted consumption route to
pass through $C$, cite $P$, and retain the local naming row $N$.

It remains to rule out partial consumption. The non-escape result of
\autoref{thm:supply-socket-ledger-audit-nonescape} says that bypassing the
audit or provenance rows would replace the displayed refusal by the refused
supply itself, which is not a coordinate of the accepted packet.
\end{proof}
