\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\UnitDiskUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-unitdisk-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\UnitDiskUp$ interface names the closed real unit-disk carrier as a finite BEDC packet assembled from real coordinate names, the product pairing surface, metric radius reads, interval or real-order bounds, and the boundary consumer already available to $\SOneUp$. In the standard topology text of Munkres, Topology, the closed unit disk and its boundary circle are read as the familiar Euclidean disk and unit circle; this chapter records only the finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ certificate surface used by BEDC consumers.

\begin{definition}[UnitDisk BHist carrier]
\label{def:unitdisk-bhist-carrier}
A $\UnitDiskUp$ carrier is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
U=(x,y,p,o,d,b,\partial,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $x$ and $y$ are two $\RealUp$ coordinate rows, each read through its own finite real-name seal;
\item $p$ is the $\ProdUp$ pair row joining the two coordinate rows as one displayed planar point;
\item $o$ is the origin row, formed from the two zero $\RealUp$ rows and paired through the same $\ProdUp$ surface;
\item $d$ is the $\MetricUp$ radius-distance witness reading the distance from $p$ to $o$ through the displayed metric route;
\item $b$ is a nonnegative bound row, supplied either by $\IntervalUp$ or by $\RealAlgOrderUp$, witnessing that the displayed radius is at most the real unit row;
\item $\partial$ is an optional boundary row recording the exact unit-radius packet consumed by $\SOneUp$ when the metric read is classified as boundary;
\item $H$ and $C$ are the componentwise $\hsame$ witnesses and displayed $\Cont$ routes for coordinate, pair, origin, radius, bound, and optional boundary transport;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance naming the consumed $\RealUp$, $\ProdUp$, $\MetricUp$, $\IntervalUp$, $\RealAlgOrderUp$, and $\SOneUp$ source surfaces;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\UnitDiskUp}$ provenance row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier classifier compares two accepted packets only by transporting these displayed rows componentwise through $H$ and $C$. It has no coordinate for host Euclidean topology, open-cover compactness, quotient boundary identification, or a host subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[UnitDisk carrier stability]
\label{thm:unitdisk-carrier-stability}
Let $U=(x,y,p,o,d,b,\partial,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\UnitDiskUp$ carrier, and let $U'=(x',y',p',o',d',b',\partial',H',C',P',N')$ be obtained by componentwise displayed $\hsame$ transport of the two $\RealUp$ coordinate rows, the $\ProdUp$ point row, the origin row, the $\MetricUp$ radius witness, the $\IntervalUp$ or $\RealAlgOrderUp$ bound row, the optional $\SOneUp$ boundary row, and the provenance rows. If the displayed $\Cont$ routes preserve the coordinate, pairing, metric-read, bound, and boundary ledgers, then $U'$ is again an accepted $\UnitDiskUp$ carrier and the local classifier relates $U$ to $U'$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier through \autoref{def:unitdisk-bhist-carrier}. The two real coordinates, product pair, origin pair, radius witness, bound row, optional boundary row, routing rows, and provenance rows are all explicit finite packet coordinates.

Transport each coordinate by the displayed $\hsame$ witnesses, then repack the transported coordinates through the same $\ProdUp$ pairing route. The origin row is transported in parallel, so the metric witness still reads a radius from the transported point to the transported origin.

Use the stated $\Cont$ routes to move the radius comparison into the transported $\IntervalUp$ or $\RealAlgOrderUp$ bound row. If a boundary row is present, the same transport carries it to the optional $\SOneUp$ consumer row; if it is absent, no boundary consumer is exported.

Repacking these rows gives the accepted packet $U'$. Since the classifier inspects exactly the displayed rows, the transported packet is classified with $U$ and no host topological coordinate is created.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[UnitDisk boundary SOne consumer]
\label{thm:unitdisk-boundary-sone-consumer}
For every accepted $\UnitDiskUp$ carrier whose optional boundary row $\partial$ is present, a downstream $\SOneUp$ consumer may read only the two $\RealUp$ coordinate rows, their $\ProdUp$ pair row, the $\MetricUp$ unit-radius witness from the origin, the exact boundary ledger carried by $\partial$, and the associated $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ provenance.

The consumer boundary does not export open-cover compactness, quotient boundary identifications, host Euclidean topology, or an ambient subset construction for the disk.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the packet through \autoref{def:unitdisk-bhist-carrier}. The boundary coordinate is optional and, when present, is the only row routed to $\SOneUp$.

The $\SOneUp$ consumer receives the coordinate pair through $\ProdUp$ and the radius equality through the same $\MetricUp$ witness used by the disk packet. The bound row confirms that the radius sits at the displayed real unit; the boundary row records exact unit-radius consumption rather than a separate host circle.

Apply \autoref{thm:unitdisk-carrier-stability} to see that these rows are stable under the displayed componentwise classifier. The exported boundary surface is therefore the finite row $\partial$ with its provenance, not a quotient of boundary points or a topological subspace.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[UnitDisk NameCert obligation surface]
\label{thm:unitdisk-namecert-obligation-surface}
The local $\NameCert_{\UnitDiskUp}$ obligation surface consists of five finite rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier inhabitation: the origin row together with the two zero $\RealUp$ coordinate rows, their $\ProdUp$ pair row, the zero $\MetricUp$ radius read, and the unit bound row inhabit the carrier of \autoref{def:unitdisk-bhist-carrier};
\item classifier laws: reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity are inherited componentwise from the displayed $\hsame$ comparisons on coordinates, product pairs, origin rows, metric witnesses, bounds, optional boundary rows, routing rows, and provenance;
\item carrier transport: \autoref{thm:unitdisk-carrier-stability} transports accepted packets along the displayed $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ rows without adding a host point set;
\item exact ledger reads: every public radius read factors through the $\MetricUp$ row $d$ and every boundary read factors through the optional row $\partial$ of the same carrier;
\item consumer boundary: \autoref{thm:unitdisk-boundary-sone-consumer} permits $\SOneUp$ consumption of exact boundary packets while excluding open-cover compactness, quotient boundary identifications, and host Euclidean topology.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The carrier row is \autoref{def:unitdisk-bhist-carrier}. Its origin coordinate gives an accepted packet by taking both real coordinates to be the displayed zero real row, pairing them through $\ProdUp$, reading zero radius through $\MetricUp$, and comparing zero to the real unit through the displayed nonnegative bound surface.

The classifier row is componentwise. Reflexivity uses each displayed row as itself, symmetry reverses the componentwise $\hsame$ witnesses, and transitivity composes them across the same coordinate, pair, origin, radius, bound, boundary, routing, and provenance positions.

Carrier transport is exactly \autoref{thm:unitdisk-carrier-stability}. It keeps the metric and bound rows tied to the transported coordinate pair and origin row, so an accepted disk packet remains inside the same finite carrier surface.

For ledger exactness, inspect the packet coordinates. The only radius coordinate is $d$, and the only boundary coordinate is $\partial$ when that row is present. A consumer can therefore read radius and boundary data only through those displayed rows and their provenance.

Finally apply \autoref{thm:unitdisk-boundary-sone-consumer}. The optional boundary handoff is a finite $\SOneUp$ consumer surface and contains no open-cover compactness datum, quotient boundary construction, or host Euclidean topology.
\end{proof}
