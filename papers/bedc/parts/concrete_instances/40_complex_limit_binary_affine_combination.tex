\begin{definition}[Pointwise binary affine combination of complex sequences]
\label{def:cplx-pointwise-binary-affine-combination}
For fixed complex histories $a,b$ and complex sequences $s,t:\Unary\to\ComplexUp$, the pointwise binary affine combination $a\cdot s+b\cdot t$ is the unary-indexed sequence $(a\cdot s+b\cdot t)(n):=(a\cdot s(n))\oplus(b\cdot t(n))$. Here the scalar products are the componentwise complex products used in \autoref{thm:cplx-lim-scalar}, and $\oplus$ is the pointwise sum of \autoref{def:cplx-pointwise-sum}.
\end{definition}
\leandef{BEDC.Derived.ComplexLimitUp.ComplexPointwiseBinaryAffineCombination}

\begin{theorem}[Complex limit respects binary affine combinations]
\label{thm:cplx-lim-binary-affine-combination}
Let $a,b$ be fixed complex histories with unary modulus bounds $A,B$, so $\mathsf{CplxMod}(a,A)$ and $\mathsf{CplxMod}(b,B)$. Let $\sigma_{ab}:=\log_2(A+B+1)$ be the standard unary scalar shift for the combined bound $A+B$. Suppose $\mathsf{CplxLim}(s,N_s,z_s,M_s)$ and $\mathsf{CplxLim}(t,N_t,z_t,M_t)$ hold. Define pasted shifted moduli by $N_{ab}(k):=\max\bigl(N_s(k+1+\sigma_{ab}),N_t(k+1+\sigma_{ab})\bigr)$ and $M_{ab}(k):=\max\bigl(M_s(k+1+\sigma_{ab}),M_t(k+1+\sigma_{ab})\bigr)$. Then the binary affine combination has the displayed complex limit:
$$
\mathsf{CplxLim}(a\cdot s+b\cdot t,N_{ab},a\cdot z_s+b\cdot z_t,M_{ab}).
$$
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The proof packages the scalar estimate of \autoref{thm:cplx-lim-scalar} with the sum-pasting mechanism of \autoref{thm:cplx-lim-sum}. Unpack the two limit hypotheses by \autoref{def:cplx-lim}. Their regularity fields give $\mathsf{CplxRegSeq}(s,N_s)$ and $\mathsf{CplxRegSeq}(t,N_t)$, and their convergence fields give the distance estimates from $s(n)$ to $z_s$ and from $t(n)$ to $z_t$ above the displayed moduli. The target point $a\cdot z_s+b\cdot z_t$ is a complex history by the componentwise complex carrier of \autoref{def:complex-carrier}, the scalar-product carrier used in \autoref{thm:cplx-lim-scalar}, and the sum carrier used in \autoref{def:cplx-pointwise-sum}.
First verify regularity. Fix a unary precision $k$ and unary indices $n,m$ accepted above $N_{ab}(k)$ in the sense of \autoref{def:cplx-regseq}. The maximum clause makes both indices accepted above $N_s(k+1+\sigma_{ab})$ and above $N_t(k+1+\sigma_{ab})$. Regularity of $s$ and $t$ supplies distance histories $D_s,D_t$ with $D_s\leq 2^{-(k+1+\sigma_{ab})}$ and $D_t\leq 2^{-(k+1+\sigma_{ab})}$. The scalar estimate underlying \autoref{thm:cplx-lim-scalar} and the bounds $\mathsf{CplxMod}(a,A)$, $\mathsf{CplxMod}(b,B)$ give distance bounds $A D_s$ for $a\cdot s(n)$ to $a\cdot s(m)$ and $B D_t$ for $b\cdot t(n)$ to $b\cdot t(m)$. Applying the triangle inequality of \autoref{lem:cplx-dist-triangle} after the pointwise sum construction of \autoref{def:cplx-pointwise-sum} bounds the affine-distance witness by $A D_s+B D_t$. The defining dyadic property of $\sigma_{ab}$ gives $A D_s+B D_t\leq (A+B)2^{-(k+1+\sigma_{ab})}\leq 2^{-k}$, so $\mathsf{CplxRegSeq}(a\cdot s+b\cdot t,N_{ab})$ holds.
It remains to verify the convergence modulus. Fix a unary precision $k$ and a unary index $n$ accepted above $M_{ab}(k)$ as in \autoref{def:cplx-lim}. The maximum clause makes $n$ accepted above both $M_s(k+1+\sigma_{ab})$ and $M_t(k+1+\sigma_{ab})$. The two limit hypotheses give distance histories $E_s,E_t$ with $E_s\leq 2^{-(k+1+\sigma_{ab})}$ and $E_t\leq 2^{-(k+1+\sigma_{ab})}$. Componentwise bilinearity gives $(a\cdot s+b\cdot t)(n)-(a\cdot z_s+b\cdot z_t)=a\cdot(s(n)-z_s)+b\cdot(t(n)-z_t)$. Using \autoref{def:cplx-distance}, the same scalar estimates bound the two displayed summands by $A E_s$ and $B E_t$, and \autoref{lem:cplx-dist-triangle} bounds their sum by $A E_s+B E_t$. The same dyadic calculation gives $A E_s+B E_t\leq 2^{-k}$. Since $k$ and $n$ were arbitrary among the accepted indices, $M_{ab}$ satisfies the convergence clause for $a\cdot s+b\cdot t$ at $a\cdot z_s+b\cdot z_t$. Together with the regularity proved above, this is exactly the asserted $\mathsf{CplxLim}$ statement.
\end{proof}
