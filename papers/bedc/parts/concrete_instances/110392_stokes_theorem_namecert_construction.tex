\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\StokesTheoremUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-stokes-theorem-namecert}
\origin{human}

Following Cartan and de Rham, the $\StokesTheoremUp$ packet names the finite BEDC surface on which exterior differentiation, oriented boundary, and integration over a carried chain can be consumed as one certificate. It reads differential forms from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-diffform-namecert}, the cochain boundary route from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-derham-namecert}, the charted domain surface from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-manifold-namecert}, and the scalar integral handoff from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-riemannintegral-namecert}. The packet is only a naming certificate for the displayed Stokes boundary route; it does not supply a host smooth manifold, a selected triangulation, a quotient de Rham class, a measure completion, or an ambient real-completeness theorem.

\begin{definition}[Stokes theorem carrier]\label{def:stokes-theorem-carrier}
A $\StokesTheoremUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
K=(M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N)
$$
whose rows have the following BEDC-facing meaning.
\begin{itemize}
\item $M$ is the carried $\ManifoldUp$ chart-and-orientation source row for the integration domain.
\item $F$ is the carried $\DiffFormUp$ form row together with the exterior-derivative row consumed by the Stokes boundary read.
\item $B$ is the oriented boundary-chain row whose source and target faces are read through the same displayed manifold surface.
\item $E$ is the $\DeRhamUp$ boundary route identifying the exterior derivative and boundary pairing as one chain-complex comparison.
\item $I$ is the $\RiemannIntegralUp$ scalar-integration handoff for the displayed domain, boundary, form, and derivative rows.
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for $M,F,B,E,I$ and the structural rows.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay of the route from manifold charts through form rows, boundary-chain rows, de Rham boundary data, and integration handoff.
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\ManifoldUp$, $\DiffFormUp$, $\DeRhamUp$, $\RiemannIntegralUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\StokesTheoremUp}$ row.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Stokes theorem NameCert obligations]\label{thm:stokes-theorem-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\StokesTheoremUp$ carrier $K=(M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N)$, the local $\NameCert_{\StokesTheoremUp}$ surface is exhausted by carrier admission for $M,F,B,E,I$, boundary-chain compatibility between $B$ and $E$, integral-handoff compatibility between $E$ and $I$, componentwise $\hsame$ transport in $H$, displayed $\Cont$ replay in $C$, provenance in $P$, and local naming in $N$. The certificate exports no host smooth map, no selected chain representative, no quotient de Rham class, no measure-completion object, and no ambient $\RealUp$ completeness coordinate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:stokes-theorem-carrier}. The analytic rows are exactly the manifold row $M$, differential-form row $F$, boundary-chain row $B$, de Rham boundary row $E$, and integration handoff row $I$.

The boundary compatibility is local: $B$ supplies the displayed oriented boundary route, while $E$ supplies the de Rham chain-complex comparison over the same form data. The integration compatibility is also local: $I$ is read only after those rows have been displayed.

Finally attach $H,C,P,N$. These rows transport, replay, source, and name the same finite route. Since the carrier has no coordinate for the excluded host objects, the listed rows exhaust the local NameCert obligations.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Stokes boundary-chain route]\label{thm:stokes-theorem-boundary-chain-route}
Let $K=(M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\StokesTheoremUp$ carrier. Any consumer of the Stokes boundary comparison must first read the manifold row $M$, the differential-form row $F$, and the oriented boundary-chain row $B$ before it can consume the de Rham boundary row $E$ or the integration handoff $I$. The comparison is therefore a finite BEDC route through $M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N$, not an appeal to a host boundary operator or an unlogged smooth-chain equality.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the carrier inventory. The row $B$ is the only boundary-chain coordinate, and it is stated over the manifold and form rows $M,F$.

Apply \autoref{thm:stokes-theorem-namecert-obligations}. Boundary-chain compatibility places $B$ before the de Rham row $E$, and integral-handoff compatibility places $I$ downstream from the same displayed route.

The structural rows $H,C,P,N$ preserve and name this route without adding a second boundary source. Hence no consumer can bypass $M,F,B$ when reading the Stokes comparison.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Stokes closed-form nonescape]\label{thm:stokes-theorem-closed-form-nonescape}
Let $K=(M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\StokesTheoremUp$ carrier whose displayed $\DiffFormUp$ row $F$ is read as closed along the de Rham route $E$. Then every zero-boundary integral read exported by the packet remains inside $M,F,B,E,I,H,C,P,N$. It may use the carried closed-form row, the boundary-chain route, the de Rham boundary comparison, and the Riemann-integral handoff, but it cannot introduce a quotient cohomology representative, a host Stokes theorem, a measure-completion argument, selected charts outside $M$, or ambient real completeness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the closed-form read inside $F$ and $E$. The form row supplies the exterior data, and the de Rham row supplies the boundary comparison over the same displayed form.

The zero-boundary integral is then read through $B$ and $I$. The boundary row supplies the oriented chain surface, and the integral row supplies only the scalar handoff already tied to $M,F,B,E$.

Transport and replay are confined to $H$ and $C$, while $P$ and $N$ attach provenance and local naming. None of these rows contains a quotient representative, host theorem, measure-completion carrier, external chart selector, or ambient completeness coordinate, so the closed-form read cannot escape the packet.
\end{proof}
