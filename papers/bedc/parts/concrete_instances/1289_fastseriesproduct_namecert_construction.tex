\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\FastSeriesProductUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-fastseriesproduct-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\FastSeriesProductUp$ interface names the finite certificate surface for the Cauchy product of two already accepted $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$ packets. Following Bishop and Bridges, \emph{Constructive Analysis}, where products of absolutely or effectively convergent series are controlled by explicit tail data and finite convolution estimates, the packet records only finite summand windows, finite convolution rows, pasted tail schedules, rational regular-sequence readbacks, real-seal rows, transport routes, package provenance, and local naming provenance. It does not form quotient streams, choose a host infinite sum, or appeal to ambient completeness of real numbers.

\begin{definition}[FastSeriesProduct carrier]
\label{def:fast-series-product-carrier}
A $\FastSeriesProductUp$ carrier is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
H=(F,G,K,W,M,T,R,E,L,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $F$ and $G$ are accepted $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$ source packets, carrying displayed summand rows, partial-sum rows, fast tail schedules, $\RegSeqRatUp$ readbacks, $\RealUp$ seal-facing rows, $\hsame$ transports, $\Cont$ read routes, package provenance, and local certificate rows;
\item $K$ is the finite convolution row whose inspected output coefficient at a displayed index $n$ is the finite sum of displayed source summands $a_i b_{n-i}$ over the inspected range $0 \leq i \leq n$;
\item $W$ is the finite partial-sum window ledger for the convolution rows, recording only displayed source windows and displayed product-prefix sums;
\item $M$ is the pasted tail schedule, obtained by reading the fast tail schedules of $F$ and $G$ together with the finite source-window bounds needed for the bilinear tail estimate;
\item $T$ is the tail-bound convolution ledger comparing displayed product-prefix sums past the threshold named by $M$;
\item $R$ is the $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff row for the displayed product partial sums and their pasted tail schedule;
\item $E$ is the $\RealUp$ seal row attached to the same regular rational handoff;
\item $L$ records the componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the two source packets, convolution rows, pasted schedule rows, tail-bound rows, regular handoff rows, and seal-facing rows;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ read routes by which consumers reach the source series, convolution window, pasted schedule, tail ledger, regular handoff, and real seal;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\RealUp$, the finite rational arithmetic rows used by the convolution, and the source certificate rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\FastSeriesProductUp}$ provenance row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier is a finite product certificate. It contains no quotient of streams, no host-level series multiplication, and no completeness theorem for real numbers.
\end{definition}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.FastSeriesProductUp.FastSeriesProductTasteGate\_single\_carrier\_alignment}

The packet is not a standalone series object: its source rows $F$ and $G$ are accepted $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$ packets from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-fastconvergentseries-namecert}, and all convolution, pasted schedule, $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff, and $\RealUp$ seal rows consume that sibling's tail-budget surface.

\begin{definition}[FastSeriesProduct classifier]
\label{def:fast-series-product-classifier}
For accepted $\FastSeriesProductUp$ packets $H$ and $H'$, the classifier $\mathsf{FastSeriesProductClassifier}(H,H')$ compares only the two accepted $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$ source packets, their displayed finite convolution rows, product partial-sum windows, pasted tail schedules, tail-bound convolution ledgers, $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff rows, $\RealUp$ seal rows, componentwise $\hsame$ transports, displayed $\Cont$ routes, $\Pkg$ provenance, and local $\NameCert$ provenance.

In particular, classifier transport is a finite-row comparison. It does not compare host sums, quotient representatives of streams, unbounded convolution functions, or a classical completeness proof for the product series.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[FastSeriesProduct tail-bound convolution]
\label{thm:fast-series-product-tail-bound-convolution}
Let $F$ and $G$ be accepted $\FastConvergentSeriesUp$ packets displayed inside a $\FastSeriesProductUp$ carrier. For every displayed product precision request, the pasted schedule row $M$ supplies a finite threshold after which any two displayed product partial sums are compared by a finite convolution estimate using only:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the source tail schedules of $F$ and $G$;
\item the finite source-window bounds recorded beside those schedules;
\item the displayed convolution rows $K$;
\item the product-prefix ledger $W$.
\end{enumerate}
The resulting tail-bound row in $T$ is therefore a finite BEDC witness for the Cauchy-product tail at that precision, with no appeal to an infinite summation object.
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.FastSeriesProductUp.FastSeriesProductCarrier\_tail\_bound\_convolution}
\begin{proof}
Read the two source packets through \autoref{def:fast-series-product-carrier}. Each source supplies a displayed fast tail schedule and the finite windows on which the requested bounds are inspected.

The convolution row $K$ computes each inspected product coefficient from finitely many displayed source summands. The product-prefix ledger $W$ therefore compares two late product prefixes by splitting the difference into the finite head-window contribution and the two source-tail contributions named by the pasted schedule.

The head-window contribution is a finite rational arithmetic row, while the two tail contributions are bounded by the source schedules of $F$ and $G$ together with the displayed source-window bounds. Repacking these rows gives the tail-bound convolution ledger $T$.

Since every row used in the estimate is a coordinate of the carrier, the argument never calls a host Cauchy-product theorem, a quotient of streams, or an ambient completeness principle.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[FastSeriesProduct RegSeqRat handoff]
\label{thm:fast-series-product-regseqrat-handoff}
For every accepted $\FastSeriesProductUp$ packet and every displayed precision request, the $\RegSeqRatUp$ row $R$ is obtained by repacking the finite convolution partial sums in $W$ together with the pasted schedule $M$ and the tail-bound convolution ledger $T$. A $\RegSeqRatUp$ consumer can read the product name only through those three displayed rows and the recorded $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ routes.
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.FastSeriesProductUp.FastSeriesProductCarrier\_regseqrat\_handoff}

H=(F,G,K,W,M,T,R,E,L,C,P,N).

The partial-sum data offered to $\RegSeqRatUp$ is exactly the product-prefix ledger $W$, whose entries are built from finite convolution rows in $K$.

The regularity witness is the pasted schedule $M$ together with the tail-bound ledger $T$. By \autoref{thm:fast-series-product-tail-bound-convolution}, those rows already contain the finite estimate needed for the requested precision.

The transport and continuation coordinates $L$ and $C$ identify how a consumer reaches the source windows, convolution windows, schedule rows, and tail ledger. Hence the handoff row $R$ exports a regular rational sequence surface, not a hidden product series or a quotient stream.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[FastSeriesProduct NameCert obligation surface]
\label{thm:fast-series-product-namecert-obligation-surface}
The local $\NameCert_{\FastSeriesProductUp}$ obligation surface consists of six rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission: an accepted packet is the finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ carrier of \autoref{def:fast-series-product-carrier};
\item classifier transport: \autoref{def:fast-series-product-classifier} compares only source packets, finite convolution rows, product-prefix windows, pasted schedule rows, tail ledgers, regular handoffs, real seals, transports, continuations, and provenance;
\item finite convolution exactness: each displayed product coefficient is read as the finite row $\sum_{0 \leq i \leq n} a_i b_{n-i}$ over inspected source summands;
\item tail-bound convolution: \autoref{thm:fast-series-product-tail-bound-convolution} supplies the finite pasted tail estimate for product prefixes;
\item $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff: \autoref{thm:fast-series-product-regseqrat-handoff} repacks the product partial sums, pasted schedule, and tail-bound ledger as the visible regular rational readback row;
\item $\RealUp$ seal compatibility: the seal row $E$ may read only the $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff and the finite source, convolution, schedule, tail, transport, continuation, package, and local naming rows carried by the packet.
\end{enumerate}
These rows are the concrete naming-certificate surface for Cauchy products of fast convergent series over the finite $\RealUp$ seal path.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission and classifier transport are projections of the two definitions. They list exactly the rows that an accepted product packet stores and the rows that an accepted transport may compare.

Finite convolution exactness is built into the row $K$: each inspected output coefficient is a finite sum over displayed source summands, so no unbounded convolution function is exported to a consumer.

The tail-bound row is \autoref{thm:fast-series-product-tail-bound-convolution}. It explains how the pasted source schedules and finite source-window bounds control late product-prefix comparisons.

The regular handoff row is \autoref{thm:fast-series-product-regseqrat-handoff}. The $\RealUp$ seal row then reads only that handoff together with the visible transport, continuation, package, and local provenance rows.

Thus the obligation surface closes around the finite certificate packet. It leaves no coordinate for host sums, stream quotients, selected limits, or ambient real completeness.
\end{proof}
