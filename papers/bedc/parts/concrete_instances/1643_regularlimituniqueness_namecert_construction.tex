The $\RegularLimitUniquenessUp$ interface records the separated uniqueness side of regular-Cauchy completion. In the Bishop--Bridges constructive-analysis presentation, a completed real is read through finite regular-name windows and explicit moduli; uniqueness is therefore a visible separated-seal certificate over two accepted completion candidates, not a quotient of streams or a selected host limit. \begin{definition}[RegularLimitUniqueness carrier]
\label{def:regular-limit-uniqueness-carrier}
A $\RegularLimitUniquenessUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
U=(F,D_0,D_1,\mu,R_0,R_1,E_0,E_1,S,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $F$ is the shared $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$ input row;
\item $D_0$ and $D_1$ are two $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$ candidate limit rows for the same $F$;
\item $\mu$ is the shared $\CauchyModulusUp$ threshold row controlling the tail windows compared by both candidates;
\item $R_0$ and $R_1$ are the $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback rows exposed by the two diagonal candidates at the threshold selected by $\mu$;
\item $E_0$ and $E_1$ are the two $\RealUp$ seal rows reached from those readbacks;
\item $S$ is the separated comparison row, read through the $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ boundary, stating that $E_0$ and $E_1$ classify at the same separated real seal;
\item $H$ records the displayed $\hsame$ transports tying $F$, $\mu$, the two diagonal rows, the two readbacks, the two seals, and the separated comparison together;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes through the shared family, threshold row, diagonal candidates, readback rows, real seals, and separated comparison;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$, $\CauchyModulusUp$, $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\StreamNameUp$, $\HausdorffCompletionUp$, $\RealUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, and $\Cont$;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\RegularLimitUniquenessUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for quotient streams, a chosen host limit, host real equality, or choice. Its public data are exactly the displayed finite rows above.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[RegularLimitUniqueness NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:regular-limit-uniqueness-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\RegularLimitUniquenessUp}$ surface has five finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item habitation by a displayed diagonal candidate pair over the carrier of \autoref{def:regular-limit-uniqueness-carrier};
\item classifier laws obtained by componentwise $\hsame$ transport of the shared family, threshold row, diagonal candidates, readbacks, real seals, separated comparison, continuation routes, package provenance, and local naming row;
\item carrier transport through the displayed routes from $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$ to $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$, then through $\RegSeqRatUp$ and $\StreamNameUp$ readbacks to the two $\RealUp$ seals;
\item exactness through the separated $\RealUp$ classifier row $S$, so the two accepted candidates are compared only at the displayed separated seal;
\item ledger non-escape excluding quotient streams, selected host limits, host real equality, and choice.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier habitation is projection of \autoref{def:regular-limit-uniqueness-carrier}. The shared family row, the two diagonal candidates, the common threshold, the two readbacks, the two seals, the separated comparison, transport rows, continuation routes, package provenance, and local certificate are finite coordinates of the same $\BHist$ packet.

Classifier stability is componentwise. Transport the shared $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$ and $\CauchyModulusUp$ rows first, then move the two $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$ rows and their $\RegSeqRatUp$ readbacks along the displayed $\hsame$ rows while replaying the corresponding $\Cont$ routes.

Exactness is the separated-seal step. The only row that compares the two completion candidates after readback is $S$, and $S$ is a displayed $\RealUp$ classifier row read through the $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ boundary.

The non-escape clauses follow from the carrier list. No coordinate contains a quotient of regular streams, a selected host limit, host real equality, or a choice principle, so the local certificate can export only the listed BEDC rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[RegularLimitUniqueness separated limit certificate]
\label{thm:regular-limit-uniqueness-separated-limit-certificate}
Let $U$ be an accepted $\RegularLimitUniquenessUp$ carrier. Suppose two accepted $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$ candidates in $U$ read the same $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$ input through the shared $\CauchyModulusUp$ threshold and reach the same separated boundary row $S$. Then their $\RegSeqRatUp$ readbacks classify through the same $\RealUp$ seal: the two candidate limits are identified exactly by the displayed separated $\RealUp$ classifier row, with visible $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ provenance.

The certificate exports no quotient stream comparison, selected host limit, host real equality, ambient completeness principle, or choice.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project $U$ as in \autoref{def:regular-limit-uniqueness-carrier}. Both candidates point to the same $\RegularCauchyFamilyUp$ row $F$ and the same $\CauchyModulusUp$ threshold $\mu$, so their readback routes begin at one displayed regular-Cauchy input surface.

Read each candidate through its diagonal row. The two $\RealDiagonalCompletionUp$ coordinates expose the corresponding $\RegSeqRatUp$ readbacks and $\StreamNameUp$ windows, and the stored $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ rows keep those reads attached to the shared family and threshold.

Now use the separated comparison row $S$. It is the only accepted comparison between $E_0$ and $E_1$, and it lives at the $\RealUp$ seal boundary through the $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ separated surface.

Repacking this route gives the stated certificate. Since the carrier contains no quotient, selected host limit, host real equality, ambient completeness, or choice coordinate, the equality claim is precisely the displayed separated classifier claim and nothing stronger.
\end{proof}
