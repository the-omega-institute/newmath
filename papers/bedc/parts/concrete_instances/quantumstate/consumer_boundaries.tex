\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp phase ledger exhaustion]
\label{thm:quantumstate-phase-ledger-exhaustion}
Every hidden row used to classify two $\QuantumStateUp$ carrier packets is
one of the displayed phase row $\phi$, inherited Hilbert classifier
$\lambda_H$, inherited projective classifier $\lambda_P$, norm-one ledger
$n$, endpoint $\hsame$ transport row, or provenance row $\rho$ from the
carrier of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. Conversely, those
rows reconstruct exactly the projective phase classifier of
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} inside the consumed
$\NameCert_{\HilbertUp}$ and $\NameCert_{\ProjectiveSpaceUp}$ packets.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start from two displayed packets
$$
\mathcal{Q}=(\mathcal{H},\mathcal{P},v,n,\phi,p,\lambda_H,\lambda_P,\rho)
$$
and
$$
\mathcal{Q}'=(\mathcal{H}',\mathcal{P}',v',n',\phi',p',\lambda'_H,\lambda'_P,\rho').
$$
The carrier definition lists the only rows available to the classifier.
Thus a hidden classifier row must be read from the phase row, one of the
inherited classifiers, the norm ledger, the endpoint $\hsame$ transport
data, or the $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance record.

For exhaustion, use \autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface}
to identify the public $\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ obligations and
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary} to rule out
consumer access to a stronger equality. The stability row in
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} accounts for every
endpoint transport through $\hsame$, while the provenance exactness of
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-provenance-row-exactness} keeps those transports
inside the same finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ package.

For reconstruction, read the Hilbert endpoint through $\lambda_H$, read the
norm ledger through $n$, and read the projective endpoint comparison through
$\phi$ and $\lambda_P$. These are precisely the inputs named by
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier}; repacking them with
$\rho$ recovers the same projective phase classifier and adds no hidden
row beyond the displayed ledger.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp consumer readback completeness]
\label{thm:quantumstate-consumer-readback-completeness}
Every downstream $\ObservableUp$, $\DensityMatrixUp$, and
$\QuantumChannelUp$ consumer row reading $\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$
factors through the five public fields named in
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}: the Hilbert source
row $\mathcal{H}$, projective endpoint row $p$, global-phase classifier
$\phi$, norm ledger $n$, and package provenance $\rho$. Conversely, any
consumer depending only on those five fields is stable under
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The forward direction is a readback claim. By
\autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}, a consumer begins with a finite
$\mathsf{BHist}$ packet carried by $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance. By
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface}, the public
$\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ surface supplies the carrier, classifier,
stability, ledger, exactness, and hidden phase obligations, but it supplies
no observable, density-matrix, channel, or spectral row.

Apply \autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}. The rows
that remain readable by $\ObservableUp$, $\DensityMatrixUp$, and
$\QuantumChannelUp$ consumers are exactly $\mathcal{H}$, $p$, $\phi$, $n$,
and $\rho$. The projective classifier theorem
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} explains how the
endpoint and phase comparison behind those five fields is classified
without exposing host vector equality or a quotient primitive.

For the converse direction, suppose a consumer depends only on
$\mathcal{H}$, $p$, $\phi$, $n$, and $\rho$. Transport the Hilbert and
projective source data by the $\hsame$ rows governed by $\lambda_H$ and
$\lambda_P$. The global-phase stability theorem
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} preserves the phase row
and norm ledger, and \autoref{thm:quantumstate-provenance-row-exactness}
preserves the finite package boundary. Therefore the consumer readback is
unchanged under certified global-phase transport.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp observable expectation input exactness]
\label{thm:quantumstate-observable-expectation-input-exactness}
An $\ObservableUp$ expectation consumer that receives
$\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ may read exactly the state rows
$\mathcal{H}$, $v$, $n$, $p$, $\phi$, $\lambda_H$, $\lambda_P$, and $\rho$
from the carrier packet of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. The
expectation input is the $\mathsf{BHist}$ endpoint $v$ scoped by the
Hilbert row $\mathcal{H}$, the norm-one ledger $n$, the projective endpoint
$p$, the global-phase row $\phi$, the inherited classifier rows
$\lambda_H$ and $\lambda_P$, and the $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance row $\rho$.
It may not read an observable spectrum row, measurement-result row,
host-level vector equality, or state data outside the displayed
$\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ classifier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack the carrier packet of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. The
rows available to any consumer are the two source certificates, the Hilbert
endpoint, the norm-one ledger, the phase and projective endpoint data, the
two inherited classifiers, and the finite provenance package. The observable
expectation site uses the state side of this packet, so its state input is
the listed eight rows
$$
\mathcal{H},v,n,p,\phi,\lambda_H,\lambda_P,\rho.
$$

The classifier is fixed by
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier}. Thus $v$ is not read
as a raw host vector: it is read as a Hilbert $\mathsf{BHist}$ endpoint
whose admissible comparisons pass through $\lambda_H$, $\phi$, and
$\lambda_P$. The stability available to the expectation consumer is exactly
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability}, so changing certified
presentations transports the same input rows by $\hsame$ rather than by a
host quotient.

Exactness follows from
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface} together with
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}. The public surface
contains no spectral, measurement, density-matrix, or channel row, and the
consumer boundary forbids equality outside the Hilbert and projective
classifiers. Therefore the observable expectation input is precisely the
displayed $\mathsf{BHist}$ and $\mathsf{Pkg}$ row family.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp density-matrix pure ray readback]
\label{thm:quantumstate-densitymatrix-pure-ray-readback}
A $\DensityMatrixUp$ pure-state consumer reading
$\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ may recover only the projective ray endpoint
$p$ together with the Hilbert source row $\mathcal{H}$, the endpoint row
$v$, the norm-one ledger $n$, the global-phase row $\phi$, the inherited
classifiers $\lambda_H,\lambda_P$, and the provenance row $\rho$. Its
readback is a certified pure ray in $\mathsf{BHist}/\hsame$ terms, not a
host vector quotient and not an already supplied density-matrix row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the carrier packet
$$
\mathcal{Q}=(\mathcal{H},\mathcal{P},v,n,\phi,p,\lambda_H,\lambda_P,\rho).
$$
The pure-density consumer needs a ray source and a unit ledger. These are
already the projective endpoint $p$ and the norm-one Hilbert ledger $n$,
with $v$ serving only as the certified Hilbert endpoint from which the ray
row is projected.

Apply \autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} to identify the
ray comparison used by the consumer. The global-phase row $\phi$ and the
projective classifier $\lambda_P$ determine sameness of pure rays, while
$\lambda_H$ scopes the Hilbert endpoint and norm ledger. Apply
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} to transport this
readback along $\hsame$ without changing the rows that the consumer may
inspect.

The exclusion is the constructive content. By
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface}, no density-matrix row
is exported by $\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$. By
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}, no host vector
quotient or equality outside the displayed classifiers is available. Thus a
pure-state density-matrix consumer can build its downstream row only from
the certified ray endpoint, the norm-one Hilbert ledger, and the same
$\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance record.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp channel consumer input boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-channel-consumer-input-boundary}
A $\QuantumChannelUp$ state-input consumer of
$\NameCert_{\QuantumStateUp}$ is stable under the same
global-phase/projective classifier as $\QuantumStateUp$. Its input rows are
limited to $\mathcal{H}$, $v$, $n$, $p$, $\phi$, $\lambda_H$, $\lambda_P$,
and $\rho$ from the carrier packet; it carries no independent channel row,
no density-matrix row, and no classifier other than the inherited
$\mathsf{BHist}$, $\hsame$, $\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$ data already
exported by the state certificate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier} and read the state input as
a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ package. The endpoint supplied to a channel is
$v$ together with its norm-one ledger $n$, its projective endpoint $p$, the
phase row $\phi$, the inherited classifiers $\lambda_H,\lambda_P$, and the
provenance row $\rho$ tying these rows to the consumed source certificates.

The classifier for this input is not chosen by the channel consumer. It is
the classifier of \autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier}:
Hilbert endpoint transport is scoped by $\lambda_H$, and ray transport is
scoped by $\phi$ and $\lambda_P$. The stability required by the channel
input is exactly \autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability}, which
keeps the same finite row family under componentwise $\hsame$ transport.

Finally apply \autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface} and
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}. The public surface
exports no channel datum and no density-matrix datum, while the consumer
boundary forbids a separate quotient or host equality. Repacking the
displayed rows therefore gives the whole channel state-input boundary in
$\mathsf{BHist}/\hsame/\mathsf{Pkg}/\NameCert$ terms.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp observable state boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-observable-state-boundary}
An $\ObservableUp$ expectation row may read a $\QuantumStateUp$ $\mathsf{BHist}$
packet only through the Hilbert source row $\mathcal{H}$, the projective
endpoint row $p$, the global-phase classifier $\phi$, the norm ledger $n$, and
the package provenance $\rho$ exposed by
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}, together with the
observable endpoint, self-adjoint witness, spectral-data row, and expectation
row displayed by $\ObservableUp$. It may not replace the certified state input
by host vector equality, a raw quotient representative, or an expectation row
whose Hilbert source is not the shared $\mathcal{H}$ endpoint.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted quantum-state packet through
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}. The only state-side
rows visible to a downstream consumer are $\mathcal{H}$, $p$, $\phi$, $n$, and
$\rho$.

Now apply the expectation boundary of
\autoref{thm:observable-quantumstate-expectation-boundary}. The observable
packet contributes its operator endpoint, self-adjoint witness, spectral-data
row, and expectation row, and that theorem requires the expectation input to be
compatible with the same Hilbert source.

Combining the two projections gives the displayed boundary. Any host vector
equality, quotient representative, or Hilbert-mismatched expectation row would
have to enter through a field not present in one of the two cited packet
surfaces, so it is not readable by the consumer.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp density-matrix boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-densitymatrix-boundary}
A $\QuantumStateUp$ $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet does not itself supply a
$\DensityMatrixUp$ row. A density-matrix consumer of a pure projective state
must add, as separate displayed rows, trace-class membership, positivity,
trace-one evidence, and any rank-one projection bridge that turns the
projective endpoint into a positive trace-class trace-one operator. These rows
are not part of the Hilbert source, projective endpoint, phase classifier, norm
ledger, or provenance exposed by
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the quantum-state packet fields from
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-consumer-boundary}. They certify the
Hilbert source, projective endpoint, phase classifier, norm ledger, and package
provenance, but no field has the form of a trace-class operator on the Hilbert
carrier.

Compare this with the density carrier obligation of
\autoref{thm:densitymatrix-carrier-obligation}. A $\DensityMatrixUp$ endpoint is
accepted only when trace-class membership, positivity, and trace-one evidence
are all displayed over the Hilbert carrier.

Therefore a pure-state-to-density-matrix use must introduce the trace-class,
positivity, trace-one, and rank-one projection bridge rows at the consumer site.
The quantum-state packet can supply the named source and projective endpoint
for such a bridge, but it does not supply the density row itself.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumStateUp quantum-channel input boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-channel-input-boundary}
A $\QuantumChannelUp$ consumer may use a $\QuantumStateUp$ packet as an input
only after a displayed $\DensityMatrixUp$-compatible input row has been supplied
for the same Hilbert source. Channel image closure can then read the
$\DensityMatrixUp$ row and the CPTP carrier of the channel, but it cannot read
host vector equality, a raw pure-state quotient, or a direct channel action on
the projective endpoint.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First apply \autoref{thm:quantumstate-densitymatrix-boundary}. The
quantum-state packet alone has no density-matrix row, so a channel input must
pass through a separately displayed trace-class, positivity, trace-one, and
rank-one projection bridge package.

Next use the CPTP carrier of \autoref{def:quantumchannel-cptp-carrier}. Its
source type is the trace-class carrier over the input Hilbert space, and its
density-state action is stated over $\DensityMatrixUp$ inputs rather than over
projective representatives.

Finally apply the density-matrix image lemma
\autoref{lem:quantumchannel-densitymatrix-image}. Once the compatible density
row is present, the channel image closure reads exactly that row and the CPTP
channel rows. The excluded host equality, quotient representative, and direct
projective action are outside the displayed density and channel carrier
surfaces.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[ObservableUp expectation-row boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-observable-expectation-row-boundary}
When $\ObservableUp$ forms expectation or eigenvalue rows from a
$\QuantumStateUp$ source, it may read only the Hilbert source row
$\mathcal{H}$, the projective endpoint row $p$, the global-phase classifier
$\phi$, the norm ledger $n$, and the provenance row $\rho$ of
\autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. The observable consumer may combine
those rows with its own observable certificate, but it receives no
density-matrix row, measurement row, channel row, spectral row, quotient
object, or host Hilbert-vector equality from $\QuantumStateUp$ itself.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack the carrier packet
$\mathcal{Q}=(\mathcal{H},\mathcal{P},v,n,\phi,p,\lambda_H,\lambda_P,\rho)$
of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. The rows available to an
observable construction are the Hilbert source row, the projected endpoint, the
phase classifier, the norm ledger, and the provenance row.

Apply the Hilbert source boundary of
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-hilbert-source-boundary} to locate all vector,
norm, and inner-product inputs inside $\mathcal{H}$ and $\lambda_H$. Apply
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} and
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} to replace any phase choice
by the projective endpoint classifier.

The public surface theorem,
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface}, identifies the
complete exported row list. Expectation and eigenvalue rows therefore require a
separate $\ObservableUp$ certificate for observable, measurement, or spectral
data. Since none of those rows occurs in $\mathcal{Q}$, and since quotient and
host-vector equalities are excluded by the classifier boundary, the displayed
rows are the whole $\QuantumStateUp$ input boundary for this consumer.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DensityMatrixUp rank-one row boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-density-matrix-row-boundary}
When $\DensityMatrixUp$ forms a rank-one projector row from a
$\QuantumStateUp$ source, it may read only the projective endpoint row $p$ and
the norm-one ledger $n$, together with the provenance row $\rho$ that records
their common packet. The density-matrix consumer may not read a host quotient,
an ambient vector equality, a measurement row, a channel row, a spectral row,
or any density-matrix row unless that row is supplied by its own downstream
certificate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. The rank-one projector input
is not a new field of $\mathcal{Q}$; the packet exposes only the projective
endpoint $p$, the norm-one ledger $n$, the inherited classifiers, and the
provenance row $\rho$.

Use \autoref{thm:quantumstate-hilbert-source-boundary} to read $n$ through the
Hilbert source row rather than through an ambient vector equality. Use
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} to read $p$ through the
projective endpoint classifier, and use
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} to ensure that replacing a
unit-vector representative by a certified phase-equivalent representative does
not change the exposed projector input.

Finally apply
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface}. Its exactness clause
contains no density-matrix, measurement, channel, or spectral row, and it
contains no quotient object or host-vector equality. Thus any matrix row,
operator row, or spectral decomposition must come from the
$\DensityMatrixUp$ certificate itself, while $\QuantumStateUp$ contributes
only the projective endpoint, norm ledger, and provenance needed to form the
rank-one boundary.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumChannelUp pure-state input boundary]
\label{thm:quantumstate-channel-pure-state-input-boundary}
When $\QuantumChannelUp$ consumes pure-state input or output rows from a
$\QuantumStateUp$ source, it may use the exported $\QuantumStateUp$ classifier
and provenance only: the Hilbert source row $\mathcal{H}$, the projective
endpoint row $p$, the global-phase classifier $\phi$, the norm ledger $n$, and
the provenance row $\rho$. Channel action, density-matrix data, measurement
data, spectral data, quotient objects, and host Hilbert-vector equalities must
be supplied by the channel-side certificate rather than by
$\QuantumStateUp$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack the packet of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-carrier}. A pure-state
input or output row can name the state only through the Hilbert source row,
the projective endpoint, the phase classifier, the norm ledger, and the
provenance row.

Apply \autoref{thm:quantumstate-hilbert-source-boundary} to keep vector and
norm data inside the consumed Hilbert certificate. Apply
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-projective-phase-classifier} to expose equality of
pure states only through the projective/global-phase classifier, and apply
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-global-phase-stability} to transport that classifier
across certified presentations.

The public obligation surface of
\autoref{thm:quantumstate-namecert-obligation-surface} is exact. It exports no
channel map, no density-matrix row, no measurement row, no spectral row, and
no quotient or host-vector equality. Therefore $\QuantumChannelUp$ may attach
its channel certificate to the exported classifier and provenance, but all
channel action and output certification remains a downstream obligation.
\end{proof}
