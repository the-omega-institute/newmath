Following Bishop and Bridges constructive analysis, the $\UniformIntegralLimitUp$ packet names the finite handoff by which a uniformly controlled sequence of integration packets reaches a single $\RealUp$ seal. It sits after the tagged-mesh route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-riemannintegral-namecert}, the upper-lower gap route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-darbouxintegral-namecert}, and the gauge-partition route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-gauge-integral-namecert}. It consumes regulated approximation rows from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-regulated-function-namecert}, regular rational handoff rows from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-regseqrat-namecert}, and the terminal real-name boundary of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}. The packet records a uniform passage of integral rows to one real seal; it does not introduce measure completion, quotient function spaces, selected limiting partitions, open-cover compactness, or ambient $\RealUp$ completeness.

\begin{definition}[Uniform integral limit carrier]
\label{def:uniform-integral-limit-carrier}
A $\UniformIntegralLimitUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
U=(I,F,T,J,A,Q,E,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $I$ is the interval row, naming the endpoint histories, order evidence, and cell boundary ledger shared by every integration packet in the window.
\item $F$ is the integrand-window sequence. Each row records a displayed function-read window, its index, and the finite observation data used by the corresponding integral packet.
\item $T$ is the uniform tail ledger. It records the common tolerance schedule and the visible index bound after which all carried integrand windows and integral rows remain in the same requested regular rational window.
\item $J$ is the Riemann, Darboux, or Gauge integral row attached to each accepted window. Its entries are routed through $\RiemannIntegralUp$, $\DarbouxIntegralUp$, or $\GaugeIntegralUp$ over the same interval row $I$.
\item $A$ is the regulated approximation row, recording the $\RegulatedFunctionUp$ step-approximation ledger used to compare the integrand windows before the integral rows are sealed.
\item $Q$ is the $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff row produced from the tail ledger and the displayed integral rows.
\item $E$ is the $\RealUp$ seal reached only after the interval, integrand-window, uniform-tail, integral, regulated-approximation, and regular rational rows have been displayed.
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for interval rows, integrand-window sequences, uniform tail ledgers, Riemann/Darboux/Gauge integral rows, regulated approximation rows, regular rational handoff, real seal, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay from
$$
I,F \longmapsto T \longmapsto J \longmapsto A \longmapsto Q \longmapsto E
$$
and then through the transport, provenance, and local naming rows.
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\UniformIntegralLimitUp$, $\RiemannIntegralUp$, $\DarbouxIntegralUp$, $\GaugeIntegralUp$, $\RegulatedFunctionUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\RealUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\UniformIntegralLimitUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The classifier compares accepted packets only through $I,F,T,J,A,Q,E,H,C,P,N$. It has no coordinate for a measure completion, a quotient of functions, selected limiting partitions, open-cover compactness, or an ambient completeness theorem for $\RealUp$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Uniform integral limit NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:uniform-integral-limit-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\UniformIntegralLimitUp$ carrier
$$
U=(I,F,T,J,A,Q,E,H,C,P,N),
$$
the local $\NameCert_{\UniformIntegralLimitUp}$ surface consists exactly of the following obligation rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission for the interval row, integrand-window sequence, uniform tail ledger, integration row family, regulated approximation row, regular rational handoff, real seal, componentwise transport, continuation replay, provenance, and local naming rows;
\item interval coherence, requiring every Riemann, Darboux, or Gauge entry in $J$ to read the same displayed interval row $I$;
\item uniform tail exposure, requiring $T$ to be present before the regular rational handoff $Q$ and the real seal $E$ are readable;
\item integration-route visibility, requiring each entry of $J$ to route through $\RiemannIntegralUp$, $\DarbouxIntegralUp$, or $\GaugeIntegralUp$ rather than through a host integral;
\item regulated approximation compatibility, requiring $A$ to expose the finite regulated-function approximation rows used to compare the integrand windows;
\item real-seal non-escape, requiring $E$ to be downstream of $T,J,A,Q$ and to export no independent completeness principle;
\item structural faithfulness, requiring $H,C,P,N$ to transport, replay, source, and name exactly the displayed rows and no measure completion, quotient function space, selected limiting partition, open-cover compactness theorem, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness witness, Lean verification, or bridge row.
\end{enumerate}
No uniform integral limit NameCert obligation is stored outside these rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from \autoref{def:uniform-integral-limit-carrier}. The packet displays the interval row, the integrand-window sequence, the uniform tail ledger, the integration row family, regulated approximation data, regular rational handoff, real seal, transport, replay, provenance, and local name.

Interval coherence and integration-route visibility are read from $I$ and $J$. Each integration row in $J$ is attached to the same interval row and is routed through one of the already named Riemann, Darboux, or Gauge packets, so the carrier never reads a detached host integral.

Uniform tail exposure is the role of $T$. The regular rational row $Q$ is available only after the common tail ledger has been displayed, and the real seal $E$ is available only after $Q$ and the carried integration rows have been read.

The regulated approximation row $A$ supplies the comparison surface for the integrand-window sequence. Transport through $H$ and replay through $C$ preserve the same order, while $P$ and $N$ record the provenance and local naming. Since the excluded analytic objects are not fields of $U$, the listed rows exhaust the local certificate surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Uniform integral limit real seal handoff]
\label{thm:uniform-integral-limit-real-seal-handoff}
Let $U=(I,F,T,J,A,Q,E,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\UniformIntegralLimitUp$ packet. Every consumer read of the $\RealUp$ seal $E$ factors through the finite route
$$
I,F \longmapsto T \longmapsto J \longmapsto A \longmapsto Q \longmapsto E .
$$
Thus the real-facing output records the common interval, the displayed integrand-window sequence, the uniform tail ledger, the Riemann/Darboux/Gauge integration rows, the regulated approximation row, and the regular rational handoff, and it does not expose measure completion, quotient function spaces, selected limiting partitions, open-cover compactness, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness, Lean verification, or bridge checking.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with a consumer read of $E$. By \autoref{def:uniform-integral-limit-carrier}, the seal is downstream of the interval and integrand-window rows, the uniform tail ledger, the integration rows, the regulated approximation row, and the regular rational handoff.

The row $T$ supplies the common tail control before $Q$ can be read. The row $J$ supplies the finite integration surface through the Riemann, Darboux, or Gauge packets, and $A$ records the regulated approximation ledger needed to compare the carried integrand windows.

Continuation replay through $C$ rebuilds this route, and componentwise transport through $H$ preserves it under $\hsame$. The provenance row $P$ and local name $N$ add no additional analytic carrier.

Therefore every real-facing consumer of $E$ sees only the displayed finite route. The excluded objects have no coordinate in the carrier and cannot be exported by the seal.
\end{proof}
