\begin{closurestatus}{\IntUp}
  \constructivestory{An integer is presented as one sign mark sitting beside one unary magnitude: the mark is b0 or b1, the magnitude is a $\NatUp$ spine, and nothing enters the carrier unless both pieces are visible. The classifier first compares signs by mark-equality and magnitudes by history-equality, then folds exactly one ambiguity back together by identifying $(b0,\emp)$ with $(b1,\emp)$ so that the two written zeroes name the same integer. With that rule in place, the source is the same signed-unary predicate, the pattern records the sign/magnitude case split, magnitude behavior under append contexts, and the zero-sign collapse, and the ledger transports the visible pair along history-sameness on the magnitude side. The five name-certificate obligations are carried by $(b0,\emp)$ as the inhabitant, by mark- and history-equality as equivalence relations, and by the zero rule being stable under symmetry and transitivity. A signed pair $(s,m)$ lies in the signature gap of an $\IntUp$ token $p$ when the source-domain accepts the pair, the signature relation supplies an answer pair with its evidence ledger, and the token-introduction relation names that answer as $p$; after unfolding, the pair has the sign and magnitude encoded by $p$, except that either sign may represent zero. The standard bridge reads the same data as a signed unary word and back, which is why $\RatUp$ can later consume it for numerator readback and algebraic packages can reuse it without changing the carrier. The chapter registers the construction under the name $\IntUp$; an integer is a sign mark next to a unary spine, not a quotient of natural-number pairs, and the only imposed equivalence is the explicit zero-sign normalization.}
