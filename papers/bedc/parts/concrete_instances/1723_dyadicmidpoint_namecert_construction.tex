\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\DyadicMidpointUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicmidpoint-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\DyadicMidpointUp$ interface names the finite midpoint ledger consumed by dyadic interval bisection. In the Bishop--Bridges style of constructive dyadic interval bisection, a midpoint is not a hidden real-number choice: it is a displayed common-denominator calculation over two dyadic endpoint rows, followed by a branch-comparison row and the finite readback route made available to interval consumers.

\begin{definition}[DyadicMidpoint carrier]
\label{def:dyadic-midpoint-carrier}
A $\DyadicMidpointUp$ carrier is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
M=(L,R,S,\mu,B,W,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $L$ and $R$ are the left and right endpoint rows accepted by $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ or exposed through a $\RationalIntervalUp$ endpoint surface;
\item $S$ is the common-denominator scaling row that transports $L$ and $R$ to one dyadic denominator before addition;
\item $\mu$ is the midpoint row obtained from the displayed numerator sum and the doubled common denominator;
\item $B$ is the branch-comparison row that records which side of $\mu$ a later bisection or locatedness consumer selects;
\item $W$ is the finite-window readback row exposing the scaled endpoints, midpoint, branch comparison, and requested dyadic tolerance;
\item $H$ stores the componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the two endpoints, the scaling row, the midpoint row, the branch row, and the finite readback row;
\item $C$ stores the displayed $\Cont$ routes for endpoint inspection, midpoint readback, branch handoff, and downstream interval consumption;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance row naming $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\RationalIntervalUp$, $\DyadicIntervalUp$, $\DyadicSubdivisionUp$, $\NestedIntervalUp$, $\StreamNameUp$, and $\NameCert$ sources that may consume the midpoint ledger;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\DyadicMidpointUp}$ registration row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for host real equality, quotient endpoints, a completed interval intersection, an ambient completeness proof, or an unrecorded midpoint decision.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicMidpoint NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:dyadic-midpoint-namecert-obligations}
The seed $\NameCert_{\DyadicMidpointUp}$ surface consists of six finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier: formation of the $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet of \autoref{def:dyadic-midpoint-carrier};
\item endpoint scaling: both endpoint rows are transported to the displayed common denominator before any midpoint row is read;
\item midpoint exactness: the row $\mu$ is the finite dyadic row obtained from the displayed scaled numerator sum and doubled common denominator;
\item branch comparison: the branch row $B$ records the side of the displayed midpoint consumed by dyadic bisection, subdivision, or located-interval readback;
\item finite-window readback: every consumer read factors through $W$, $H$, $C$, $P$, and $N$;
\item non-escape: no host real equality, quotient endpoint, completed intersection, or ambient completeness row is exported.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier formation is the finite packet assembly of \autoref{def:dyadic-midpoint-carrier}. The two endpoint rows, the common-denominator row, the midpoint row, the branch row, the readback row, and the provenance rows are all visible coordinates of the same $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet.

Endpoint scaling and midpoint exactness are read from $S$ and $\mu$. The scaling row names the common dyadic denominator, and the midpoint row is built by the displayed numerator-sum and denominator-doubling calculation.

Branch comparison is the row $B$. A bisection or locatedness consumer may route left or right only through that displayed comparison row, so the branch decision is not imported from an external real-order oracle.

Finally, every public read must pass through $W$, then through the displayed $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ routes and the package and naming rows. Since the carrier lists no host equality, quotient endpoint, completed intersection, or completeness coordinate, none is available to export.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicMidpoint endpoint classifier stability]
\label{thm:dyadic-midpoint-endpoint-classifier-stability}
Let
$$
M=(L,R,S,\mu,B,W,H,C,P,N)
$$
and
$$
M'=(L',R',S',\mu',B',W',H',C',P',N')
$$
be accepted $\DyadicMidpointUp$ carriers. If the endpoint rows $L,R$ and $L',R'$ are transported by the displayed $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ rows to the same finite dyadic endpoint window, then their common-denominator rows, midpoint rows, branch-comparison rows, and finite-window readbacks classify together through the local $\DyadicMidpointUp$ classifier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First transport the two endpoint pairs along the displayed endpoint routes in $H,C$ and $H',C'$. The hypothesis places both carriers over the same finite dyadic endpoint window.

The common-denominator rows $S$ and $S'$ are then read over that shared window. Because both rows scale the same two transported endpoint rows, the scaled endpoint numerators and common denominator are the same finite ledger data.

The midpoint rows $\mu$ and $\mu'$ are obtained from those scaled endpoint numerators by the same displayed denominator-doubling calculation. Thus the midpoint readbacks classify together.

It remains to compare consumer-visible branching and readback. The branch rows $B,B'$ inspect the same midpoint row against the same transported endpoint window, and $W,W'$ expose only those transported rows through the package and naming provenance. The local classifier therefore preserves the full midpoint packet surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicMidpoint branch comparison handoff]
\label{thm:dyadic-midpoint-branch-comparison-handoff}
For every accepted $\DyadicMidpointUp$ carrier $M=(L,R,S,\mu,B,W,H,C,P,N)$, any $\DyadicBisectionUp$, $\DyadicSubdivisionUp$, or $\LocatedIntervalUp$ consumer that branches at the midpoint consumes exactly the displayed branch-comparison row $B$ together with the endpoint rows $L,R$, the common-denominator row $S$, the midpoint row $\mu$, the finite readback row $W$, and the provenance rows $H,C,P,N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier. The only row that records a left/right comparison at the midpoint is $B$, and the only midpoint value available to that row is $\mu$.

The endpoint and scaling prerequisites for the comparison are exactly $L,R$ and $S$. Reading $B$ therefore forces the consumer to read the same common-denominator endpoint window and the same midpoint row that produced the comparison.

The consumer-facing handoff is $W$. It exposes the endpoint, scaling, midpoint, and branch rows through the displayed transports, continuation routes, package provenance, and local naming row.

Thus the bisection, subdivision, or located-interval consumer receives the stated finite handoff and no hidden branch oracle.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicMidpoint finite-window consumer route]
\label{thm:dyadic-midpoint-finite-window-consumer-route}
For every accepted $\DyadicMidpointUp$ carrier and every finite precision request, the consumer-visible route to $\RationalIntervalUp$, $\DyadicIntervalUp$, $\NestedIntervalUp$, $\FiniteWindowEnvelopeUp$, $\DyadicBisectionUp$, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, or $\RealUp$ factors through the finite readback row $W$, the displayed $\hsame$ transports $H$, the displayed $\Cont$ routes $C$, the package row $P$, and the local $\NameCert_{\DyadicMidpointUp}$ row $N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the finite precision request. The carrier answers it by selecting the finite readback row $W$, which contains the scaled endpoints, midpoint row, branch comparison, and dyadic tolerance visible at that request.

Endpoint and midpoint transport then proceeds through $H$ and $C$. These rows move the endpoint, scaling, midpoint, and branch data componentwise, so every downstream interval or real-name consumer reads the same finite ledger.

The package row $P$ records which surrounding certificates may consume the route, and $N$ records the local naming registration. Therefore the route into rational intervals, dyadic intervals, nested intervals, finite-window envelopes, dyadic bisection, dyadic rational rows, and real names is exhausted by the displayed finite packet.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicMidpoint nested interval non-escape]
\label{thm:dyadic-midpoint-nested-interval-non-escape}
Suppose a $\NestedIntervalUp$ or $\DyadicBisectionUp$ packet consumes a $\DyadicMidpointUp$ carrier during a finite refinement step. The retained subinterval endpoint rows, the selected midpoint row, and the branch comparison remain inside the displayed $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet of \autoref{def:dyadic-midpoint-carrier}; the handoff exports no host real equality, no quotient endpoints, no completed interval intersection, and no ambient completeness principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Apply \autoref{thm:dyadic-midpoint-branch-comparison-handoff}. A refinement step can choose a retained subinterval only through the displayed branch row $B$ and the midpoint row $\mu$.

Next apply \autoref{thm:dyadic-midpoint-finite-window-consumer-route}. The selected endpoint rows and midpoint row are routed to the nested or bisection consumer through $W$, $H$, $C$, $P$, and $N$.

All rows used in the handoff are coordinates of the finite carrier or provenance attached to that carrier. Since \autoref{def:dyadic-midpoint-carrier} contains no host real equality, quotient endpoint, completed interval intersection, or ambient completeness field, the finite refinement cannot export any such object.
\end{proof}
