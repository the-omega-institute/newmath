\begin{theorem}[AbsolutelyContinuousFunction NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:absolutely-continuous-function-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\AbsolutelyContinuousFunctionUp$ carrier $A=(I,F,M,P,V,G,T,Q,D,R,H,C,S,N)$, the local $\NameCert_{\AbsolutelyContinuousFunctionUp}$ surface consists exactly of carrier admission; source interval visibility through $I$; displayed function reads through $F$; modulus-budget exposure through $M$; finite disjoint-partition coverage through $P$; variation control through $V$; gauge-integral handoff through $G$; regulated approximation through $T$; regular rational and dyadic readback through $Q,D$; Real sealing through $R$; componentwise transport, displayed replay, provenance, and local naming through $H,C,S,N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier of \autoref{def:absolutely-continuous-function-carrier}. The visible rows are exactly $I,F,M,P,V,G,T,Q,D,R,H,C,S,N$, so every local naming obligation must be read from one of those rows.

The source and control obligations are $I,F,M,P,V$. The interval and function rows determine the inspected endpoint windows, while the modulus row chooses the finite length budget. The partition and variation ledgers then record the only admissible test that small total displayed length forces small total displayed jump.

The analytic handoff obligations are $G,T,Q,D,R$. The regulated row exposes stepwise endpoint behavior, the gauge row carries the integration-facing request, and the regular and dyadic rows provide the finite numerical readbacks before the terminal Real seal is visible.

Finally $H,C,S,N$ transport, replay, source, and name the same packet. Since no carrier coordinate stores host function equality, quotient real functions, measure completion, a selected derivative, countable additivity, ambient variation space completion, or a completed $L^1$ representative, none of those objects can appear in the local NameCert surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[AbsolutelyContinuousFunction gauge handoff]

\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
