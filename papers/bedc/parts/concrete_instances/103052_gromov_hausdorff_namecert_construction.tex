\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\GromovHausdorffUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-gromov-hausdorff-namecert}
\origin{human}

Following Gromov's metric-geometry formulation and Hausdorff's compact-subset distance, the $\GromovHausdorffUp$ packet records compact-metric comparison by finite correspondence rows and dyadic distortion bounds. It sits over the metric source of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert}, the compact-metric surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-compactmetric-namecert}, the compact-subset ledger of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hyperspace-namecert}, the finite-distance interface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hausdorff-metric-namecert}, and the distance-function route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-kuratowski-embedding-namecert}. The packet compares two displayed compact metric endpoints; it is not a quotient compactum, a selected optimal correspondence, an open-cover compactness theorem, a host metric-space equality principle, or an ambient $\RealUp$ completeness claim.

\begin{definition}[Gromov--Hausdorff comparison carrier]
\label{def:gromov-hausdorff-carrier}
A $\GromovHausdorffUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
G=(X,Y,K_X,K_Y,R,D,H,Q,U,T,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $X$ and $Y$ are the two compact-metric endpoint names. Each endpoint row records the displayed $\MetricUp$ source, point-name admission data, compact finite-net schedules, and the request for which a comparison certificate is exposed.
\item $K_X$ and $K_Y$ are the endpoint $\CompactMetricUp$ rows consumed before any correspondence row may be inspected.
\item $R$ is the finite correspondence table. Its rows list finitely many endpoint point-name pairs, the two projection-coverage ledgers, and the local inhabitedness witnesses needed for a comparison read.
\item $D$ is the dyadic distortion ledger. It records finite $\RatUp$ or $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ bounds comparing the two endpoint distance reads along every displayed pair of correspondence rows.
\item $H$ records the $\HausdorffMetricUp$ and $\HyperspaceUp$ comparison rows used to read the two projection-coverage ledgers as compact-subset distance data rather than as host powersets.
\item $Q$ records the $\KuratowskiEmbeddingUp$ handoff used when a consumer asks to view the same comparison through distance-function observations.
\item $U$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for endpoints, compact-metric rows, correspondence rows, dyadic distortion bounds, Hausdorff and hyperspace comparison rows, Kuratowski handoff, replay, provenance, and local naming rows.
\item $T$ records displayed transport of the finite comparison surface across accepted endpoint renamings; it carries only rows already present in $X,Y,K_X,K_Y,R,D,H,Q,U$.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay of the route
$$
X,Y,K_X,K_Y \longmapsto R \longmapsto D \longmapsto H,Q \longmapsto U,T .
$$
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\GromovHausdorffUp$, $\MetricUp$, $\CompactMetricUp$, $\HausdorffMetricUp$, $\HyperspaceUp$, $\KuratowskiEmbeddingUp$, $\RatUp$, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\GromovHausdorffUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The classifier compares accepted packets only through $X,Y,K_X,K_Y,R,D,H,Q,U,T,C,P,N$. It has no coordinate for quotient compacta, selected optimal correspondences, open-cover compactness, host metric-space equality, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness, or semantic isometry of completed spaces.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[GromovHausdorffUp NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:gromov-hausdorff-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\GromovHausdorffUp$ carrier
$$
G=(X,Y,K_X,K_Y,R,D,H,Q,U,T,C,P,N),
$$
the local $\NameCert_{\GromovHausdorffUp}$ surface consists exactly of the following finite obligation rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item endpoint admission for the two compact-metric names $X,Y$ and their $\MetricUp$ and $\CompactMetricUp$ support rows $K_X,K_Y$;
\item correspondence admission for the finite table $R$, requiring both endpoint projections and local inhabitedness witnesses to be displayed before any distortion read is public;
\item dyadic distortion exactness for $D$, requiring every displayed pair of correspondence rows to read both endpoint distances and the shared dyadic bound through the same finite table;
\item compact-subset comparison compatibility for $H$, requiring the projection-coverage rows to be read through the $\HausdorffMetricUp$ and $\HyperspaceUp$ surfaces;
\item Kuratowski observation compatibility for $Q$, requiring any distance-function view of the same comparison to pass through the displayed $\KuratowskiEmbeddingUp$ handoff;
\item classifier transport through $U,T,C$, requiring componentwise $\hsame$ transport and displayed $\Cont$ replay to preserve endpoints, correspondence rows, distortion bounds, comparison handoffs, provenance, and local name;
\item provenance and name exhaustion through $P,N$, excluding quotient compacta, selected correspondences, open-cover compactness, host metric-space equality, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness, and completed-space isometry.
\end{enumerate}
No Gromov--Hausdorff obligation is stored outside these rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier of \autoref{def:gromov-hausdorff-carrier}. The endpoint rows $X,Y$ and compact-metric support rows $K_X,K_Y$ are the only compact metric surfaces that a consumer may inspect.

The finite correspondence table $R$ supplies both endpoint projections and the inhabitedness witnesses. The dyadic distortion ledger $D$ then reads every displayed pair of correspondence rows against the two endpoint distance witnesses, so no distortion statement can be formed from data outside the finite table.

The compact-subset comparison row $H$ routes projection coverage through $\HausdorffMetricUp$ and $\HyperspaceUp$, while $Q$ routes distance-function observations through $\KuratowskiEmbeddingUp$. These rows are handoffs inside the packet, not ambient compacta or host function spaces.

Finally $U,T,C$ preserve and replay the same route componentwise, and $P,N$ name exactly that finite route. Since the carrier has no row for quotient compacta, selected optimal correspondences, open-cover compactness, host metric-space equality, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness, or completed-space isometry, the seven listed rows exhaust the local naming obligations.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Correspondence bridge to compact-metric comparison]
\label{thm:gromov-hausdorff-correspondence-bridge}
Every $\MetricUp$-, $\CompactMetricUp$-, $\HausdorffMetricUp$-, $\HyperspaceUp$-, or $\KuratowskiEmbeddingUp$-facing consumer of an accepted $\GromovHausdorffUp$ carrier factors through the finite route
$$
X,Y,K_X,K_Y \longmapsto R \longmapsto D \longmapsto H,Q \longmapsto U,T,C,P,N .
$$
Consequently the consumer receives only the displayed compact-metric endpoints, finite correspondence rows, dyadic distortion bounds, compact-subset comparison rows, Kuratowski observation handoff, componentwise transport, replay, provenance, and local name. It cannot derive a quotient compactum, selected optimal correspondence, open-cover compactness proof, host metric-space equality, ambient $\RealUp$ completeness theorem, or isometry of completed spaces from the packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the endpoint rows. Any metric or compact-metric read reaches $X,Y,K_X,K_Y$ first, so the comparison is anchored in displayed point names, distance witnesses, and compact finite-net schedules.

Next inspect the correspondence route. The row $R$ is the only finite relation between the endpoints, and $D$ is the only dyadic distortion ledger attached to it; therefore a compact-metric comparison consumer must pass through $R$ before reading any bound.

The bridge rows then determine the available external views. A Hausdorff or hyperspace consumer reads $H$, and a Kuratowski-style distance-function consumer reads $Q$, but both rows consume the same correspondence and distortion data.

The structural rows $U,T,C,P,N$ preserve the route under componentwise transport, replay it, source it, and name it. Because no further coordinate is present, the consumer boundary is exactly the displayed finite comparison surface and nothing beyond it.
\end{proof}
