\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-runtime-boundary-audit-namecert}
\origin{ai}

The $\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp$ packet realizes the runtime-boundary claim of \autoref{ch:visions-self-description-truth-boundary} as a finite audit surface. It binds the object-level candidate boundary of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-runtimecandidateboundary-namecert}, the runtime-request and refusal surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-runtime-reflection-boundary-namecert}, the meta-closure obstruction of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metaclosureobstruction-namecert}, the structural reflection limit of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-reflectionlimitboundary-namecert}, and the claim ledger of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-auditsystem-namecert} into a single NameCert packet. The carrier records an auditable boundary without converting the host proof checker into an object-layer row.

\begin{definition}[Runtime-boundary audit carrier]
\label{def:runtime-boundary-audit-carrier}
A $\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
A=(S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $S$ is the structural self-description row, recording formation, replay, and certificate-shape data described by \autoref{def:philosophy-structural-self-description};
\item $R$ is the runtime-candidate row inherited from $\RuntimeCandidateBoundaryUp$, naming the object-level candidate, transition, and accepted-result boundary;
\item $E$ is the runtime-reflection refusal row inherited from $\RuntimeReflectionBoundaryUp$, recording the request for validation and the execution refusal report;
\item $M$ is the meta-closure obstruction row inherited from $\MetaClosureObstructionUp$, blocking conversion of the runtime request into an unwitnessed closure principle;
\item $L$ is the reflection-limit row inherited from $\ReflectionLimitBoundaryUp$, separating structural reflection from truth-total or runtime-total claims;
\item $G$ is the audit-system row inherited from $\AuditSystemUp$, recording the admissible claim, positive or negative certificate face, export grade, and ledger closure;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for $S,R,E,M,L,G,C,P,N$;
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ routes by which consumers read the structural description, candidate boundary, runtime refusal, obstruction, reflection limit, audit ledger, and transports;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\RuntimeCandidateBoundaryUp$, $\RuntimeReflectionBoundaryUp$, $\MetaClosureObstructionUp$, $\ReflectionLimitBoundaryUp$, $\AuditSystemUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, $\NameCert$, and $\TasteGate$ rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The classifier compares accepted carriers only through the displayed rows $S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N$. No accepted carrier has an additional row for host-runtime execution, runtime validation, or a BEDC truth predicate.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Runtime-boundary audit NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:runtime-boundary-audit-namecert-obligations}
For the carrier of \autoref{def:runtime-boundary-audit-carrier}, the local $\NameCert_{\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp}$ surface consists exactly of carrier admission for $A=(S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N)$, structural-description exactness through $S$, runtime-candidate accountability through $R$, runtime-reflection refusal through $E$, meta-closure obstruction through $M$, reflection-limit boundary through $L$, audit-ledger closure through $G$, and transport, consumer, provenance, and local naming closure through $H,C,P,N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from the finite packet of \autoref{def:runtime-boundary-audit-carrier}. The rows $S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N$ are the complete displayed surface.

The rows $S,R,E,M,L,G$ supply the substantive audit coordinates. They record structural self-description, the object-level candidate boundary, runtime-request refusal, meta-closure obstruction, reflection-limit separation, and claim-ledger status.

The rows $H,C,P,N$ transport, replay, package, and name the same packet. Since those rows only preserve the displayed coordinates, they cannot add a hidden validator, a runtime-total row, or a truth predicate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Runtime-boundary audit validation non-escape]
\label{thm:runtime-boundary-audit-validation-nonescape}
Every accepted $\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp$ packet exports runtime-boundary evidence only through the finite rows $S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N$. A consumer asking for host-runtime validation must therefore encounter the refusal row $E$, the meta-closure obstruction row $M$, and the reflection-limit row $L$ before any audit export through $G$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Let $A=(S,R,E,M,L,G,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted carrier. By \autoref{def:runtime-boundary-audit-carrier}, every consumer route is displayed in $C$ and every route is transported by $H$ over the same coordinates.

A runtime-validation request is first classified as an object-level candidate read by $R$. It is then recorded as a runtime-reflection request and refusal by $E$.

The request cannot bypass the obstruction and limit rows. Row $M$ blocks the conversion of the request into an unwitnessed closure principle, while row $L$ blocks the conversion of structural reflection into a truth-total or runtime-total claim.

The audit-system row $G$ can export only the resulting finite claim status. It has no coordinate for replacing $E,M,L$ by host execution, so validation does not escape the displayed boundary packet.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Runtime-boundary audit consumer boundary]
\label{thm:runtime-boundary-audit-consumer-boundary}
Every consumer of an accepted $\RuntimeBoundaryAuditUp$ packet that reports structural self-description must preserve the distinction between the structural row $S$ and the runtime-validation refusal path $R,E,M,L$. In particular, the consumer cannot certify self-description by replacing $R,E,M,L$ with a host-checker row outside the packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Consumer access is mediated by the displayed route row $C$. Reading the structural description therefore begins at $S$ and remains inside the same finite carrier.

The runtime-facing path is separate. It passes through the candidate row $R$, the runtime-reflection refusal row $E$, the meta-closure obstruction row $M$, and the reflection-limit row $L$.

The provenance and local naming rows $P,N$ name that route as part of the same packet, and $H$ transports it componentwise. None of these rows supplies a host-checker coordinate. The consumer boundary is therefore exactly the displayed separation between $S$ and $R,E,M,L$.
\end{proof}
