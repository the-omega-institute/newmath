\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-urysohn-metrization-namecert}
\origin{human}

Following Urysohn's metrization theorem, the $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$ packet records the finite BEDC route by which a displayed normal, second-countable, Hausdorff topological source exposes a metric handoff. It sits between the normal-space separation surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-normalspace-namecert}, the countable-base schedule of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-second-countable-namecert}, the Hausdorff separation row of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hausdorffspace-namecert}, the open-set carrier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-topology-namecert}, and the metric consumer of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert}. The packet names only the displayed finite normal-space and countable-base route; it does not assert arbitrary classical metrization, selected embeddings into host sequence spaces, excluded middle, or ambient point-set equality.

\begin{definition}[Urysohn metrization carrier]
\label{def:urysohn-metrization-carrier}
An $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
U=(T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D,M,G,R,Q,C,P,L)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $T$ is the $\TopologyUp$ source row, recording the open-set classifier and finite neighbourhood histories consumed by the construction;
\item $N$ is the $\NormalSpaceUp$ separation row for closed pairs presented by the topology source;
\item $S$ is the $\SecondCountableUp$ countable-base schedule row, exposing only finite base windows and refinement requests;
\item $Hd$ is the $\HausdorffSpaceUp$ separation row, used as the target separatedness boundary for metric consumers;
\item $B$ is the finite base-window ledger read from $S$ before any separating function request is made;
\item $F$ is the closed-pair separation ledger routed through $N$ for the displayed base-window pairs;
\item $D$ is the dyadic distance-readback ledger assembled from the finite family of displayed separation rows;
\item $M$ is the $\MetricUp$ handoff row whose distance witness is read only after $T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D$ have been displayed;
\item $G$ records compatibility between the generated metric-open rows and the original topology row $T$ on the inspected finite windows;
\item $R$ records the reverse finite-window readback from topology neighbourhood requests to metric-ball requests;
\item $Q$ is the refusal row excluding classical metrization data not present in $T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D,M,G,R$;
\item $C$ is the displayed $\Cont$ replay from a topology request through the normal-space and second-countable rows to the metric handoff;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$, $\TopologyUp$, $\NormalSpaceUp$, $\SecondCountableUp$, $\HausdorffSpaceUp$, $\MetricUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows;
\item $L$ is the local $\NameCert_{\UrysohnMetrizationUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for arbitrary host spaces, choice-selected separating functions, a classical embedding into a product cube, quotient equality of points, or metrization outside the displayed normal second-countable Hausdorff route.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Urysohn metrization NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:urysohn-metrization-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$ carrier $U=(T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D,M,G,R,Q,C,P,L)$, the local $\NameCert_{\UrysohnMetrizationUp}$ surface consists exactly of topology-source admission through $T$, normal-space closed-pair separation through $N$, second-countable finite-window scheduling through $S,B$, Hausdorff separatedness through $Hd$, distance-ledger assembly through $F,D$, metric handoff through $M$, topology-to-metric and metric-to-topology compatibility through $G,R$, refusal of non-displayed classical data through $Q$, replay through $C$, provenance through $P$, and local naming through $L$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:urysohn-metrization-carrier}. Each named obligation is one displayed row of the same finite packet, so a consumer can inspect only the topology, normal-space, second-countable, Hausdorff, distance-ledger, metric-handoff, compatibility, refusal, replay, provenance, and naming rows.

The normal-space route is read through $N$ and $F$, while the countable-base route is read through $S$ and $B$. These rows determine which finite separation requests can contribute to the dyadic distance ledger $D$.

The metric handoff is therefore the row $M$ together with the compatibility rows $G,R$. Since $Q$ excludes host embeddings, selected separating functions, quotient equality, and any metrization principle not routed through the displayed rows, the listed rows exhaust the local NameCert surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Urysohn normal second-countable scope]
\label{thm:urysohn-metrization-normal-second-countable-scope}
Let $U=(T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D,M,G,R,Q,C,P,L)$ be an accepted $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$ packet. Any consumer that treats the source topology as metrizable must read the normal-space row $N$, the second-countable schedule $S,B$, and the Hausdorff row $Hd$ before reading the metric handoff $M$. The packet exports no metrization claim for a source lacking any of these displayed rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the topology source $T$. A topology row alone supplies open-neighbourhood data, but it does not supply the closed-pair separation route, the countable-base schedule, or the separatedness boundary.

The required route is exactly $T,N,S,Hd,B,F,D,M$. The rows $N$ and $F$ expose normal closed-pair separation; the rows $S$ and $B$ expose the finite base windows; and $Hd$ records the Hausdorff separation boundary consumed by the metric handoff.

If one of these rows is absent, the carrier has no alternate coordinate from which a metric can be read. The refusal row $Q$ therefore blocks any broader classical metrization assertion.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Urysohn metric handoff]
\label{thm:urysohn-metrization-metric-handoff}
Every $\MetricUp$ consumer of an accepted $\UrysohnMetrizationUp$ packet receives the route
$$
\TopologyUp,\ \NormalSpaceUp,\ \SecondCountableUp,\ \HausdorffSpaceUp,\ B,\ F,\ D,\ M,\ G,\ R,\ C,\ P,\ L.
$$
The metric row $M$ is consumed as a downstream handoff tied to the displayed topology-compatibility rows $G,R$; it is not a host metric, not a selected embedding metric, and not a claim that arbitrary normal spaces are metrizable.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the carrier rows in order. The source topology, normal-space separation, countable-base schedule, Hausdorff boundary, base ledger, closed-pair separation ledger, and dyadic distance ledger are displayed before $M$ is available.

The compatibility rows $G$ and $R$ bind the handoff back to finite topology windows. Thus a metric consumer receives a named route from the original topology surface to the displayed metric row and back to inspected neighbourhood requests.

Finally use the refusal row $Q$. Since no row stores a host embedding, a chosen sequence of separating functions, arbitrary classical metrization, or quotient point equality, the handoff remains the finite BEDC route named above.
\end{proof}
