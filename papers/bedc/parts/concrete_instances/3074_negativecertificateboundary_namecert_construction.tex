\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-negativecertificateboundary-namecert}
\origin{ai}

The $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ packet realizes apophatic refusal as a finite NameCert surface. It names the socket row at which an external supply is requested, records the attempted internalizer that must be refused, keeps the refusal in a $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, exposes the result through an audit-gate readback, and carries the transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows needed by downstream checks.

The packet is a boundary certificate. It does not prove that the external supply is absent from every possible context, and it does not turn absence into a positive object. It records that the named socket has no admitted internalizer inside the displayed BEDC packet.

$\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ specializes the apophatic-name surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticname-namecert} by adding the refused-internalizer row, the $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger row, and the audit-gate readback row. $\ApophaticNameUp$ supplies the named externality and refusal discipline; $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ packages the concrete socket, refusal, ledger, and audit surface that downstream checks can read without converting the refused content into substrate data.

The socket row is read through \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticsocket-namecert}, the audit readback row is consumed through \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-auditgateboundary-namecert}, and the refusal boundary remains distinct from the negative-name surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-negative-name-boundary-namecert}: this packet certifies a concrete refused internalizer at a named socket rather than renaming the whole negative-name boundary.

\begin{definition}[NegativeCertificateBoundary carrier]
\label{def:negative-certificate-boundary-carrier}
A $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
B=(S,R,G,A,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $S$ is the named socket row, recording the apophatic boundary at which an external supply is requested without being internalized;
\item $R$ is the refused internalizer row, recording the proposed constructor, theorem, axiom, quotient representative, host datum, or setup object whose admission would collapse the boundary;
\item $G$ is the $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger row, recording the finite refusal entry for $R$ at socket $S$;
\item $A$ is the audit-gate readback row, exposing the socket, refused internalizer, and $\mathsf{GAP}$ decision to downstream checks;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for the socket, refusal, $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, audit readback, provenance, and local certificate rows;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes from the socket row to the refusal row, from the refusal row to the $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, and from the ledger to the audit readback;
\item $P$ records $\Pkg$ provenance over $\BHist$, $\mathsf{GAP}$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier contains no coordinate for a positive witness of the requested external supply and no route that converts the refusal into an internal object.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[NegativeCertificateBoundary NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:negative-certificate-boundary-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ carrier
$$
B=(S,R,G,A,H,C,P,N),
$$
the local $\NameCert_{\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp}$ surface consists of five finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission: the accepted object is exactly the displayed $\BHist$ packet $B$ with socket, refused-internalizer, $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger, audit readback, transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows;
\item refusal classifier stability: any classified replacement of $S$, $R$, $G$, or $A$ is transported componentwise through $H$ and replayed only through the displayed routes in $C$;
\item $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger exactness: the refusal recorded in $G$ is attached to the named socket $S$ and to the proposed internalizer $R$, with no anonymous refusal source and no unrecorded acceptance route;
\item audit-gate handoff: every downstream read of the boundary factors through $A$ after reading the socket, refused internalizer, and $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger rows;
\item non-internalization: the packet exports the refusal boundary and its audit readback, but it exports no constructor, theorem, axiom, quotient representative, host datum, setup object, or positive supply witness for the requested externality.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp.NegativeCertificateBoundaryTasteGate\_single\_carrier\_alignment}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from \autoref{def:negative-certificate-boundary-carrier}. The tuple exposes exactly $S,R,G,A,H,C,P,N$, so every admitted boundary row is part of the finite packet.

Classifier stability is componentwise. The transport row $H$ moves the socket, refusal, ledger, and readback together, and the continuation row $C$ replays only the displayed route from socket to refusal to ledger to audit readback.

Ledger exactness follows from the role of $G$. It records the refusal of the proposed internalizer $R$ at the named socket $S$, and the audit readback $A$ is the only downstream surface that exposes that decision.

Finally, non-internalization is a consequence of the carrier inventory. No coordinate of $B$ supplies the requested external content or admits the refused internalizer, so the NameCert packet certifies the boundary rather than converting it into substrate content.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[NegativeCertificateBoundary audit surface]
\label{thm:negative-certificate-boundary-audit-surface}
Every consumer-facing read of an accepted $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ packet factors through the finite rows
$$
S,R,G,A,H,C,P,N.
$$
The audit readback exposes the named socket, the refused internalizer, the $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger entry, componentwise transport, continuation replay, provenance, and local naming certificate. It exposes no separate positive object for the requested external supply.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the carrier definition. The only readback row available to a downstream consumer is $A$, and $A$ is attached to the socket row $S$, refused internalizer row $R$, and $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger row $G$.

Use the continuation routes in $C$ to order the read. A consumer first reaches the socket, then the refusal row, then the ledger, and only then the audit readback.

The transport and provenance rows preserve the same surface. Since the tuple has no coordinate for a positive supply object, the audit surface is exhausted by the displayed rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[NegativeCertificateBoundary apophatic sibling route]
\label{thm:negative-certificate-boundary-apophatic-sibling-route}
Every accepted $\NegativeCertificateBoundaryUp$ packet reads its socket row through \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticname-namecert} and \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticsocket-namecert}, and reads its refused-internalizer and audit-gate route through \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apophaticgatequestion-namecert} before exposing the local $\mathsf{GAP}$ ledger.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier as
$$
B=(S,R,G,A,H,C,P,N).
$$
The row $S$ is the named apophatic socket: it is the place where the requested external supply is named without being admitted as substrate content.

The rows $R,G,A$ give the refusal and audit route. The refused internalizer $R$ is attached to the socket, the ledger row $G$ records the finite $\mathsf{GAP}$ entry for that refusal, and the audit row $A$ is the downstream readback surface.

Thus the packet first inherits the apophatic-name and apophatic-socket sibling route, then passes through the apophatic-gate question route for the refused internalizer and audit readback. The exposed boundary is therefore a downstream finite refusal boundary, not an isolated kernel-only object.
\end{proof}
