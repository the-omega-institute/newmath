The $\KernelObservationSieveUp$ surface realizes the inscription-event route of \autoref{ch:visions-inscription-event-as-concrete-instance} as a finite observation sieve. It consumes the observer-history identity packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-observerhistoryidentity-namecert}, the selector-ledger packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-selectorledger-namecert}, and the observer-filter split of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-observer-filter-namecert}. The carrier records accepted rows and rejected rows together, so the public inscription-facing route cannot erase the rows that failed the sieve.

\begin{definition}[Kernel observation sieve carrier]
\label{def:kernel-observation-sieve-carrier}
A $\KernelObservationSieveUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
K=(O,S,F,A,R,T,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $O$ is the source observer-history row supplied by $\ObserverHistoryIdentityUp$;
\item $S$ is the selector-ledger row supplied by $\SelectorLedgerUp$;
\item $F$ is the observer-filter row that classifies source observations as accepted or rejected;
\item $A$ is the accepted-row ledger, listing the finite observation rows admitted to the inscription-facing route;
\item $R$ is the rejected-row ledger, listing the finite observation rows refused by the same sieve together with their boundary reasons;
\item $T$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for $O,S,F,A,R,P,N$;
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ routes through the source history, selector ledger, filter row, accepted ledger, and rejected ledger;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\ObserverHistoryIdentityUp$, $\SelectorLedgerUp$, $\ObserverFilterUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\KernelObservationSieveUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The classifier compares accepted carriers only by these displayed rows. It has no coordinate for a hidden observation oracle, host equality of histories, quotient stream equality, unledgered deletion, or an inscription route that forgets the rejected-row ledger.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Kernel observation sieve NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-namecert-obligations}
For the carrier of \autoref{def:kernel-observation-sieve-carrier}, the local $\NameCert_{\KernelObservationSieveUp}$ surface consists of five finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier exposure: every accepted packet exposes exactly $K=(O,S,F,A,R,T,C,P,N)$;
\item selector accountability: the selector-ledger row $S$ is read before an observation row can enter the accepted ledger $A$;
\item paired-ledger exactness: every source observation accounted for by the sieve is visible in exactly one of the accepted ledger $A$ or the rejected ledger $R$;
\item transport and route stability: $T$ and $C$ move and replay only the displayed observer-history, selector, filter, accepted, rejected, provenance, and naming rows;
\item provenance exposure: $P$ and $N$ bind the sieve to $\ObserverHistoryIdentityUp$, $\SelectorLedgerUp$, $\ObserverFilterUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ data rather than to a host-side observation source.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier exposure is projection from \autoref{def:kernel-observation-sieve-carrier}. The observer-history, selector-ledger, filter, accepted-ledger, rejected-ledger, transport, route, provenance, and naming rows are all displayed coordinates.

Selector accountability reads $S$ beside $O$ and $F$. An observation reaches the accepted ledger only after the selector and filter rows have classified it inside the finite packet.

Paired-ledger exactness is the defining split between $A$ and $R$. The accepted side can feed an inscription-facing consumer, while the rejected side remains visible as boundary data.

Transport, route stability, and provenance exposure are supplied by $T,C,P,N$. Since the carrier lacks a hidden observation oracle, host history equality, quotient stream equality, unledgered deletion, and any route that forgets $R$, these rows exhaust the local certificate surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Kernel observation sieve non-escape]
\label{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-nonescape}
No consumer of an accepted $\KernelObservationSieveUp$ packet can recover an omitted observation row without passing through the rejected-row ledger $R$. A consumer may read accepted observations through $A$ and displayed routes $C$, but omitted rows remain boundary data in $R$ and cannot be reconstructed from $\ObserverHistoryIdentityUp$, $\SelectorLedgerUp$, $\ObserverFilterUp$, host equality, quotient stream equality, or an unrecorded inscription event.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier $K=(O,S,F,A,R,T,C,P,N)$. The omitted observations are named only in the rejected-row ledger $R$, while accepted observations are named in $A$.

Apply \autoref{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-namecert-obligations}. Paired-ledger exactness places every accounted observation in exactly one of the two ledgers, and selector accountability requires the route through $S$ and $F$ before either ledger can be consumed.

The transport and route rows $T$ and $C$ replay this same split. The provenance row $P$ names only the observer-history, selector-ledger, and observer-filter sibling certificates, so no hidden source remains from which a consumer could recover an omitted row without reading $R$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Kernel observation sieve inscription handoff]
\label{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-inscription-handoff}
The finite observation sieve hands data to the inscription-event surface of \autoref{ch:visions-inscription-event-as-concrete-instance} only through the accepted ledger $A$, while the rejected ledger $R$ remains part of the same public certificate. Thus the inscription-facing route receives $O,S,F,A,T,C,P,N$ together with the visible refusal boundary $R$, and it receives no unfiltered observation stream, hidden selector, or host-level source of omitted rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start from the vision handoff. The inscription-event route requires a finite public record surface rather than an unbounded observation stream.

Use \autoref{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-namecert-obligations}. The accepted ledger $A$ is available only after $O,S,F$ are displayed, and the rejected ledger $R$ remains in the same packet as the boundary for omitted rows.

Finally apply non-escape from \autoref{thm:kernel-observation-sieve-nonescape}. Since omitted rows cannot be recovered without $R$, the inscription-facing consumer receives the accepted ledger together with the refusal boundary and no hidden observation source.
\end{proof}
