\begin{definition}[Minkowski rate geometry carrier]
\label{def:minkowski-rate-geometry-carrier}
A $\MinkowskiRateGeometryUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
M=(G,X,R,L,D,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $G$ is an accepted $\MultiHistConfigUp$ row displaying the finite observer-history configuration and its no-global-sync boundary;
\item $X$ is the finite family of $\CrossHistCausalUp$ rows over selected pairs of histories in $G$;
\item $R$ is an accepted $\MaxCausalRateUp$ row whose unary bound is read over $G$ and the causal family $X$;
\item $L$ is an accepted $\LorentzFrameRateUp$ row tying the same $G,X,R$ surface to an observer-symmetric frame-rate ledger;
\item $D$ is the symmetric distance-shape ledger, recording the rate-induced light-boundary comparisons licensed by $L$ and requiring the same row after swapping the two displayed observer histories;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for $G$, $X$, $R$, $L$, $D$, the continuation routes, provenance, and local naming row;
\item $C$ records visible $\Cont$ routes by which a consumer reads $G$, then $X$, then $R$, then $L$, then $D$, and transports the packet through $H$ into the local certificate;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\MultiHistConfigUp$, $\CrossHistCausalUp$, $\MaxCausalRateUp$, $\LorentzFrameRateUp$, the distance-shape ledger, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\MinkowskiRateGeometryUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier exports only this finite ledger surface. The word ``geometry'' names the symmetric boundary shape induced by the maximum-rate invariant, not an imported continuum, coordinate chart, or metric tensor.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Minkowski rate geometry NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:minkowski-rate-geometry-namecert-obligations}
For the carrier of \autoref{def:minkowski-rate-geometry-carrier}, the local $\NameCert_{\MinkowskiRateGeometryUp}$ surface consists exactly of six finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission for the displayed packet $M=(G,X,R,L,D,H,C,P,N)$;
\item upstream exactness, requiring $G$, $X$, $R$, and $L$ to be the displayed multi-Hist, causal, maximum-rate, and Lorentz-frame-rate rows read in that order;
\item distance-shape exactness, requiring every entry of $D$ to be induced by the observer-symmetric maximum-rate row of $L$;
\item observer symmetry, requiring the $D$ row to be stable under swapping the two displayed observer histories and transporting the swap through $H$;
\item route stability, requiring all reads of $D$ to pass through the visible $\Cont$ routes in $C$ and the provenance row $P$;
\item non-escape, excluding global frames, synchronized clocks, metric tensors, host real-valued coordinates, ambient Lorentz manifolds, and distance rows not generated by $G,X,R,L,D,H,C,P,N$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from \autoref{def:minkowski-rate-geometry-carrier}. The packet displays the configuration, causal family, maximum-rate row, frame-rate row, distance-shape ledger, transport rows, continuation routes, provenance, and local name before any consumer reads the certificate.

Upstream exactness reads the rows in dependency order. The maximum-rate row $R$ is tied to $G$ and $X$, and the Lorentz-frame-rate row $L$ is tied to the same $G,X,R$ surface.

Distance-shape exactness then reads $D$ only after $L$. Thus the boundary shape is a rate-induced ledger row, not an independent metric object.

Observer symmetry and route stability are the componentwise $H$ and $C$ clauses. They move the displayed rows together and keep every read inside the same finite packet.

The non-escape clause is the carrier inventory. Since no excluded structure is a coordinate of $M$, no local naming projection can export one.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Minkowski rate geometry symmetry]
\label{thm:minkowski-rate-geometry-symmetry}
Let
$$
M=(G,X,R,L,D,H,C,P,N)
$$
be an accepted $\MinkowskiRateGeometryUp$ carrier. Every distance-shape comparison in $D$ is observer-symmetric: swapping the two displayed observer histories in a listed pair and transporting the packet through $H$ returns the same distance-shape row in $D$ along the visible routes in $C$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the distance-shape ledger $D$ through the route required by \autoref{def:minkowski-rate-geometry-carrier}. Each row of $D$ is attached to the observer-symmetric frame-rate packet $L$.

The frame-rate row inherits its maximum-rate row from the same $G$ and $X$ surface, so swapping the displayed observers does not select a different rate source. It only reorders the same selected pair inside the finite configuration.

Finally transport through $H$ and replay the routes in $C$. The comparison returns to the same distance-shape ledger row, because $D$ records symmetric shape data and has no coordinate for a privileged observer slot.
\end{proof}

\label{thm:minkowski-rate-geometry-nonescape}

A consumer can read a Minkowski-like boundary only through the finite route
$$

\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier. Its coordinates are exactly the upstream configuration, causal, maximum-rate, and Lorentz-frame-rate rows, the distance-shape ledger, transport rows, continuation routes, provenance, and local name.

The NameCert obligations require every distance-shape row to be induced by $L$, and $L$ is itself tied to the displayed maximum-rate row $R$ over $G$ and $X$. Hence no detached distance comparison is available.

The symmetry theorem keeps the boundary observer-symmetric under componentwise transport. It does not add a metric tensor, coordinate chart, or global frame.

Therefore the public packet is a finite rate-to-geometry boundary. Any richer geometric structure would require a coordinate absent from $G,X,R,L,D,H,C,P,N$.
