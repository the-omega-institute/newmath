The $\RealInverseUp$ interface names the finite reciprocal packet for a real source whose nonzero domain is witnessed by apartness. It follows the Bishop--Bridges discipline: the reciprocal route is available only after an $\ApartnessRealUp$ witness supplies the constructive guard, and the resulting packet hands a product-seal route to $\RealUp$ without importing trichotomy, host division, quotient real equality, a selected limit, or completeness.

The reciprocal packet records its sibling route explicitly. It consumes the nonzero-domain witness from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-apartnessreal-namecert}, the product verification route from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-cauchyproduct-namecert}, the finite-window route from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-streamname-namecert}, the regular rational readback from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-regseqrat-namecert}, and the seal surface from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}. This is the dependency route for the displayed rows $x,a,p,w,r,s,h,c,l,n$; it is not another theorem and not a host division principle.

\begin{definition}[Real inverse carrier]
\label{def:real-inverse-carrier}
A $\RealInverseUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
I=(x,a,p,w,r,s,h,c,l,n)
$$
with the following displayed coordinates:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $x$ is the source $\RealUp$ row whose reciprocal is being named;
\item $a$ is an $\ApartnessRealUp$ nonzero-domain witness licensing the reciprocal route for $x$;
\item $p$ is the $\CauchyProductUp$ product route that checks the candidate inverse against the source;
\item $w$ is the $\StreamNameUp$ finite-window row used to read the guarded source and candidate reciprocal;
\item $r$ is the $\RegSeqRatUp$ regular rational window row exposed through $w$;
\item $s$ is the $\RealUp$ seal row receiving the guarded reciprocal packet;
\item $h$ stores componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the source, apartness witness, product route, windows, regular rational row, seal, provenance, and local name;
\item $c$ stores the displayed $\Cont$ routes from the apartness witness to the reciprocal window, from the window to the regular rational row, from the product route to the seal, and from the finite packet to the local name;
\item $l$ records $\Pkg$ provenance over $\RealUp$, $\ApartnessRealUp$, $\CauchyProductUp$, $\StreamNameUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$;
\item $n$ is the local $\NameCert_{\RealInverseUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for trichotomy, host division, quotient real equality, selected real values, infinite tails, or $\RealUp$ completeness.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Real inverse NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:real-inverse-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\RealInverseUp}$ surface for \autoref{def:real-inverse-carrier} consists exactly of source $\RealUp$ admission, $\ApartnessRealUp$ nonzero-domain admission, $\CauchyProductUp$ product-route exposure, $\StreamNameUp$ finite-window readback, $\RegSeqRatUp$ regular rational exposure, $\RealUp$ seal handoff, componentwise $\hsame$ transport, displayed $\Cont$ routes, $\Pkg$ provenance, and the local naming row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from the displayed packet $I$. Its rows list the real source, apartness guard, product route, finite window, regular rational readback, seal, transport, continuation, provenance, and local certificate.

The reciprocal obligation is guarded by the apartness row. The certificate first reads $a$, then uses the continuation rows to expose the finite window $w$ and the regular rational row $r$ that describe the candidate reciprocal.

The product route $p$ supplies the only multiplication-facing check before the seal $s$ is exposed. The remaining rows $h,c,l,n$ transport and name this finite route, so the certificate contains no trichotomy, host division, quotient real equality, selected limit, infinite tail, or completeness coordinate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Real inverse apartness domain]
\label{thm:real-inverse-apartness-domain}
Every accepted $\RealInverseUp$ packet licenses reciprocal use only through its displayed $\ApartnessRealUp$ witness $a$. A consumer that cannot read $a$ receives no reciprocal window, no regular rational candidate row, and no product-seal route.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier $I=(x,a,p,w,r,s,h,c,l,n)$. The only coordinate naming a nonzero domain is the apartness row $a$.

Read the continuation routes in $c$. They place the reciprocal window $w$ and the regular rational row $r$ downstream of the apartness witness, and the product route $p$ is exposed only along the same guarded path.

Thus the reciprocal is not a total host operation on $\RealUp$. Without the constructive apartness witness, the packet has no route to a reciprocal candidate, product check, or seal handoff.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Real inverse product seal handoff]
\label{thm:real-inverse-product-seal-handoff}
Every $\RealUp$ consumer of an accepted $\RealInverseUp$ packet factors through the $\CauchyProductUp$ product route $p$, the $\StreamNameUp$ window $w$, the $\RegSeqRatUp$ row $r$, the displayed transport and continuation rows, the provenance ledger, and the seal row $s$. The handoff exposes reciprocal information only as the apartness-guarded product certificate named by the packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the apartness-domain theorem. It places every reciprocal read downstream of the nonzero-domain witness $a$.

The finite observation then runs through $w$ and $r$. The $\StreamNameUp$ row supplies the bounded read, and the $\RegSeqRatUp$ row is the regular rational surface passed to the product check.

Finally the $\CauchyProductUp$ route $p$ supplies the product certificate consumed by the seal row $s$. The transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows record this route, so the real-facing handoff receives only the guarded finite packet.
\end{proof}
