\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\ModNUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-modn-namecert}

The modular-arithmetic interface $\ModNUp$ packages the carrier of integers modulo a positive natural $n$. It depends on $\NameCert_{\IntUp}$ (\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-int-namecert}) and is the canonical instance of $\NameCert_{\QuotientRingUp}$ (\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-quotientring-namecert}) at the ideal $n\mathbb{Z}$.

\begin{definition}[ModN quotient presentation]
\label{def:modn-nz-quotient-presentation}
Let $R_{\mathbb{Z}}$ be a displayed $\RingUp$ certificate for $\IntUp$ with carrier $C_{\mathbb{Z}}$, classifier $\sim_{\mathbb{Z}}$, addition $+_{\mathbb{Z}}$, additive inverse $-_{\mathbb{Z}}$, zero $0_{\mathbb{Z}}$, and multiplication $\cdot_{\mathbb{Z}}$. Let $n:\NatUp$ be positive, and let $I_n(x)$ be a certified $\IdealUp$ predicate over $R_{\mathbb{Z}}$ whose intended membership is $x\in n\mathbb{Z}$. The quotient presentation of $\ModNUp(n)$ is the specialization
$$
\ModNUp(n):=\QuotientRingUp(R_{\mathbb{Z}},I_n).
$$
Its public classifier is the ideal-coset classifier
$$
\mathsf{ModNClassifier}_n(a,b)\Longleftrightarrow a\equiv_{I_n}b,
$$
where $\equiv_{I_n}$ is the relation of \autoref{def:quotientring-ideal-coset-relation}. Thus, after unfolding the quotient-ring relation,
$$
\mathsf{ModNClassifier}_n(a,b)\Longleftrightarrow
C_{\mathbb{Z}}(a)\land C_{\mathbb{Z}}(b)\land I_n(a-_{\mathbb{Z}}b).
$$
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[ModN as the nZ quotient certificate]
\label{thm:modn-nz-quotient-certificate}
Under the hypotheses of \autoref{def:modn-nz-quotient-presentation}, the quotient-ring certificate at $(R_{\mathbb{Z}},I_n)$ supplies a $\NameCert$ for $\ModNUp(n)$ with exactly the quotient-ring classifier. More precisely,
$$
\NameCert_{\ModNUp(n)}=
\NameCert_{\QuotientRingUp(R_{\mathbb{Z}},I_n)}
$$
as a five-field certificate in the sense of \autoref{def:typed-naming-certificate}, and for all carried endpoints $a,b$,
$$
\mathsf{Classifier}_{\ModNUp(n)}(a,b)
\Longleftrightarrow
\mathsf{Classifier}_{\QuotientRingUp(R_{\mathbb{Z}},I_n)}(a,b)
\Longleftrightarrow
C_{\mathbb{Z}}(a)\land C_{\mathbb{Z}}(b)\land I_n(a-_{\mathbb{Z}}b).
$$
The additive, inverse, and multiplicative operations on $\ModNUp(n)$ are representative-independent by the same quotient-ring descent rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The positive $\NatUp$ witness selects the modulus parameter $n$ and the predicate $I_n$; it does not add a second quotient relation. The certified $\IdealUp$ hypothesis puts $I_n$ in exactly the scope required by \autoref{sec:ideal-certificate} and by the ideal-coset relation of \autoref{def:quotientring-ideal-coset-relation} over the displayed integer ring $R_{\mathbb{Z}}$.

Apply \autoref{thm:quotientring-ideal-coset-equivalence} with $R=R_{\mathbb{Z}}$ and $I=I_n$. This gives reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity for the quotient classifier on carried integer endpoints. Carrier transport for the core $\NameCert$ of \autoref{def:lean-carrier-naming-certificate} is obtained by unfolding $a\equiv_{I_n}b$: the relation itself stores the two endpoint carrier witnesses $C_{\mathbb{Z}}(a)$ and $C_{\mathbb{Z}}(b)$.

The additive and inverse representative-independence rows are \autoref{thm:quotientring-additive-descent} specialized to $R_{\mathbb{Z}}$ and $I_n$. The multiplicative representative-independence row is \autoref{thm:quotientring-multiplication-descends-to-cosets} with the same specialization. Hence the quotient-ring source, pattern, classifier, stability, and ledger fields form the five fields demanded by \autoref{def:typed-naming-certificate}.

By \autoref{def:modn-nz-quotient-presentation}, $\ModNUp(n)$ is this quotient-ring presentation with no classifier-changing map inserted. Therefore every field of $\NameCert_{\ModNUp(n)}$ is the corresponding field of $\NameCert_{\QuotientRingUp(R_{\mathbb{Z}},I_n)}$. In particular, the classifier field is copied. The first displayed equivalence is this field identity, and the second is exactly \autoref{def:quotientring-ideal-coset-relation} unfolded at $R_{\mathbb{Z}}$ and $I_n$.
\end{proof}
