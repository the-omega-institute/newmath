\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-finitewitnessroute-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ interface names the finite route packet that carries closed-observation witness records into Real-and-Completion consumers. It is a concrete $\NameCert$ surface for the passage from a selected observation request through $\StreamNameUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, and $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ readbacks to a $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ or $\RealUp$ seal row, with every transport, continuation, and provenance step present in the finite history.

The route is downstream of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-streamname-namecert}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-regseqrat-namecert}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicratcore-namecert}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-cauchylimitseal-namecert}, and \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}. $\StreamNameUp$ supplies the finite window, $\RegSeqRatUp$ supplies the readback, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ supplies the comparison ledger, $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ supplies the sealed completion boundary, and $\RealUp$ supplies the terminal real-facing seal.

\begin{definition}[Finite witness-route carrier]
\label{def:finite-witness-route-carrier}
A $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $q$ is the closed-observation request row naming the finite witness demand, tolerance scale, and downstream Real-and-Completion consumer;
\item $w$ is the selected $\StreamNameUp$ window inspected for that request;
\item $r$ is the $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback row exposed through the selected window;
\item $d$ is the $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ observation ledger containing the dyadic comparisons, radius splits, and finite arithmetic reads used by the route;
\item $s$ is the $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ or $\RealUp$ seal row that receives the completed finite witness route;
\item $h$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the request row, selected window, regular rational readback, dyadic ledger, seal row, provenance, and local name;
\item $c$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes from request to window, from window to regular rational readback, from readback to dyadic ledger, and from the dyadic ledger to the seal row;
\item $p$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\BHist$, $\StreamNameUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\CauchyLimitSealUp$, $\RealUp$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$;
\item $n$ is the local $\NameCert_{\FiniteWitnessRouteUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier contains no host-level infinite trace, no quotient stream equality, no selected real value, no unrecorded limit, and no consumer route outside the displayed finite packet.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\FiniteWitnessRouteUp}$ surface for \autoref{def:finite-witness-route-carrier} consists exactly of carrier admission, request exposure, selected $\StreamNameUp$ window admission, $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback admission, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ ledger admission, $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ or $\RealUp$ seal handoff, componentwise $\hsame$ transport, displayed $\Cont$ route replay, $\Pkg$ provenance, and the local naming row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from the displayed packet $R$. The rows name the request, selected observation window, rational readback, dyadic ledger, seal, transports, continuations, provenance, and local certificate.

The request and window obligations are read through $q$ and $w$. They ensure that a consumer sees a finite observation demand and exactly the selected $\StreamNameUp$ window used to answer it.

The readback and ledger obligations are read through $r$ and $d$. The regular rational row supplies the observed sequence data, and the dyadic row supplies the comparison and radius ledger used by the route.

The remaining rows $s,h,c,p,n$ expose the seal handoff, componentwise transports, route replay, package provenance, and local naming. Since every coordinate of the packet is accounted for, these obligations exhaust the local certificate surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route non-escape]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-non-escape}
Every accepted $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ carrier exports witness information only through the finite rows
$$
q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n.
$$
It exports no host-level infinite trace, no quotient stream equality, no selected real value, no unrecorded limit, and no Real-and-Completion consumer route that bypasses the displayed $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted carrier as $R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n)$. The definition lists every exported row of the finite witness route.

The observation side is exhausted by $q,w,r,d$. These rows record the request, selected stream window, regular rational readback, and dyadic comparison ledger.

The consumer side is exhausted by $s,h,c,p,n$. These rows record the seal handoff, transport, continuation, provenance, and local name.

No coordinate remains from which to read an infinite trace, quotient stream equality, selected real value, unrecorded limit, or bypass route. The accepted packet therefore has the stated non-escape boundary.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route transport stability]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-transport-stability}
Let
$$
R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n)
$$
be an accepted $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ carrier. Any $\hsame$ transport recorded in $h$ preserves the request row, selected $\StreamNameUp$ window, $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ ledger, seal row, continuation routes, package provenance, and local name as one finite route packet for the same Real-and-Completion consumer.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the transport row $h$. Its components are indexed by the coordinates of the carrier, so each displayed row has a matching transport witness.

Transporting $q,w,r,d$ preserves the finite observation side of the route: the request, selected window, regular rational readback, and dyadic ledger stay attached to the same finite demand.

Transporting $s,c,p,n$ preserves the consumer side of the route: the seal handoff, continuation replay, provenance, and local certificate stay attached to that demand. Thus the transported data is again one finite witness-route packet for the same consumer boundary.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route consumer factorization]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-consumer-factorization}
Every $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ or $\RealUp$ consumer that accepts a $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ packet factors through the selected $\StreamNameUp$ window $w$, the $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback $r$, the $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ ledger $d$, the seal row $s$, the displayed transports $h$, the continuation routes $c$, the package provenance $p$, and the local $\NameCert_{\FiniteWitnessRouteUp}$ row $n$. The consumer receives the finite witness route, not an independent route ledger.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with an accepted packet $R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n)$. The request row $q$ names the finite demand and the downstream consumer boundary.

The only observation path in the carrier runs through $w,r,d$. The selected stream window exposes the inspected records, the regular rational row exposes their sequence readback, and the dyadic ledger exposes the comparison data.

The handoff to the consumer then runs through $s,h,c,p,n$. These rows seal, transport, replay, package, and name the same finite route. Hence any accepting consumer factors through the displayed packet rather than through an independent ledger.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route request-window admission]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-request-window-admission}
Let
$$
R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n)
$$
be an accepted $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ packet. Every finite witness demand read by a Real-and-Completion consumer must pass through the request row $q$ and the selected $\StreamNameUp$ window row $w$. No consumer may read a route from $R$ without first exposing those two rows.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:finite-witness-route-carrier}. The row $q$ names the finite witness demand, tolerance scale, and downstream consumer, while $w$ names the selected stream window inspected for that request.

Apply \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-namecert-obligations}. Its request exposure and selected-window admission clauses make $q$ and $w$ part of the local $\NameCert_{\FiniteWitnessRouteUp}$ surface.

Thus any accepted consumer route that reads the finite witness demand must open $q$ and then the selected window $w$. A route that omits either row is not a read of the displayed packet.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route regular readback admission]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-regular-readback-admission}
For an accepted $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ packet
$$
R=(q,w,r,d,s,h,c,p,n),

every accepted regular rational readback in the route is exposed through the same selected $\StreamNameUp$ window $w$ and through the $\RegSeqRatUp$ row $r$. The dyadic comparison ledger $d$ records the finite comparison data, and the route imports neither quotient stream equality nor a selected real.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}

Use \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-namecert-obligations} to attach those three rows to the local certificate surface. The readback admission row is therefore tied to the same selected window that answers the request.

Finally apply \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-non-escape}. It excludes quotient stream equality, selected real values, unrecorded limits, and host-level infinite traces from the accepted packet, so the regular readback remains a finite row of the displayed route.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route consumer route exactness]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-consumer-route-exactness}
Every Real-and-Completion consumer route that accepts a $\FiniteWitnessRouteUp$ packet factors through the displayed rows
$$

$$

\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-consumer-factorization}. It sends every accepting $\CauchyLimitSealUp$ or $\RealUp$ consumer through the selected window, regular readback, dyadic ledger, seal, transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows of the packet.

Attach \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-request-window-admission}. The consumer route cannot reach the observation side without the request row $q$ and the selected window row $w$.

Next use \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-regular-readback-admission}. The readback and dyadic comparison rows are the finite rows $r$ and $d$, not quotient stream equality or a selected real value.

The seal row $s$ is therefore downstream of $q,w,r,d$, and the remaining rows $h,c,p,n$ preserve, replay, package, and name the same route. Hence the accepting consumer factors through the displayed dependency order.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Finite witness-route selected-window seal uniqueness]
\label{thm:finite-witness-route-selected-window-seal-uniqueness}
Let
$$

Inside the packet there is no second selected window, readback, dyadic ledger, or seal handoff available for that consumer route.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The request-window admission theorem fixes the route entrance. By \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-request-window-admission}, every accepted read of the finite witness demand opens the request row $q$ and the selected window row $w$.

The regular readback admission theorem then fixes the observation rows below that window. By \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-regular-readback-admission}, the readback and dyadic comparison data are exactly the displayed rows $r$ and $d$, and the packet imports neither quotient stream equality nor a selected real value.

Finally apply \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-consumer-route-exactness}. The consumer route reaches the seal row $s$ only after $q,w,r,d$ have been exposed in that dependency order. The non-escape boundary of \autoref{thm:finite-witness-route-non-escape} excludes an external equality, selected real, or route ledger that could furnish a second handoff, so the seal read is unique to the displayed packet rows.
\end{proof}
