\begin{theorem}[Continuation morphism endpoint cycle boundary]
\label{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary}
For histories $a,b,c:\mathsf{BHist}$, let
$left:\mathsf{ContinuationMorphism}(a,b)$ and
$right:\mathsf{ContinuationMorphism}(b,c)$. If the outer endpoints close as
$\hsame(a,c)$, then both displayed tails and both adjacent endpoint pairs
collapse:
$$
\hsame(\mathsf{tail}(left),\emp)\land
\hsame(\mathsf{tail}(right),\emp)\land
\hsame(a,b)\land\hsame(b,c).
$$
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Transport the right continuation relation across the outer endpoint comparison
to obtain a return continuation from $b$ to $a$. Mutual-extension empty-tail
inversion applied to the left relation and this transported return relation
forces both displayed tails to be empty. Transporting each original relation
along its empty tail and comparing with the corresponding right-unit
continuation gives the adjacent endpoint comparisons.
\end{proof}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.CategoryUp.ContinuationMorphism\_comp\_endpoint\_cycle\_boundary}

\begin{theorem}[Continuation morphism endpoint cycle boundary iff]
\label{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary-iff}
For histories $a,b,c:\mathsf{BHist}$, let
$left:\mathsf{ContinuationMorphism}(a,b)$ and
$right:\mathsf{ContinuationMorphism}(b,c)$. The outer endpoints close exactly
when both displayed tails are empty and both adjacent endpoint pairs collapse:
$$
\hsame(a,c)\Longleftrightarrow
\hsame(\mathsf{tail}(left),\emp)\land
\hsame(\mathsf{tail}(right),\emp)\land
\hsame(a,b)\land\hsame(b,c).
$$
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The forward implication is the endpoint-cycle boundary theorem. Conversely,
compose the adjacent endpoint comparisons $\hsame(a,b)$ and $\hsame(b,c)$ to
obtain $\hsame(a,c)$.
\end{proof}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.CategoryUp.ContinuationMorphism\_comp\_endpoint\_cycle\_boundary\_iff}

\begin{lemma}[Continuation morphism endpoint-cycle bridge determinacy]
\label{lem:continuation-morphism-endpoint-cycle-bridge-determinacy}
For histories $a,b,c:\BHist$, let $left:a\to b$ and $right:b\to c$ be
continuation morphisms, and write $\ell:\BHist$ and $r:\BHist$ for the left
and right continuation-tail history witnesses associated to $left$ and $right$.
The consumer-facing endpoint-cycle bridge for this composable pair is determined
by the outer endpoint row and the four local boundary rows
$$
\hsame(\ell,\emp),\qquad
\hsame(r,\emp),\qquad
\hsame(a,b),\qquad
\hsame(b,c).
$$
Concretely, every accepted route, transport, readback, or handoff from the
outer cycle to boundary data factors through the projection supplied by
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary}, with transport
constrained by \autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-hsame-tail-transport},
and every accepted route, transport, readback, or handoff from boundary data
back to the outer cycle factors through the reverse implication supplied by
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary-iff}, with the
same transport constraint. Hence the bridge exports exactly the equivalence
$$
\hsame(a,c)\Longleftrightarrow
\hsame(\ell,\emp)\land
\hsame(r,\emp)\land
\hsame(a,b)\land
\hsame(b,c),
$$
and it exports no separate composite-tail coordinate, alternate middle endpoint
coordinate, hidden continuation tail, ambient category carrier, host equality,
or external handoff.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Assume first that a consumer reads the bridge from the outer endpoint cycle.
The only local theorem with that source row is
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary}. Applying it
to $left$, $right$, and the row $\hsame(a,c)$ gives precisely the four displayed
boundary rows: the empty left tail, the empty right tail, the source-middle
endpoint comparison, and the middle-target endpoint comparison. Any route,
transport, readback, or handoff that starts from the outer cycle and reaches
consumer boundary data is therefore one of these four projections, or a
repackaging of their conjunction, with its transport component governed by
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-hsame-tail-transport}.

Conversely, assume that a consumer starts from the four displayed boundary
rows. The reverse direction of
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary-iff} composes
the adjacent endpoint comparisons $\hsame(a,b)$ and $\hsame(b,c)$ and returns
exactly the outer endpoint row $\hsame(a,c)$. Thus any readback from the
boundary packet to the endpoint cycle factors through that reverse implication.

The forward boundary theorem and the two-way boundary theorem mention no data
beyond $\hsame(a,c)$ and the four displayed rows. A proposed coordinate outside
this list is not a conjunct produced by the forward theorem and is not a premise
consumed by the reverse direction of the equivalence. Such a coordinate has no
local factor through which this endpoint-cycle bridge can expose it to a
consumer.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Continuation morphism endpoint-cycle boundary consumer determinacy]
\label{cor:continuation-morphism-endpoint-cycle-boundary-consumer-determinacy}
For histories $a,b,c:\BHist$, let $left:a\to b$ and $right:b\to c$ be
continuation morphisms, and let $\ell:\BHist$ and $r:\BHist$ be their
associated left and right continuation-tail witnesses. Once the outer endpoint
cycle row and the four boundary rows
$$
\hsame(a,c),\qquad
\hsame(\ell,\emp),\qquad
\hsame(r,\emp),\qquad
\hsame(a,b),\qquad
\hsame(b,c)
$$
are fixed, every accepted consumer-facing use of this endpoint-cycle boundary
surface has the same chapter-named finite data. In the outward direction the
route is the projection of the four boundary rows supplied by
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary}; in the
readback direction the route is the reverse implication of
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary-iff}; and every
normal form or exactness decision exposed to a consumer is one of those five
rows or their displayed conjunction
$$
\hsame(a,c)\Longleftrightarrow
\hsame(\ell,\emp)\land
\hsame(r,\emp)\land
\hsame(a,b)\land
\hsame(b,c).
$$
Thus two accepted consumers over the same fixed rows cannot choose different
finite routes, different boundary normal forms, or different exactness
decisions, and no accepted use can introduce an unlisted hidden coordinate,
ambient category carrier, host equality, or external handoff.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
The endpoint-cycle bridge determinacy lemma lists the complete consumer-facing
surface for the composable pair. Its outward clause says that any accepted
route, transport, readback, or handoff starting from the outer endpoint cycle
factors through the projection supplied by
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary}. That theorem
has exactly one displayed boundary packet for the fixed pair: the empty left
tail row, the empty right tail row, the source-middle endpoint row, and the
middle-target endpoint row. Hence every outward consumer route has the same
finite target packet and the same boundary normal form.

The same lemma also gives the only accepted readback from boundary data to the
outer cycle: it factors through the reverse implication of
\autoref{thm:continuation-morphism-comp-endpoint-cycle-boundary-iff}. With the
four boundary rows fixed, that reverse implication returns exactly the row
$\hsame(a,c)$, so every readback normal form and exactness decision about the
endpoint cycle is determined by the same displayed equivalence.

It remains to exclude further consumer choices. By
\autoref{lem:continuation-morphism-endpoint-cycle-bridge-determinacy}, the
bridge exports no separate composite-tail coordinate, no alternate middle
endpoint coordinate, no hidden continuation tail, no ambient category carrier,
no host equality, and no external handoff. A proposed consumer coordinate
outside the five fixed rows is therefore neither a conjunct produced by the
forward boundary theorem nor a premise consumed by the reverse readback. It has
no local factor through the endpoint-cycle boundary bridge, so it is not an
accepted route, normal form, or exactness decision for this surface.
\end{proof}
