\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-dimliftboundary-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ packet turns the AxisZeckendorf dimension-lift refusal into a finite boundary certificate. It records the carry rows, normal-address rows, cannot-claim rows, full-axis context, real-facing refusal, transport routes, package provenance, and local naming row that a later dimension-lift acceptance flow must inspect before it may claim any primitive dimension reading.

The boundary packet consumes the refusal surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-axiszeckendorfcannotclaim-namecert}, the carry row of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zcarry-namecert}, the finite normal-address surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-zeckendorf-normal-namecert}, and the $\RealUp$ handoff of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}. These sibling chapters supply displayed dependency rows for the boundary packet, not primitive dimension data.

\begin{definition}[DimLiftBoundary carrier]
\label{def:dimlift-boundary-carrier}
A $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
D=(Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $Z$ is the $\ZCarryUp$ carry-classifier ledger over the relevant AxisZeckendorf histories;
\item $N$ is the $\ZeckendorfNormalUp$ normal-address row for the same finite histories;
\item $A$ is the $\AxisZeckendorfCannotClaimUp$ refusal row containing the dimension-lift non-claim;
\item $F$ is the $\FullAxisUp$ context row through which the carry and normal-address data are read;
\item $R$ is the $\RealUp$ refusal boundary, recording that no real, metric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, or geometric dimension row is exported;
\item $H$ stores componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the carry row, normal-address row, cannot-claim row, full-axis context, real-facing boundary, and local naming row;
\item $C$ stores the finite $\Cont$ routes by which consumers read the displayed rows from the packet boundary;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\ZCarryUp$, $\ZeckendorfNormalUp$, $\AxisZeckendorfCannotClaimUp$, $\FullAxisUp$, $\RealUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$;
\item $Q$ is the local $\NameCert_{\DimLiftBoundaryUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier is a boundary packet, not a dimension object. It exposes the finite rows that explain why a dimension-lift reading is blocked, while leaving any accepted dimension interface to a separate certificate.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[DimLiftBoundary classifier]
\label{def:dimlift-boundary-classifier}
For accepted packets $D=(Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q)$ and $D'=(Z',N',A',F',R',H',C',P',Q')$, the classifier $\mathsf{DimLiftBoundaryClassifier}(D,D')$ holds exactly when the displayed $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ rows transport $Z$ to $Z'$, $N$ to $N'$, $A$ to $A'$, $F$ to $F'$, $R$ to $R'$, $P$ to $P'$, and $Q$ to $Q'$ componentwise. The classifier preserves carry and normal-address rows while blocking any read that treats those rows as a primitive dimension.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:dimlift-boundary-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\DimLiftBoundaryUp}$ surface for \autoref{def:dimlift-boundary-carrier} consists of five finite rows: carrier admission for $D$; classifier stability for $Z,N,A,F,R$ under the displayed transports $H$; route exactness for the consumer reads in $C$; package provenance through $P$; and local naming exactness for $Q$. These rows preserve the carry and normal-address evidence while keeping primitive dimension readings outside the certificate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection of the finite tuple $D$. Each dependency row named in the statement is a visible coordinate of the same $\BHist$ packet.

Classifier stability is componentwise. The $\hsame$ table $H$ and the routes $C$ transport the carry ledger, normal-address row, cannot-claim row, full-axis context, and real-facing refusal boundary without adding a dimension coordinate.

The package and naming rows then repack exactly those coordinates. Since the carrier has no row for geometric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, metric, or real dimension data, the NameCert surface is exhausted by the boundary packet.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary non-escape]
\label{thm:dimlift-boundary-non-escape}
Every consumer read exported by an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier factors through the displayed rows $Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q$. A read that asks to interpret the carry classifier or normal-address row as a primitive dimension lands in the refusal boundary $A$ and $R$, not in a dimension-valued row.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the consumer request through the finite routes in $C$. If it asks for carry evidence, normal-address data, the cannot-claim row, the full-axis context, transport, provenance, or local naming, the corresponding coordinate of $D$ supplies that read.

If the request asks for a primitive dimension interpretation, no coordinate of $D$ has that type. The only visible rows governing the request are the cannot-claim row $A$ and the real-facing refusal boundary $R$.

Thus the request is recorded as boundary evidence. It cannot escape as a geometric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, metric, or real dimension object.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary public export]
\label{thm:dimlift-boundary-public-export}
The carrier of \autoref{def:dimlift-boundary-carrier}, the classifier of \autoref{def:dimlift-boundary-classifier}, and the local obligations of \autoref{thm:dimlift-boundary-namecert-obligations} export a public boundary packet for later $\DimLiftUp$ acceptance-flow work. Downstream consumers may read the displayed $\ZCarryUp$, $\ZeckendorfNormalUp$, $\AxisZeckendorfCannotClaimUp$, $\FullAxisUp$, $\RealUp$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows, but they receive no primitive dimension interface from this chapter.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The carrier supplies the public rows $Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q$. The classifier preserves those rows componentwise, so a downstream consumer can transport the boundary packet without changing its visible dependency surface.

The NameCert obligations identify the exact admission, transport, route, provenance, and naming rows. The non-escape theorem then blocks every request that would turn the carry or normal-address evidence into a primitive dimension object.

Therefore the public export is a boundary carrier for future acceptance-flow chapters. It supplies the refusal surface that such chapters must consume before supplying any separate dimension certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary axis refusal obligations]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-axis-refusal-obligations}
For an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier $D=(Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q)$, the AxisZeckendorf-facing refusal surface consists exactly of the $\ZCarryUp$ carry rows in $Z$, the $\ZeckendorfNormalUp$ normal-address rows in $N$, the $\AxisZeckendorfCannotClaimUp$ cannot-claim rows in $A$, the $\FullAxisUp$ context row in $F$, and the transport, route, provenance, and local naming rows $H,C,P,Q$. The surface refuses any export that reads $Z$ or $N$ as a primitive geometric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, metric, or real dimension.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier and read the AxisZeckendorf coordinates $Z,N,A,F$. The carry and normal-address rows are visible only inside the full-axis context, and the cannot-claim row records the refusal attached to that context.

The remaining rows $H,C,P,Q$ transport, route, package, and name this same boundary. None of them supplies a dimension-valued coordinate or a classifier into one.

Therefore every axis-facing consumer receives the carry and normal-address evidence as refusal evidence. It cannot repack the same rows as a primitive dimension interface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary Real handoff non-escape]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-real-handoff-nonescape}
Every $\RealUp$-facing read of an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier factors through the displayed refusal boundary $R$ together with $Z,N,A,F,H,C,P,Q$. The handoff may report that the AxisZeckendorf carry and normal-address rows have no real-dimension export, but it may not produce a real coordinate, real metric dimension, host-real equality row, or completion-facing dimension object.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the real-facing coordinate $R$. By definition of the carrier, $R$ is reached only after the carry rows, normal-address rows, cannot-claim rows, and full-axis context have already been displayed.

The continuation routes in $C$ and transports in $H$ replay the same refusal packet for a real-facing consumer. Package provenance $P$ and local naming $Q$ record the dependency surface without adding any real-valued coordinate.

Thus the real handoff is a non-escape boundary. It can expose refusal of a real dimension reading, but it cannot export a real dimension object or any host-real comparison datum.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary Zeckendorf carrier scope]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-zeckendorf-carrier-scope}
The Zeckendorf scope of an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier is the finite packet $Z,N,A,F,H,C,P,Q$ together with the real-facing refusal row $R$. It is closed under the displayed $\hsame$ transports and $\Cont$ routes for AxisZeckendorf carry rows, normal-address rows, cannot-claim rows, full-axis context, RealUp refusal, package provenance, and NameCert rows, and it contains no primitive geometric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, metric, or real dimension carrier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the finite packet componentwise. The Zeckendorf data are exactly $Z,N,A,F$, and the only consumer-visible extensions are the transport, route, provenance, local naming, and real-facing refusal rows.

Closure under the displayed transports follows because $H$ names the componentwise $\hsame$ rows for the same coordinates. Closure under consumer access follows because $C$ lists the only continuation routes out of the packet.

Since no coordinate of $D$ is a primitive dimension carrier, the Zeckendorf scope remains a carrier for refusal evidence. The scope ends at the listed boundary rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary route-exactness refusal]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-cannot-claim-route-exactness}
For an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier $D=(Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q)$, every consumer request that tries to read $Z$ or $N$ as a primitive dimension must pass through the cannot-claim row $A$ and the real-facing refusal row $R$. The route table $C$ and transport table $H$ may expose carry evidence, normal-address evidence, full-axis context, provenance, and local naming, but they do not expose a dimension-valued row.

Thus route exactness for primitive-dimension requests is a refusal route. The exact target of such a request is the displayed pair $A,R$, not a geometric, topological, vector-space, homological, projective, metric, or real dimension object.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the request through the continuation table $C$. If it asks for carry, normal-address, full-axis, transport, package, or naming evidence, the corresponding coordinate of the carrier supplies that displayed row.

If the same request asks for a primitive dimension interpretation of $Z$ or $N$, no carrier coordinate has that type. The only displayed rows governing that request are the cannot-claim row $A$ and the real-facing refusal boundary $R$.

Transport through $H$ preserves this boundary componentwise. It can move the refusal packet along accepted presentations, but it cannot add a new dimension row to the carrier.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary axis normal-address lock]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-axis-normal-address-lock}
For an accepted $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ carrier $D=(Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q)$, the rows $Z$ and $N$ are locked to AxisZeckendorf carry and normal-address evidence under the full-axis context $F$, the transport rows $H$, the consumer routes $C$, the package provenance $P$, and the local naming row $Q$. Any read of $Z$ or $N$ must preserve this axis-normal address and cannot reinterpret it as a primitive dimension carrier.

The lock is local to the displayed packet: $Z$ supplies carry evidence, $N$ supplies normal-address evidence, $F$ supplies the full-axis context, and $H,C,P,Q$ supply only transport, route, provenance, and naming evidence.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier rows. The carry coordinate $Z$ and normal-address coordinate $N$ occur together with the full-axis context $F$, so each read of either row is already addressed inside the AxisZeckendorf packet.

Use the Zeckendorf carrier scope of \autoref{thm:dimliftboundary-zeckendorf-carrier-scope}. It closes the displayed packet under the $\hsame$ transports and $\Cont$ routes while keeping primitive dimension carriers outside the scope.

The package row $P$ and local naming row $Q$ record the same dependency boundary. They name the axis-normal evidence already present; they do not provide a separate dimension row or classifier.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DimLiftBoundary downstream acceptance boundary]
\label{thm:dimliftboundary-downstream-acceptance-boundary}
Any later $\DimLiftUp$ acceptance certificate that exports a dimension row must first consume the displayed $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ refusal packet $Z,N,A,F,R,H,C,P,Q$. In particular, the later certificate must account for the cannot-claim row $A$, the real-facing refusal row $R$, the axis-normal lock on $Z,N$, and the transport, route, provenance, and naming boundary $H,C,P,Q$ before it exports any separate dimension evidence.

Consequently $\DimLiftBoundaryUp$ is a required refusal boundary for downstream acceptance work, not an acceptance certificate and not a primitive dimension source.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with \autoref{thm:dimliftboundary-cannot-claim-route-exactness}. It forces primitive-dimension requests against the boundary carrier through the refusal rows $A$ and $R$.

Next use \autoref{thm:dimliftboundary-axis-normal-address-lock}. It fixes $Z$ and $N$ as AxisZeckendorf carry and normal-address evidence under $F,H,C,P,Q$ rather than as dimension-valued rows.

A later acceptance certificate may add a separate dimension row only after this packet has been consumed as refusal evidence. Without that consumption, the later certificate would be reading through a row that the present carrier explicitly does not export.
\end{proof}
