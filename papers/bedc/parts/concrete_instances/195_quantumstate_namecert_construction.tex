\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\QuantumStateUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-quantumstate-namecert}

The quantum-state interface $\QuantumStateUp$ packages a unit vector in a Hilbert space carrier together with the equivalence under global phase. It depends on $\NameCert_{\HilbertUp}$ (\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hilbert-namecert}) and $\NameCert_{\ProjectiveSpaceUp}$ (\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-projectivespace-namecert}).

\section{Carrier definition}
\label{sec:quantumstate-carrier}

\begin{definition}[QuantumState BHist ray packet]
\label{def:quantumstate-bhist-ray-packet}
A $\QuantumStateUp$ carried row is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
Q=(H,P,u,r,\phi,\rho,\lambda)
$$
whose entries are read as follows. The row $H$ is a $\HilbertUp$ dependency row from $\NameCert_{\HilbertUp}$. The row $P$ is a $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ dependency row over the same displayed vector surface. The endpoint $u$ is a unit-vector row in the Hilbert carrier of $H$. The row $r$ records the projective ray projection sending $u$ to the carried projective endpoint in $P$. The ledger $\phi$ records the finite global-phase orbit witnesses consumed by the projective classifier. The provenance row $\rho$ is the $\Pkg$ row naming the Hilbert and projective dependency packages, and $\lambda$ is the $\Cont$ and $\hsame$ transport ledger tying the Hilbert carrier, unit-vector endpoint, ray projection, and projective endpoint to the same displayed vector surface.

Thus $\QuantumStateUp$ carries no host quotient object. It carries only the finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet whose public endpoints are the Hilbert unit-vector row and the projective ray row, together with the ledgers needed to transport them through the consumed $\NameCert_{\HilbertUp}$ and $\NameCert_{\ProjectiveSpaceUp}$ surfaces.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[QuantumState phase-orbit classifier]
\label{def:quantumstate-phase-orbit-classifier}
For $\QuantumStateUp$ packets
$$
Q=(H,P,u,r,\phi,\rho,\lambda)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
Q'=(H',P',u',r',\phi',\rho',\lambda'),
$$
the classifier $Q\sim_{\QuantumStateUp}Q'$ holds exactly when the following componentwise rows are present:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $H$ and $H'$ are related by the consumed $\HilbertUp$ classifier;
  \item $P$ and $P'$ are related by the consumed $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ scaling-orbit classifier over the transported vector surface;
  \item $u$ and $u'$ are related as unit-vector endpoint rows over the related Hilbert carriers;
  \item $r$ and $r'$ are related as ray-projection rows landing in the related projective endpoints;
  \item $\phi$ and $\phi'$ record the same finite global-phase orbit ledger after the projective classifier witnesses are consumed;
  \item $\rho$ and $\rho'$ are related by $\msame$ as $\Pkg$ provenance rows;
  \item $\lambda$ and $\lambda'$ are related by $\hsame$ as $\Cont$ transport ledgers.
\end{enumerate}
Equivalently, the quantum-state classifier is the componentwise classifier induced by the Hilbert row, projective row, unit-vector endpoint, ray projection, phase-orbit ledger, package provenance, and transport ledger; it does not identify density matrices, measurement records, spectral rows, or channel data.
\end{definition}

\section{The certificate}
\label{sec:quantumstate-certificate}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumState Hilbert unit-vector surface]
\label{thm:quantumstate-hilbert-unit-vector-surface}
Every carried $\QuantumStateUp$ packet of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-ray-packet} projects to a public $\HilbertUp$ endpoint together with a unit-vector row over that endpoint. Conversely, any $\HilbertUp$ dependency row $H$, unit-vector endpoint $u$ over $H$, projective row $P$ over the same displayed vector surface, ray-projection row $r$, phase-orbit ledger $\phi$, $\Pkg$ provenance $\rho$, and $\Cont/\hsame$ ledger $\lambda$ repack as a carried $\QuantumStateUp$ packet when they satisfy the component requirements of \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-ray-packet}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the finite packet $Q=(H,P,u,r,\phi,\rho,\lambda)$ onto its first and third coordinates. The first coordinate is already a $\HilbertUp$ dependency row, and the third coordinate is required by the packet definition to be a unit-vector endpoint over that Hilbert carrier.

For the converse direction, place the displayed data back into the seven-coordinate $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet in the order fixed by \autoref{def:quantumstate-bhist-ray-packet}. The Hilbert surface is supplied by $\NameCert_{\HilbertUp}$, and the unit-vector endpoint remains attached to the same Hilbert carrier through the $\Cont/\hsame$ ledger.

The construction does not pass through equality of a host quotient. It exposes only the Hilbert row and unit-vector row, while density matrices, measurement rows, spectral data, and channel structure remain outside this certificate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumState projective phase surface]
\label{thm:quantumstate-projectivespace-phase-surface}
Every carried $\QuantumStateUp$ packet projects to a public $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ endpoint and a finite global-phase orbit ledger. The projective endpoint is the ray row $r$ landing in the dependency row $P$, and the visible comparison of two such endpoints is exactly the scaling-orbit comparison consumed from \autoref{thm:projectivespace-scaling-orbit-classifier-obligation} together with the phase ledger component of \autoref{def:quantumstate-phase-orbit-classifier}.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project a carried packet onto the projective dependency row $P$, the ray-projection row $r$, and the phase ledger $\phi$. The packet definition requires $P$ to be a $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ row over the same displayed vector surface as the Hilbert row, and $r$ records the image of the unit-vector endpoint in that projective row.

Apply the $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ public surface of \autoref{thm:projectivespace-punctured-carrier-obligation}, \autoref{thm:projectivespace-scaling-orbit-classifier-obligation}, and \autoref{thm:projectivespace-ledger-and-exactness-obligation}. These theorems make the scaling-orbit comparison public while keeping representative choices and scaling chains as ledger data.

The $\QuantumStateUp$ phase ledger is the specialization of that scaling ledger to unit scalar phase witnesses. No density operator, spectral decomposition, measurement outcome, or channel action is added to the projective surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[QuantumState public ray certificate surface]
\label{thm:quantumstate-public-ray-certificate-surface}
The public $\QuantumStateUp$ certificate surface consists exactly of the $\HilbertUp$ unit-vector projection of \autoref{thm:quantumstate-hilbert-unit-vector-surface}, the $\ProjectiveSpaceUp$ ray and phase projection of \autoref{thm:quantumstate-projectivespace-phase-surface}, the $\Pkg$ provenance row, and the $\Cont/\hsame$ transport ledger. Its classifier is the componentwise classifier of \autoref{def:quantumstate-phase-orbit-classifier}. Therefore two public quantum-state packets classify together exactly when the Hilbert dependency rows, projective dependency rows, unit-vector rows, ray rows, phase ledgers, package provenance rows, and transport ledgers classify componentwise.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with a carried packet and project its seven coordinates. The previous two theorems identify the Hilbert unit-vector surface and the projective phase surface. The remaining coordinates are precisely the $\Pkg$ provenance row and the $\Cont/\hsame$ transport ledger.

For soundness of the public classifier, unfold \autoref{def:quantumstate-phase-orbit-classifier}. Each visible comparison is one of the consumed component comparisons, and the hidden phase data are used only as the ledger certifying the projective scaling-orbit comparison.

For completeness, assume the seven component comparisons listed in \autoref{def:quantumstate-phase-orbit-classifier}. Repack them in the displayed packet order and fold the classifier definition. The result is the public ray certificate comparison for $\QuantumStateUp$.

The certificate surface is restricted to pure ray data. It does not claim host quotient equality, density-matrix structure, measurement rows, spectral data, or quantum-channel structure.
\end{proof}
