\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\HausdorffReflectionUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-hausdorff-reflection-namecert}
\origin{human}

Following Bourbaki and Kelley on separated reflection, the $\HausdorffReflectionUp$ packet records the L10 boundary where a metric or uniform carrier is read through displayed zero-distance separation data before entering the completion route. It sits after the metric carrier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert} and the separated metric packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-separatedmetric-namecert}, and before the separated completion surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hausdorffcompletion-namecert} and the reflection packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-completionreflection-namecert}. The carrier keeps separated reflection quotient-free: it records classifier collapse as finite rows, not as host equality, selected representatives, quotient completion, trichotomy, countable choice, or ambient completeness.

\begin{definition}[HausdorffReflection carrier]
\label{def:hausdorff-reflection-carrier}
A $\HausdorffReflectionUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
R=(M,S,Z,U,A,L,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $M$ is the source $\MetricUp$ row whose distance observations are visible before any separated read is consumed.
\item $S$ is the $\SeparatedMetricUp$ row that records zero-distance separation over the same metric carrier.
\item $Z$ is the finite zero-distance classifier ledger used to identify reflection-stable reads.
\item $U$ is the uniform or entourage-facing route through which later completion consumers inspect the separated carrier.
\item $A$ is the downstream handoff row to $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ and $\CompletionReflectionUp$.
\item $L$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transport for the metric, separated, zero-distance, uniform, handoff, provenance, and local naming rows.
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ replay of the route
$$
M,S,Z \longmapsto U \longmapsto A .
$$
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\MetricUp$, $\SeparatedMetricUp$, $\HausdorffCompletionUp$, $\CompletionReflectionUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\HausdorffReflectionUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier admits exactly these rows. It has no coordinate for quotient classes, host equality of points, selected representatives, trichotomy, countable choice, or ambient separated completion.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[HausdorffReflection NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:hausdorff-reflection-namecert-obligations}
For an accepted $\HausdorffReflectionUp$ carrier $R=(M,S,Z,U,A,L,C,P,N)$, the local $\NameCert_{\HausdorffReflectionUp}$ surface consists exactly of the following finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission for the metric, separated metric, zero-distance ledger, uniform route, completion handoff, transport, replay, provenance, and local naming rows;
\item metric source exactness, requiring every reflected read to expose $M$ before the separated metric row $S$ or zero-distance ledger $Z$ is consumed;
\item separated classifier stability, requiring the zero-distance ledger $Z$ to be transported only through the componentwise $\hsame$ row $L$ and displayed replay row $C$;
\item completion handoff, requiring every completion-facing consumer to pass through $U$ and $A$ before reading $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ or $\CompletionReflectionUp$ data;
\item non-escape, excluding quotient classes, host point equality, selected representatives, trichotomy, countable choice, ambient completeness, Lean verification, and bridge checking.
\end{enumerate}
No NameCert obligation is stored outside $M,S,Z,U,A,L,C,P,N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from \autoref{def:hausdorff-reflection-carrier}. The accepted packet exposes exactly the metric source, separated metric row, zero-distance ledger, uniform route, downstream handoff, transport, replay, provenance, and local naming rows.

Metric source exactness starts at $M$. A reflected read cannot inspect $S$ or $Z$ until the metric observations to be separated have been displayed.

Separated classifier stability is finite. The row $Z$ records the zero-distance classifier ledger, while $L$ and $C$ transport and replay that ledger without adding a quotient class or host equality proof.

The completion handoff is confined to $U$ and $A$. These rows place the separated carrier on the route consumed by \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-hausdorffcompletion-namecert} and \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-completionreflection-namecert}. Since the carrier has no excluded host-side coordinate, the listed rows exhaust the local NameCert surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[HausdorffReflection separated handoff]
\label{thm:hausdorff-reflection-separated-handoff}
Every $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ or $\CompletionReflectionUp$ consumer of an accepted $\HausdorffReflectionUp$ carrier receives separated data only through the finite route
$$
\MetricUp,\ \SeparatedMetricUp,\ Z,\ U,\ A,\ L,\ C,\ P,\ \NameCert_{\HausdorffReflectionUp}.
$$
The route makes the separated boundary explicit before completion data are consumed. It does not export quotient completion classes, host point equality, selected representatives, trichotomy, countable choice, or ambient completeness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with a completion-facing consumer of $R$. The consumer must first read the metric source row $M$ and the separated metric row $S$, because those are the only carrier coordinates that expose source observations and separated classification.

The zero-distance ledger $Z$ is then replayed through $U$ and $A$ toward the downstream completion packets. Componentwise transport through $L$ and displayed continuation through $C$ preserve the same finite route.

Finally $P$ and $N$ record provenance and local naming. Thus the handoff to $\HausdorffCompletionUp$ and $\CompletionReflectionUp$ is exactly the displayed separated route, with no path to quotient classes, host equality, selected representatives, choice data, or ambient completeness.
\end{proof}
