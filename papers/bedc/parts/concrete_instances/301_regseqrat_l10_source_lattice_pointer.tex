\begin{theorem}[RegSeqRat L10 source lattice pointer]
\label{thm:regseqrat-l10-source-lattice-pointer}
Every accepted $\RegSeqRatUp$ carrier consumes the finite observation schedules
of $\StreamNameUp$ from
\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-streamname-namecert} and the endpoint and
radius ledgers of $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ from
\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicratcore-namecert} before it can be
consumed by the $\RealUp$ seal surface of
\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}. The source lattice is therefore
the visible route
$$
\StreamNameUp,\ \DyadicRatCoreUp \longrightarrow \RegSeqRatUp \longrightarrow \RealUp .
$$
Along this route the accepted packet carries only scheduled finite stream
windows, dyadic endpoint rows, dyadic radius rows, finite dyadic-distance
ledgers, regularity ledgers, classifier rows, $\hsame$ comparisons, displayed
$\Cont$ routes, $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance, and local $\NameCert$ rows. It
does not expose quotient streams, host real equality, selected limits, ambient
completeness, or a hidden completion carrier.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start from the carrier definition in \autoref{def:regseqrat-stream-carrier}.
Its stored coordinates are the scheduled finite windows supplied by
$\StreamNameUp$ together with the $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ endpoint and radius rows
attached to those windows.

Next read the classifier in \autoref{def:regseqrat-classifier}. The classifier
adds only finite dyadic-tolerance rows over the same scheduled observations:
endpoint rows, radius rows, finite distance ledgers, regularity rows, and the
displayed provenance needed to transport them.

The exact handoff theorem
\autoref{thm:regseqrat-streamname-dyadic-real-handoff-exactness} identifies
the complete route from the stream-name schedule and dyadic ledgers through
the $\RegSeqRatUp$ classifier to the $\RealUp$ seal rows. Applying
\autoref{thm:regseqrat-real-seal-handoff} then shows that a seal consumer
reads this regular-rational source surface itself.

Since all cited surfaces are finite BEDC rows, no quotient stream, host real
equality, selected limit, ambient completeness datum, or hidden completion
carrier can be consumed along the route.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[RegSeqRat L10 source lattice exhaustion]
\label{thm:regseqrat-l10-source-lattice-exhaustion}
Every accepted $\RegSeqRatUp$-to-$\RealUp$ seal read on the L10 route has exactly four row owners. $\StreamNameUp$ owns the finite observation schedules, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ owns endpoint rows, radius rows, and finite dyadic-distance ledgers, $\RegSeqRatUp$ owns regularity rows and classifier rows, and $\RealUp$ owns the terminal seal row. The accepted handoff has no row supplied by a quotient stream, host real equality, selected limit, completion witness, or ambient completeness principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the source lattice pointer, \autoref{thm:regseqrat-l10-source-lattice-pointer}. It fixes the route as $\StreamNameUp,\DyadicRatCoreUp \to \RegSeqRatUp \to \RealUp$ and lists the visible source rows before the seal can be read.

Apply the exact handoff theorem, \autoref{thm:regseqrat-streamname-dyadic-real-handoff-exactness}. It identifies the StreamName schedules, dyadic endpoint and radius ledgers, finite dyadic-distance ledgers, regularity rows, classifier rows, and seal rows as one finite handoff rather than as separate ambient services.

Finally use the Real seal handoff rows. They make the $\RealUp$ seal the terminal row of the same route, so the ownership list is exhausted by the four displayed chapters and cannot be enlarged by quotient streams, host equality, selected limits, completion witnesses, or ambient completeness.
\end{proof}
