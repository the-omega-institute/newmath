\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-twinsubstratesynthesis-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp$ packet realizes the twin-substrate architecture of \autoref{ch:twin-substrate-synthesis} as a finite cross-substrate audit object. It sits beside the shared audit-map interface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-auditmapinterface-namecert} and the GroundCompiler audit-map packet of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-groundcompilerauditmap-namecert}: those siblings supply the generic audit rows and the compiler-facing theorem-family boundary, while this chapter names the finite route that keeps MetaCIC and GroundCompiler reads separate until a displayed cross-substrate handoff is chosen.

\begin{definition}[Twin-substrate synthesis packet]
\label{def:twin-substrate-synthesis-packet}
A $\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
T=(M,G,I,A,F,X,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $M$ is the MetaCIC audit-surface row, recording closed-calculus theorem inventory, conditional fronts, and obstruction boundaries named by \autoref{ch:twin-substrate-synthesis};
\item $G$ is the GroundCompiler audit-surface row, recording event-flow, recognizer, report-boundary, cannot-claim, and theorem-family data from \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-groundcompilerauditmap-namecert};
\item $I$ is the shared audit-map interface row read through \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-auditmapinterface-namecert};
\item $A$ is the architecture row naming the permitted cross-substrate handoff from compiler-facing artifacts to closed-CIC audit obligations;
\item $F$ is the frontier row separating MetaCIC open proof-theoretic boundaries from GroundCompiler pipeline boundaries;
\item $X$ is the finite consumer row for downstream chapters that need both substrate names without importing either substrate's hidden coordinates;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transports for the MetaCIC row, GroundCompiler row, interface row, architecture row, frontier row, consumer row, provenance row, and local naming row;
\item $C$ records displayed $\Cont$ routes by which a consumer reads $M,G,I,A,F,X$ in finite order;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over \autoref{ch:twin-substrate-synthesis}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-auditmapinterface-namecert}, \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-groundcompilerauditmap-namecert}, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no coordinate for collapsing MetaCIC into GroundCompiler, turning compiler reports into proof evidence, importing hidden host structure, or discharging a MetaCIC frontier by a compiler-layer row.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Twin-substrate synthesis NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:twin-substrate-synthesis-namecert-obligations}
For every accepted $\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp$ carrier
$$
T=(M,G,I,A,F,X,H,C,P,N),
$$
the local $\NameCert_{\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp}$ surface consists exactly of MetaCIC row readback through $M$, GroundCompiler row readback through $G$, shared interface readback through $I$, architecture handoff through $A$, frontier separation through $F$, downstream consumer readback through $X$, componentwise transport through $H$, displayed continuation replay through $C$, package provenance through $P$, and local naming through $N$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the finite carrier. Its first six coordinates are the only substrate, interface, architecture, frontier, and consumer rows available to the local certificate.

Read the substrate rows separately. The MetaCIC coordinate $M$ records closed-calculus audit content and frontiers, while the GroundCompiler coordinate $G$ records compiler-facing theorem-family and report-boundary content. The interface row $I$ gives a common audit-map shape, but it does not identify the two substrate rows.

Next inspect $A$ and $F$. The architecture row permits a named handoff only after the source substrate row has been displayed, and the frontier row records which claims remain outside that handoff. Thus a compiler-facing row cannot discharge a proof-theoretic frontier, and a MetaCIC theorem row cannot certify a hidden compiler input.

Finally, $X,H,C,P,N$ route consumer reads, transport the same coordinates componentwise, and bind provenance with local naming. Since no other coordinate is present, the listed rows exhaust the NameCert obligation surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Twin-substrate cross-substrate non-escape]
\label{thm:twin-substrate-synthesis-cross-substrate-nonescape}
Every public read from an accepted $\TwinSubstrateSynthesisUp$ carrier factors through the displayed MetaCIC row, GroundCompiler row, shared audit-map interface row, architecture handoff row, frontier row, downstream consumer row, transport row, continuation row, provenance row, and local naming row of \autoref{def:twin-substrate-synthesis-packet}. Such a read cannot collapse the two substrates, convert a compiler report into proof evidence, import hidden host input, or solve a MetaCIC obstruction by a GroundCompiler route.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the NameCert obligations. They force every public read to choose one of the displayed coordinates $M,G,I,A,F,X,H,C,P,N$ before it can reach either substrate.

For a MetaCIC-facing read, the consumer receives $M$ together with the architecture, frontier, transport, continuation, provenance, and naming rows. For a GroundCompiler-facing read, the consumer receives $G$ through the same displayed routing discipline. The shared interface row $I$ supplies common audit-map shape only after the substrate row remains visible.

The frontier row $F$ records the non-conversions: report evidence is not proof evidence, compiler legality is not closed-CIC discharge, and hidden host input is not a carrier coordinate. The handoff row $A$ therefore routes between named substrate surfaces without merging their claims.

Coordinate exhaustion gives the non-escape clause. The carrier contains no field for substrate collapse, report-to-proof conversion, hidden host import, or obstruction discharge, so no accepted public read can export one.
\end{proof}
