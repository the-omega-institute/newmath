\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\DyadicBallUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicball-namecert}

Following the Bishop--Bridges constructive-analysis treatment of rational
balls, the $\DyadicBallUp$ interface names a finite center-radius enclosure
packet for L10 real-name observations. It sits between the dyadic arithmetic
surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-dyadicratcore-namecert}, the
regular rational stream surface of
\autoref{ch:concrete-instances-regseqrat-namecert}, and the real-name seal
surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}.

\section{Finite center-radius carrier}
\label{sec:dyadicball-finite-center-radius-carrier}

\begin{definition}[DyadicBall finite enclosure carrier]
\label{def:dyadicball-carrier}
A $\DyadicBallUp$ carrier is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
B=(c,r,k,o,\eta,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $c$ is a center row accepted by $\DyadicRatCoreUp$;
\item $r$ is a positive dyadic radius row, displayed by the same dyadic
arithmetic surface and readable as a one-sided
$\DyadicIntervalUp$ tolerance window;
\item $k$ is a precision and schedule row supplied by $\StreamNameUp$, with
the requested tolerance coordinated by $\CauchyModulusUp$ when a regular
source provides an explicit rate;
\item $o$ is the scheduled observation row, either a finite
$\RegSeqRatUp$ window endpoint or the finite source read of a $\RealUp$
seal;
\item $\eta$ is the containment ledger proving that the displayed observation
lies within the radius row around the center row;
\item $H$ and $C$ are the componentwise $\hsame$ witnesses and displayed
$\Cont$ routes for the center, radius, schedule, observation, and
containment rows;
\item $P$ is the $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance naming the consumed
$\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\DyadicIntervalUp$, $\StreamNameUp$,
$\CauchyModulusUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, and $\RealUp$ source surfaces;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert$ row recording that classification is by
componentwise finite transport of the displayed rows.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier has no quotient ball space, no host real equality, no completed
metric object, and no hidden real-radius coordinate. Its classifier compares
two accepted packets only by transporting the listed center, radius, schedule,
observation, containment, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$
rows.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall classifier laws]
\label{thm:dyadicball-classifier-laws}
For accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ carriers
$B=(c,r,k,o,\eta,H,C,P,N)$ and
$B'=(c',r',k',o',\eta',H',C',P',N')$, the componentwise classifier on the
center, radius, schedule, observation, containment, $\hsame$, $\Cont$,
$\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$ rows is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
Whenever it relates $B$ to $B'$, the displayed finite routes transport carrier
acceptance from $B$ to $B'$.

These laws act only on the rows exposed by
\autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. They introduce no quotient ball, host real
equality, hidden metric point, or completed real.
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.DyadicBallUp.DyadicBallPacket\_classifier\_transport}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.DyadicBallUp.DyadicBallPacket\_classifier\_laws}
\begin{proof}
Project the accepted packets through \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. The
only rows available to the classifier are the center, radius, schedule,
observation, containment, routing, provenance, and naming rows.

Reflexivity uses the componentwise reflexive $\hsame$ witness on each visible
row and the displayed identity $\Cont$ routes. Symmetry reverses the same
componentwise witnesses and routes. Transitivity composes the center, radius,
schedule, observation, containment, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\mathsf{Pkg}$, and
$\NameCert$ comparisons row by row.

For carrier transport, move the accepted rows of $B$ along the classifier
routes and keep the stated containment ledger in the target packet. Repacking
the transported rows gives the accepted carrier $B'$. Since the repacking uses
only the finite rows of the carrier, it cannot create a quotient ball, host
real equality, hidden metric point, or completed real.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall radius refinement closure]
\label{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure}
Let $B=(c,r,k,o,\eta,H,C,P,N)$ be an accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ carrier, and let
$r'$ be a positive dyadic radius row whose displayed comparison ledger proves
$r'\leq r$. If the same center row $c$, schedule row $k$, observation row $o$,
and containment ledger are transported through finite $\Cont$ rows so that the
observation remains inside the smaller displayed radius, then the repacked
tuple
$$
B'=(c,r',k,o,\eta',H',C',P',N')
$$
is again an accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ carrier.

The refinement changes only the visible radius and containment rows. It does
not form a quotient of balls and does not compare centers by host real
equality.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. The center, radius,
schedule, observation, containment, routing, provenance, and naming
coordinates are all explicit finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ rows.

Use the displayed comparison $r'\leq r$ to replace the radius coordinate. The
extra hypothesis supplies the finite containment ledger $\eta'$ for the same
center and observation at the smaller radius.

Transport the remaining coordinates by the componentwise $\hsame$ witnesses
and $\Cont$ routes. Repacking these rows gives the accepted packet $B'$, and
the construction has no coordinate from which a quotient ball space or host
real equality could be read.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall RegSeqRat window handoff]
\label{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff}
Every accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ packet supplies exactly the finite dyadic rows
that a scheduled $\RegSeqRatUp$ window may consume: the $\StreamNameUp$
precision row, the $\DyadicRatCoreUp$ center row, the positive dyadic radius
row, the scheduled endpoint row, the containment ledger, and the associated
$\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$ provenance.

The handoff may be read as a dyadic endpoint-radius window for the regular
stream, but it exports no quotient stream, no completion object, and no
external real value.
\end{theorem}
\leanchecked{BEDC.Derived.DyadicBallUp.DyadicBallFiniteEnclosure\_regseqrat\_window\_handoff}
\begin{proof}
Read the packet through \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. The schedule row is
already a $\StreamNameUp$ finite request, and the center and radius rows are
accepted dyadic rows.

For a $\RegSeqRatUp$ consumer, keep exactly the scheduled observation row and
the containment ledger that compares it to the displayed center and radius.
These are the endpoint and tolerance rows consumed by a finite regular-stream
window.

The componentwise $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$ rows are
the same rows listed in the carrier. Since the carrier contains no stream
quotient or completed limit, the handoff cannot export one.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall Real seal boundary]
\label{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary}
A $\RealUp$ seal reading an accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ packet may inspect only
the center row, the positive radius row, the scheduled observation row, the
containment ledger, the componentwise $\hsame$ rows, the displayed $\Cont$
routes, the $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance, and the local $\NameCert$ row.

The seal boundary does not add a real-equality classifier, a quotient of
regular streams, a completeness witness, or a host real ball. It merely records
that the displayed finite observation lies in the displayed dyadic
center-radius enclosure.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the public coordinates of \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. These
coordinates already include the finite source read that a $\RealUp$ seal may
consume: center, radius, schedule, observation, containment, routing,
provenance, and naming data.

Apply the handoff statement of
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff} when the seal source is read
through a regular-rational scheduled window. The same finite dyadic rows are
then the only source rows available to the real-name seal.

No carrier coordinate stores a real quotient, a completed metric point, or a
host ball. Therefore the seal boundary is exactly the listed finite row
surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall intersection refinement handoff]
\label{thm:dyadicball-intersection-refinement-handoff}
Let an accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ packet be read through the finite
center-radius carrier of \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. If the packet is
refined to a smaller positive dyadic radius by
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure}, then the refined packet
exports the same scheduled $\StreamNameUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, and $\RealUp$
finite-boundary rows as the original packet, except for the transported radius
row and the containment ledger supplied for that smaller radius.

Concretely, the $\mathsf{BHist}$ center, schedule, scheduled observation,
componentwise $\hsame$ transports, displayed $\Cont$ windows, $\mathsf{Pkg}$
provenance, and local $\NameCert$ rows remain the same rows of the carrier.
The $\RegSeqRatUp$ endpoint-radius window of
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff} and the $\RealUp$ seal
surface of \autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary} are read from those
rows after replacing only the radius and containment coordinates. The handoff
does not introduce quotient balls, host real equality, completed metric
points, or selected limits.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack the carrier of \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. Its public rows are the
finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ center, positive radius, schedule, scheduled
observation, containment ledger, $\hsame$ transports, $\Cont$ windows,
$\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance, and $\NameCert$ certificate.

Apply \autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure}. The theorem repacks
the accepted packet at the smaller displayed dyadic radius, using the supplied
containment ledger for that radius and leaving the center, schedule,
observation, transport, window, provenance, and certificate rows in place.

Project the repacked packet through
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff} and
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary}. Both projections inspect only the
carrier rows just listed, so their finite consumer surfaces agree with the
original packet except at the transported radius and containment entries.

Since no quotient-ball coordinate, host real equality, completed metric point,
or selected limit occurs in the carrier, none is created by the repacking or by
either outgoing handoff.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall NameCert obligation surface]
\label{thm:dyadicball-namecert-obligation-surface}
The local $\NameCert_{\DyadicBallUp}$ obligation surface consists of five
finite requirements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier: the finite center-radius packet of
\autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier};
\item classifier: the componentwise classifier laws of
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-classifier-laws};
\item radius-refinement stability:
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure} repacks a smaller displayed
positive radius when the containment ledger is supplied for that radius;
\item $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff:
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff} exposes exactly the finite
endpoint-radius rows consumed by a scheduled regular-rational window;
\item $\RealUp$ seal boundary:
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary} restricts real-name reads to the
center, radius, observation, containment, routing, package, and naming rows.
\end{enumerate}
These requirements are the full public obligation surface for
$\DyadicBallUp$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The carrier requirement is \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. It lists every
coordinate that can appear in an accepted packet.

The classifier requirement is \autoref{thm:dyadicball-classifier-laws}. It
records reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity, and carrier transport for exactly
the componentwise rows named by the carrier.

Radius-refinement stability, regular-window handoff, and real-seal boundary
are the three theorem rows just stated. Together they cover the local ways a
dyadic ball packet is refined, consumed by a regular stream window, and read by
a real-name seal.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall obligation closure]
\label{thm:dyadicball-obligation-closure}
The carrier, classifier, radius-refinement stability, $\RegSeqRatUp$ handoff,
and $\RealUp$ seal boundary listed in
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-namecert-obligation-surface} exhaust the local
obligation set for $\DyadicBallUp$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
The carrier row is the finite center-radius packet of
\autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. Its classifier row compares exactly the
center, radius, schedule, scheduled observation, containment ledger,
$\Cont$ routes, $\mathsf{Pkg}$ rows, and $\NameCert$ rows named by that
carrier.

The three outgoing obligation rows are the radius-refinement stability theorem
of \autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure}, the scheduled
regular-rational handoff of
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff}, and the real-seal boundary
of \autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary}. These are the only local
operations that refine, export, or seal a displayed dyadic-ball packet.

No quotient ball space, host real equality, metric topology, or completeness
coordinate is present in the carrier. Hence there is no additional local
obligation row beyond the five rows enumerated in
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-namecert-obligation-surface}.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall center-radius public interface]
\label{thm:dyadicball-center-radius-public-interface}
The carrier, classifier laws, radius-refinement closure, $\RegSeqRatUp$ window
handoff, $\RealUp$ seal boundary, and obligation-closure theorem assemble the
public $\NameCert_{\DyadicBallUp}$ interface over the displayed center,
positive radius, schedule, observation, containment, $\hsame$, $\Cont$,
$\mathsf{Pkg}$, and $\NameCert$ rows. Downstream $\RegSeqRatUp$ and $\RealUp$
consumers may cite this single public interface instead of reopening the scoped
center-radius packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Assemble the public certificate by projecting the scoped packet into the rows
listed by \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. The exported object has only the
center, positive radius, schedule, observation, containment, transport,
provenance, and naming coordinates already present in that packet.

The classifier row is supplied by
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-classifier-laws}. Its $\hsame$ stability transports
accepted packets across the displayed componentwise rows, and the associated
$\Cont$ routes are precisely the routes allowed to downstream consumers.

The remaining certificate fields are the refinement, regular-window, and
real-seal rows. Apply \autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure} for
radius ledger transport, \autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff} for
the $\RegSeqRatUp$ consumer row, and
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary} for the $\RealUp$ seal boundary.

Finally use \autoref{thm:dyadicball-obligation-closure} to see that these rows
exhaust the local obligation surface. Repacking them yields the public
$\NameCert_{\DyadicBallUp}$ interface, with no quotient ball, host real
equality, metric topology, or extra completeness coordinate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall common observation overlap]
\label{thm:dyadicball-common-observation-overlap}
Let
$B=(c,r,k,o,\eta,H,C,P,N)$ and
$B'=(c',r',k,o,\eta',H',C',P',N')$ be accepted
$\DyadicBallUp$ packets with the same scheduled observation row $o$ and the
same schedule row $k$. Suppose the displayed center and radius rows admit the
componentwise $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ transports required by
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-classifier-laws}, and suppose a finite dyadic positive
radius row $\rho$ is supplied with containment ledgers into both packets. Then
the common observation determines a single finite $\RegSeqRatUp/\RealUp$
consumer package whose visible rows are exactly $o$, $k$, the transported
center rows, the transported radius row $\rho$, the two containment ledgers,
the displayed $\hsame$ rows, the visible $\Cont$ routes, the $\mathsf{Pkg}$
provenance rows, and the two $\NameCert$ rows.

This common package exports no quotient ball, host real equality, metric
completeness claim, or selected real point.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project both accepted packets through the carrier of
\autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}. The two projections expose only their
displayed centers, radii, common schedule row, common scheduled observation,
containment ledgers, $\hsame$ rows, $\Cont$ routes, package provenance, and
local naming rows.

Use \autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure} on each side with the
finite radius row $\rho$ and its displayed containment ledger. This repacks
both packets at the same radius row while retaining only the transported
center, schedule, observation, containment, transport, package, and naming
coordinates.

The shared schedule and observation rows now feed
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff}. That handoff identifies one
finite regular-rational window surface, because the visible scheduled rows are
the same and the remaining differences have already been transported into the
displayed center, radius, and containment ledgers.

Apply \autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary} to the same repacked rows.
The $\RealUp$ seal can read only the finite carrier coordinates named above, so
it cannot distinguish the two packets by any quotient ball, host real equality,
metric-completeness datum, or selected point.

Finally cite \autoref{thm:dyadicball-center-radius-public-interface} to package
the common finite surface as the public $\NameCert_{\DyadicBallUp}$ consumer
interface. The resulting $\RegSeqRatUp/\RealUp$ package has exactly the rows
listed in the statement.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall refinement overlap transitivity]
\label{thm:dyadicball-refinement-overlap-transitivity}
Let
$B_0=(c_0,r_0,k,o,\eta_0,H_0,C_0,P_0,N_0)$,
$B_1=(c_1,r_1,k,o,\eta_1,H_1,C_1,P_1,N_1)$, and
$B_2=(c_2,r_2,k,o,\eta_2,H_2,C_2,P_2,N_2)$ be accepted
$\DyadicBallUp$ packets with the same schedule row $k$ and the same scheduled
observation row $o$. Suppose $B_0$ and $B_1$ admit a common refined positive
dyadic radius row $\rho$ with displayed containment ledgers into both packets,
and suppose $B_1$ and $B_2$ admit a common refined positive dyadic radius row
$\sigma$ with displayed containment ledgers into both packets.

If a positive dyadic radius row $\tau$ is displayed together with refinement
ledgers from $\tau$ to both $\rho$ and $\sigma$, and with containment ledgers
from $\tau$ into all three packets, then the three packets determine one finite
$\RegSeqRatUp/\RealUp$ consumer package. Its visible rows are exactly the
shared schedule $k$, the shared observation $o$, the transported center rows,
the positive radius row $\tau$, the three containment ledgers, the displayed
$\hsame$ rows, the visible $\Cont$ routes, the $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance rows,
and the three $\NameCert$ rows.

The package introduces no quotient ball, host real equality, completeness
datum, or selected real point.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the three accepted packets through \autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier}.
This exposes only center rows, radius rows, the shared schedule, the shared
observation, containment ledgers, transport rows, package provenance, and local
naming rows.

Use \autoref{thm:dyadicball-radius-refinement-closure} separately for $B_0$,
$B_1$, and $B_2$ at the displayed radius row $\tau$. The refinement ledgers
from $\tau$ to $\rho$ and $\sigma$, together with the containment ledgers into
the three packets, supply the radius and containment coordinates required for
each repacked packet.

Apply \autoref{thm:dyadicball-common-observation-overlap} to the pair
$(B_0,B_1)$ using the row $\rho$, and again to the pair $(B_1,B_2)$ using the
row $\sigma$. The two overlap packages have the same schedule and scheduled
observation, and their middle packet rows are transported through the same
carrier coordinates.

Now read both overlap packages through
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-regseqrat-window-handoff}. The handoff identifies the
single finite regular-rational consumer surface carried by the shared schedule,
shared observation, transported center rows, radius row $\tau$, and the three
containment ledgers.

Finally apply \autoref{thm:dyadicball-real-seal-boundary} to the same finite
rows. The real-name seal has no coordinate for a quotient ball, host real
equality, completeness datum, or selected point, so the composite overlap
remains a finite $\RegSeqRatUp/\RealUp$ package with precisely the rows listed
above.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[DyadicBall center-radius bridge boundary]
\label{thm:dyadicball-center-radius-bridge-boundary}
Let $B$ be an accepted $\DyadicBallUp$ packet whose center-radius public
interface is supplied by
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-center-radius-public-interface}. Suppose its common
observation overlap with any compared packet is supplied by
\autoref{thm:dyadicball-common-observation-overlap}. Then the bridge rows
available to $\RegSeqRatUp$ and $\RealUp$ consumers are exactly the finite
center row, positive radius row, schedule row, observation window, containment
ledger, $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ transport rows, $\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance, and
local $\NameCert$ row displayed by the public interface.

Consequently, the bridge boundary exposes no host real ball, selected point,
metric-completion datum, quotient stream equality, or hidden tail coordinate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Unpack the public interface theorem. It packages the center, radius, schedule,
observation, containment, transport, provenance, and local naming rows of
\autoref{def:dyadicball-carrier} as the reusable
$\NameCert_{\DyadicBallUp}$ surface.

Route the common observation theorem through the same schedule and observation
rows. The overlap proof can only compare accepted packets by their displayed
center-radius rows, containment ledgers, $\hsame$ witnesses, $\Cont$ routes,
$\mathsf{Pkg}$ provenance, and local $\NameCert$ rows.

Every $\RegSeqRatUp$ or $\RealUp$ consumer read therefore factors through one
of the listed finite rows. Since neither cited theorem supplies a coordinate
for a host real ball, selected point, metric completion, quotient stream
equality, or hidden tail, no such datum is present at the bridge boundary.
\end{proof}
