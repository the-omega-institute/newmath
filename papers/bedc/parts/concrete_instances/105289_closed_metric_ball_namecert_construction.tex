\chapter{A Constructive Closed Metric Ball Certificate for $\ClosedMetricBallUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-closed-metric-ball-namecert}
\origin{human}

Following Bishop and Bridges, \emph{Constructive Analysis}, the $\ClosedMetricBallUp$ packet records the center-radius containment object of a constructive metric space as finite BEDC NameCert data. It sits after the metric carrier of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-metric-namecert}, the terminal real-name seal of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-real-namecert}, the separated comparison boundary of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-separatedmetric-namecert}, the complete-metric limit surface of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-completemetric-namecert}, and the regular-Cauchy equality route of \autoref{ch:concrete-instances-cauchysequenceequivalence-namecert}. The packet names non-strict metric containment for one displayed center and radius; it does not import host subsets, open-cover compactness, quotient point equality, or ambient completeness.

\begin{definition}[Closed metric ball carrier]
\label{def:closed-metric-ball-carrier}
A $\ClosedMetricBallUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
B=(M,X,c,r,\rho,D,R,S,Q,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $M$ is the $\MetricUp$ source row supplying the distance-read interface.
\item $X$ is the inspected point row.
\item $c$ is the displayed center row.
\item $r$ is the $\RealUp$ or rational radius row.
\item $\rho$ is the nonnegative-radius ledger, read before any containment classifier is consumed.
\item $D$ is the metric-distance witness row for the finite comparison $d(c,X)$.
\item $R$ is the ordered-real comparison row recording $d(c,X)\leq r$ through the $\RealUp$ seal.
\item $S$ is the $\SeparatedMetricUp$ compatibility row, used only to transport zero-distance agreement through the same metric source.
\item $Q$ is the optional $\CompleteMetricUp$ or $\CauchySequenceEquivalenceUp$ handoff row for consumers that arrive from completed or regular-Cauchy names.
\item $H$ stores componentwise $\hsame$ transport for $M,X,c,r,\rho,D,R,S,Q$ and the structural rows.
\item $C$ stores displayed $\Cont$ replay from the metric source and radius ledger to the distance witness, ordered comparison, separated route, and completion-facing handoff.
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\ClosedMetricBallUp$, $\MetricUp$, $\RealUp$, $\SeparatedMetricUp$, $\CompleteMetricUp$, $\CauchySequenceEquivalenceUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ rows.
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\ClosedMetricBallUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier compares accepted containment packets only through $M,X,c,r,\rho,D,R,S,Q,H,C,P,N$. It has no coordinate for arbitrary subsets, topology closure, selected limits, open-cover compactness, quotient point equality, countable choice, trichotomy, or ambient $\RealUp$ completeness.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Closed metric ball NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:closed-metric-ball-namecert-obligations}
For an accepted $\ClosedMetricBallUp$ carrier $B=(M,X,c,r,\rho,D,R,S,Q,H,C,P,N)$, the local $\NameCert_{\ClosedMetricBallUp}$ surface consists exactly of carrier admission, metric-source exposure, center and point exposure, radius nonnegativity, distance-witness visibility, ordered-real containment, separated-metric compatibility, completion-facing handoff when $Q$ is present, componentwise transport, continuation replay, provenance, and local naming. Every containment read therefore follows the displayed route
$$
M,X,c,r,\rho\longmapsto D\longmapsto R\longmapsto S,Q\longmapsto H,C,P,N
$$
and no host subset or compactness principle supplies an extra membership coordinate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier rows. The metric source, inspected point, center, radius, nonnegative-radius ledger, distance witness, ordered-real comparison, separated route, completion-facing row, transport, replay, provenance, and local name are the only public coordinates.

The containment classifier reads the distance witness $D$ only after $M,X,c,r,\rho$ have been displayed. The row $R$ then records the non-strict comparison through the $\RealUp$ seal rather than through an ambient subset predicate.

Separated and completed consumers are downstream. The row $S$ can transport only zero-distance agreement already visible in the same metric source, and the row $Q$ can hand a completed or regular-Cauchy read to the same comparison route only after the closed-ball rows are present.

Finally $H,C,P,N$ preserve, replay, source, and name the route. Since the carrier contains no host subset, topological closure, open-cover, selected-limit, quotient, choice, trichotomy, or ambient-completeness coordinate, the listed rows exhaust the NameCert surface.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[Closed metric ball containment handoff]
\label{thm:closed-metric-ball-containment-handoff}
Every separated, bounded, complete, or compact metric consumer of an accepted $\ClosedMetricBallUp$ packet must read the finite containment route $M,X,c,r,\rho,D,R,S,Q,H,C,P,N$ before it may use the point as lying in the closed ball. The exported handoff is only the center-radius comparison $d(c,X)\leq r$ with its displayed transport, replay, provenance, and local name; it does not provide a compactness theorem, a host-level closed subset, quotient equality of points, a selected completion limit, or an ambient $\RealUp$ completeness principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start from the containment request. The rows $M,X,c,r,\rho$ fix the metric source, point, center, radius, and nonnegative-radius ledger before a distance comparison can be consumed.

The distance and ordered-real rows are next. The row $D$ supplies the displayed metric-distance read, and $R$ supplies the non-strict $\RealUp$ comparison. This is the only route by which the packet exposes membership.

Downstream metric consumers then read $S$ or $Q$ only as compatibility rows. They preserve the same center-radius comparison for separated or completed contexts, but they cannot replace it by topology closure, host set equality, compactness, or selected limits.

The structural rows $H,C,P,N$ carry the route forward without adding mathematical content. Therefore every exported handoff remains the finite closed-ball containment certificate described above.
\end{proof}
