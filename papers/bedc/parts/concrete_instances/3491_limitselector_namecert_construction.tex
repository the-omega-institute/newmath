\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\LimitSelectorUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-limitselector-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\LimitSelectorUp$ surface packages the finite selector used by the diagonal-limit construction of \autoref{def:real-completeness-diagonal-limit}. It names the precision request, the modulus-selected member, the finite observation window, the regular-rational readback, the RealUp seal, and the BEDC transport rows as one inspectable packet. It is a selector certificate over displayed finite data, not a host-selected limit or a quotient of streams.

\begin{definition}[LimitSelector carrier]
\label{def:limit-selector-carrier}
A $\LimitSelectorUp$ carrier is a finite $\BHist$ packet
$$
L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)
$$
with the following displayed rows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $R$ is the $\RegSeqRat$ input row for the regular Cauchy sequence whose diagonal representative is being read;
\item $n$ is the unary precision request row;
\item $\mu$ is the displayed modulus row assigning a finite selector bound to each unary precision request;
\item $k$ is the selected index row, fixed as the value of $\mu$ at the precision requested by $n$;
\item $W$ is the finite $\StreamNameUp$ window row read from the selected member and the selected diagonal index;
\item $D$ is the dyadic readback row produced from the finite window;
\item $S$ is the $\RealUp$ seal row reached after the $\RegSeqRatUp$ readback and dyadic endpoint rows are fixed;
\item $H$ records componentwise $\hsame$ transports for $R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S$ and the local structural rows;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ route from the precision request through the modulus, selected index, window, dyadic readback, and seal rows;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance over $\RegSeqRat$, $\StreamNameUp$, $\DyadicRatCoreUp$, $\RegSeqRatUp$, $\RealUp$, $\BHist$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and local certificate rows;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\LimitSelectorUp}$ row.
\end{enumerate}
The local classifier compares accepted packets only through the displayed rows $R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}
The local $\NameCert_{\LimitSelectorUp}$ surface for \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier} consists of five finite obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier admission, requiring every accepted selector object to expose the packet $L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)$;
\item modulus-selector exactness, requiring the selected index row $k$ to be read from the displayed modulus $\mu$ at the unary precision request $n$;
\item finite-window readback, requiring the diagonal observation to pass through the finite $\StreamNameUp$ window $W$ and dyadic readback row $D$ before any regular-rational read is exported;
\item seal handoff, requiring the $\RealUp$ row $S$ to be reached only through the selected finite witness route $n,\mu,k,W,D$ and the displayed transport, route, package, and naming rows;
\item non-escape, requiring the selector to export no host-selected limit, quotient stream equality, countable choice data, ambient completeness principle, or unrecorded diagonal observation.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier admission is projection from the finite tuple in \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier}. The input, precision, modulus, selected index, window, dyadic readback, seal, transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows are all displayed coordinates.

Modulus-selector exactness reads $n$ and $\mu$ before the index row $k$ is available. Thus the selected index is a row of the packet, fixed by the displayed modulus route, rather than an external choice of a subsequence.

The finite-window readback then routes the selected member through $W$ and $D$. Since the window and dyadic rows are named before the readback is exported, a consumer cannot replace them by a quotient stream or by an unbounded host observation.

Finally the seal handoff and non-escape clauses inspect $S,H,C,P,N$. These rows carry the selected finite witness route into the RealUp seal and provide no coordinate for host-selected limits, choice data, ambient completeness, or an unrecorded diagonal observation.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector finite witness route]
\label{thm:limit-selector-finite-witness-route}
Every diagonal-limit consumer of an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet factors through the selected finite witness route
$$
n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N.
$$
The consumer may inspect the precision request, displayed modulus, selected index, finite stream window, dyadic readback, RealUp seal, componentwise $\hsame$ transports, displayed $\Cont$ routes, package provenance, and local naming row. It receives no host-selected limit, no quotient-stream equality, no countable-choice selector, and no ambient completeness witness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Start with the consumer route $C$. Its first reads are the precision request $n$ and the modulus row $\mu$, followed by the selected index $k$ named by the modulus-selector obligation of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}.

Next the consumer reads the finite observation data. The selected index opens only the displayed window $W$, and the dyadic row $D$ is the readback attached to that window.

Finally the route reaches the seal row $S$ and the BEDC structural rows $H,C,P,N$. The carrier has no second coordinate for a host-selected limit, quotient-stream equality, countable choice, or ambient completeness, so every diagonal-limit read remains inside the selected finite witness route.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector observation-budget handoff]
\label{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}
Let
$$
L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)
$$
be an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet, and let an accepted $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ packet share the displayed precision request, finite window, dyadic readback, RealUp seal, componentwise transport, continuation route, package provenance, and local naming rows $n,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$. Then every diagonal-limit consumer of $L$ that reaches the shared rows factors through the same finite observation-budget route before it reads the RealUp seal.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
First project the selected route of $L$ by \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier} and \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}. The selector supplies $n,\mu,k$ before the finite window $W$ and dyadic readback $D$ are available.

Next apply the finite witness route of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-finite-witness-route}. Once $W,D,S,H,C,P,N$ are displayed, the diagonal-limit consumer has no route around the named window and readback rows.

Finally read the shared observation-budget packet. The seal-route totality and consumer exactness theorems for $\RealObservationBudgetUp$, \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-finite-window-seal-route-totality} and \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-consumer-exactness}, identify the same finite budget route through the precision request, window, dyadic ledger, seal, transport, continuation, provenance, and naming rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector Real seal non-escape]
\label{thm:limit-selector-real-seal-nonescape}
For an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet, the RealUp seal row $S$ is reachable only after the selector has displayed $n,\mu,k,W,D$ and the transport, continuation, provenance, and local naming rows $H,C,P,N$. No consumer may read $S$ as a host-selected limit, quotient stream, countable-choice selector, or ambient completeness witness.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Use carrier admission first. In \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier}, the seal row $S$ is a coordinate following the precision request, modulus row, selected index, finite window, and dyadic readback.

The local NameCert obligations then impose the seal handoff. By \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}, the seal row is reached only through the selected finite witness route and the displayed structural rows.

The observation-budget seal boundary gives the Real-facing side of the same claim. \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-real-seal-boundary} places a budget seal after its finite observation rows, and \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-nonescape} excludes quotient streams, host real equality, hidden limit selection, unbounded schedules, and ambient completeness from the shared route.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector shared budget Real seal determinacy]
\label{thm:limit-selector-shared-budget-real-seal-determinacy}
Let
$$
L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)
$$
be an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet, and let an accepted $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ packet share the rows $n,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$. Then the $\RealUp$ seal row reached by any diagonal-limit consumer of $L$ is determined by the shared observation-budget route. The consumer cannot read a second seal, a host-selected limit, quotient stream equality, or an ambient completeness route.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the $\LimitSelectorUp$ carrier. The only seal coordinate of $L$ is the displayed row $S$, and it appears after the precision, selector, finite window, dyadic readback, transport, route, provenance, and naming rows.

Apply \autoref{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}. The accepted $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ packet shares $n,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$, so the diagonal-limit consumer reaches the seal through the same finite observation-budget route before any Real-facing read is available.

Apply \autoref{thm:limit-selector-real-seal-nonescape}. The seal cannot be read as a host-selected limit, quotient stream, countable-choice selector, or ambient completeness witness.

Finally use the RealObservationBudget seal boundary rows already cited above, namely \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-real-seal-boundary} and \autoref{thm:real-observation-budget-nonescape}. They identify the shared route as the finite seal boundary, leaving no second seal or ambient route for the diagonal-limit consumer.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector diagonal consumer exhaustion]
\label{thm:limit-selector-diagonal-consumer-exhaustion}
Every diagonal-limit consumer of an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet is exhausted by the carrier rows of \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier}, the local obligations of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}, the finite witness route of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-finite-witness-route}, the observation-budget handoff of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}, and the seal non-escape row of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-real-seal-nonescape}. The consumer receives exactly the displayed selector, window, readback, seal, transport, route, provenance, and naming rows, with no host-selected limit or ambient completeness coordinate.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the selector packet. The carrier and local obligations display the complete finite surface $R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$ and require the selected index to be read from the precision and modulus rows.

Next use the finite witness route and the observation-budget handoff. Together they force every diagonal read through the selected index, the finite window, the dyadic readback, and the shared $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ route before the seal row is consumed.

Finally apply seal non-escape. Since neither the selector carrier nor the observation-budget packet contains a coordinate for quotient streams, countable choice, selected host limits, or ambient completeness, the diagonal consumer has no remaining export beyond the displayed finite rows.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector scoped source rows]
\label{thm:limit-selector-scoped-source-rows}
Every accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet exposes its scoped source as the displayed $\BHist$ carrier $L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)$, with the $\StreamNameUp$ finite window $W$, the $\RegSeqRat$ input row $R$, the $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ route through $n,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$, the componentwise $\hsame$ transports and $\Cont$ route, the $\Pkg$ provenance row, and the local $\NameCert_{\LimitSelectorUp}$ row. No source row is supplied outside this displayed packet.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the carrier of \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier}. The accepted packet displays the $\BHist$ tuple $L=(R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N)$, so the regular-sequence source, precision request, modulus, selected index, finite stream window, dyadic readback, seal, transport, route, provenance, and naming rows are all visible coordinates.

Apply the local obligations of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}. They require the selected index to be read from $n$ and $\mu$, and they require the finite-window readback to pass through $W$ and $D$ before the seal row is exported.

Use the observation-budget handoff of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}. It identifies the shared budget route through $n,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$, while \autoref{thm:limit-selector-diagonal-consumer-exhaustion} exhausts the remaining diagonal-consumer reads by the same displayed packet.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector modulus-seal scope]
\label{thm:limit-selector-modulus-seal-scope}
For an accepted $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet, the selector modulus $\mu$ and the $\RealUp$ seal row $S$ read only the displayed finite window route
$$
n,\mu,k,W,D,S
$$
and the observation-budget route carried by $H,C,P,N$. The seal cannot read an unrecorded stream observation, an external selector, or any ambient completion principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Begin with the finite witness route of \autoref{thm:limit-selector-finite-witness-route}. The precision request $n$ and displayed modulus $\mu$ determine the selected index row $k$, after which the consumer reads the finite window $W$ and dyadic readback $D$.

The seal row is then governed by \autoref{thm:limit-selector-real-seal-nonescape}. That theorem places $S$ after $n,\mu,k,W,D$ and the structural rows $H,C,P,N$, so the seal has no independent route to stream data or host completeness data.

Finally use \autoref{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}. The shared $\RealObservationBudgetUp$ route accounts for the finite observation-budget side of the same seal read, and \autoref{thm:limit-selector-diagonal-consumer-exhaustion} leaves no further consumer coordinate.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LimitSelector non-escape scope]
\label{thm:limit-selector-nonescape-scope}
The scoped $\LimitSelectorUp$ packet exports no quotient stream, no host limit selection, no ambient completeness principle, and no open-cover compactness datum. Its exported content is exhausted by the carrier, finite witness route, observation-budget handoff, Real seal non-escape, and diagonal consumer exhaustion theorems of this chapter.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the packet through \autoref{def:limit-selector-carrier} and \autoref{thm:limit-selector-namecert-obligations}. The only exported coordinates are $R,n,\mu,k,W,D,S,H,C,P,N$ and the finite obligations attached to them.

Apply \autoref{thm:limit-selector-finite-witness-route}, \autoref{thm:limit-selector-observation-budget-handoff}, and \autoref{thm:limit-selector-real-seal-nonescape}. These results route every diagonal-limit and Real-seal read through the selected finite witness route and the displayed observation-budget rows.

Conclude with \autoref{thm:limit-selector-diagonal-consumer-exhaustion}. Since the exhausted surface has no coordinate for quotient streams, host-selected limits, ambient completeness, or open-cover compactness, none of those objects can be exported by the scoped selector packet.
\end{proof}
