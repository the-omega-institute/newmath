\chapter{A Concrete Naming Certificate for $\LocatedIntervalUp$}
\label{ch:concrete-instances-locatedinterval-namecert}
\origin{human}

The $\LocatedIntervalUp$ interface names the finite query ledger used when interval consumers need located endpoint and refinement data without importing an open-cover principle or a host real interval subset. It is the certificate surface read by dyadic bisection, finite interval nets, compact uniform continuity, and real-completion handoffs: a consumer asks for a displayed rational or dyadic window, receives a finite interval row with its comparison ledger, and can transport that row through $\mathsf{BHist}$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ provenance.

\begin{definition}[LocatedInterval BHist carrier]
\label{def:locatedinterval-bhist-carrier}
A $\LocatedIntervalUp$ carrier is a finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet
$$
L=(I,D,B,J,R,Q,T,C,P,N)
$$
with the following visible coordinates:
\begin{enumerate}
\item $I$ is the source $\RationalIntervalUp$ row, including lower-endpoint, upper-endpoint, endpoint-order, and endpoint-readback histories;
\item $D$ is the displayed $\DyadicMeshUp$ query row naming the finite mesh, tolerance, and requested observation window;
\item $B$ is the $\DyadicBisectionUp$ branch ledger available when the located query is refined by midpoint inspection;
\item $J$ is the $\NestedIntervalUp$ refinement row obtained by retaining the visible interval windows selected by the query ledger;
\item $R$ is the $\RealUp$ consumer boundary, consisting only of the finite rational endpoint windows and the real-name read routes that consume them;
\item $Q$ records the located query answers: lower-bound reads, upper-bound reads, inside-window certificates, outside-window certificates, and the finite comparison rows attached to the mesh request;
\item $T$ records the componentwise $\hsame$ rows for interval rows, mesh rows, bisection rows, nested-interval rows, located-query rows, and real-consumer rows;
\item $C$ records the displayed $\Cont$ routes for endpoint inspection, mesh refinement, bisection handoff, nested-window handoff, and real-name consumption;
\item $P$ is the $\Pkg$ provenance row naming $\RationalIntervalUp$, $\DyadicMeshUp$, $\DyadicBisectionUp$, $\NestedIntervalUp$, $\RealUp$, $\mathsf{BHist}$, $\hsame$, $\Cont$, and $\NameCert$ source certificates;
\item $N$ is the local $\NameCert_{\LocatedIntervalUp}$ registration row.
\end{enumerate}
The carrier exports only these finite rows. It has no coordinate for open-cover compactness, trichotomy on host reals, a host interval-subset predicate, a completed nested intersection, or a completeness theorem.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[LocatedInterval classifier]
\label{def:locatedinterval-classifier}
Two accepted $\LocatedIntervalUp$ carriers classify together when their rational interval rows, dyadic mesh requests, bisection branch ledgers, nested interval windows, located query answers, real-consumer rows, transport rows, continuation routes, package rows, and local naming rows compare componentwise after the displayed $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ transports.

In particular, the classifier compares only finite endpoint reads and finite query answers. It does not compare host subsets of real intervals, completed intersections, real trichotomy witnesses, open covers, or quotient representatives.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[LocatedInterval NameCert obligations]
\label{thm:locatedinterval-namecert-obligations}
The seed $\NameCert_{\LocatedIntervalUp}$ surface has five obligations:
\begin{enumerate}
\item carrier: formation of the finite $\mathsf{BHist}$ packet of \autoref{def:locatedinterval-bhist-carrier};
\item classifier: the finite query classifier of \autoref{def:locatedinterval-classifier};
\item stability: componentwise preservation of rational-interval rows, dyadic mesh requests, bisection rows, nested-window rows, located answers, and real-consumer rows under displayed $\hsame$ transport and $\Cont$ refinement routes;
\item query exactness: every inside-window, outside-window, lower-bound, and upper-bound answer is one of the rows recorded in the finite located-query ledger $Q$;
\item handoff: dyadic, nested-interval, compact-uniform-continuity, interval-net, and real-completion consumers read only the displayed finite interval rows, package provenance, and local naming row.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Carrier formation is the assembly of the packet in \autoref{def:locatedinterval-bhist-carrier}. Each coordinate is either a finite history row, a transport row, a continuation route, package provenance, or the local naming row.

The classifier is finite because every comparison is made at a displayed query window. After transporting two packets through their $\hsame$ and $\Cont$ rows, the rational interval, mesh, branch, nested-window, located-answer, real-boundary, package, and naming rows are compared in the listed order.

Stability follows by the same component order. Endpoint and mesh rows transport first, the bisection and nested-window rows transport next, and the query-answer and real-consumer rows transport last.

Query exactness is a projection from $Q$: a consumer-visible answer must be a recorded lower-bound read, upper-bound read, inside-window row, outside-window row, or mesh comparison row. The handoff obligation repacks those rows with $T$, $C$, $P$, and $N$, so no consumer can read an unrecorded host interval object.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LocatedInterval dyadic query handoff]
\label{thm:locatedinterval-dyadic-query-handoff}
For every accepted $\LocatedIntervalUp$ carrier and every displayed dyadic mesh request, the consumer-visible query answer factors through the $\RationalIntervalUp$ endpoint rows, the $\DyadicMeshUp$ tolerance row, the relevant $\DyadicBisectionUp$ branch-prefix ledger when midpoint refinement is used, the retained $\NestedIntervalUp$ window, the located-query ledger $Q$, and the displayed $\hsame$, $\Cont$, $\Pkg$, and $\NameCert$ rows.

The handoff exports no host trichotomy decision and no completed intersection of the retained intervals.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the mesh coordinate $D$ and the source interval coordinate $I$. The query request supplies a finite tolerance and a finite endpoint window, so the first read is through the rational endpoint and endpoint-order rows.

If midpoint refinement is requested, project the branch ledger $B$. The relevant finite prefix identifies the retained interval window, and the nested-row coordinate $J$ records that retained window as visible BEDC data.

The query answer is then read from $Q$. Each answer is one of the displayed lower-bound, upper-bound, inside-window, outside-window, or comparison rows, transported through $T$ and routed through $C$.

Finally, $P$ and $N$ package the same finite rows as the local certificate surface. Since the carrier contains no real-trichotomy coordinate and no completed intersection coordinate, the dyadic handoff cannot export either object.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LocatedInterval real consumer boundary]
\label{thm:locatedinterval-real-consumer-boundary}
At the $\RealUp$ boundary, an accepted $\LocatedIntervalUp$ carrier exposes exactly the finite rational endpoint windows, dyadic query answers, retained nested-window rows, displayed $\hsame$ transports, displayed $\Cont$ routes, $\Pkg$ provenance, and local $\NameCert_{\LocatedIntervalUp}$ row.

Thus real and real-completion consumers may use the located interval as a finite readback certificate, but they do not receive open-cover compactness, host interval subset membership, real trichotomy, or a completeness theorem.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Project the real-consumer coordinate $R$. Its inputs are the rational endpoint windows from $I$, the mesh and query rows from $D$ and $Q$, the retained nested-window row $J$, the transports in $T$, the continuation routes in $C$, the package row $P$, and the local certificate row $N$.

Those inputs are exactly finite ledger rows. A real-name or completion consumer can therefore read an endpoint enclosure, a displayed query answer, or a retained interval window, but every such read remains routed through the same certificate coordinates.

No coordinate of the carrier is an open cover, a host subset relation on real intervals, a trichotomy proof for host reals, or a completeness witness. The boundary therefore stops at the finite readback surface stated above.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}[LocatedInterval dependency anchor package]
\label{thm:locatedinterval-dependency-anchor-package}
The $\LocatedIntervalUp$ package is a dependency anchor for interval-shaped real and completion consumers: every accepted carrier records its $\RationalIntervalUp$, $\DyadicMeshUp$, $\DyadicBisectionUp$, $\NestedIntervalUp$, and $\RealUp$ dependencies in the single provenance row $P$, and every downstream read must pass through $P$ together with the local $\NameCert_{\LocatedIntervalUp}$ row $N$.

Consequently, dyadic bisection, compact uniform continuity, finite interval nets, and real Cauchy completion can depend on the located interval ledger without importing an additional compactness, trichotomy, host-subset, or completeness principle.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Read the provenance coordinate $P$. It names the interval, dyadic mesh, bisection, nested-window, real-boundary, history, transport, continuation, and naming dependencies used by the carrier.

Now read the local certificate coordinate $N$. A downstream consumer must enter through the certificate name, then follow the provenance row to the specific endpoint, mesh, branch, nested-window, query, and real-boundary rows it needs.

This makes the package an anchor rather than an analytic principle. The consumer receives the finite ledger and its declared dependencies, while compactness, trichotomy, host interval-subset semantics, and completeness remain outside the packet.
\end{proof}
