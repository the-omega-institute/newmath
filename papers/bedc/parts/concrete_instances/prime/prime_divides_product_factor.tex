\begin{theorem}[Prime divisor of a unary product exposes a factor]
\label{thm:prime-divides-product-factor}
For histories $p,a,b,n$, if $\mathsf{NatPrime}(p)$, $\mathsf{NatMul}(a,b,n)$, and $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,n)$, then $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,a)$ or $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,b)$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Induct on the concrete unary multiplication witness $\mathsf{NatMul}(a,b,n)$, viewing the right factor $b$ as the induction spine.

In the zero-product case, the result is $\emp$. A divisor witness for $p$ into $\emp$ would make $\emp$ a multiple of the prime $p$. The non-empty condition in $\mathsf{NatPrime}(p)$, together with the empty-result inversion for $\mathsf{NatMul}$, rules this case out, so the conclusion follows vacuously.

In the successor case, the product is obtained by appending one more copy of $a$ to the previous product. If $p$ already divides the previous product, the induction hypothesis gives $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,a)$ or divisibility into the predecessor of $b$; in the latter subcase, the successor multiplication constructor and \autoref{lem:divides-transitive} transport the divisor to $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,b)$.

It remains when the final appended copy is the first place where the divisor can occur. The $\mathsf{NatMul}$ result and functionality facts identify the last continuation with the factor $a$ up to $\hsame$. Applying the defining divisor alternative in $\mathsf{NatPrime}(p)$ to this non-trivial divisor yields either the unit case, which is incompatible with the prime's strict-greater field, or $\hsame$-equality with $a$. This gives $\mathsf{NatDivides}(p,a)$, completing the induction.
\end{proof}
