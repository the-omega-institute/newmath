\section{Gap-Aware Planning}
\label{sec:gap-aware-planning}

The planner should not treat all predicted states equally.  A low predicted
task cost is not enough when the world model itself predicts that a trajectory
passes through high-gap regions.

\begin{definition}[Gap-sound model]
A model is gap-sound at tolerance $\epsilon$ and threshold $\tau$ on a declared
scope if
$$
  g(o,a)<\tau \Rightarrow \operatorname{PredError}(o,a)\le\epsilon
$$
and
$$
  \operatorname{PredError}(o,a)>\epsilon \Rightarrow g(o,a)\ge\tau .
$$
The first implication says that low-gap states are reliable.  The second says
that high-error states are ledgered.
\end{definition}

Given a predicted rollout, BEDC-JEPA planning minimizes
$$
  \sum_t c(z_t,a_t)+\lambda_g\sum_t \lVert g_t\rVert_1 .
$$
The second term is not an interpretability report.  It changes the selected
plan by making high-gap trajectories expensive.

\begin{proposition}[Gap penalty blocks cheap high-gap plans]
Suppose a low-gap feasible trajectory has predicted task cost at most $C$.
Suppose every high-gap trajectory has gap penalty at least $G$.  If
$\lambda_g G$ is larger than the task-cost advantage of every high-gap
trajectory over the low-gap trajectory, then the gap-aware planner selects a
low-gap trajectory.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For any high-gap trajectory, the BEDC-JEPA objective equals its predicted task
cost plus at least $\lambda_g G$.  By hypothesis this exceeds the objective of
the low-gap feasible trajectory.  Therefore no high-gap trajectory can be an
optimizer of the penalized planning objective.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Experimental target.}
The planning experiment should compare a vanilla latent planner and a
gap-aware planner on the same learned transition model.  The primary measures
are unsafe-state rate, high-gap trajectory rate, success rate, and unlogged
failure rate.  The expected BEDC-JEPA advantage is not only lower prediction
loss, but fewer selected trajectories through regions where the model itself
has marked its world-state claim as unsupported.

\paragraph{Current result.}
In the boundary-gated rollout, both planners use the same candidate actions and
the same target.  The vanilla planner optimizes only predicted task cost.  The
gap-aware planner adds the learned gap penalty.  On eight trajectories with an
eight-step horizon, the high-gap state rate drops from $0.109375$ to
$0.015625$, while success remains $1.000000$.  The regret proxy increases
slightly from $0.123423$ to $0.128087$, which records the expected cost of
avoiding uncertified boundary states.

In the grid-pixel learned-transition benchmark, planning uses the trained
action-conditioned transition model.  The vanilla planner enters high-gap
states at rate $0.520833$ and unsafe states at rate $0.333333$.  The gap-aware
planner reduces both rates to $0.000000$ in the same rollout setting.  This is
not a robotics-scale control result, but it is no longer an oracle-state
planning-only artifact: the rollout is mediated by a learned transition model
trained from pixel observations.

The larger grid planning sweep uses three targets and thirty-six trajectories.
The gap-aware planner lowers high-gap state rate by $0.170139$ and unsafe-state
rate by $0.104167$ on average.  It improves the risk-adjusted planning cost by
$0.233564$, while reducing raw success rate by $0.250000$.  This is recorded as
a real tradeoff: the current planner buys gap avoidance by accepting some
short-horizon reachability loss.
