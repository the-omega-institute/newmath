\section{Reality Boundary}
\label{sec:reality-boundary}

This article separates three layers.  The first layer is external JEPA
research: representation-space prediction, world-model learning, and latent
identifiability results.  The second layer is the BEDC modeling commitment:
world states should expose stable distinctions and explicit gap ledgers.  The
third layer is the local executable artifact in \texttt{papers/bedc-quality-lab},
which supplies a small boundary-gated toy protocol and evidence envelope.

\paragraph{External research input.}
The JEPA line supplies the representation-space prediction setting.  A model
learns an encoder whose state is useful for predicting another representation
of the world.  Recent identifiability work supplies the calibration target:
under explicit assumptions, a learned representation can recover latent state
geometry up to a gauge.  This paper does not reprove that result.

\paragraph{Internal BEDC derivation.}
BEDC contributes the distinction-and-ledger principle.  A world model should
not only encode a latent coordinate.  It should form claims about operational
distinctions and expose gap states when a claim is near a boundary, outside the
declared scope, unstable under continuation, or unsupported by its evidence
surface.

\paragraph{Falsifiable probe.}
The first falsifiable probe is not whether a toy encoder has high $R^2$ alone.
The probe is whether, at comparable latent recovery, a BEDC-JEPA model can
reduce unlogged distinction errors and false claims inside a declared gap band.

\paragraph{Mismatch ledger.}
If a system only writes a report after training, it remains a reporting
baseline.  It is not BEDC-JEPA.  If a system predicts $z_t$ but has no trained
distinction head or gap head, it remains a JEPA-style latent model.  If a
system marks gaps but planners ignore the gap signal, the planning layer has
not yet crossed the BEDC-JEPA gate.
