\title{BEDC-JEPA: Learning World Models with Certifiable Distinctions and Gap-Aware Planning}
\author{The Omega Institute}

\begin{abstract}
Joint-embedding predictive architectures learn world models by predicting
target representations rather than reconstructing observations.  Recent
identifiability results show that, under explicit Gaussian and transition
assumptions, such objectives can recover latent world variables up to a linear
or orthogonal gauge.  This paper proposes a different learning target:
BEDC-JEPA, a world-model principle in which the learned state includes
continuous latent coordinates, operational distinction heads, and explicit gap
ledger heads.  The goal is not to attach a post-hoc report to a representation,
but to train the model to know which distinctions are stable enough to claim
and where its own world-state predictions require a ledger entry.  A
boundary-gated Ornstein--Uhlenbeck toy world gives the first executable
experiment: a continuous latent trajectory, an inside/outside operational
distinction, and a boundary gap band.  A four-system comparison separates
latent-only prediction, a post-hoc probe, a post-hoc BEDC report, and a trained
BEDC-JEPA gap readback.  Under equal latent recovery, the trained BEDC-JEPA
system lowers unlogged error and scope-relative debt against the post-hoc
report baseline, and gap-aware planning reduces selected high-gap states in a
small rollout.
\end{abstract}

\section{Scope}
BEDC-JEPA is not a formalization of an existing JEPA paper and not a post-hoc
model card.  The object of study is a BEDC-native world-model objective:
$$
\text{world state} = z_t + d_t + g_t .
$$
Here $z_t$ is a continuous latent state, $d_t$ is a vector of operational
distinctions, and $g_t$ is a vector of gap-ledger states.  A distinction is not
a natural-language label.  It is a stable predicate with transition,
intervention, or planning consequences inside a declared scope.  A gap state is
not an after-the-fact explanation.  It is a model output indicating that the
current prediction or distinction claim is not certified.
