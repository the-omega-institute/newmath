\paragraph{Finite carrier and observed boundary row.}
The record uses the six-coordinate codon cube as its finite display space.  Each codon is treated as a six-bit window over the standard four-letter codon alphabet, and the only biological contact used here is the curated standard-code assignment of codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  On that contact, the distinguished boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$ and is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\\
&\mathrm{CUU},\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured-cube pattern with face-count vector $(13,16,4)$.  This is a code-label observation, not a biochemical mechanism: the finite row says that these thirteen codons occupy the local boundary pattern seen by the window-six coordinate probe, while the amino-acid or stop labels are supplied by the curated table.

\paragraph{Local scope boundary.}
The curated standard-code table is sufficient for codon-label readback and for testing the local split described above.  It is not a contact for ribosomal realization, kinetic translation, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, or a universal mechanism.  Any statement crossing those layers would require a separate observation source designed for that layer.  In particular, amino-acid label sequences obtained from the table are still labels; no folding, structural order, or functional realization is observed by this record.
