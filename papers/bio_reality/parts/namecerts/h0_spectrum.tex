\subsection{Window-Six Spectrum for the Standard-Code Boundary Tile}\label{sec:bioreality-h0-spectrum}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and external contact.} The record is a window-six, code-layer observation on the codon cube $Q_6$.  The carrier is the six-bit codon display; the distinctions are local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The only external contact used here is the curated standard-code label table: a codon is read back as an amino-acid label or a stop label.  This contact is sufficient for code-read comparisons, local label splits, and finite-row closure checks.  It is not a contact with translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Boundary row.} The distinguished boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  In codon notation the row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is verified as a punctured-cube shape with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the spectrum is not being attached to an arbitrary list of codons: it is attached to a concrete finite subcomplex of the codon cube, with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-faces, and four two-faces.  The code table supplies the labels on these codons, while the BEDC coordinate supplies the window-six geometry.

\paragraph{Spectral invariant.} The finite-row spectrum separates the measured boundary row $R$ from the ambient measured module $M$ by two spectral radii.  The verified values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial polynomial check gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\mu)&=-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12},
\end{aligned}
$$
with the same residual recorded as the cubic-polynomial satisfaction error.  At the numerical precision of this finite run, the row therefore satisfies the intended cubic spectral constraint, and the measured spectral radius in tolerance is $0.6752479308830213$ for $M$.  The point of the invariant is local: it certifies a code-layer boundary spectrum on the supplied finite codon display.  It does not certify a biological law.

\paragraph{Shape significance.} Against the coordinate null model with $N=2000$ trials and seed $42$, the observed shape has exactly one exact-shape match.  The exact finite-row $p$-value is
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There are $25$ matches at Jaccard threshold at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The sampling record also reports $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries, so the number is a finite sampling statistic, not an asymptotic theorem.  Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed module size is $20$, the exact-shape match count is $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size is $7$.  The corresponding finite-row values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These values support the statement that the observed local shape is rare in the tested finite null families.  They do not promote rarity to necessity, mechanism, or function.

\paragraph{Leave-one readback checks.} The code-read behavior is stable under two leave-one tests.  In the leave-one-code-out experiment, $23$ tables were evaluated and $63$ total targets were tested.  The baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline is $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate in $M\setminus T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate in $M\setminus T$ plus boundary is $0.9841269841269841$.  In the leave-one-codon-out experiment, the anchor count is $0$, the boundary count is $5$, the high-coverage closure count is $8$, the predictable count is $8$, and the recovery rate is $1.0$.  These finite numbers show that the boundary tile has code-layer recovery power under the supplied table contact.

\paragraph{Family and module decomposition.} The leave-one-code-out family split has five families.  The family hit rates above baseline are $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial plastid, $1.0$ for candidate other, $1.0$ for mitochondrial, and $1.0$ for nuclear ciliate.  The thirteen boundary codons are also fully assigned to six named modules:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The module-classification row reports $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no missing codons from modules, and no unassigned codons.  Every module has either predictable or boundary status.  This gives a finite internal decomposition of the row rather than a merely scalar summary.

\paragraph{Cannot-claim boundary.} The verified content of this chapter is exactly a local code-read spectrum for the standard-code table on a window-six codon carrier.  A local codon tile is not a global biological law.  Window-six geometry alone is not translation, folded protein structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback is still a code-layer readback; it is not a folded structure contact.  Any later claim crossing from code labels to translation, folding, admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality requires a separate external contact and cannot be inferred from the finite-row spectrum recorded here.
