\paragraph{Finite codon display.}
The record studied here is the standard-code codon table viewed only as a six-bit codon display with curated amino-acid or stop labels attached.  The carrier is the $4^3=64$ codon cube, encoded by three nucleotide coordinates and read only at the code-label layer.  The finite boundary set $R$ extracted by the run has cardinality $13$ and is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same run reports that this set has the punctured-cube face vector $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the finite observation is not merely a count: it is a small codon subcomplex with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four local square faces inside the codon cube.  The singleton-corner and three-point label-class distinctions are therefore treated as features of this local display, not as a claim about the full biological implementation of translation.

\paragraph{Shape rarity under finite null models.}
The shape comparison run \texttt{0b267c88f8e4} sampled $N=2000$ null rows using seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and rejected $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among the accepted null rows there was exactly one exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005,\\
p_{J\geq 0.9} &= 0.0125,
\end{aligned}
$$
with $25$ rows reaching Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$.  A separate amino-acid-class-preserving null run \texttt{e8d5de747a0c}, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, observed $M$-size $20$, found no exact shape match, and counted $7$ rows in the lower tail at or below the observed size.  Its reported values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class, exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class, lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These finite probabilities support the statement that the observed local tile is uncommon under the stated row generators.  They do not identify a biochemical cause, and they do not convert the coordinate pattern into a universal law.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary of interpretation.}
The only biological contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact is sufficient for code-layer readback and local label-split checks.  It is not an observation of ribosomal translation dynamics, protein folding, molecular admissibility, biological function, or a universal mechanism across organisms.  Consequently, the chapter claims a finite spectral and combinatorial signature of a local codon-label tile in the standard code.  It does not claim that window-six geometry produces translation, that label sequences determine folded structure, that the tile is physically necessary, that it realizes function, or that it holds as a global biological law.  Any such stronger assertion would require a separate empirical contact at the corresponding layer.
