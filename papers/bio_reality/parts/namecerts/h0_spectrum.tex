\subsection{Spectral Window on a Local Codon Boundary}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-spectrum}

\paragraph{Finite object under test.}
The finite object is the standard-code codon table read only as a code-layer labelling of the six-coordinate codon cube.  The coordinate object has sixty-four codon vertices, and the tested boundary row isolates a thirteen-codon subset
$R=\{\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}$.
The recorded cardinality check gives $|R|=13$.  The same row identifies this subset with a punctured-cube pattern whose finite cell count is
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the observation is not a free verbal description of codon usage.  It is a finite subset of the codon cube together with a small face pattern and a standard-code label readback.  The biological contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code table, whose role is only to say which amino-acid or stop label is attached to each codon.

\paragraph{Spectral row.}
The spectral computation attached to this boundary gives two reported radii and one radial parameter:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The numerical residual for the cubic relation is $-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12}$, and the same value is recorded as the radial-polynomial residual.  Within the tolerance of the finite computation, the spectral radius entry is therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
\rho=0.6752479308830213.
\end{aligned}
$$
This row is evidence for a local spectral signature of the selected codon boundary.  It is not evidence that codons are generated by the spectral equation, nor that the equation supplies a biochemical mechanism.  The equation records a finite coordinate regularity after the curated code labels have already been supplied by the external table.

\paragraph{Shape significance against finite null rows.}
A separate shape test used $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$.  The sampler made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among these alternatives there was $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ alternatives with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A class-preserving amino-acid null model, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, recorded observed $M$-size $20$, exact-match count $0$, and lower-tail count $7$.  Its reported probabilities were
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers make the row suitable as a finite local signal: the shape is rare in the tested null ensembles.  They do not convert rarity into necessity, organism-wide law, translation dynamics, folded structure, physical admissibility, or biological function.

\paragraph{Recovery checks.}
The leave-one-code-out row evaluated $23$ tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate in $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the rate in $M\setminus T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  The leave-one-codon-out row had anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, closure coverage high $8$, predictable count $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  These checks say that the finite coordinate pattern has predictive stability at the code-label level.  They still do not observe translation realization or any downstream protein property.

\paragraph{Family and module decomposition.}
The family-level row groups $23$ code tables into $5$ families.  The hit rates above baseline were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial and plastid codes, $1.0$ for candidate other codes, $1.0$ for mitochondrial codes, and $1.0$ for nuclear-ciliate codes.  The module classification is complete on the thirteen codons: assigned count $13$, no missing codons, no unassigned codons, and no extra module codons.  The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{AGR}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{AR\_Lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{CUN}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{Ile\_Met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{SSN}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{Stop\_Trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module has either predictable or boundary status in the recorded classification.  This is the correct scale of the claim: a local codon-cube boundary with a spectral signature, rare finite shape statistics, and stable code-label recovery behavior.

\paragraph{Local boundary of interpretation.}
The result is a code-read observation.  The curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-label assignments, and the coordinate computation exposes a local tile-like boundary inside that finite labelling.  No claim about translation, folding, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, biochemical mechanism, or universality follows from these rows.  Any such higher-layer assertion would require a separate contact that directly observes the relevant layer.  In particular, amino-acid label sequences are not folded proteins, CUN or UUR wording is not structure evidence, and a local codon-tile pattern is not a global law of biology.
