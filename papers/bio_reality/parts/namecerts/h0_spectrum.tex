\subsection{Spectral boundary in the window-six codon cube}
\label{sec:bioreality-h-zero-spectrum}

\paragraph{Finite coordinate object.}
The object recorded here is the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon display.  The carrier is the codon cube $Q_6$, and the only external contact used in this record is the curated standard-code assignment from codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  The construction therefore lives at the code-read layer: it may compare finite codon coordinates with curated labels, but it does not observe translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Those higher layers require distinct contacts, and no such contact is part of this row.

\paragraph{Distinguished boundary row.}
The finite boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  Its observed codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The punctured-cube check returns the same thirteen codons and the face data have $f$-vector $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the finite row is not merely a list of exceptional labels; it has a local tile boundary shape inside $Q_6$.  The row is still local: a tile boundary in codon coordinates is not a law of DNA, a translation mechanism, or a protein-level statement.

They do not convert the alignment into a claim about folded structure, physical viability, function, or global biological necessity.
