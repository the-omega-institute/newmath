\subsection{Window-six spectrum of the standard-code boundary tile}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-spectrum}

\paragraph{Finite object.}
The object examined here is the standard-code codon table after each codon has been placed in the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The six coordinates are not treated as a biochemical mechanism.  They are a finite display language for asking whether the curated codon-to-label table has a local boundary pattern visible at code-read level.  The contact used by this chapter is the curated standard genetic-code table: it assigns each codon to an amino-acid or stop label, and nothing in this contact observes ribosomal translation, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, or a universal generative mechanism.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The finite row $R$ singled out by the coordinate-and-label construction has cardinality $13$.  The verified row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is verified as a punctured-cube pattern with face data
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the observed local tile has $13$ vertices, $16$ edges, and $4$ two-faces in the finite six-bit display.  This is a statement about the row extracted from the standard-code label table, not about the inevitability of that table in biology.

\paragraph{Spectral calculation.}
The spectral probe attached to this row reports two radii and one scalar coupling.  The measured values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The cubic constraint was checked numerically by the same finite computation.  Its residual was
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\lambda_M,\lambda_R,\mu)=-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12},
\end{aligned}
$$
reported equivalently as the radial residual.  The recorded spectral radius in tolerance is $0.6752479308830213$, matching $\lambda_M$ at the displayed precision.  The useful content is therefore not merely that $R$ is a list of codons, but that the list carries a stable local spectral signature in the coordinate display.

\paragraph{Shape rarity under finite nulls.}
Two finite null checks quantify how accidental the extracted shape appears under the supplied randomizations.  In the first null run, $N=2000$ distinct samples were sought with seed $42$; producing these samples required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  Exactly one sample matched the observed shape, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The amino-acid-class-preserving null used $N=2000$ samples with the same seed.  It recorded observed $M$-size $20$, no exact shape match, and $7$ samples in the lower tail at or below the observed $M$-size.  The finite probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite row supplied here does not include the member list for $M$, so this chapter uses only the verified scalar $|M|=20$ rather than inventing an unobserved membership table.

\paragraph{Readback performance.}
The leave-one-code-out probe evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The boundary-tile readback rose to $0.9365079365079365$ inside $M-T$, and to $0.9841269841269841$ inside $M-T$ plus the boundary row.  The recorded hit rate above baseline was also $0.9841269841269841$.  By family, the finite row reports $5$ families: alternative yeast has hit rate above baseline $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial plastid, candidate other, mitochondrial, and nuclear ciliate each report $1.0$.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon modules.}
The leave-one-codon-out probe records recovery rate $1.0$ and recovery rate above threshold $1.0$.  It reports $8$ predictable codons, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-coverage closure cases, and $0$ anchors.  The module classification is complete: the row has $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, no missing row codons, no unassigned codons, and no extra module codons.  The verified modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module contains a predictable or boundary codon according to the supplied finite classification.  This gives a compact code-layer organization of the row: the $13$ codons are not an arbitrary leftover set, but a complete partition into the six displayed modules.

\paragraph{Scientific interpretation.}
The chapter-level claim is deliberately narrow.  A six-bit codon coordinate system, together with the curated standard-code labels, exposes a local boundary tile whose row size, punctured-cube face vector, spectral values, null-model rarity, leave-one-code-out behavior, and module partition are all finite and checkable.  The contact is still only a code table.  It licenses codon-label readback and local split tests.  It does not license any conclusion about translation being realized, protein folding, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism of code formation, or a universal biological law.  Any such stronger claim would require a separate contact that directly observes the higher-layer object rather than reusing the codon-label table.
