\subsection{Window-Six Spectrum of the Standard-Code Boundary Tile}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-spectrum}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Carrier and contact.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read as a codon display with three nucleotide positions and four letters per position. The biological contact used here is only the curated standard-code label table: each of the $64$ codons is assigned an amino-acid label or a stop label. This contact is a code-read contact, not a contact with translation dynamics, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality. The local object studied in this row is therefore a boundary tile inside $Q_6$ whose labels are read back from the standard code. The row does not claim that window-six geometry is a biological law; it claims that the finite code table exposes a reproducible local spectral and combinatorial pattern.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$. In codon notation the row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same finite row is recorded as a punctured cube with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the boundary is not merely a list of exceptional codons: it carries a local face incidence pattern with $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional adjacencies, and $4$ two-dimensional tile faces. The finite data also partition the $13$ codons into six named modules. The complete module assignment is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The module audit reports $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, $13$ assigned codons, no missing codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons. Every module has a predictable-or-boundary certificate in the finite row.

The finite computation reports the radial cubic residual
$$
\begin{aligned}
P(\mu)=-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12},

$$

The content of the certificate is limited: it says that the window-six codon tile has this finite spectral profile after the standard-code labels are read into the row. It does not say that spectra cause translation, folding, or function.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
The local shape row was compared against $N=2000$ finite samples with seed $42$. The sampling process made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries. Exactly one sample matched the observed shape, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
These values make the local tile atypical under the sampled row model. A second null model preserves amino-acid classes. Under that constraint the observed $M$-size is $20$, the exact match count is $0$ out of $2000$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed size is $7$. The corresponding values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the standard-code row is not only spectrally structured; its finite local shape is also rare in both the sampled shape model and the amino-acid-class-preserving comparison. The row nevertheless remains a finite code-read row: the null results measure rarity of this boundary-tile geometry, not biological necessity.

\paragraph{Leave-one readback.}
The leave-one-code-out row evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ targets. Its baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate inside $M-T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, while the hit rate inside $M-T$ together with the boundary is $0.9841269841269841$. The recorded hit rate above baseline is also $0.9841269841269841$. By family, the finite audit reports $5$ families with rates
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{alternative\ yeast}&:0.8333333333333334,\\
\mathrm{bacterial\ plastid}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{candidate\ other}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{mitochondrial}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{nuclear\ ciliate}&:1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The leave-one-codon-out row records anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, high closure coverage $8$, predictable count $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$. These numbers show that the boundary tile is useful as a finite readback scaffold for code labels. They still do not supply a higher-layer contact. A recovered codon label is not a recovered translation process, not a folded protein structure, and not a biological function.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
The row supports the following local claim: in the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, the curated standard-code label table exposes a $13$-codon boundary row with punctured-cube incidence vector $(13,16,4)$, a closure-side spectral radius $0.6752479308830213$, a boundary-side value $0.4758352843197002$, a radial scalar $3.051487585298128$, rare sampled shape statistics, and strong leave-one readback rates. The row blocks every stronger promotion. No translation realization, no folded structure, no physical admissibility, no function realization, no mechanism, and no universality follows from this contact unless a separate reality contact supplies that layer. The standard-code table is enough for codon-label readback and local split checks; it is not enough for protein realization or structural order.
