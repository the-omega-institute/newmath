\paragraph{Shape significance against row shuffles.}
The observed local shape is rare under the supplied finite nulls.  In the unconstrained shape test with $N=2000$ sampled tables and seed $42$, there was one exact shape match after $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  The exact-shape probability estimate is therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005,
\end{aligned}
$$
while the high-overlap event with Jaccard score at least $0.9$ occurred $25$ times, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A label-class-preserving null gives a stricter shape outcome on the same sample count $N=2000$: the observed module size is $|M|=20$, the exact match count is $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed module size is $7$.  The corresponding finite-row probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers support the claim that the code-read row is a non-generic local boundary tile under the stated finite randomizations.  They do not promote the tile to a global biological law.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
The resulting claim is deliberately local.  Window-six codon coordinates expose a finite spectral and module pattern in the standard-code label table: a thirteen-codon residual row, a punctured-cube face vector $(13,16,4)$, a spectral triple $(0.6752479308830213,0.4758352843197002,3.051487585298128)$ satisfying its cubic constraint to residual $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, and statistically rare shape behavior under the stated finite nulls.  The contact that makes the row biological is only the curated standard-code table.  Therefore no translation process, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality follows from this subsection unless a separate higher-layer reality contact supplies it.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback remains code readback; it is not folded structure, and it is not a functional assay.
