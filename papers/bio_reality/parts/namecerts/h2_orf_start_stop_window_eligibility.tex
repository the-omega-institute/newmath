\paragraph{Object of the record.}
This record isolates the weakest biological assertion made by a finite start--stop codon enumeration: a nucleotide window can be sorted by whether it carries the formal boundary pattern expected of an open-reading-frame candidate.  The claim is intentionally narrow.  It is not a claim that the interval is translated, that a peptide is produced, that the peptide folds, that the molecule is physically admissible, that it has a function, that a mechanism is known, or that a universal biological law has been obtained.  Those stronger assertions require a separate contact with curated sequence annotation, transcript evidence, proteomic observation, structural evidence, or another suitable biological source.  The present packet records only the finite classification of windows by start and stop positions, together with the rule that such classification cannot travel upward into those stronger biological layers by geometry alone.

\paragraph{Local boundary of interpretation.}
The combined record therefore supports a limited statement: finite start--stop rules classify the enumerated window data, and curated interval contacts can test the same eligibility rule against positive intervals, matched decoys, and boundary controls.  The self-check reports three checked gates and states that no codon geometry is promoted across biological layers.  Accordingly, the chapter does not infer translation, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality from start--stop enumeration or from the codon quotient geometry.  Any one of those stronger assertions must be supplied by an additional contact designed for that layer.  Within this scope, the ORF eligibility layer is a finite, audited boundary claim: it records what was classified, how many rows were tested, which decoys and boundary cases were rejected or withheld from promotion, and where the biological interpretation must stop.
