\paragraph{Claim.} The record $h2.orf.start\_stop.window\_eligibility$ isolates a narrow BioReality question: whether a finite nucleotide window can be sorted by the presence of start and stop codon boundaries. The biological object is open-reading-frame eligibility under start--stop enumeration, but the claim is deliberately weaker than an open reading frame in the biochemical sense. In the recorded run $c26a0d6656f7$, the external window manifest was loaded with value $1$, the window count was $1$, and the classifier reported
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{classified} &= 1,\\
\mathrm{input\_windows} &= 1.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the finite row says only that one supplied window was classified by the predeclared start--stop enumeration rule. The run scope is $\mathrm{external\_window\_start\_stop\_enumeration\_only}$, with snapshot date $2026$-$05$-$25$. The source description identifies the row as a curated finite nucleotide-window manifest whose records are annotation inputs to the experiment, not facts generated by the BEDC kernel itself.

\paragraph{Boundary.} The present chapter uses those finite rows only to delimit what has been observed. The one-window start--stop run establishes that an externally supplied window can be classified by the frozen boundary rule. The larger curated-interval contact supplies a separate biological annotation test for ORF-interval eligibility on its own finite dataset. Neither row licenses a jump from boundary eligibility to translation, protein folding, physical admissibility, biological function, molecular mechanism, or a general biological law. The self-check row states the same limitation in audit form: three gates were checked, and no codon geometry is promoted across biological layers. Consequently, the chapter records an eligibility-level contact and its surrounding finite codon geometry, while leaving all higher biological layers outside the claim unless an additional contact is supplied specifically for that layer.
