\paragraph{Saturation check.}
The $f_3$ saturation audit gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Sat}_{f_3}(R)=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AAG},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{AUG},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\\
&\mathrm{CUU},\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UCG},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UGG},\mathrm{UUA},\mathrm{UUG}\},
\end{aligned}
$$
so the saturated set has $18$ codons and omits $\mathrm{ACA}$ and $\mathrm{ACG}$ from $M$.  The same audit records an empty $f_3$ boundary, and the mediated union with $\mathrm{ACA}$ recovers the full $20$-element set $M$.  This is useful as a boundary discipline: the finite geometry has an internally checked closure calculation, but the closure calculation remains a calculation on codon rows.  It is not a measurement of transcription, translation, molecular stability, phenotype, selection, or biological role.
