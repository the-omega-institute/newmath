\subsection{Amino-acid-quotiented Q6 codon residuals across three organism tables}\label{subsec:h3-codon-usage-topology-residual-basis-q6-topology-after-aa-quotient}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Claim coordinate.}
This record concerns the codon-level residue left after amino-acid composition has been made part of the conditioning data. The carrier is the per-organism coding-sequence codon-count table projected to the amino-acid residual basis $B^{*}_{Q6}$. The intended biological reading is deliberately narrow: the basis is a coordinate system for synonymous codon contrasts, not an assertion that any contrast has already been shown to alter translation, folding, molecular function, or fitness. The finite run named \texttt{residual\_basis\_q6\_topology\_validation}, with run identifier \texttt{971b9c868480}, reached status \texttt{passed} on the stored finite checks.

\paragraph{Observed finite contact.}
The contact consists of one codon-count table per organism for three organisms: \texttt{homo\_sapiens}, \texttt{saccharomyces\_cerevisiae}, and \texttt{escherichia\_coli\_k12}. The NCBI genetic code was loaded, and the organism count was therefore $3$. This is a finite contact with three named tables, not a population estimate over organisms and not a universal statement about codon usage. In symbols, the run supplies a table-level map
$$
\begin{aligned}
  o &\longmapsto C_o,\\
  o &\in \{\texttt{homo\_sapiens},\texttt{saccharomyces\_cerevisiae},\texttt{escherichia\_coli\_k12}\},
\end{aligned}
$$
where $C_o$ denotes the observed codon-count summary for organism $o$. The table is used only to test whether the proposed residual coordinates have nontrivial finite variation after the amino-acid quotient is imposed.

\paragraph{Residual basis.}
The computed basis contains $9$ named residual coordinates:
$$
\begin{aligned}
B^{*}_{Q6}=\{&K_{AAA},\ Arg_{AGR},\ Ile_{AUA},\ Leu_{CUN\,vs\,UUR},\ Leu_{UUA\,vs\,UUG},\\
&Ser_{UCR\,vs\,AGY},\ Ser_{UCA\,vs\,UCG},\ Thr_{ACR\,vs\,ACY},\ f_3\text{-stress-pair-antisymmetric}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Each element is a synonymous-codon residual coordinate or a stress-pair antisymmetry coordinate. The amino-acid quotient is essential: the coordinate is not allowed to win merely by re-encoding the amino-acid sequence. The readout being prepared for later controlled tests has the form
$$
\begin{aligned}
  S_Q(r\mid Z) &= J_0(Z)-J_Q(Z,q),\\
  Z &=(y,c,\text{simpler codon-feature baselines}),
\end{aligned}
$$
where $y$ is the amino-acid sequence, $c$ is contextual data, $q\in B^{*}_{Q6}$, and $r$ is a bounded readout such as a codon-adaptation weight, ribosome-occupancy measure, or protein-abundance measurement. The present finite run does not supply those controlled $S_Q$ values; it supplies the coordinate-level check that the residual basis is not empty or vacuous on the three tables.

\paragraph{Finite nontriviality result.}
At variance threshold $0.01$, the run reports $7$ nonzero residual coordinates out of $9$ total coordinates. The stored nonzero fraction is
$$
\begin{aligned}
  \frac{7}{9} &= 0.7777777777777778.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus, after amino-acid composition has been quotiented out, the finite organism set still shows nonzero variation in most of the specified $B^{*}_{Q6}$ coordinates. This supports the narrow coordinate claim that the residual basis is empirically populated on the three named codon-count contacts. It does not by itself identify which of the nine coordinates would survive a controlled translation-layer comparison, nor does it rank organisms or assign a universal sign to any coordinate.

\paragraph{Relation to M-density baselines.}
The claim also records a design pressure on models that retain only an $M$-density summary. An $M$-only baseline can be a useful local codon-feature summary, but it cannot by itself test contrasts such as $CUN$ versus $UUR$ inside leucine or $AAA$ versus $AAG$ inside lysine when the question is explicitly about amino-acid-residual codon topology. The appropriate test is not whether $M$ is dense or locally optimized; it is whether adding some $q\in B^{*}_{Q6}$ improves the conditional score over the $M$ baseline after amino-acid composition is controlled:
$$
\begin{aligned}
  S_Q(r\mid Z,\Delta_M) &> 0
\end{aligned}
$$
for at least one organism, one residual coordinate, and one specified readout. No such positive inequality is present in the finite row. The stored result therefore prepares the coordinate family for such a test but does not report that the test has succeeded.

\paragraph{Blocked readout statistic.}
The available contact classes include per-organism codon-usage tables, tRNA gene-copy spectra, and possible tAI or stAI weights. These are separate contact channels. The row does not contain an organism-matched table joining codon usage, tRNA copy number, and tAI or stAI into an amino-acid-composition-controlled statistic. Consequently the absence of a controlled $S_Q(r\mid Z,\Delta_M)$ estimate is a boundary on inference, not negative evidence against the residual basis. In particular, the finite run verifies that $B^{*}_{Q6}$ has nontrivial codon-count variation on the three named organisms; it does not verify a positive survival score against an $M$-density baseline.

\paragraph{Local non-promotion rule.}
The result must remain at the level supplied by the contact. Codon-count residuals alone do not establish ribosome-mediated translation realization, protein output, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biochemical function, biochemical mechanism, or a universal biological law. A separate and matched contact would be required before any such interpretation could be made. A future translation-layer statement would need an explicit readout $r$, the conditioning variables $Z$ including amino-acid sequence $y$, and the comparison baseline $\Delta_M$. Until those data are present, the correct conclusion is finite residual-coordinate nontriviality on three organism codon-count tables, with $7/9$ nonzero coordinates at threshold $0.01$ and no cross-layer promotion.
