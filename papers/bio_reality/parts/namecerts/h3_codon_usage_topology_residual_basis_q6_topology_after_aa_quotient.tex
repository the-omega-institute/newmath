In this row the residual computation reports $9$ basis elements, with $7$ nonzero elements under variance threshold $0.01$.  Equivalently,

\begin{aligned}

|\{q\in B^*_{Q6}: \operatorname{Var}(q)>0.01\}| &= 7,\\

This number is a finite-row geometry fact about the three observed organism tables.  It says that the chosen residual coordinates are not all collapsed by the amino-acid quotient in this contact, but it does not by itself say that any residual coordinate changes translation output.

\paragraph{Conditional survival test.}
The intended readout is a controlled survival comparison.  For a candidate readout $r$ such as a tAI or stAI weight, ribosome-occupancy statistic, or protein-abundance statistic, the residual coordinate $q$ is tested only after the amino-acid sequence $y$ and contextual variables $c$ have been included in the control set $Z$.  The comparison has the form
$$
\begin{aligned}
S_Q(r\mid Z) &= J_0(Z)-J_Q(Z,q),\\
Z &= (y,c,\hbox{simpler codon-feature baselines}).
\end{aligned}
$$
A positive value would mean that adding the residual coordinate improves the bounded readout under the stated controls.  The finite row available here does not contain organism-matched estimates of $S_Q(r\mid Z)$, nor does it contain $p$-values, fitted coefficients, likelihood scores, or module-classification statistics for a controlled translation readout.  Thus the present result is the coordinate-preparation and finite-contact part of the test, not the full survival comparison.

\paragraph{Why the quotient matters.}
A model that looks only at membership in an $M$-type codon class can be locally informative while still being unable to ask several residual questions in this basis.  The listed coordinates include contrasts such as $Leu_{CUN/UUR}$ and $K_{AAA}$ versus its synonymous alternative class, which are precisely the kind of distinctions that may disappear if the analysis treats synonymous codons through a coarser local-density variable alone.  The finite row therefore supports a sharply bounded statement: after controlling for amino-acid identity, the selected $Q6$ residual basis still has nontrivial organism-level variation in $7$ out of $9$ coordinates across human, yeast, and \textit{E. coli} K-12 codon-count summaries.  It does not yet decide whether those coordinates beat an $M$-density baseline on a translation-layer readout.

\paragraph{Reality contacts and blocked statistic.}
Three contact classes are named for the complete test: per-organism codon-usage tables, organism-level tRNA gene-copy spectra, and organism-scoped tAI or stAI weights.  The available finite row confirms the codon-count side and names the bounded translation-weight category, but it keeps the required bridges separate.  The missing paired object is an organism-matched table containing codon usage, tRNA-copy annotations, and tAI or stAI weights together with amino-acid-composition-controlled estimates of $S_Q(r\mid Z,\Delta_M)$.  Until that statistic exists, the absence of a positive residual score is not negative evidence; it is an uncomputed comparison.

\paragraph{Local claim boundary.}
The chapter records a finite codon-usage residual fact and the exact next statistic needed to test it against translation readouts.  Nonzero residual variance in codon-count tables does not establish translation realization, ribosome-mediated mechanism, protein abundance, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, biochemical mechanism, or a universal law.  Any such promotion would require an additional contact that directly supplies the relevant readout under the required controls.  In the current row, the only ready conclusion is that the amino-acid quotient leaves a nontrivial $B^*_{Q6}$ residual geometry in the three-organism finite contact, with $7/9$ residual coordinates nonzero at threshold $0.01$.
