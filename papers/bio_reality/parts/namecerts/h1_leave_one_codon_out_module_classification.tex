\paragraph{Finite codon carrier.}
The record is a finite code-read experiment on the six-bit codon display $Q_6$.  The carrier is not treated as a mechanism for translation, folding, admissibility, or function.  It is used only as a coordinate substrate on which curated standard-code labels can be read back.  The distinguished finite row is the thirteen-codon set
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The cardinality check gives $|R|=13$.  The same finite row is reported as a punctured-cube object with face-vector $(13,16,4)$, so the data are not merely a list of labels: they carry a local tile shape with $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional incidences, and $4$ two-dimensional faces.  This is the geometric object tested by the leave-one-codon-out module classifier.

Thus the reported class-preserving exact-shape value is $0.0$ and the lower-tail value is $0.0035$.  No finite list of the $20$ elements of $M$ is supplied here; the available evidence is its size and the null-model statistics.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code and family checks.}
The broader leave-one-code-out test evaluates $23$ tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$, whereas the hit rate above baseline is $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M-T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary row is again $0.9841269841269841$.  The family stratification has $5$ families.  The family hit rates above baseline are $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for the alternative-yeast family.  These numbers support a code-layer readback claim: the local tile has predictive value for curated codon-label changes in this finite corpus.  They do not establish a biological necessity law.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out module assignment.}
The module classifier partitions all thirteen codons of $R$ into named local modules with no unassigned residue.  The reported modules are

\begin{aligned}

The completeness audit reports $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, $\mathrm{assigned\_count}=13$, no extra module codons, no codons missing from modules, and no unassigned codons.  The leave-one-codon-out row also reports $\mathrm{predictable\_count}=8$, $\mathrm{boundary\_count}=5$, $\mathrm{anchor\_count}=0$, $\mathrm{closure\_coverage\_high}=8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  Thus each module is covered by either predictable or boundary behavior, and the classifier is complete on the finite row actually observed.

\paragraph{Code-read interpretation.}
The biological contact is the curated standard-code table: a codon-to-amino-acid-or-stop label bridge.  The appropriate statement is therefore local and finite.  In the six-bit display, the thirteen-codon row $R$ forms a punctured local tile whose module decomposition is complete, whose leave-one-codon-out recovery is $1.0$, and whose leave-one-code-out behavior is far above the reported baseline.  The contact reads labels from the standard code table and tests local codon-tile label splits.  It does not observe translation dynamics, ribosomal realization, molecular structure, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or evolutionary necessity.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
The cannot-claim boundary is part of the result.  A local codon tile is not a global biological law.  Window-six geometry alone is not translation, not structure, not physical admissibility, not function, not a mechanism, and not universality.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure.  Any crossing from code-read to translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality requires a separate reality contact supplying that higher-layer observation.  Without such a contact, the present row remains a finite code-layer classification of a curated standard-code label table.
