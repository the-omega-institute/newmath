Thus the codon-level evidence is finite and directly enumerable: there are thirteen codon labels in the boundary set, sixteen one-dimensional incidences in the local tile, and four two-dimensional faces. \paragraph{Biological interpretation.}
The reality contact used here is a curated standard-code table: it states which codons carry amino-acid or stop labels in the standard code. This contact is sufficient to test whether the coordinate tile and its modules align with codon-label readback. It is not an observation of translation in a cell, not a structure experiment, not a thermodynamic admissibility measurement, and not a functional assay. Consequently the present result may say that a six-coordinate codon geometry exposes a finite local organization of the standard-code labels, but it may not say that DNA or RNA must obey this geometry as a universal biological rule. It also may not infer folded protein structure from the amino-acid label sequence. Any claim about translation realization, folding, physical feasibility, biochemical function, mechanistic necessity, or universality would require a separate contact designed for that higher layer.

For the finite data supplied here, the module-classification claim is ready at the code-read layer. The decisive finite facts are the exact thirteen-codon boundary set, the complete six-module assignment with no missing or extra codons, the leave-one-codon recovery rate $1.0$, and the leave-one-code hit-rate improvement from baseline $0.19821739130434785$ to $0.9841269841269841$. The sampling and spectral rows add quantitative support for non-random local shape, but the scientific scope remains deliberately narrow: the chapter records a codon-label classification phenomenon, not a claim about protein structure or biological mechanism.
