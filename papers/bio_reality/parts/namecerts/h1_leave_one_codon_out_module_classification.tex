\subsection{Leave-one-codon-out module classification in the codon cube}\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out-module-classification}

\paragraph{Claim.}
The claim recorded here is that the window-six codon coordinate system gives a finite local organization of the standard-code boundary rows, and that the leave-one-codon-out failures are not scattered singletons once read in this coordinate system.  The biological object is the curated standard genetic-code table, used only as a codon-to-label contact.  The internal object is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, where codons are displayed as finite coordinate rows and tested against local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The readback layer is therefore the code-label layer: codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels.  Nothing in this row promotes the pattern to translation dynamics, folded structure, physical feasibility, biological function, a causal mechanism, or a universal biological law.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The verified row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same run verifies that this is the punctured-cube row under the local codon display, with face-vector data $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the observed object is not merely a list of exceptional codons: it is a finite coordinate subcomplex with $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional incidences, and $4$ two-dimensional faces in the selected codon-cube geometry.

There were $25$ matches at Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving $p=0.0125$. In the amino-acid-class-preserving comparison, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed row size was $20$, the exact-shape count was $0$, and only $7$ samples landed in the lower tail at or below the observed $M$ size. Since no explicit list of the $20$ members of $M$ is included in this finite row, this chapter uses only its recorded size and null statistics.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out behavior.}
The leave-one-code-out test evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The hit rate above that baseline was $0.9841269841269841$, and the same value was obtained when boundary support was included with $M-T$.  Without the boundary addition, the hit rate in $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$.  Broken down by family, the finite run found $5$ families.  The hit-rate-above-baseline values were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, mitochondrial, nuclear-ciliate, and candidate-other families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast.  These numbers support a local codon-label classification pattern, not a statement that organisms implement this geometry as a global rule.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out recovery.}
The leave-one-codon-out row separates the observed targets into predictable and boundary-supported cases.  The run reports $8$ predictable codons, $5$ boundary codons, and $0$ anchors.  The high-coverage closure count is $8$, and the recovery rate is $1.0$, with the above-threshold recovery rate also $1.0$.  The absence of anchor cases matters: the evidence here is a local recovery and boundary organization result rather than a claim that a special codon forces the whole code table.

\paragraph{Module classification.}
The complete module classification assigns all $13$ codons of $R$ and leaves no residue: $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no missing codons from the modules, and no unassigned codons.  The verified module partition is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{AGR} &= \{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{AR\_Lys} &= \{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{CUN} &= \{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\

\mathrm{Stop\_Trp} &= \{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module has at least one codon class that is either predictable by the local coordinate probe or retained as a boundary-supported case.  The classification is therefore total on the $13$-codon row and empty outside it, relative to the reported module inventory.

\paragraph{Reality contact.}
The only empirical contact used here is the curated standard-code label table: a codon is read as an amino-acid or stop label.  This contact can test code-layer readback and local codon-tile label splits.  It cannot test translation realization, structural order, physical admissibility, or functional realization.  In particular, a codon-to-label table does not contain an observation of a folded protein, a dynamical translation process, a biochemical viability condition, or an organism-level mechanism.  The local tile pattern remains a finite code-layer observation unless a separate measurement supplies the missing layer.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The finite evidence supports the following bounded statement.  In the six-bit codon-cube display, the standard-code boundary row $R$ is a $13$-codon punctured-cube configuration with stable local module coverage.  Its shape is rare under the sampled null comparisons, its spectral diagnostics are internally coherent, and its leave-one-code-out and leave-one-codon-out tests recover the boundary row with high finite hit rates.  The strongest warranted conclusion is a local code-read classification: the curated labels line up with a compact coordinate-and-boundary structure.  The result does not by itself explain why translation exists, how any protein folds, which amino-acid sequence is physically admissible, whether any sequence has function, or whether the pattern is universal across biological contexts.
