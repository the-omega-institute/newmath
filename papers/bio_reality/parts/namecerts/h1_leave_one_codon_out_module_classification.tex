\subsection{Leave-one-codon-out module classification in the window-six codon cube}
\label{subsec:h1-leave-one-codon-out-module-classification}

\paragraph{Question.}
This record asks whether the codons singled out by the window-six codon geometry fall into a finite module decomposition that is visible at the code-read layer of the standard genetic code. The carrier is the six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$, read as a binary display of the three nucleotide positions. The observable contact is a curated standard-code table assigning each codon to an amino-acid or stop label. The question is deliberately local: it concerns codon labels and finite neighborhood structure, not translation dynamics, folding, physical viability, organismal function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Boundary set.}
The distinguished set $R$ has cardinality $13$ and is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same finite row reports the punctured-cube face data
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the object being classified is not merely a list of codons but a finite tile with vertices, edges, and two-dimensional faces in the codon-coordinate cube. The spectral summary associated with this tile gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128,
\end{aligned}
$$
with the recorded cubic residual $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$. This residual is used here only as numerical evidence that the finite spectral calculation closed to the stated tolerance; it is not interpreted as a biological law.

\paragraph{Module classification.}
The leave-one-codon-out run partitions the $13$ codons of $R$ into six named modules, each of which has at least one predictable or boundary-supported member. The complete classification is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The accounting row is exact: $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, $\mathrm{assigned\_count}=13$, and the lists of extra module codons, missing codons, and unassigned codons are all empty. Thus the classification is a finite exhaustion of the observed boundary set, not a partial clustering. The singleton modules $\{\mathrm{AAA}\}$ and $\{\mathrm{AUA}\}$ show that the local geometry does not require every code-read unit to appear in a multi-codon block, while the $\mathrm{cun}$ and $\mathrm{stop\_trp}$ modules show that larger codon families can be recovered as coherent boundary pieces.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out behavior.}
In the leave-one-codon-out experiment, the recorded boundary count is $5$, the predictable count is $8$, the anchor count is $0$, and the high-coverage closure count is $8$. The recovery rate is $1.0$, with the threshold comparison also recorded as $1.0$. These numbers mean that, within this finite table, every held-out codon in the tested boundary family is recovered either by predictability or by boundary support. The result is stronger than a statement about one favored codon: it is a module-level accounting statement over all $13$ codons in $R$.

\paragraph{Code-table comparison.}
The companion leave-one-code-out calculation evaluated $23$ tables and $63$ targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, whereas the reported hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$. The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ together with the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. Family-wise, the five-family comparison gave hit rates above baseline of $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, mitochondrial, nuclear-ciliate, and candidate-other families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for the alternative-yeast family. These comparisons support the narrower claim that the local codon-coordinate packet retains label-read information under leave-one-out stress.

\paragraph{Null checks.}
The finite shape test used $N=2000$ samples with seed $42$. It found $1$ exact shape match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and $25$ matches with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$. The run required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries, so the empirical comparison is visibly constrained by the finite sampling regime. In the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, the exact match count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$. The corresponding values were $p=0.0$ for exact shape under that sample and $p=0.0035$ for the lower tail. These null checks make the module pattern difficult to dismiss as an ordinary relabeling accident, while still leaving the conclusion at the code-read layer.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The verified finite rows support the following local conclusion. In the six-bit codon display, the standard-code labels define a $13$-codon boundary tile whose codons are completely assigned to six modules, with no missing or surplus entries. The module decomposition is stable under the recorded leave-one-codon-out check and is supported by shape, spectrum, and null-model summaries. The biological contact used here is only the curated standard-code label table. Therefore the result licenses codon-label readback and local tile classification. It does not license claims about translation realization, folded protein structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, a universal mechanism, or any necessity that DNA must obey this geometry. Any such higher-layer claim would require an independent contact that measures that higher layer directly.
