\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
This record treats the standard-code codon table as a code-read contact over the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The observed object is not a mechanism of translation and not a structure experiment; it is a curated codon-to-label table.  The relevant BEDC distinctions are the six-bit codon display, a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class.  The finite readback isolates a distinguished row $R$ of size $13$, namely
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA,AGA,AGG,AUA,CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU,\\
&UAA,UAG,UCA,UGA,UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The punctured-cube row test gives the same thirteen codons, with $f$-vector $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the row is not merely a label inventory: it also carries a finite local cubical shape.  The chapter uses that shape only at the code-read layer, where codons are compared with labels in the curated standard-code table.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
The supported claim is only that window-six codon coordinates expose a finite local boundary-tile structure whose labels can be read back through the curated standard-code contact.  No translation realization follows from this row.  No folded protein structure follows from amino-acid label readback.  No physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or necessity of BEDC geometry follows from the finite tile.  Any such higher-layer assertion would require a separate contact: for example, a translation experiment for translation, a structure contact for folding or structural order, and an independent functional assay for biological function.  The present row is aligned precisely because it stops at the code-read boundary: it supports local codon-label split checks and blocks promotion to translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality.
