\subsection{Leave-one-codon-out module classification}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out-module-classification}

\paragraph{Finite codon arena.}
The object studied here is the standard-code codon table as a finite label assignment on the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The six-bit display is used only as a coordinate system for codons; the amino-acid and stop labels are supplied by the curated standard-code table.  Thus the finite packet has two distinct ingredients: an internal codon geometry, and an observed code-layer readback assigning labels to codons.  The claim tested in this row is that the leave-one-codon-out residual set admits a complete module classification in which every residual module is supported by either predictable recovery behavior or by a local boundary role.

The residual codon set has cardinality $13$.  The recorded set is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU, \\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same finite row identifies this set with a punctured-cube shape having face vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
This is a code-layer shape statement: it says that the residual codons occupy a particular local cubical pattern inside the coordinate display.  It does not say that biological translation is generated by that display.

\paragraph{Module partition.}
The module classification row assigns all $13$ residual codons and leaves no extra or missing codons.  Its accounting is
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| &= 13,\\
\mathrm{assigned} &= 13,\\
\mathrm{unassigned} &= 0,\\
\mathrm{missing\ from\ modules} &= 0,\\
\mathrm{extra\ module\ codons} &= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The six modules are the following finite classes:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
These classes are not introduced as biochemical mechanisms.  They are finite code-read modules: groups of codons whose labels and local positions can be checked against the curated table and the leave-one-codon-out probes.  The decisive feature of the row is completeness.  Every codon in $R$ appears in exactly one displayed module, and every displayed module contains only codons from $R$.

\paragraph{Recovery and boundary evidence.}
The leave-one-codon-out probe reports no anchor codons, five boundary codons, and eight predictable codons.  The recorded recovery rate is $1.0$, with the same value reported for recovery above threshold.  In formulas, the finite summary is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{anchor\ count} &= 0,\\
\mathrm{boundary\ count} &= 5,\\
\mathrm{predictable\ count} &= 8,\\
\mathrm{closure\ coverage\ high} &= 8,\\
\mathrm{recovery\ rate} &= 1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Together with the module partition, this supports the local statement that each residual module is represented by predictable or boundary behavior.  The row therefore justifies the classification as a finite code-layer phenomenon: the modules are not merely named after familiar codon families, but are exactly accounted for inside the residual set.

A related leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus boundary was again $0.9841269841269841$.  These values give independent finite support for the same local phenomenon: code-level labels and nearby boundary information recover most tested targets far above the baseline rate.  By family, five families were reported.  The family hit rates above baseline were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate rows, and $0.8333333333333334$ for the alternative-yeast row.

\paragraph{Shape and spectral checks.}
The local residual geometry is also constrained by a shape comparison and a spectral row.  In the shape-significance experiment, $N=2000$ samples were used with seed $42$.  There was one exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} = 0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9} = 0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The sampling run recorded $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  A stricter amino-acid-class-preserving null model also used $N=2000$ and seed $42$.  It found no exact shape matches, observed $M$-size $20$, and counted $7$ lower-tail samples at or below that observed size, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These rows do not identify a physical law.  They say that the observed code-layer shape is uncommon under the stated finite null models.

The spectral row records three scalars:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The associated radial cubic check has numerical residual
$$
\begin{aligned}
-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12},
\end{aligned}
$$
which is also the recorded cubic-polynomial residual.  This supplies a numerical consistency check for the local coordinate construction.  It is not evidence that codon geometry determines protein folding or organism-level function.

\paragraph{Code-layer interpretation.}
The verified conclusion is therefore narrow and finite.  In the standard-code table, the residual set $R$ of $13$ codons is completely covered by six named modules, and every module is witnessed by predictable or boundary behavior in the leave-one-codon-out analysis.  The local cubical shape, the recovery rates, the family rates, the null-model statistics, and the spectral numbers all point to a coherent code-read pattern.

The interpretation stops at that layer.  The curated table supplies codon-to-label observations, not ribosomal translation dynamics, folded protein structure, thermodynamic admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or cross-organism necessity.  A sequence of amino-acid labels is still only a label sequence.  Any promotion from this local codon-tile classification to translation, folding, physical realization, function, mechanism, or universality would require a separate empirical contact designed for that higher layer.
