\subsection{Leave-One-Codon-Out Module Classification}
\label{subsec:bioreality-leave-one-codon-out-module-classification}

\paragraph{Object and coordinate carrier.}
This record treats the standard-code codon table as a finite code-read contact on the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The BEDC-side carrier is not a biochemical mechanism; it is the finite coordinate display in which a codon is read through six binary coordinates, local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The reality contact is the curated standard-code label table, namely the finite assignment of each codon to an amino-acid label or to stop.  The chapter therefore asks a sharply bounded question: after the window-six construction has isolated the exceptional row $R$, does the leave-one-codon-out probe classify every member of $R$ into a local module having predictable or boundary support?

\paragraph{Exceptional row.}
The exceptional row has cardinality $13$.  In the observed finite row it is exactly
$R=\{AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU, UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}$.
The punctured-cube readback gives the same codon set and has face-vector data
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R|&=13,\\
f(R)&=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the row being classified is not an implicit or asymptotic object.  It is the explicit thirteen-codon finite row listed above.  The module classifier is judged only against this row.

\paragraph{Spectral coordinate check.}
The finite coordinate computation also records the spectral quantities
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial polynomial residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, recorded both as the cubic-polynomial residual and as the radial-polynomial value.  This is used here only as a numerical consistency check for the coordinate layer.  It does not promote the coordinate layer to translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Shape and null comparisons.}
Against $2000$ sampled null rows with seed $42$, the exact shape match count was $1$, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$.  The high-Jaccard count with threshold $0.9$ was $25$, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$.  The sampling procedure recorded $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, the exact match count was $0$, the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, and the corresponding lower-tail value was $0.0035$.  These numbers support the finite-row distinctiveness of the observed code-read pattern.  They do not identify a physical cause.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code and family readback.}
The leave-one-code-out probe evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The hit rate in $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, while the hit rate in $M-T$ together with the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$; the reported above-baseline hit rate was also $0.9841269841269841$.  By family, the above-baseline rates were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, mitochondrial, nuclear-ciliate, and candidate-other families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for the alternative-yeast family.  This shows that the same finite local-boundary signal survives a family-level readback, with the stated alternative-yeast reduction, but it remains a code-label result.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out coverage.}
The leave-one-codon-out probe recorded $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary cases, $8$ high-closure-coverage cases, $8$ predictable cases, and recovery rate $1.0$.  The recovery rate above threshold was also $1.0$.  In the local interpretation used here, a held-out codon is counted as supported when the remaining finite coordinate data place it either in the predictable part of the closure pattern or on the local boundary required by the tile.  The zero-anchor count is important: the classifier is not claiming that a singleton anchor mechanically determines the codon label.  It is reporting that the finite row decomposes into predictable or boundary-supported module positions.

\paragraph{Module classification.}
The complete module assignment is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The classification audit reports $R_{\mathrm{size}}=13$ and assigned count $13$.  The missing-from-modules list is empty, the extra-module-codons list is empty, and the unassigned-codons list is empty.  Hence the finite statement is exact: every codon of $R$ is assigned to one of the six displayed modules, and no codon outside $R$ is introduced by the module table.

\paragraph{Verdict.}
The finite-row claim is ready at the code-read layer.  The thirteen-codon exceptional row is explicit, the punctured-cube face-vector is recorded, the spectral residual is near numerical zero at the stated tolerance scale, the shape and amino-acid-class-preserving null comparisons give small empirical tail values, the leave-one-code-out probes show high above-baseline recovery, and the leave-one-codon-out module audit assigns all $13$ row codons with no missing, extra, or unassigned entries.  The supported conclusion is therefore: window-six codon coordinates expose a local boundary-tile module classification for the standard-code label table.

\paragraph{Boundary.}
This chapter makes no claim of translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or biological necessity.  The curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-label readback only.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback is still a code-layer observation, not a protein-structure observation.  Any future promotion beyond code-read would require a separate contact that actually observes translation, structure, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality at that higher layer.
