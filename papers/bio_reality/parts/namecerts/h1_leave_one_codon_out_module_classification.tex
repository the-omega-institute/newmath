\paragraph{Finite coordinate record.} The finite object in this chapter is the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon display on the codon cube $Q_6$. The reality contact is the curated codon-to-label table: each codon is read only as an amino-acid or stop label, and no higher biological layer is imported from that table. The row under study is the leave-one-codon-out module classification on the exceptional set $R$. The verified cardinality row gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\},\qquad |R|=13.
\end{aligned}
$$
The punctured-cube check reports the same thirteen codons and the face-vector signature $(13,16,4)$. Thus the finite record is not merely a list of codon names: it also carries a local cubical incidence profile, with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four two-dimensional faces in the observed punctured-cube presentation.

\paragraph{Module classification.} The module row partitions all thirteen codons in $R$ into six named modules:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{AGR}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{AR\_Lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{CUN}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{Ile\_Met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{SSN}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{Stop\_Trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The classification-completeness row reports $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no codons missing from the modules, and no unassigned codons. Hence the finite statement is exact at the row level: the six displayed modules account for precisely the thirteen codons of $R$. The additional module condition says that every module has predictable or boundary support. In this chapter that phrase is used only as a property of the finite probe output. It is not a claim that the codon table is forced by cube geometry, nor that the listed modules are universal biological mechanisms.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary of interpretation.} The only external contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels. That contact can test code-layer readback and a local label split on the codon tile. It cannot test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, or a universal mechanism. In particular, amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure. The observed alignment is therefore this: the finite codon-coordinate rows identify a locally organized thirteen-codon boundary pattern, and the curated label table lets that pattern be read as a standard-code label pattern. Any step from this local code-layer record to translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality would require a separate reality contact designed for that higher layer.
