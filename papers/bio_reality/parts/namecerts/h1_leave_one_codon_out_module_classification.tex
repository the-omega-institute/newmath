\subsection{Leave-One-Codon-Out Module Classification in the Window-Six Codon Cube}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out-module-classification}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and external readback.}
The finite carrier for this record is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$ equipped with the standard-code label table as an external readback contact. The carrier-side distinctions are a six-bit codon display, a local tile face, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes. The biological object touched by the contact is only the standard-code codon table: a curated assignment of codons to amino-acid or stop labels. This chapter therefore treats the observed pattern as a code-layer finite-row record. It does not treat the local tile as translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{The punctured cube row.}
The distinguished row $R$ has cardinality $13$. The verified codon list is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same finite set is certified as a punctured-cube row with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the object under discussion is not a vague enrichment of the genetic code. It is a concrete thirteen-codon subset of $Q_6$ whose local face structure has four two-dimensional faces and sixteen one-dimensional incidences. The current finite data supply the row $R$ explicitly; they do not supply a separate membership list for the full ambient $M$, although they do report an observed $M$-size of $20$ in the amino-acid-class-preserving null comparison.

In this chapter the spectral numbers are used only as finite-row diagnostics for the code-read geometry. They do not convert the row into a dynamical law or a biophysical mechanism.

\paragraph{Null comparisons.}
Two null comparisons constrain the reading of the row. In the shape-significance run, $N=2000$ samples were evaluated with seed $42$. There was $1$ exact shape match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and $25$ high-Jaccard matches with threshold $0.9$, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$. The run required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries. In the amino-acid-class-preserving null comparison, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$-size was $20$, the exact-match count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$-size was $7$. The corresponding reported values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers support the claim that the observed local shape is non-generic under the stated finite shuffles. They do not say that the genetic code had to arise from this geometry, and they do not identify a causal biochemical process.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out and family rows.}
The leave-one-code-out evaluation used $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$, the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ plus boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. The by-family row reports $5$ families. Their above-baseline hit rates are $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial plastid, $1.0$ for candidate other, $1.0$ for mitochondrial, and $1.0$ for nuclear ciliate. These rows show that the finite predictor is stable across the reported table families at the code-read layer, with the alternative-yeast family as the one family below perfect above-baseline recovery in the supplied summary.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out boundary behavior.}
The leave-one-codon-out row reports $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-closure-coverage codons, $8$ predictable codons, and recovery rate $1.0$. The recovery-rate-above-threshold flag is also $1.0$. The absence of anchors is important: this is not a record in which the codon is recovered because a single external anchor forces it. The row is instead a local closure-and-boundary classification inside the finite codon cube. The five boundary events and eight predictable events account for the thirteen-codon row without promoting the result beyond codon-label readback.

\paragraph{Module classification.}
The module classification is complete. It assigns all $13$ codons of $R$, with assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no codons missing from the modules, and no unassigned codons. The verified module partition is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module has at least a predictable or boundary witness. The classification therefore closes the finite-row accounting problem for the thirteen-codon punctured cube: the row is neither over-assigned nor under-assigned, and the six named modules exactly cover the verified $R$ list.

\paragraph{Reality-contact boundary.}
The only reality contact used here is the curated standard-code table. That contact can test codon-label readback and local codon-tile label splits. It cannot test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, function realization, mechanism, or universality. The correct conclusion is therefore narrow: window-six coordinates expose a statistically non-generic local boundary-tile and module structure for the standard-code label table. No higher-layer biological claim follows unless a separate contact supplies the missing layer.
