\subsection{A punctured local cube in the standard-code table}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-punctured-cube}

\paragraph{Finite row under test.}
The finite object in this record is a window-six codon display, written as a six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$, together with the curated standard-code label table used only as a code-level contact.  The tested row is the subset $R$ of codons at which the local tile separates from the surrounding code pattern.  In the verified run, the row is not an inferred biological mechanism; it is the following thirteen-codon subset of the standard-code table:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The independent cardinality check gives $|R|=13$ and returns the same ordered codon list.  Thus the finite row has a stable combinatorial identity before any biological interpretation is attached to it.  The local face count attached to this row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the object behaves as a punctured cube-shaped tile rather than as an arbitrary thirteen-point subset.  The phrase ``punctured'' is used here only for this finite cubical incidence signature: thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional incidences, and four square faces in the induced local cell structure.

\paragraph{Coordinate and spectral checks.}
The same finite row is supported by a spectral calculation.  The verified values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The associated radial cubic check returns $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, which is numerically indistinguishable from zero at the stated tolerance of the computation.  This does not make the codon table obey a mathematical law of nature.  It says only that, after the codons have been placed in the chosen six-bit coordinate display and the curated labels have been read at the code layer, the row $R$ lands on a reproducible local spectral profile.

\paragraph{Shape significance against finite nulls.}
The shape comparison was run against $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$.  The exact-shape test found $1$ exact match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$.  The high-overlap test found $25$ rows with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$.  The sampling process attempted $57377$ distinct draws and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries, so the reported probabilities should be read as finite empirical rates for this sampling regime rather than as asymptotic laws.  A second null preserving amino-acid classes found $0$ exact matches among $N=2000$ trials and gave a lower-tail count of $7$ at the observed module size $20$, hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These two finite nulls agree on the relevant point: the observed local row is uncommon under the tested reshufflings.  They do not identify a molecular cause, a selection principle, or a universal rule for genetic codes.

\paragraph{Module-level readout.}
The thirteen codons of $R$ are also exhausted by six named local modules.  The verified classification is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The module audit reports $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, no missing codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons.  Thus the row is not merely a set with the right size: its codons are completely covered by the local module partition used in the finite test.  In the leave-one-codon-out check, $8$ codons were classified as predictable, $5$ as boundary cases, and $0$ as anchors, with closure coverage $8$ and recovery rate $1.0$.  This supports the weaker statement that the finite local row has recoverable internal organization under this probe.

\paragraph{Code-family and codon-holdout behavior.}
The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate in $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$ and the hit rate in $M-T$ plus boundary codons was $0.9841269841269841$.  The corresponding rate above baseline was also $0.9841269841269841$.  Family-level aggregation over $5$ families gave hit rates above baseline of $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for the alternative-yeast family.  These finite numbers justify treating the punctured-cube row as a useful code-read pattern across the tested table collection.  They still remain observations about codon-label assignments; they do not observe ribosomal execution, peptide folding, biochemical admissibility, organismal function, or historical necessity.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary of inference.}
The only external contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code table, whose role is to assign each codon to an amino-acid or stop label.  This contact has the right resolution for checking local codon-label splits.  It has the wrong resolution for claims about translation as a realized process, three-dimensional protein structure, physical feasibility, functional phenotype, mechanism, or universality.  Consequently, the valid conclusion is narrow: in the six-coordinate codon display, the standard-code labels determine a thirteen-codon row $R$ with punctured-cube incidence vector $(13,16,4)$, nontrivial spectral values $\lambda_M$, $\lambda_R$, and $\mu$, uncommon shape statistics under the stated finite nulls, and complete module coverage by the six listed codon families.  Any stronger biological conclusion requires an additional contact that directly measures the higher layer being claimed.
