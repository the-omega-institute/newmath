\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, obtained by reading each codon as three two-bit nucleotide coordinates.  The biological contact used here is only the curated standard-code label table: it assigns each codon to an amino-acid or stop label, and thereby permits code-layer readback.  The distinguished row set $R$ is the thirteen-codon subset
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The cardinality check gives $|R|=13$, and the direct punctured-cube check returns the same ordered row list.  In the cubical cell count induced by the window-six coordinate display, the observed face data have
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus this record is not merely a list of exceptional labels: it is a finite cubical boundary tile with thirteen occupied vertices, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four local square faces.  The readback remains a standard-code label readback, not an assertion about translation dynamics.

The phrase ``punctured cube'' means that the thirteen rows form a local codon-tile shape in $Q_6$ whose cell count is consistent with a cube-like tile missing part of its ambient closure. \begin{aligned}

These six displayed modules account for all thirteen rows: the module-classification audit reports $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, assigned count $13$, no missing module codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons.  This makes the boundary tile internally closed as a finite row object: every codon in the punctured-cube row set has a module-level explanation, and no module row lies outside the recorded set.

\paragraph{Shape significance against row-preserving nulls.}
The shape-significance run used $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and incurred $55377$ duplicate retries.  In that sample, the exact shape match count was $1$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap comparison counted $25$ samples with Jaccard overlap at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The amino-acid-class-preserving null gives a sharper boundary check.  With $N=2000$ and seed $42$, it reports observed $M$-size $20$, exact-match count $0$, lower-tail count $7$, exact-shape value $0.0$, and lower-tail value $0.0035$.  Therefore the punctured-cube row pattern is not a generic artifact of preserving amino-acid classes in this finite experiment.  The correct conclusion is local: the standard-code labels, when projected into this window-six coordinate frame, expose a statistically uncommon local tile.  The result does not promote the tile to a global law of the genetic code.

The hit rate in $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate in $M\setminus T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  By family, the above-baseline hit rates were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, and $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families.  In the leave-one-codon-out run, the anchor count was $0$, boundary count $5$, predictable count $8$, high closure coverage $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  These checks say that the local boundary description is reusable for code-label recovery inside the tested finite rows.  They do not say that the boundary implements translation, chooses codons mechanistically, or determines biological function.

The curated standard-code table supplies the external labels; the coordinate construction supplies the finite geometry; the probes supply local boundary checks. Amino-acid label-sequence readback is still label readback. The punctured-cube result is therefore a finite BioReality contact at the code layer, bounded by the curated table that made it observable.
