\subsection{Punctured-Cube Boundary Row in the Standard-Code Codon Display}\label{sec:bior-reality-h0-r-punctured-cube}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
This record concerns the six-bit codon display $Q_6$ equipped with the curated standard-code label table as its only external readback contact.  The carrier is finite: each codon is treated as a coordinate row in the codon cube, and the readback assigns that row to an amino-acid or stop label.  The claim tested here is not that the genetic code is generated by this geometry.  The claim is narrower: within the standard-code table, one distinguished local row set $R$ has the incidence pattern of a punctured local cube boundary.  The finite-row certificate is the explicit set
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The independently recorded cardinality check gives $|R|=13$, with the same thirteen codons listed in the cardinality row.  Thus the row set used by the punctured-cube check and the row set used by the cardinality check agree exactly.

\paragraph{Punctured-cube incidence certificate.}
The geometric finite-row result is that the above thirteen rows form the tested punctured-cube pattern, with recorded face data
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R) = (13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Here the three entries are the counted vertex, edge, and two-face data of the local cubical row complex used by the probe.  The term ``punctured cube'' is therefore a finite incidence statement about this observed row set: it says that the local tile has thirteen occupied codon rows, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four two-dimensional faces under the coordinate display used by the test.  It does not state that a molecular process builds a cube, nor that translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality follows from the tile.

\paragraph{Shape rarity under finite null probes.}
Two null probes constrain how accidental the row shape appears under the sampled finite models.  In the shape-significance run with seed $42$, the sampler used $N=2000$ accepted samples, made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts, and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries.  It found $1$ exact shape match and $25$ matches with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$.  The corresponding recorded probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005,\\
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9} &= 0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second null model preserved amino-acid class information.  In that run the observed comparison size was $20$, the exact match count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$ out of $2000$.  The recorded probabilities were $p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}=0.0$ and $p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035$.  These rows make the local tile a statistically sharp finite object relative to the tested nulls, while still leaving the biological interpretation bounded by the external table contact.

\paragraph{Local boundary verdict.}
The ready verdict is a bounded finite-row statement.  The standard-code contact supplies codon-to-label readback; the coordinate probe isolates the thirteen-row set $R$; the incidence check records $f(R)=(13,16,4)$; the spectral row records $\lambda_M=0.6752479308830213$, $\lambda_R=0.4758352843197002$, and $\mu=3.051487585298128$; the null probes record small exact-shape probabilities; and the module row assigns every codon in $R$ with no missing or extra module codons.  Therefore the chapter may claim a local punctured-cube boundary tile in the standard-code codon display.  It may not claim translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality unless a separate reality contact supplies those higher-layer facts.
