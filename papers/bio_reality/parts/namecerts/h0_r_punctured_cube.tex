\subsection{Window-Six Punctured-Cube Boundary in the Standard Code}
\label{sec:h0-r-punctured-cube}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and observed row.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon display $Q_6$ used only as a code-read coordinate system.  The row under audit is the codon subset
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The verified row count is $|R|=13$, with the same thirteen codons returned by the independent cardinality check.  The local cubical summary has $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the finite observation is not merely a list of labels but a small local tile with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four two-dimensional faces in the chosen window-six coordinate presentation.  This section records that finite tile and its boundary discipline; it does not turn the tile into a biological law.

\paragraph{Label contact and readback scope.}
The only external contact used here is a curated standard genetic-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact supports code-layer readback: it lets the row $R$ be interpreted as a specific subset of standard-code labels and lets the singleton-corner and three-point-class behavior be checked against the table.  The contact does not observe translation as a biochemical process, folded structure, physical admissibility, cellular function, or a universal mechanism.  Thus the admissible claim is local: window-six coordinates expose a punctured-cube boundary pattern in the standard-code label table.  No higher-layer claim follows unless a separate higher-layer contact supplies it.

\paragraph{Shape significance under random rows.}
The shape-significance probe used $N=2000$ random samples with seed $42$.  Because the sampling enforced distinct rows, it made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries.  Only one exact shape match was observed, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9} &= 0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
These finite-row p-values support the statement that the observed punctured-cube tile is unusual relative to the sampled null, but they do not identify a causal mechanism.  The result is a code-table regularity measured inside the selected coordinate presentation.

These rows say that the boundary coordinates remain predictive for code-table label changes across the tested table family, but only at the code-read layer.
