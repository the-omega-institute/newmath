\subsection{Punctured-cube boundary row in the codon label table}\label{sec:h0-r-punctured-cube}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and contact.} The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read only as a coordinate display for codon labels. The reality contact used here is a curated standard-code table assigning each codon to an amino-acid or stop label. This contact is deliberately narrow: it tests code-layer readback and local codon-tile label splits, but it does not test translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism, or universality. The row under study is the subset $R$ selected by the local boundary-tile construction. In the finite record, $R$ has cardinality $13$ and is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured cube with face data
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the chapter claim is not that the genetic code is forced by cube geometry. The claim is only that this curated label table contains a distinguished thirteen-codon local tile whose finite incidence data matches the punctured-cube boundary row above.

\paragraph{Shape significance under finite null rows.} Against the finite shape null with $N=2000$ samples and seed $42$, the run required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and rejected $55377$ duplicate retries. The exact punctured-cube shape appeared once, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
A high-overlap criterion with Jaccard score at least $0.9$ appeared $25$ times, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second null constrained by amino-acid class preservation used $N=2000$ samples with the same seed. In that null, the observed module size was $20$, the exact-shape count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$. The corresponding finite-row probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers support the limited statement that the local tile is not a common accident under the sampled row models. They still remain code-read statistics over a curated label table.

By family, the hit rates above baseline were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast and $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families. The leave-one-codon-out test recorded $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary cases, $8$ high-closure-coverage cases, $8$ predictable cases, and recovery rate $1.0$. These tests say that the local boundary tile is useful for label recovery inside the finite code-read task. They do not say that a missing codon is biologically reconstructed by a physical process.

\paragraph{Local boundary.} The correct BioReality verdict is therefore bounded. The standard-code table supplies a real contact for codon-to-label readback, and the finite coordinate row $R$ supplies a punctured-cube local tile with cardinality $13$, face vector $(13,16,4)$, nontrivial spectrum, significant finite-null behavior, and complete module accounting. Nothing in this contact supplies translation dynamics, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a universal mechanism, or a global biological law. Any claim across those boundaries requires a separate contact with its own carrier, observation protocol, and finite-row audit.
