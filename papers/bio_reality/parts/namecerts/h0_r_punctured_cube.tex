\subsection{Punctured-Cube Readback for the Standard-Code Boundary Tile}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-punctured-cube}

\paragraph{Finite packet.}
The packet \texttt{h0.R.punctured.cube} records a window-six codon calculation on the standard-code label table.  The carrier is the six-bit codon cube, read only through finite codon labels and finite adjacency data.  The distinguished region $R$ has thirteen codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
A separate cardinality row records the same roster and gives $|R|=13$.  The cubical face count attached to this region is
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the observed object is not merely a list of exceptional labels.  It is a finite subcomplex with thirteen occupied vertices, sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies, and four local square faces.  This is the sense in which the packet calls $R$ a punctured-cube pattern: the evidence is a concrete finite face vector together with the displayed codon roster, not an unrestricted biological principle.

\paragraph{Label contact.}
The only biological contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact tests code-layer readback and local tile splitting.  It does not test translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality.  The codons in $R$ are therefore treated as positions in a finite label table and as vertices of a six-coordinate display.  Any stronger reading would require an additional measurement or curated contact whose observations live at that stronger layer.

\paragraph{Spectral row.}
The finite spectrum attached to the same calculation gives the scalars

\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.

$$
The radial polynomial residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, and the cubic-polynomial residual is the same value.  The spectral-radius row reports $0.6752479308830213$ inside tolerance.  These numbers support a finite numerical profile for the displayed split: $R$ is not only countable in the table but also has a reproducible spectral signature relative to the measured companion region $M$.  The full roster of $M$ is not supplied in this finite row, so this chapter uses the supplied numerical size and spectral quantities rather than reconstructing absent codons.

\paragraph{Null comparison.}
The shape-significance run used $N=2000$ samples with seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries.  Exact shape agreement occurred once, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$.  The high-overlap threshold $J\geq 0.9$ occurred $25$ times, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$.  An amino-acid-class-preserving null model, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, reported observed $|M|=20$, exact-match count $0$, and lower-tail count $7$.  Its exact-shape value is $0.0$, and its lower-tail value is $0.0035$.  Thus the local geometry is not being asserted from visual impression alone: the packet contains finite null-model contrasts showing that the recorded shape is uncommon under the sampled alternatives available in the row.

\paragraph{Leave-out checks.}
The leave-one-code-out row evaluated $23$ tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The hit rate in $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate after adjoining the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$; the recorded hit rate above baseline is also $0.9841269841269841$.  The leave-one-codon-out row gives anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, predictable count $8$, high closure coverage $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  These rows say that the local tile has predictive stability at the code-label layer, especially when boundary codons are allowed to participate.  They do not say that a codon string is translated in vivo, that a protein folds, or that a function is realized.

\paragraph{Module accounting.}
The module-classification row assigns all thirteen codons of $R$ without remainder.  The six modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row records $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, no missing codons, no unassigned codons, and no extra module codons.  The family-level leave-one-code-out row gives five families: alternative yeast has hit rate above baseline $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial plastid, candidate other, mitochondrial, and nuclear ciliate each have hit rate above baseline $1.0$.  This supports a finite module-level reading of the punctured cube: every displayed codon belongs to one of the named local classes, and each class participates in the predictable-or-boundary accounting.

\paragraph{Boundary verdict.}
The chapter-level conclusion is therefore narrow.  The finite rows support the claim that the standard-code table exposes a thirteen-codon punctured-cube region with face vector $(13,16,4)$, a reproducible spectral profile, nontrivial sampled-null contrast, and complete module assignment.  The result remains a code-read result.  It may guide later biological probes, but by itself it does not establish translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism, or a universal law.  Crossing any of those boundaries requires a separate contact whose observations directly address the stronger layer.
