\subsection{Punctured-cube boundary row for the standard-code table}
\label{sec:h0-r-punctured-cube}

\paragraph{Finite codon row.}
This NameCert row records a code-layer observation on the standard genetic-code table, using the six-bit codon display only as a finite coordinate system. The distinguished boundary set is the thirteen-codon row
$R=\{\texttt{AAA},\texttt{AGA},\texttt{AGG},\texttt{AUA},\texttt{CUA},\texttt{CUC},\texttt{CUG},\texttt{CUU},\texttt{UAA},\texttt{UAG},\texttt{UCA},\texttt{UGA},\texttt{UUA}\}$.
The cardinality check gives $|R|=13$, and the independent punctured-cube check returns the same ordered support. In the cubical face enumeration the row has
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the observed object is not being summarized only by a label count. It carries a local face pattern: thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional boundary incidences, and four two-dimensional faces in the chosen window-six coordinate display. The row is therefore a finite local tile of the code table, not a claim about every biological process that can be downstream of the table.

\paragraph{Spectral and sampling checks.}
The companion spectral computation gives a stable numerical profile for the same coordinate construction. The recorded values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial cubic residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, which is small enough to treat the reported spectral identity as numerically closed at the recorded precision. The shape test against coordinate-preserving random samples used $N=2000$ trials with seed $42$. It found one exact shape match, hence $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and twenty-five samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, hence $p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125$. The sampler required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and incurred $55377$ duplicate retries, which should be read as a finite-search diagnostic rather than as biological mechanism. The amino-acid-class-preserving null gave no exact matches in $2000$ trials, so the recorded exact-shape frequency is $0.0$ in that run. Its lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, with observed $|M|=20$ and lower-tail value $0.0035$. These figures support the claim that the row is a nontrivial local code-table pattern under the stated finite nulls.

\paragraph{Readback behavior.}
The row is attached to a curated standard-code table, so the permissible readback is codon-to-label information at the code layer. Leave-one-code-out evaluation considered $23$ tables and $63$ total targets. The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the boundary-augmented rate was $0.9841269841269841$; the rate in $M\setminus T$ alone was $0.9365079365079365$. Thus the local tile gives strong finite recovery behavior for code-table labels in the tested family, without converting that recovery into a claim about translation dynamics. The family breakdown covers five families: alternative yeast has rate $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial plastid, candidate other, mitochondrial, and nuclear ciliate each have rate $1.0$ in the recorded run.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The external contact used here is only the curated standard-code label table. It states that codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels, and it is adequate for testing code-layer label splits and local tile behavior. It does not observe ribosomal realization, molecular folding, structural order, physical admissibility, downstream function, a causal mechanism, or universality across biological systems. Consequently, the punctured-cube row may be reported as a finite coordinate pattern in the standard-code readback, with significant local recovery and null-model separation, but no stronger biological layer follows from this row alone. Any later promotion to translation, structure, admissibility, function, mechanism, or a universal law would require a separate contact that directly measures that higher layer.
