\subsection{Leave-One-Codon Local Tile Test}\label{subsec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Question.}
The finite row tests whether a six-coordinate codon display can recover local code-layer information after one codon in the active codon row is withheld. The object under inspection is the standard-code codon table, read only as a codon-to-label table. The experiment does not treat the codon cube as a complete account of molecular translation. It asks a narrower question: when one codon is removed from the observed code row, does the local coordinate closure around the remaining codons identify the missing codon as predictable, boundary-supported, or outside the tested local tile?

\paragraph{Finite codon row.}
The tested row $R$ has cardinality $13$. Its elements are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The row is also recorded as a punctured cube with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the finite input is not an arbitrary list of codons: it has $13$ vertices, $16$ observed one-dimensional adjacencies, and $4$ observed square faces in the local codon geometry. This is the geometric datum used by the leave-one-codon test.

\paragraph{Spectral scale.}
The row has two recorded spectral quantities, one for the observed module $M$ and one for the row $R$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The associated radial polynomial check is numerically zero to tolerance, with recorded value
$$
\begin{aligned}
-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12}.
\end{aligned}
$$
This supports treating the row as a measured finite spectral pattern rather than only as a named biological list.

\paragraph{Local recovery result.}
For the leave-one-codon experiment itself, the finite outcome is sharp. There are no anchor recoveries, five boundary cases, and eight predictable cases:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\#\mathrm{anchor} &= 0,\\
\#\mathrm{boundary} &= 5,\\
\#\mathrm{predictable} &= 8,\\
\#\mathrm{high\ closure\ coverage} &= 8.
\end{aligned}
$$
The recovery rate is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{recovery\ rate}=1.0,
\end{aligned}
$$
and the recorded threshold comparison is also $1.0$. In this finite row, every held-out codon is accounted for either by predictable closure behavior or by boundary placement; none is recovered as an anchor point.

\paragraph{Module assignment.}
The row decomposes into six named modules, and the module classification is complete. The assigned codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA, AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{UCA, UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{UAA, UAG, UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The classification accounts for all $13$ codons in $R$: the assigned count is $13$, the recorded $R$ size is $13$, and the extra, missing, and unassigned lists are all empty. In the finite test, every module has either predictable behavior or boundary support. This matters because the recovery result is not produced by ignoring a difficult residue class; each row codon is placed in a named finite module.

A related leave-one-code-out test evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$, the hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. By family, the hit rates above baseline were
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{alternative\ yeast} &= 0.8333333333333334,\\

\mathrm{candidate\ other} &= 1.0,\\

These auxiliary rows do not replace the codon-level test, but they show that the same finite coordinate method has a strong code-layer signal across a small table family.

\paragraph{Null comparisons.}
The row also has finite null-model evidence. In the shape-significance run with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, there was $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ matches with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The run required $57377$ distinct sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries. In the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed module size was $20$, the exact match count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed size was $7$. The corresponding recorded probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact\ shape}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These values support a local finite pattern claim at the code-read layer. They do not by themselves identify a biochemical mechanism.

\paragraph{Reality contact.}
The empirical contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code table, treated as an observed codon-to-amino-acid-or-stop label assignment. This contact has exactly the resolution needed for codon label readback. It supplies labels for codons; it does not supply ribosomal dynamics, molecular energetics, tRNA abundance, protein folding, organismal fitness, or functional selection. Therefore the finite row can be checked against label assignments, but any claim that passes beyond label readback requires a separate observation of the relevant higher-level phenomenon.

\paragraph{Local conclusion.}
The finite record supports the following local statement. In the six-bit codon display, the row
$$
\begin{aligned}
\{AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU, UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}
\end{aligned}
$$
forms a punctured local tile whose leave-one-codon tests are completely covered by predictable or boundary behavior. The strongest recorded codon-level number is the recovery rate $1.0$, with $8$ predictable cases, $5$ boundary cases, and $0$ anchor cases. The strongest shape-null numbers are $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, $p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\geq 0.9}=0.0125$, and $p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035$.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
No statement about translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, generative mechanism, or universality follows from this row alone. A codon-label table can confirm that a finite coordinate pattern lines up with code-layer labels. It cannot show that a peptide is produced, that a protein folds, that a molecule is physically admissible, that a biological function is realized, or that all organisms must obey this local geometry. Those stronger claims remain outside this chapter unless a separate contact supplies the missing measurement.
