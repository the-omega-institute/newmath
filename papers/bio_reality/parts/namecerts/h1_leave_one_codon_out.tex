\paragraph{Finite object.}

The carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read through a curated standard-code label table that assigns each codon an amino-acid or stop label. The BEDC-side distinctions used here are the six-bit codon display, a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class. The internal structure is coordinate, closure, spectrum, and relation data. The biological contact is therefore deliberately narrow: it is a codon-label readback contact, not a translation apparatus, not a folded-protein contact, not a physical-admissibility experiment, and not a function assay.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary.}
The only reality contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code bridge. It observes that a standard-code table assigns codons to amino-acid or stop labels. It can test code-read behavior and the local codon-tile label split. It cannot test translation realization, structural order, physical admissibility, or function realization. Consequently the admissible conclusion is local and finite: window-six codon coordinates expose a boundary-tile pattern in the standard-code label table, and the leave-one-codon-out row recovers the held-out code labels with recovery rate $1.0$ under the recorded protocol.

\paragraph{Cannot-claim boundary.}
No translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality follows from this chapter. In particular, a local codon tile is not a global biological law; window-six geometry alone is not translation, structure, physical admissibility, or function; and amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure. Any claim crossing those boundaries requires a separate higher-layer reality contact. Without such a contact, the result remains a finite code-read statement about the standard-code codon table, its $13$-codon distinguished row, its punctured-cube face vector, its spectral coordinate, its null-model separation, and its complete predictable-or-boundary module classification.
