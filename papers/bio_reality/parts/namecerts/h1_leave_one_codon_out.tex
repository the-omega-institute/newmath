\subsection{Leave-one-codon recovery inside the window-six codon cube}
\label{subsec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}

\paragraph{Finite object under test.}
This record treats the standard-code codon table as a finite label-readout problem on the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The internal data are the codon coordinates, local face closures, spectrum values, and label relations; the biological contact is only the curated standard-code assignment from codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  The row named $h1.leave.one.codon.out$ asks whether a held-out codon from the distinguished local tile can be recovered from the remaining finite code-layer data without promoting the tile to a translation mechanism or to a structural claim.

\paragraph{Distinguished codon set.}
The finite rows isolate a set $R$ of size $13$.  The explicit members are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row verifies $|R|=13$, and the punctured-cube row reports the face-vector data $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the local object is not just a list of anomalous labels: it has a recorded cubical boundary profile with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional incidences, and four two-dimensional faces after the puncture.  This is the finite geometric setting in which leave-one-codon recovery is evaluated.

\paragraph{Spectral and shape checks.}
The spectral row gives $\lambda_M=0.6752479308830213$, $\lambda_R=0.4758352843197002$, and $\mu=3.051487585298128$.  Its radial polynomial residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, the same value reported for the cubic-polynomial check, so the numerical witness is within the stated tolerance for the finite computation.  A separate shape-significance row used $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$; it required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  Only $1$ exact shape match occurred, giving $p_{exact}=0.0005$, while $25$ samples reached Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$.  Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null with $N=2000$, the observed $M$ size is $20$, the exact-shape count is $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size is $7$, giving $p_{lower}=0.0035$.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon result.}
For the codon-level leave-one experiment, the verified finite row reports $anchor\_count=0$, $boundary\_count=5$, $closure\_coverage\_high=8$, and $predictable\_count=8$.  The recovery rate is $1.0$, and the threshold comparison is also $1.0$.  Read literally, the row says that the eight predictable held-out codons are recovered by the finite closure signal, while five codons remain boundary cases and no codon is counted as an anchor in this experiment.  The result is therefore a complete recovery statement only for the row's own predictable class; it is not a claim that every codon in the biological code is generated by the window-six geometry.

\paragraph{Relation to code-table leave-one tests.}
The code-table leave-one row gives a wider code-read comparison: $23$ tables were evaluated over $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$, the hit rate inside $M-T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus boundary is $0.9841269841269841$.  The reported excess above baseline is also $0.9841269841269841$.  Family-level aggregation covers $5$ families: alternative yeast has hit rate $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families each report $1.0$.  These figures support the interpretation that the local tile has code-layer predictive structure, especially after boundary codons are admitted, but they do not identify a molecular process.

\paragraph{Module partition.}
The module-classification row assigns all thirteen $R$ codons, with no missing codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons.  The six modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
agr&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
ar\_lys&=\{AAA\},\\
cun&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
ile\_met&=\{AUA\},\\
ssn&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
stop\_trp&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The completeness row records $R\_size=13$ and $assigned\_count=13$.  Thus the finite codon tile is fully sorted into named local modules for this experiment.  The singleton modules $ar\_lys$ and $ile\_met$, the two-codon modules $agr$ and $ssn$, the three-codon stop--tryptophan module, and the four-codon $CUN$ module together account for every codon in $R$.

\paragraph{Reality contact and local boundary.}
The only biological contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact can test code-layer label readback and local codon-tile label splits.  It cannot test whether translation is realized, whether an amino-acid sequence folds, whether a molecular configuration is physically admissible, whether a biological function occurs, or whether a universal mechanism has been found.  Consequently the correct reading of the row is narrow: window-six coordinates expose a finite local boundary structure whose codon-label readback agrees with the curated table in the reported leave-one tests.

\paragraph{Negative scope.}
No conclusion about folded protein structure follows from this chapter.  No conclusion about biological function follows from it.  No conclusion about physical admissibility, ribosomal translation dynamics, organism-level universality, or a global law of the genetic code follows from it.  Any such stronger statement would require a separate contact supplying the relevant observation at that higher layer.  The present record remains a code-read result: the standard-code table is used as the label source, the cubical window supplies a finite coordinate probe, and the verified rows quantify the local recovery and boundary behavior.
