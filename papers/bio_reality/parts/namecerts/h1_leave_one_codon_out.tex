\subsection{Leave-One-Codon-Out Boundary Recovery in the Window-Six Codon Cube}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
The finite carrier is the window-six codon cube $Q_6$, read as a six-bit codon display with codons labelled by the curated standard genetic-code table. The chapter studies only the code-read layer: the external contact supplies codon-to-amino-acid or codon-to-stop labels, while the internal BEDC data supply coordinates, closures, spectra, and local relations on finite rows. Thus the object under test is not translation, not protein structure, and not function. It is the finite question whether deleting one codon from the labelled standard-code row leaves enough local boundary and closure information to recover the held-out codon at the code-label level.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$. The observed codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured cube with $f$-vector $(13,16,4)$. This is the finite geometric substrate used by the leave-one-codon-out test: each target codon is removed from the standard-code label table, the remaining local tile is read in $Q_6$, and the held-out codon is scored by whether the closure or boundary signal predicts its code-level placement.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
Against the recorded shape null model with $N=2000$ sampled rows, there was $1$ exact shape match after $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries. The exact-shape probability was $p=0.0005$. The high-overlap event, measured by Jaccard threshold at least $0.9$, occurred $25$ times, giving $p=0.0125$. The amino-acid-class-preserving null model was also finite-row: with $N=2000$, the observed $M$-row size was $20$, the exact-shape count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$. Thus the exact-shape probability in that null was $0.0$, and the lower-tail probability was $0.0035$. The supplied data do not include an explicit list of the $20$ codons in $M$, so this chapter uses the row size and null-model statistics without inventing an $M$ codon list.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out comparison.}
The code-table comparison supplies a second finite control. Across $23$ evaluated code tables and $63$ total targets, the baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate in $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, while adjoining the boundary gave $0.9841269841269841$. The rate above baseline was also $0.9841269841269841$. By family, the above-baseline hit rates were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, mitochondrial, nuclear-ciliate, and candidate-other families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative-yeast families. These rows show that the local codon tile is not merely tuned to one aggregate table count; it persists across the recorded family partition at the code-read level.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out verdict.}
For the specific leave-one-codon-out row, the finite counts are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{anchor count} &= 0,\\
\text{boundary count} &= 5,\\
\text{predictable count} &= 8,\\

The recovery rate above threshold is also $1.0$. In this chapter's finite sense, every held-out codon tested by the row is accounted for either by predictable closure behavior or by the boundary class. Since the anchor count is $0$, the result should not be read as recovery from fixed singleton anchors. It is a boundary-and-closure recovery statement for a labelled codon tile.

The completeness check records $R$-size $13$ and assigned count $13$. Moreover, each module has a predictable-or-boundary witness. This is the local combinatorial content of the claim: the codons in the punctured-cube row are not left as isolated labels, but are partitioned into finite modules whose held-out behavior is covered by the same prediction-or-boundary dichotomy.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The standard-code table is a reality contact for codon-label readback only. A local codon tile is not a global biological law. Window-six geometry alone is not translation, not folded protein structure, not physical admissibility, not biological function, not mechanism, and not universality. Amino-acid label-sequence readback is also not folded protein structure. Any claim crossing from this finite code-read row to translation realization, structural order, physical admissibility, function realization, or universal mechanism requires a separate external contact with its own measured rows. The present result is therefore exactly a finite boundary-tile statement: in the supplied standard-code contact, the leave-one-codon-out row has $13$ boundary codons, complete module assignment, $8$ predictable held-out cases, $5$ boundary cases, and recovery rate $1.0$ at the code-label layer.
