\subsection{Leave-One-Codon-Out Boundary Recovery in the Window-Six Codon Cube}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and contact.}
The finite carrier is the window-six codon cube $Q_6$, read as a six-bit display of the standard-code codon table.  The only external contact used here is the curated standard-code bridge: a table assigning each codon to an amino-acid or stop label.  This contact tests code-layer readback and local codon-tile label splitting.  It does not test translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  The record is therefore a code-read record, not a biological-law record.

The distinguished row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The finite row is

The interpretation is strictly local: the row specifies a boundary-sensitive codon tile inside $Q_6$, with one corner or face-like component removed or distinguished by label behavior.  The finite shape is not promoted to a global principle about DNA, translation, or protein function.

$$

Thus, for this finite row, every tested held-out codon is recovered either through predictable closure behavior or through boundary membership.  The result supports the claim that the window-six coordinate presentation exposes a robust local tile around the standard-code labels.

\paragraph{Cannot-claim boundary.}
The result is a local code-layer boundary result.  It says that, in the supplied finite row of the standard-code table, window-six coordinates and closure probes recover the held-out codon behavior at rate $1.0$, with $5$ boundary cases and $8$ predictable cases.  It does not say that DNA obeys this geometry as a biological necessity.  It does not prove translation realization, folded protein structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Amino-acid label readback is still only label readback.  Any move from this code-read layer to translation, structure, admissibility, function, or mechanism would require a separate reality contact that directly observes that higher layer.
