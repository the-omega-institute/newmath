\subsection{Leave-One-Codon Boundary Recovery in the Window-Six Codon Cube}\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite object under test.}
The object studied here is the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon display.  The finite carrier is the codon cube $Q_6$, and the external readout is only the curated assignment of codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  The row is therefore a code-layer test: it asks whether local coordinate, closure, spectral, and relation data in the cube expose a stable boundary pattern when one codon is withheld.  It does not assert that the local pattern is a biological mechanism.  In particular, the data in this chapter do not by themselves establish translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, or universality.

\paragraph{Recovered boundary set.}
The distinguished residual set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The verified row identifies it as
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row verifies that this is a punctured-cube shaped set with face-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the finite evidence is not merely a list of codons: it contains a specific local cell count, a specific edge count, and a specific square-face count inside the six-bit cube.  The row is compatible with a local tile-face interpretation, but that interpretation remains confined to codon-label readback.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
Against the recorded finite null sample, the exact-shape test used $N=2000$ draws with seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries, which is a useful warning that the sampled space is highly collision-prone under this protocol.  Within those draws, there was $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap comparison found $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second null row preserves amino-acid class information.  In that class-preserving run, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the exact match count was $0$, so the recorded exact-shape probability is $0.0$.  The lower-tail count at or below the observed marked-set size was $7$, with observed $|M|=20$, producing
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{lower\ tail}}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These rows support the narrower claim that the displayed local codon pattern is unusual relative to the stated finite nulls.  They do not turn the pattern into a general law of molecular biology.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary of interpretation.}
The only external contact used here is a curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact is enough to test code-read labels and local label splits.  It is not a structure experiment, not a translation assay, not a thermodynamic admissibility measurement, and not a functional phenotype.  Therefore an amino-acid label sequence read from the table remains a label sequence.  No folded protein structure, structural order, physical feasibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or organism-general law follows from this row unless an additional contact is supplied for that higher layer.

\paragraph{Local conclusion.}
The finite record supports a narrow conclusion.  In the six-bit codon cube, the verified residual set $R$ has size $13$, the listed punctured-cube face-vector is $(13,16,4)$, the spectral summaries separate $M$ and $R$, the shape is rare under the stated finite nulls, and the leave-one-codon protocol recovers all tested cases through predictable-or-boundary accounting.  The module classification is complete for the recorded $13$ codons.  The claim is therefore ready as a code-layer boundary-recovery statement, with its biological interpretation deliberately stopped at curated codon-label readback.
