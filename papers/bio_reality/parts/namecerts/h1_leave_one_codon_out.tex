\subsection{Leave-one-codon-out boundary recovery}
\label{subsec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}

\paragraph{Finite object.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$ equipped with the standard-code label readback supplied by the curated codon table. The observed row is the leave-one-codon-out probe \texttt{h1.leave.one.codon.out}. Its purpose is deliberately narrow: after one codon label is hidden, the BEDC coordinate, closure, spectrum, and relation data are asked whether the omitted codon lies in a locally recoverable boundary tile. The probe is not a claim about translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality. It is a code-layer readback test against a finite external table.

\paragraph{Recovered boundary row.}
The run reports no anchor-only recovery and a nonempty boundary recovery. The finite scalars are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{anchor\_count} &= 0,\\
\operatorname{boundary\_count} &= 5,\\
\operatorname{closure\_coverage\_high} &= 8,\\
\operatorname{predictable\_count} &= 8,\\
\operatorname{recovery\_rate} &= 1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus every recovered case in this row is accounted for by the local closure-and-boundary mechanism, not by an independent anchor class. The threshold flag is also saturated, with $\operatorname{recovery\_rate\_above\_threshold}=1.0$. The finite interpretation is that the hidden codon is recoverable at the code-read layer whenever it belongs to the detected predictable-or-boundary region of the six-bit display.

\paragraph{Exceptional codon set.}
The related cardinality and punctured-cube rows identify the same thirteen-codon set
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\\
&\mathrm{CUU},\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The size row gives $|R|=13$, and the punctured-cube row gives the face vector $(13,16,4)$. This makes the support of the leave-one-codon-out behavior a finite local tile rather than a global biological law. The row therefore reads as a geometry-of-labels statement: these thirteen codons form the punctured-cube boundary object against which the hidden-label recovery is measured.

\paragraph{Module accounting.}
The module classification is complete: $R$ has size $13$, all $13$ codons are assigned, and there are no missing, extra, or unassigned codons. The finite module split is

Every displayed module has at least one predictable-or-boundary witness. The classification is therefore not merely a count agreement; it is a finite partition of the thirteen-codon boundary object into named codon modules, with no residual codons outside the partition.

\paragraph{Spectral and shape controls.}
The supporting spectral row supplies $\lambda_M=0.6752479308830213$, $\lambda_R=0.4758352843197002$, and $\mu=3.051487585298128$. The radial polynomial residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, the same value reported for the cubic-polynomial check, and the spectral radius in tolerance is $0.6752479308830213$. The shape significance row used $N=2000$ sampled rows, with $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries. It found one exact shape match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and $25$ rows with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving $p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125$. These controls do not convert the tile into biology; they say that the finite shape and spectrum are unusually structured under the recorded comparison procedures.

\paragraph{Label-class control.}
The amino-acid-class-preserving null row also used $N=2000$. It reported observed $M$-size $20$, exact-match count $0$, lower-tail count $7$, $p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact\ shape}}=0.0$, and $p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035$. The finite fact is that preserving amino-acid class alone did not reproduce the exact local tile in the sampled null population. Since the row contains the scalar $M$ size but not an explicit codon list for $M$, the present statement uses only the available $M$ cardinality and null-tail statistics.

\paragraph{Cross-row consistency.}
The related leave-one-code-out row evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$. The hit rate in $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate in $M\setminus T$ plus boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. By family, the above-baseline hit rates were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative-yeast. These rows support the same code-read theme: finite local boundary information tracks many held-out label cases better than the recorded baseline, while still remaining a statement about codon-label tables.

\paragraph{Reality contact and boundary.}
The only reality contact used here is the curated standard-code table, whose observed fact is that codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels. That contact can test code readback and local codon-tile label splits. It cannot test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or necessity of the BEDC geometry in DNA. Amino-acid label readback is therefore not protein-structure readback. The correct local conclusion is that the six-bit codon display exposes a finite boundary tile in the standard-code label table, with thirteen exceptional codons, complete module assignment, full recovery in the leave-one-codon-out row, and significant finite controls. No higher biological layer follows unless a separate higher-layer reality contact supplies it.
