\subsection{Leave-one-codon recovery inside the window-six codon tile}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-codon-out}

\paragraph{Claim.}
This record concerns the standard-code codon table after it is read only at the code-label layer. The coordinate object is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, equipped with local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes. The claim is not that the cube geometry is itself a biological law. The claim is narrower: when one codon is held out from the local standard-code boundary row, the remaining coordinate and closure data recover the held-out codon class at the code-read layer with the finite rates reported below.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished row $R$ has cardinality $13$. Its codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured-cube shape with face vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the row is not treated as a free list of codons only: it also carries a finite local incidence shape, with $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional adjacencies, and $4$ two-dimensional faces in the induced codon-cube tile.

\paragraph{Spectral check.}
The finite operator summary attached to this row reports
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial cubic residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, the same value reported for the cubic relation check. This places the numerical row inside the stated tolerance rather than merely giving an informal visual similarity of shapes.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
Against the reported coordinate null sample with seed $42$, the shape row was compared across $N=2000$ sampled tables. There was $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ matches at Jaccard threshold at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The sample required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and had $55377$ duplicate retries, so the quoted probabilities should be read as finite Monte Carlo row summaries rather than closed-form distributional values.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class-preserving null.}
A second null comparison preserves amino-acid class information. In this run, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$. The exact-shape count was $0$, so the finite sample gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}}=0.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This second null is important because it makes the row harder to dismiss as a generic consequence of preserving broad amino-acid classes.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon result.}
For the direct leave-one-codon test, the finite row reports anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, high closure coverage $8$, and predictable count $8$. The recovery rate is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{recovery\ rate}=1.0,
\end{aligned}
$$
with the threshold comparison also reported as $1.0$. Since the anchor count is $0$, the recovery is not being credited to fixed singleton anchors. It is credited to the local closure and boundary behavior of the row.

\paragraph{Module accounting.}
The module classification covers all $13$ codons of $R$. The reported modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{AGR}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{AR\_Lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{CUN}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{Ile\_Met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{SSN}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{Stop\_Trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The assigned count is $13$, the row size is $13$, and the classification reports no missing module codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons. Equivalently, each module has a predictable-or-boundary witness inside the finite classification:
$$
\begin{aligned}
13=2+1+4+1+2+3.
\end{aligned}
$$
This equality is only an accounting identity, but it is a useful guardrail: the leave-one-codon row is not silently dropping a codon outside the named module classes.

\paragraph{Code-table readback.}
The larger leave-one-code-out table test evaluated $23$ genetic-code tables and $63$ targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$, the hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. By family, the above-baseline hit rates were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial and plastid, $1.0$ for candidate-other, $1.0$ for mitochondrial, and $1.0$ for nuclear-ciliate families, across $5$ reported families. These figures support the code-read interpretation: the local tile has predictive content for codon-label changes, especially once the boundary component is admitted.

\paragraph{Reality contact.}
The only biological contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels. That table is sufficient for checking codon-label readback, singleton-label corners, three-point label classes, and local boundary splits. It is not a measurement of translation dynamics, folded structure, thermodynamic admissibility, organismal function, or a universal mechanism. The result therefore remains a code-layer statement about finite label recovery in $Q_6$.

\paragraph{Scope.}
No conclusion in this section promotes the local codon tile to protein realization, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, or universality. In particular, amino-acid label sequences are still labels, not folded proteins. A separate contact would be required before any claim about translation realization, folding, physical chemistry, function, or cross-organism mechanism could be made. The finite row supports a local code-read boundary phenomenon and blocks the higher-layer readings unless such contacts are supplied independently.
