\subsection{Leave-one-code-out recovery by genetic-code family}\label{sec:bioreality-h1-leave-one-code-out-by-family}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite object and contact.}
This record treats the standard-code codon table as a finite code-read contact, not as a mechanism. The carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, with coordinates read through the usual codon display and with labels supplied by a curated standard genetic-code table. The local object under test is a boundary tile in this finite cube: it has coordinate faces, singleton label corners, three-point label classes, and a readback into amino-acid or stop labels. The contact says only that the curated table assigns codons to labels. It does not say that translation is realized, that a protein folds, that a molecular state is physically admissible, that a biological function occurs, or that the observed tile is a universal law.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The primary residual boundary set has cardinality $13$. In the supplied finite row it is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is certified as a punctured-cube object with $f$-vector $(13,16,4)$. Thus the record is not merely a list of exceptional labels: it is a finite cubical boundary datum with thirteen vertices, sixteen local edges, and four two-dimensional faces. The spectral row gives

\begin{aligned}

The near-zero polynomial residual records the numerical closure check for the reported radial value; it is used here only as finite-row evidence for the code-read geometry.

\paragraph{Global table deletion test.}
The leave-one-code-out table test evaluated $23$ genetic-code tables and $63$ target events. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ together with the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. Equivalently, the reported hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$. At the family level the finite row separated the tables into five families. Their above-baseline hit rates were
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{alternative\ yeast}&:0.8333333333333334,\\
\mathrm{bacterial\ plastid}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{candidate\ other}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{mitochondrial}&:1.0,\\
\mathrm{nuclear\ ciliate}&:1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the family aggregation does not dilute the leave-one-code-out signal. Four of the five families have perfect reported family-level recovery above the baseline criterion, and the remaining family, alternative yeast, still reports $5/6$ recovery. The finite conclusion is therefore family-stratified: the local tile is not only visible in the pooled deletion test, but remains visible after the genetic-code rows are grouped by family.

\paragraph{Codon deletion and module classification.}
The corresponding leave-one-codon-out row reports $8$ predictable codons, $5$ boundary codons, $0$ anchor codons, closure coverage $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$. The module row accounts for all thirteen members of $R$ with no extras, no missing codons, and no unassigned codons. The assigned modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
This partition is important because the family-level recovery could otherwise be an artifact of untracked exceptional codons. Here every codon in the boundary row belongs to a named module, and the row explicitly reports $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, empty extra-module codons, empty missing-from-modules, and empty unassigned codons. The finite datum therefore supports a module-level reading of the same boundary pattern.

\paragraph{Null comparison.}
Two null rows bound the interpretation. In the shape-significance run, $N=2000$ distinct samples were targeted using seed $42$, with $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries. There was $1$ exact shape match, so $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and there were $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, so $p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125$. In the amino-acid-class-preserving null row, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, the exact-match count was $0$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$. The reported values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These comparisons say that the finite shape and the class-preserving lower-tail event are uncommon under the stated sampling procedures. They do not identify a biochemical cause. They only justify treating the codon-window tile as a non-generic finite code-read feature of the supplied rows.

\paragraph{Verdict and boundary.}
The claim is ready at the code-read layer. The evidence consists of a thirteen-codon punctured-cube boundary, a stable module partition, a leave-one-codon-out recovery rate of $1.0$, a leave-one-code-out aggregate hit rate above baseline of $0.9841269841269841$, and five family rows with rates $0.8333333333333334,1.0,1.0,1.0,1.0$. The curated table is the only reality contact used here. Consequently the chapter claims a finite local label-split phenomenon in the codon cube and nothing beyond it. No translation process, folded structure, physical admissibility claim, function claim, mechanism claim, or universality claim follows unless a separate higher-layer contact is supplied.
