\paragraph{Finite question.}
This BioReality packet tests whether the standard-code codon labels can be treated as a purely internal consequence of the six-coordinate codon display, or whether the label table remains an external contact even after the window-six geometry has selected a local tile.  The carrier is the codon cube $Q_6$, read as a finite six-bit codon display whose vertices are codons.  The distinctions used in the test are a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class.  The internal data available to the packet are coordinate position, closure, spectrum, and finite relations among selected codons.  The biological object is only the standard-code codon table: a curated assignment from codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  The layer under test is therefore code readback, not translation dynamics.

$$
\begin{aligned}
|M|_{\mathrm{obs}}=20.
\end{aligned}
$$

$$
\begin{aligned}

$$
\begin{aligned}

The point is narrower than a biological law: the test says that the observed code-layer geometry is rare under the stated finite null, not that biology must implement the geometry as a cause.

\paragraph{Reality contact.}
The only external contact used here is a curated standard genetic-code table.  It supplies the observed fact that codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels in the standard code.  Its resolution is codon label readback.  It does not contain ribosomal dynamics, tRNA charging kinetics, folding observations, energetic admissibility, organism-level fitness, or function assays.  Consequently, the contact can test whether the local tile aligns with codon-label splits, and it can test whether the finite row remains visible under code-layer probes.  It cannot test translation realization, protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, or a universal mechanism.
