\subsection{Amino-acid-class-preserving null test for the codon tile}\label{subsec:h0-null-model-aa-class-preserving}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Object and finite display.}
The finite object in this record is the standard-code codon table read at the code-label layer.  Codons are represented as vertices of a six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$, while the biological labels are supplied by a curated standard-code table assigning each codon to an amino-acid label or to stop.  The tile under test is local: it concerns a distinguished boundary-shaped subset and its relation to the codon-label table.  It is not a claim about translation dynamics, folding, physical feasibility, biological role, a generative mechanism, or a universal law.  Those stronger layers would require separate observations of their own.

\paragraph{Observed boundary row.}
The observed boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The finite row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
As a cubical subobject of the six-bit display, this row has the recorded face-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the signal is not merely a count of labels: the row contains $13$ named codons and carries a small two-dimensional cubical trace with $16$ local edges and $4$ local square faces.  This is the finite shape against which the amino-acid-class-preserving null model is compared.

\paragraph{Null model.}
The null comparison keeps the amino-acid class structure fixed while randomizing within that constraint.  In the recorded run, the sample size was $N=2000$ with seed $42$.  The observed comparison size was $|M|=20$.  Among the $2000$ sampled class-preserving alternatives, the exact observed shape occurred $0$ times, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
For the lower-tail size statistic, $7$ sampled rows were at most as small as the observed $M$-row, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{lower\ tail}}=\frac{7}{2000}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite conclusion is therefore narrow but strong: after preserving amino-acid class, the observed local codon-tile shape remains unusual in this sample.  The test does not say that the table was produced by the geometry; it says that this finite row is not reproduced by the sampled class-preserving nulls in the exact-shape statistic, and is rare in the lower-tail statistic.

\paragraph{Comparison with the broader shape sample.}
A separate shape-randomization row used the same sample size $N=2000$ and seed $42$, but required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts, with $55377$ duplicate retries.  In that run the exact observed shape appeared once, giving $p=0.0005$, while $25$ rows had Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving $p=0.0125$.  These numbers agree with the amino-acid-class-preserving test in the direction of the signal: the observed local shape is scarce under the sampled alternatives.  The class-preserving test is the stricter datum for the present claim because it keeps a biological label-class constraint in place.

\paragraph{Local conclusion.}
The amino-acid-class-preserving null row supports a code-layer statement: the standard-code label table, read through the six-coordinate codon display, contains a local boundary tile whose exact shape is absent from $2000$ class-preserving samples and whose lower-tail statistic has value $0.0035$.  Together with the $13$-codon punctured-cube row, the spectral residual near zero, the leave-one-code and leave-one-codon recovery rows, and the complete module accounting, this is a finite code-read signal.  It remains confined to codon labels.  No translation process, folded protein structure, physical admissibility claim, biological function, mechanism, or universality statement follows from these rows unless an additional contact directly observes that higher layer.
