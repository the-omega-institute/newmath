\subsection{Amino-acid-class-preserving null model for the codon boundary tile}\label{subsec:h0-null-model-aa-class-preserving}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite object under test.}
The finite object is the standard-code codon table viewed only at the code-read level.  The coordinate carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, and the only external contact used here is the curated assignment of codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  Thus the chapter tests whether a local boundary-tile pattern survives an amino-acid-class-preserving null model.  It does not test translation dynamics, folding, physical admissibility, cellular function, a biochemical mechanism, or universality across living systems.

The distinguished boundary row $R$ contains exactly thirteen codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The row-count certificate records $|R|=13$.  The punctured-cube check gives the same thirteen codons and the local face data
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4),
\end{aligned}
$$
so the tested object is not an unrestricted codon subset but a specific local tile with thirteen vertices, sixteen edges, and four square faces in the induced finite geometry.

\paragraph{Observed size and class-preserving null.}
The observed matching set $M$ has size $20$.  The null model keeps the amino-acid-class information fixed while resampling the compatible finite rows.  With seed $42$ and $N=2000$ sampled null rows, the exact-shape count is zero:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\#\{\hbox{exact null matches}\}&=0,\\
p_{\mathrm{exact,class}}&=0.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The lower-tail count for null rows with size at most the observed size is seven:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\#\{|M_{\mathrm{null}}|\leq 20\}&=7,\\
p_{\mathrm{lower,class}}&=7/2000=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus, inside this finite experiment, the standard-code row is not reproduced by the class-preserving null sample, and rows no larger than the observed $M$ occur in only $0.35\%$ of the sampled cases.  The result is a code-table readback statement: it says that the curated label table and the six-bit coordinate geometry meet in an unusually constrained local pattern under this null.  It does not by itself identify a causal biological process.

\paragraph{Shape and spectral checks.}
A separate shape-significance run with seed $42$ and $N=2000$ sampled rows recorded one exact shape match after $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  Its exact-shape probability is
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact,shape}}=1/2000=0.0005,
\end{aligned}
$$
and the high-overlap count at Jaccard threshold $0.9$ is $25$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=25/2000=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The spectral row check records
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The associated cubic residual is $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, the same value reported for the radial polynomial evaluation.  This residual is numerically at tolerance scale and serves as a finite consistency check tying the local row spectrum to the recorded geometry.  It is not used as a physical-energy, kinetics, or folding invariant.

\paragraph{Prediction rows and held-out checks.}
The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ targets.  Its baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$, while the recorded hit rate above baseline is $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, and the rate inside $M\setminus T$ augmented by the boundary row is again $0.9841269841269841$.  These numbers show that, for the tested finite table family, the local row supplies strong code-layer recovery information.

The leave-one-codon-out run records no anchor codons, five boundary codons, eight high-coverage closure cases, and eight predictable cases.  Its recovery rate is
$$
\begin{aligned}
8/8=1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
By family, the hit rates above baseline are $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, and $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families.  The family count is $5$.

\paragraph{Module-level finite classification.}
The thirteen boundary codons are partitioned by the recorded module classification with no missing or extra codons.  The assigned count is $13$, the recorded row size is $13$, and both the missing-from-modules and unassigned-codons lists are empty.  The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module has either predictable or boundary status in the finite readback.  This is the strongest local statement supported by the supplied rows: the codons in $R$ form a complete module cover for the observed boundary tile, and the amino-acid-class-preserving null rarely produces rows as small as the observed matching set.

\paragraph{Scope of the conclusion.}
The chapter therefore supports a narrow finite claim.  The standard-code label table, when read through the six-bit codon coordinates, exposes a thirteen-codon boundary row with punctured-cube face data $(13,16,4)$, stable module coverage, strong held-out code-read recovery, and low class-preserving null probabilities.  The conclusion remains at the code-label layer.  No statement about actual translation realization, protein folding, structural order, physical admissibility, biochemical function, mechanistic necessity, or universal biological law follows from these rows unless an additional contact specifically observes that higher layer.
