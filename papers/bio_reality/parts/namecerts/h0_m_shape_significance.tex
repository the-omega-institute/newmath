\paragraph{Observed shape significance.}
The shape test used $N=2000$ sampled comparison rows with seed $42$. Because the sampler enforced distinct rows, it made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries. Among the $2000$ accepted rows, exactly one had the same shape as the observed row, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=\frac{1}{2000}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
A high-overlap relaxation using Jaccard threshold $0.9$ found $25$ matches, hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=\frac{25}{2000}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
Both reported values are finite-row empirical rates from the stored run, not asymptotic probabilities. They support the narrow claim that the observed standard-code label table gives a rare local tile shape under the sampled comparison procedure.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class-preserving null.}
A stricter null model preserving amino-acid class structure was also run with $N=2000$ and seed $42$. In that null, the observed $M$-size is $20$. The exact-shape count was $0$, so the stored empirical exact-shape rate is
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\text{-}class,exact}}=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$-size was $7$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\text{-}class,lower\ tail}}=\frac{7}{2000}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This second null is important because it shows that the local shape is not only rare against an unconstrained finite sample; it remains lower-tail significant after preserving the amino-acid-class layer used by the comparison row.
