\subsection{Window-six local tile significance for the standard-code readout}
\label{subsec:h0-m-shape-significance}

\paragraph{Finite object under test.}
The object considered here is the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The coordinate display records each codon as a finite word over the codon alphabet and then reads the curated amino-acid or stop label only as a code-level observation.  The tested claim is not that the cube generates biology.  It is the narrower statement that the observed standard-code label arrangement determines a local tile shape whose boundary behavior is unusually sharp under finite randomization tests.

The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  In codon notation it is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same finite set is recovered by the punctured-cube calculation, with face-vector $(13,16,4)$.  Thus the row-level evidence is not merely a count: it identifies the same thirteen codons as a local boundary complex, and it records the finite incidence profile by vertices, edges, and two-dimensional faces.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo significance of the observed shape.}
The principal shape test used seed $42$ and drew $N=2000$ distinct randomized samples.  Because the sampling was constrained to distinct outcomes, the run required $57377$ sample attempts and rejected $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among the $2000$ retained samples, exactly one matched the observed shape exactly, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
 p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005,\\
 \#\{\hbox{exact matches}\} &= 1.
\end{aligned}
$$
A relaxed high-overlap comparison using Jaccard threshold $0.9$ produced $25$ matches, hence
$$
\begin{aligned}
 p_{J\geq 0.9} &= 0.0125,\\
 \#\{J\geq 0.9\hbox{ matches}\} &= 25.
\end{aligned}
$$
These figures support the local claim that the standard-code table occupies a rare window-six shape among the sampled alternatives.  The evidence is finite and row-bound: it concerns the observed codon-label table and the displayed tile boundary, not any downstream biochemical realization.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class preserving null comparison.}
A second null model preserved amino-acid class structure.  In that run, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$-side size was $20$.  The exact-shape count was $0$, so the recorded exact-shape probability was $0.0$.  The lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
 p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}} &= 0.0,\\
 p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This class-preserving comparison is important because it blocks a weak explanation in which the observed effect is only a trivial consequence of preserving broad label classes.  The test still remains a code-table test: it does not observe translation kinetics, molecular stability, folding, phenotype, or selection.

\paragraph{Spectral row summary.}
The associated spectral calculation records three scalars:
$$
\begin{aligned}
 \lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
 \lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
 \mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial cubic residual was $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, the same value recorded for the cubic-polynomial check.  This near-zero residual is a numerical consistency check for the finite spectral summary.  It is not a mechanistic law; it only says that the coordinate and relation data give a stable scalar fingerprint for this local codon-window arrangement.

\paragraph{Leave-one tests and module structure.}
The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables against $63$ targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The observed hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$, the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  By family, the above-baseline rates were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative-yeast tables.

The leave-one-codon-out row gives no anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-closure-coverage codons, $8$ predictable codons, and recovery rate $1.0$.  The module classification accounts for all thirteen $R$ codons with no missing, extra, or unassigned codons.  The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The assigned count is $13$, equal to $|R|$, and every module has either predictable or boundary status.  This is the strongest row-level form of the claim: the local boundary is not only rare under the shape tests, but also decomposes cleanly into named codon modules in the finite table.

\paragraph{Scope of the result.}
The curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-label readout.  It does not supply a ribosomal translation experiment, a folded-protein structure, a physical admissibility certificate, a function assay, a mechanism, or a universal biological principle.  Therefore the chapter records a local code-read phenomenon only.  Any statement about translation realization, folding, physical feasibility, organismal function, or universality would require an additional contact that directly measures that layer.  In particular, amino-acid label sequences read from the table are not protein structures, and cross-organism codon wording does not by itself become evidence about folding, admissibility, or function.
