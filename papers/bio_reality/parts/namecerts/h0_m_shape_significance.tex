\paragraph{Shape-significance row.}
For the primary significance test, the experiment run was $0\mathrm{b}267\mathrm{c}88\mathrm{f}8\mathrm{e}4$ with seed $42$ and $N=2000$ distinct sampled rows.  Because the sampler rejected duplicate samples, it made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and incurred $55377$ duplicate retries.  The exact observed shape occurred once among the $2000$ sampled rows, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
\#\{\mathrm{exact\ shape\ matches}\}&=1,\\
p_{\mathrm{exact}}&=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap test counted $25$ sampled rows with Jaccard overlap at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
\#\{J\geq 0.9\}&=25,\\
p_{J\geq 0.9}&=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the finite row records both an exact-shape tail and a high-Jaccard tail: the exact local shape is rare in the sampled null, and close local shapes remain uncommon but not unique.  The row-level verdict is therefore a local boundary-tile significance claim for $Q_6$ codon coordinates, not a universal law about DNA or protein biology.

It still remains a code-layer row: it says that the observed local shape is unusual under the stated finite nulls, not that the coordinate geometry causes translation or folding.
