\subsection{Window-Six Codon Tile Shape and Code-Layer Significance}\label{subsec:h0-m-shape-significance}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and contact.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read as a coordinate display for the standard-code codon table. The reality contact used here is only the curated standard-code label table: it assigns each codon to an amino-acid or stop label, and it is used as a code-layer readback, not as a biological mechanism. The relevant BEDC distinctions are the six-bit codon display, a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class. The internal operations used in this row are coordinate placement, closure, spectrum, and relation extraction. Thus the object tested here is not DNA translation itself, but the finite-row question of whether the standard-code labels induce an unusually shaped local tile inside $Q_6$.

\paragraph{Observed boundary relation.}
The distinguished relation $R$ has cardinality $13$. Its codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same list is reported by the punctured-cube test, whose face-count vector is

The point of this row is that the readback is finite and checkable: the codon set above is the whole relation under discussion, and no unlisted biological layer is silently imported. The observed $M$-shape is not supplied here as an explicit codon list, but the finite run records its observed size as $20$ under the amino-acid-class-preserving null model and tests its shape against randomized tables.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
For claim $h0.M.shape.significance$, the Monte Carlo row used seed $42$ and $N=2000$ randomized samples. The run required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries. Exactly one sampled table matched the observed shape, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap test counted $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers support a narrow finite-row statement: the observed local $M$-shape is rare under the sampled null ensemble. They do not say that the shape is forced by biology, only that the displayed standard-code label arrangement is not reproduced often by the sampled coordinate-preserving randomization.

\paragraph{Class-preserving null row.}
A second null model preserves amino-acid classes. In that row, again with seed $42$ and $N=2000$, the exact-match count is $0$. The lower tail count at or below the observed $M$ size $20$ is $7$, producing

\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This strengthens the local reading: even after preserving the amino-acid class structure, the observed finite tile remains uncommon in the tested ensemble. The conclusion is still code-layer only, because the only external contact in the row is the curated label table.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
The local verdict is that window-six codon coordinates expose a statistically unusual and module-complete local boundary tile in the standard-code label table. The claim stops there. A local codon tile is not a global biological law. Window-six geometry alone is not translation, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or an account of why any organism realizes the code. Amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure. Any promotion beyond code-layer readback would require a separate reality contact supplying the missing translation, structural, physical, functional, mechanistic, or universality evidence.
