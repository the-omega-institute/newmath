\subsection{Window-Six Shape Significance for the Standard-Code Boundary Tile}\label{subsec:h0-m-shape-significance}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
The finite carrier is the codon cube $Q_6$, read as a six-bit display of the $64$ codons after the four nucleotide symbols have been encoded by two binary coordinates per codon position.  The biological contact used in this chapter is only the curated standard-code label table: it assigns each codon to an amino-acid label or to a stop label.  The BEDC object tested here is therefore not translation, protein realization, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  It is a code-layer readback test: do the curated labels carve out a local window-six tile whose finite shape is unusual relative to label-preserving nulls and random codon selections?

\paragraph{The observed boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The observed row is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured-cube shape with finite face vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the first coordinate records the $13$ codon vertices, the second records $16$ local one-dimensional adjacencies, and the third records $4$ local square faces in the induced tile.  This is a finite-row statement: the row is exactly the displayed set of codons, and the shape statistic is exactly the displayed triple.

\paragraph{Shape significance against the sampled null.}
The main shape-significance run used seed $42$ and $N=2000$ sampled comparison rows.  To obtain the $2000$ distinct sampled rows, the sampler made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among the sampled rows, exactly $1$ matched the observed shape exactly, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap statistic counted $25$ sampled rows with Jaccard overlap at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
Both numbers are code-layer finite-row statistics.  They say that the observed local tile is rare under the stated sampled comparison procedure; they do not say that the genetic code is forced by window-six geometry.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class-preserving null.}
A second null model preserved amino-acid class information and again used $N=2000$ samples with seed $42$.  In that run the observed $M$-tile had size $20$.  The exact-shape count was $0$, so the recorded exact-shape value was
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}=0.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The lower-tail count for rows with size at most the observed $M$ size was $7$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This reinforces the same finite claim from a stricter comparison family: the observed code-read tile is not behaving like a generic amino-acid-class-preserving reshuffle.  The conclusion remains local to the codon-label table.

The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate was
$$
\begin{aligned}
0.19821739130434785.
\end{aligned}
$$
The observed hit rate above baseline was
$$
\begin{aligned}
0.9841269841269841,
\end{aligned}
$$
with hit rate $0.9365079365079365$ inside $M-T$ and $0.9841269841269841$ inside $M-T$ plus the boundary.  By family, the hit-rate-above-baseline values were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial plastid, $1.0$ for candidate other, $1.0$ for mitochondrial, and $1.0$ for nuclear ciliate, across $5$ families.  The finite conclusion is that the row has high code-label recovery power across the tested table family; no higher biological layer is supplied by this statistic.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out and module classification.}
The leave-one-codon-out experiment recorded $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-coverage closures, $8$ predictable codons, and recovery rate $1.0$.  The module classification accounts for all $13$ codons of $R$: assigned count $13$, no missing module codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons.  The six displayed modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every module has predictable-or-boundary status in the finite row.  This is the strongest local reading of the chapter: the observed row is not merely a list of exceptional codons but a completely classified boundary tile in the window-six display.

\paragraph{Local verdict and cannot-claim boundary.}
The chapter-level verdict is that the standard-code table supplies a concrete code-read contact in which the finite row $R$ has size $13$, punctured-cube face vector $(13,16,4)$, statistically rare shape behavior, coherent spectral separation, high leave-one-code-out recovery, perfect leave-one-codon-out recovery in the recorded test, and complete module classification.  The claim stops there.  No translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or biological necessity follows from the window-six tile unless a separate reality contact supplies that higher-layer evidence.
