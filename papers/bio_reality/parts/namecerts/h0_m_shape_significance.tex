\paragraph{Finite carrier and contact.}
This BioReality record treats the standard-code codon table as a finite code-read contact over the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The carrier is not a mechanism for translation; it is a coordinate display in which each codon receives the externally curated amino-acid or stop label supplied by the standard-code table.  The finite distinctions used here are the six-bit codon display, a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class.  The internal structure is coordinate, closure, spectrum, and relation data, and the readback is only the standard-code label table.  Thus the observed biological object is the standard-code codon table at the $code\_read$ layer.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The finite boundary set $R$ contains exactly thirteen codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}

&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The recorded cardinality check gives $|R|=13$.  The punctured-cube row reports the same thirteen codons and the face-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
$$
This is the finite local shape used by the chapter: thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional incidences, and four two-dimensional faces in the punctured cube view.  The row is a geometric readback from the codon-coordinate construction; it is not a claim that codon biology is governed by this geometry.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo shape test.}
The main shape-significance run used seed $42$ and $N=2000$ sampled comparison shapes.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts, with $55377$ duplicate retries, before collecting the finite sample set.  Among the $2000$ samples, exactly one sample matched the observed shape exactly, and twenty-five samples had Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$.  The recorded finite-row probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{exact}&=0.0005,\\
p_{Jaccard\geq 0.9}&=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same values are also recorded as $p\_value\_exact\_shape\_significant=0.0005$ and $p\_value\_high\_jaccard\_significant=0.0125$.  The local tile therefore has finite-sample evidence of shape specificity under this sampling protocol.  The statement is deliberately narrow: it says that the window-six coordinate readback found a rare local tile shape relative to the row's null comparison, not that the tile is a biological law.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The only reality contact used here is the curated standard-code table, whose observed fact is that codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact can test code-read behavior and a local codon-tile label split.  It cannot test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure.  A local codon tile is not a global biological law.  Window-six geometry alone is not translation, structure, physical admissibility, function, or a universal mechanism.  Any promotion beyond the $code\_read$ layer would require a separate higher-layer contact, such as a translation experiment, a structural observation, a physical-admissibility assay, or a function-level measurement.

\paragraph{Chapter verdict.}
The finite rows support the following bounded claim.  In the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, the standard-code label table exposes a rare local boundary-tile pattern: $|R|=13$, $f(R)=(13,16,4)$, the observed $M$ row has size $20$, the exact shape probability is $0.0005$, the high-Jaccard probability is $0.0125$, the amino-acid-class preserving lower-tail probability is $0.0035$, and the leave-one-code-out and leave-one-codon-out rows show high recovery at the code-read layer.  The claim is significant as a finite local readback of curated codon labels.  It is not a claim about translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.
