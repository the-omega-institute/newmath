\subsection{Window-six local shape significance for the standard code}
\label{sec:h0-m-shape-significance}

\paragraph{Finite codon display.}
This record treats the standard genetic-code table as a finite code-layer contact with a six-coordinate codon display.  The carrier is the codon cube $Q_6$, whose points are codons written in the usual alphabet and then read as six binary coordinate positions after base encoding.  The relevant distinctions are local: a six-bit codon display, a tile face in the cube, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The contact used here is only the curated standard-code assignment from codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  It is enough to test whether a finite coordinate pattern and its label readback match the standard-code table, but it is not an observation of translation dynamics, molecular folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Observed boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The verified codon list is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is also verified as a punctured-cube shape with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the boundary object is not merely a label collection: it has a finite cubical incidence profile.  The row also records that the observed $M$-side size used in the amino-acid-class preserving null comparison is $20$.  The finite data supplied for this claim do not include a codon-by-codon list for $M$, so this chapter uses the verified $M$ size, spectrum, and shape-count statistics without inventing an omitted membership list.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
For the direct shape-significance test, the run used seed $42$ and $N=2000$ distinct accepted samples.  Producing those accepted samples required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and incurred $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among the $2000$ accepted samples, exactly one matched the observed shape exactly, and $25$ samples had Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$.  The reported empirical probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005,\\
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\geq 0.9} &= 0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers support the narrow claim that the observed local shape is uncommon under the sampled comparison family.  They do not, by themselves, show that the geometry is a law of biology.  They say that the standard-code labels, when placed in the selected window-six coordinate system, expose a rare local pattern relative to the finite null draw used in the experiment.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class preserving null.}
A stricter label-aware comparison preserves amino-acid class information.  In that run, again with seed $42$ and $N=2000$, the exact-shape match count is $0$.  The observed $M$ size is $20$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size is $7$.  The finite-row probabilities are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
The zero exact count should be read as zero among the $2000$ sampled trials, not as a proof of mathematical impossibility.  The lower-tail probability $0.0035$ gives a second finite indication that the observed local object is not a routine outcome of the sampled class-preserving rearrangements.

The useful content here is numerical coherence: the local shape, the boundary set $R$, and the spectral coordinate for $M$ fit the recorded finite calculation to near-zero residual.  This is still a code-layer calculation over codon labels, not a structural or functional assay.

This is evidence that the local code-layer pattern has predictive stability across the finite evaluated table family.  It is not evidence that a codon sequence is translated, folded, viable, functional, or governed by a universal mechanism.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out behavior.}
The leave-one-codon-out row reports anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, high closure coverage $8$, predictable count $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  The recovery-rate-above-threshold field is also $1.0$.  The module classification is complete: $R$ has size $13$, the assigned count is $13$, and the extra-module, missing-from-module, and unassigned-codon lists are all empty.  The module partition is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA, AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\

\end{aligned}
$$
Every listed module has a predictable-or-boundary assignment, and the classification accounts for exactly the thirteen codons in $R$.  This supports a local codon-tile reading of the standard-code table: the pattern is finite, module-wise complete for the boundary row, and recoverable under the leave-one-codon test.

\paragraph{Interpretation boundary.}
The result is a code-read result.  The curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-label assignments, and the window-six coordinate calculation exposes a rare local boundary-tile profile in that finite table.  Nothing in this contact observes ribosomal realization, kinetic pathway, folded protein structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological activity, organismal fitness, or a universal law.  A promotion to any of those layers would require an additional contact designed for that layer.  In particular, amino-acid label-sequence readback is not folded protein structure, and a local tile in the codon cube is not a global biological necessity.
