\subsection{Finite significance of the window-six $M$ shape}
\label{sec:h0-m-shape-significance}

\paragraph{Finite carrier.}
The carrier is the six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$, read as a finite display of codons rather than as a biochemical dynamical system. A codon is represented by its window-six coordinate, while the curated standard-code table supplies the external labels: amino-acid labels and stop labels. The internal BEDC distinctions used here are the coordinate, the closure, the spectrum, and the finite relation between the local tile and the externally supplied label table. Thus the object under test is not translation itself, but a code-read pattern in which a local codon tile can be compared against the standard-code label table.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$ and is exactly the following finite row of codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row is verified as a punctured-cube shape with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
This records the finite local geometry of the boundary tile: $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional adjacencies, and $4$ two-dimensional faces. The statement is deliberately local. It says that the codon-coordinate display contains this punctured-cube boundary row; it does not say that the boundary row is a global biological law.

\paragraph{Monte Carlo significance of the $M$ shape.}
The shape test for $M$ was run with seed $42$ and sample size $N=2000$. The sampler required $57377$ distinct sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries, so the empirical comparison was made against $2000$ distinct sampled shapes. Among those $2000$ finite rows, there was exactly $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=\frac{1}{2000}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ sampled rows with Jaccard overlap at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=\frac{25}{2000}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite-row conclusion is therefore a code-layer significance statement: the observed $M$ shape is rare under this sampled null comparison, with exact-shape frequency $0.0005$ and high-Jaccard frequency $0.0125$. The row also records the corresponding significance fields as $p\_value\_exact\_shape\_significant=0.0005$ and $p\_value\_high\_jaccard\_significant=0.0125$.

\paragraph{Amino-acid-class preserving null.}
A second null model preserves amino-acid class information. In that comparison the observed $M$ size is $20$, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$. The exact match count is $0$, hence the empirical exact-shape value is
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,exact}}=0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size is $7$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,lower\ tail}}=\frac{7}{2000}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
This strengthens the finite observation in a controlled way: even when the comparison respects amino-acid-class structure, the observed size-and-shape behavior remains low-frequency in the sampled finite universe. The conclusion remains at the code-read layer because the class-preserving null is still a label-table comparison, not an experiment in translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The contact used here is the curated standard-code table. It supports codon-to-label readback and local split checks. It does not supply a translation realization, a folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism, or a universality theorem. Therefore the verified finite-row content is the following: in the six-bit codon display, the standard-code labels expose a statistically rare local $M$ shape, a $13$-codon punctured-cube boundary row $R$, a compatible spectral signature, strong leave-one-code-out readback, and a complete module classification of the boundary codons.
