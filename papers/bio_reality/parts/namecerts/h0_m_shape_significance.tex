\paragraph{Finite carrier and code-layer contact.}
This packet studies the standard-code codon table through the finite six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$.  Each codon is read as a six-bit window, with two coordinates for each nucleotide position.  The construction uses four local distinctions: the six-bit display itself, a local tile face inside the cube, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The only biological contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code table, which assigns each codon to an amino-acid or stop label.  Consequently the object under test is a code-layer label table, not a translation apparatus and not a structural or functional observation.

\paragraph{Shape test against randomized tables.}
The main shape test used $N=2000$ sampled tables with seed $42$.  Producing the $2000$ distinct samples required $57377$ sample attempts, including $55377$ duplicate retries.  Only $1$ sampled table had the exact observed shape, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}} &= 0.0005,\\
p_{J\geq 0.9} &= 0.0125,\\
\#\{J\geq 0.9\text{ samples}\} &= 25.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite row therefore behaves as a rare local shape under this null sampling scheme.  The claim is deliberately narrow: rarity here concerns the window-six code-label geometry, not a law of molecular biology.

$$
\begin{aligned}

This check says that the observed local arrangement is still unusual after preserving the coarse amino-acid-class constraint recorded by the finite table.  It does not say that the table was generated by the coordinate system.
