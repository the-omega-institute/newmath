\subsection{Window-Six Codon Tile Boundary}\label{sec:codon-window-local-tile-boundary}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and readback contact.}
This record treats the standard-code codon table as a finite readback problem over the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The carrier is not asserted to be a biological mechanism; it is the finite coordinate display on which codon labels can be compared.  The reality contact is the curated standard genetic-code table, used only as a codon-to-label bridge.  Thus the admissible observation is code-layer readback: each codon is assigned an amino-acid label or a stop label.  The contact does not observe translation realization, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, or any universal mechanism.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  In the verified finite row it is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The punctured-cube check gives the same list and records the face-count vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
f(R)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
This is the local tile datum: thirteen codons form the row, with sixteen one-dimensional incidences and four two-dimensional faces in the tested cube display.  The point of the row is not that biology must obey this geometry.  The point is narrower: after the standard-code labels are read back into the six-bit coordinate display, this finite row is the observed boundary object whose shape can be tested against null rows.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}
The shape test for the corresponding $M$-tile used $N=2000$ sampled null rows with seed $42$.  The run required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries, so the null draw was visibly constrained by the finite state space.  Among the $2000$ null rows there was $1$ exact shape match and $25$ rows with Jaccard score at least $0.9$.  The resulting empirical probabilities were
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}&=0.0005,\\
p_{J\ge 0.9}&=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite-row record therefore supports a local statement: the observed $M$-shape is uncommon under the sampled coordinate null, both at exact shape equality and at high Jaccard overlap.  The amino-acid-class-preserving null gives a compatible check.  In that run, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, the exact match count was $0$, and only $7$ null rows lay in the lower tail at or below the observed $M$ size.  The recorded probabilities were
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class,lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
No list of the members of $M$ is part of this finite row; only its recorded size, shape-match counts, and null probabilities are used here.

\begin{aligned}

\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The cubic or radial polynomial residual was $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, and the spectral-radius value in tolerance was $0.6752479308830213$.  This numerical residual is small enough to function as a finite consistency check on the coordinate computation, not as an independent biological law.  The spectrum is attached to the displayed coordinate object and the curated label readback; it is not evidence of folding, biochemical feasibility, translation dynamics, or organismal function.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out behavior.}
The leave-one-code-out probe evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, while the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  The reported hit rate above baseline was also $0.9841269841269841$.  By family, the row records $5$ families: alternative yeast at $0.8333333333333334$, bacterial plastid at $1.0$, candidate other at $1.0$, mitochondrial at $1.0$, and nuclear ciliate at $1.0$.  These numbers say that the local tile and boundary are strong code-read predictors in the tested finite family split.  They do not say that the tile performs translation or explains why any organism realizes a code.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out behavior and modules.}
The leave-one-codon-out probe recorded $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ predictable codons, closure coverage high at $8$, and recovery rate $1.0$.  The module classification is complete: $R$ has size $13$, all $13$ row codons are assigned, and there are no extra module codons, no missing row codons, and no unassigned codons.  The assigned modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every module therefore has a predictable-or-boundary witness in the finite classification.  This is the strongest local form of the claim: the row decomposes into named codon modules whose members are completely accounted for by the tested boundary and prediction categories.

\paragraph{Local verdict and cannot-claim boundary.}
The finite evidence supports a code-read claim for the standard-code table: in the six-bit codon display, the curated labels expose a local boundary-tile structure with a thirteen-codon row, punctured-cube face vector $(13,16,4)$, uncommon shape statistics, a stable spectral readout, high leave-one-code-out recovery, and complete module coverage.  The claim stops exactly there.  A local codon tile is not a global biological law.  Window-six geometry alone is not translation, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback is still only label readback.  Any stronger biological conclusion requires a separate higher-layer contact, such as a translation experiment, a structure contact, a physical-admissibility assay, or a function measurement.
