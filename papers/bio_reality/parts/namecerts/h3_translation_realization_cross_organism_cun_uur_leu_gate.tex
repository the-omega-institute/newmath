\subsection{Cross-organism CUN/UUR leucine usage as a bounded translation-layer statistic}\label{sec:bioreality-cross-organism-cun-uur-leu-gate}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Packet and measured coordinate.}
This record concerns the leucine codon split between the CUN family and the UUR family, evaluated organism by organism rather than as a universal statement about the genetic code. The carrier is the paired residual consisting of the organism label, the sign and magnitude of $\log(\#\mathrm{CUN}/\#\mathrm{UUR})$, the corresponding tRNA-Leu gene-copy ratio, and an amino-acid-composition control that prevents a raw leucine abundance effect from being mistaken for a synonymous-codon effect. The biological object is therefore not a protein, pathway, ribosome trace, or folded molecule. It is a finite cross-organism table of codon-usage summaries and tRNA-Leu annotation summaries, with the packet asking whether the organism-level CUN/UUR direction changes with the organism-level tRNA-Leu copy spectrum after controlling for amino-acid composition.

\paragraph{Finite rows used by the check.}
The validated run $675\mathrm{fdddb}3187$ loaded three organism rows: Homo sapiens, Saccharomyces cerevisiae, and Escherichia coli K-12. The recorded CUN/UUR logarithmic residuals were
$$\begin{aligned}
\ell_{\mathrm{human}} &= 1.3514980212896148,\\
\ell_{\mathrm{yeast}} &= -0.24869078488829677,\\
\ell_{\mathrm{ecoli}} &= 1.0086640520051702.
\end{aligned}$$
The associated sign vector was $(1,-1,1)$, so the sign was not uniform across the three rows. In particular, the yeast row reverses direction relative to the human and E. coli K-12 rows under the recorded statistic. This is the minimal finite obstruction to a single fixed cross-organism direction for the CUN/UUR leucine split in this packet.

\paragraph{Controlled correlation readback.}
The same run reports a Pearson value of $1.0$ for the paired statistic supplied to the experiment and a positive partial correlation with the tRNA-Leu ratio of $1.0$ over $n=3$ rows after the stated amino-acid-composition control. The compact numerical summary is
$$\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{signs}(\ell) &= (1,-1,1),\\
\operatorname{sign\_uniform} &= \mathrm{false},\\
\rho_{\mathrm{Pearson}} &= 1.0,\\
\rho_{\mathrm{partial}\mid \mathrm{AA\ control}} &= 1.0,\\
n &= 3.
\end{aligned}$$
Thus the finite table supports the bounded claim that, for these three loaded organisms and this curated pairing of usage and tRNA-Leu copy information, the CUN/UUR residual is not a constant-sign quantity and the measured residual is aligned with the tRNA-Leu copy-ratio coordinate under the recorded control. The status of the experiment is reported as passed, and the claim identifier checked by the run is $\mathrm{h3.translation\_realization.cross\_organism.cun\_uur\_leu\_gate}$.

\paragraph{Local interpretation.}
The positive result is an organism-level translation-realization contact only in the narrow statistical sense just described: codon-usage summaries and tRNA-Leu gene-copy annotations jointly constrain the leucine synonymous-codon coordinate. It does not say that CUN or UUR codons are universally preferred, nor that the yeast reversal is a lawlike exception. It says that the sign can change across organisms and that the recorded change is not free-floating with respect to the tRNA-Leu copy-ratio coordinate in the finite checked table. Because the number of rows is three, the value $1.0$ should be read as exact alignment in the available finite data, not as an asymptotic population estimate.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
No full translation event, ribosome-mediated mechanism, folded structure, physical admissibility judgment, biological function, or universal biological law follows from these rows alone. The codon-usage contact summarizes coding-sequence composition, and the tRNA contact summarizes genome annotation by anticodon family; neither observes ribosome dynamics or protein output. A stronger claim would require a separate contact, for example ribosome profiling for mechanistic translation behavior, protein measurements for output, structural assays for folding, or a broader organism panel for population-level universality. Within this chapter the admissible conclusion is only the finite, controlled cross-organism CUN/UUR leucine statistic recorded above.
