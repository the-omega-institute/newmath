\subsection{Boundary record for ORF eligibility}
\label{sec:bioreality-self-claim-or February-eligibility-boundary}

\paragraph{Object and layer.}
This BioReality record concerns open-reading-frame eligibility as a finite start/stop-window question.  The claimed layer is \texttt{orf\_eligibility}, and the biological object is not a protein, not a translated molecule, and not a functional genomic unit.  It is the weaker finite-row question of whether an externally supplied nucleotide interval is eligible under start/stop codon enumeration.  The record is therefore a boundary record: it may describe what would have to be checked once an external sequence, transcript, or curated interval annotation is attached, but it does not itself supply that biological contact.

The BEDC minimal form has carrier ``awaiting reality contact'', a single distinction also recorded as ``awaiting reality contact'', no internal structure, and readback ``awaiting reality contact''.  Thus the internal coordinate is deliberately null at the biological layer.  The packet is not a promoted biological claim; it is a guard against such promotion.  The local self-check records the boundary as ``BioReality self-check only; no codon geometry is promoted across biological layers'', with checked gate count $3$.

\paragraph{Boundary probes.}
Two boundary probes enforce the local interpretation.  The first says that a start/stop-window ORF eligibility record must stay at boundary status unless an external sequence or transcript contact supplies the observed biological interval; it breaks if the pipeline asserts ORF eligibility, translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, or biological function from start/stop-window enumeration alone.  The second says that an ORF eligibility record is admissible only as a boundary probe while it lacks an external biological interval contact; it breaks if an ORF eligibility claim is recorded while no sequence database, transcript evidence, or curated annotation contact can test the proposed interval.  Both probes require the contact type \texttt{curated.orf.interval.annotation}.

\paragraph{Cannot-claim boundary.}
The local conclusion is negative in exactly the intended sense.  The finite rows above verify codon sets, saturation, quotient geometry, and one externally loaded start/stop-window classification row.  They do not verify translation, folding, physical admissibility, biochemical mechanism, organism-level function, evolutionary universality, or a general rule for biological coding.  No such claim follows unless a separate contact supplies the relevant biological evidence.  In particular, start/stop-window eligibility is not translation realization, is not folded structure, is not physical admissibility, and is not biological function.  Because the present contact list is empty, the chapter remains a boundary certificate for what must be tested rather than a certificate that an ORF exists in biological reality.
