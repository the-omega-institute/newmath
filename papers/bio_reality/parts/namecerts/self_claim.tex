\paragraph{Claim packet.}
The record named \texttt{self.claim} is a self-checking BioReality packet for the layer $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$. Its biological object is not a translated gene product and not a protein-level assertion. It is the more modest question of whether a finite nucleotide interval can be treated as eligible under start- and stop-codon enumeration. The packet is deliberately initialized with no internal biological structure: the carrier, the distinction list, and the readback field all say that reality contact is still pending, while the internal-structure entry is empty. Thus the row does not turn codon geometry into a biological event. It records a boundary: a start/stop pattern is a candidate for a later test only after an observed sequence interval, transcript datum, or curated annotation supplies the biological contact.

\paragraph{Local boundary.}
The self-check row itself records \texttt{checked\_gate\_count}=3 and the boundary sentence that no codon geometry is promoted across biological layers. Therefore the chapter-level conclusion is intentionally narrow. The finite rows certify that the enumerations, quotient coordinates, manifest counts, curated-interval split checks, decoy rejections, boundary non-promotions, and paired-sign advantage are present in the supplied record. They do not certify that an eligible interval is translated in vivo, that a peptide folds, that a molecule is physically admissible, that a biological function exists, that a mechanism has been found, or that a universal law holds across organisms. Such claims would require additional contact appropriate to the target layer. In the absence of that additional contact, the proper reading of \texttt{self.claim} is a disciplined boundary packet: codon and interval evidence may test ORF eligibility within the supplied finite rows, while every higher biological interpretation remains outside the claim.
