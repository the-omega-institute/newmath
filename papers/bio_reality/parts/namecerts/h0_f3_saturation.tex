\paragraph{Third-face saturation.}
The saturation computation verifies that the third-face closure of $R$ reaches precisely the following $18$ triples:
$$
\begin{aligned}

Equivalently, relative to $M$, the only two triples not reached by this saturation row are $\mathrm{ACA}$ and $\mathrm{ACG}$.  The companion equality records
$$
\begin{aligned}

This is a closed finite computation inside the packet: it says which codon triples are generated by the stated third-face operation and which two remain outside that generated list.  It does not say that an organism uses any of these triples in a transcript or protein.

\paragraph{Boundary status.}
For ORF eligibility, the packet is allowed to ask what a start--stop interval test would require, but it is not allowed to count such an interval as observed merely from the codon geometry.  The seed record therefore remains at boundary status unless a separate curated sequence, transcript, or interval annotation supplies the biological interval being tested.  This local rule is part of the mathematical content of the row: start/stop enumeration can define an eligibility classifier, while biological realization must enter through a distinct observation channel.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Those contact statistics show how an outside curated interval can test an ORF-eligibility classifier when such a contact is actually present.  They do not retroactively make the $h0.f3.saturation$ geometry into a translation claim.  The finite saturation row proves only the displayed codon-set equalities, the $18$-element third-face saturation, the two-element complement $\{\mathrm{ACA},\mathrm{ACG}\}$ inside $M$, and the zero boundary obstruction recorded for that operation. Any such assertion would require its own separately declared contact and its own finite audit.
