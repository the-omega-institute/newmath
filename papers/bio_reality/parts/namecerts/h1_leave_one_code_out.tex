\subsection{Leave-One-Code-Out Boundary Readback}
\label{subsec:leave-one-code-out-boundary-readback}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and code-layer contact.}
The finite object in this record is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read through a curated standard-code label table.  The carrier is not treated as a biochemical mechanism.  It is a finite coordinate display whose observable labels are supplied by the external code table: each codon receives an amino-acid label or a stop label, and the only directly tested layer is code readback.  The local distinctions used here are the six-bit codon display, local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes.  The internal structure recorded by the computation is coordinate, closure, spectrum, and relation data; the biological readback is only the standard-code label table.

\paragraph{Distinguished boundary row.}
The relational row $R$ has cardinality $13$.  The observed codons are
$$\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}$$
The same row is recorded as a punctured-cube pattern with $f$-vector
$$\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}$$
Thus the finite signature is not just a count of recoded codons: it has a face structure with thirteen vertices, sixteen edges, and four square faces in the induced local tile.  This is the finite BEDC-side boundary object against which leave-one-code-out recovery is tested.

They do not assert translation dynamics, protein structure, energy admissibility, function, or evolutionary mechanism.

\paragraph{Shape significance against finite nulls.}
The basic shape null used $N=2000$ samples with seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and incurred $55377$ duplicate retries.  The exact finite shape appeared once, giving
$$\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}$$
The high-overlap test counted $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}$$
A stricter amino-acid-class-preserving null, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, observed no exact shape matches.  Its observed $M$-size was $20$; the lower-tail count at or below that size was $7$, so
$$\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}$$
These finite nulls support the claim that the row is an unusual coordinate-and-label shape under the tested randomizations.  They do not convert the row into a universal biological law.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out readback.}
The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was
$$\begin{aligned}
0.19821739130434785,
\end{aligned}$$
while the observed hit rate above baseline was
$$\begin{aligned}
0.9841269841269841.
\end{aligned}$$
The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  Thus almost all withheld code-table targets fall back into the finite coordinate row once the local boundary allowance is included.  This is the central code-read result of the chapter: the window-six tile has strong leave-one-code-out recovery at the label layer.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code bridge.  It supports codon-to-label readback and local split checks.  It does not observe translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  Therefore the result is deliberately local: window-six codon coordinates expose a statistically sharp boundary-tile structure for code labels, but the curated labels remain external reality contacts.  An amino-acid label sequence is not a folded protein, and a local codon tile is not a global biological law.  Any claim about translation, folding, admissible molecular physics, function, mechanism, or universality would require a separate contact that directly measures that higher layer.
