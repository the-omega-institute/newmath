\subsection{Leave-One-Code-Out Readback for the Window-Six Codon Tile}\label{sec:h1-leave-one-code-out}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and external contact.}
This record concerns the standard-code codon table as a code-layer readback problem on the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$.  The finite display is not treated as a biological mechanism.  It is treated as a coordinate carrier whose rows are codons, whose labels are amino-acid or stop labels supplied by a curated standard-code table, and whose local geometry can be queried by tile, corner, class, closure, spectrum, and relation probes.  The reality contact is therefore narrow: a curated standard genetic-code bridge assigns each codon to an amino-acid or stop label.  That contact tests code readback and local codon-tile label split only.  It does not test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

$$
\begin{aligned}

&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.

$$
The same finite row is reported as a punctured-cube shape with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).

Thus the boundary object is not a vague visual resemblance: the row count, edge count, and square-face count are fixed finite data.  The available finite row does not supply an explicit codon-by-codon list for $M$; it supplies the scalar facts that the observed $M$ size is $20$ and that shape comparisons against null samples are sharply nonuniform.

\paragraph{Shape and null-model checks.}
Against $N=2000$ sampled comparison rows with seed $42$, the exact shape match count is $1$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The number of samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$ is $25$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\ge 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
The sampler required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries, so the finite comparison space is visibly constrained rather than freely continuous.  Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the exact-match count is $0$ and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size $20$ is $7$.  The corresponding finite-row values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These values are evidence that the observed local tile is not a common outcome of the tested row-preserving nulls.  They are not evidence that the geometry is a global biological law.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out result.}
The leave-one-code-out probe evaluates $23$ tables and $63$ total targets.  Its baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$.  The observed hit rate above baseline is
$$
\begin{aligned}
0.9841269841269841.
\end{aligned}
$$
The hit rate inside $M-T$ is $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary is $0.9841269841269841$.  The numerical content of the claim is therefore precise: in this finite row, almost all leave-one-code-out targets are recovered by the local window-six tile plus its boundary allowance, while the baseline recovery rate is about one fifth.  The result is a code-read result.  It says that curated codon labels, when one code table is held out, are largely compatible with the local tile boundary derived from the remaining finite structure.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The chapter-level claim is deliberately narrow.  Window-six codon coordinates expose a local boundary-tile structure in the standard-code label table, and the leave-one-code-out experiment reports $0.9841269841269841$ recovery above baseline over $63$ targets across $23$ tables.  The curated table supplies codon-to-label readback only.  No translation event is observed here.  No folded protein structure is observed here.  No structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality follows from this row.  Crossing any of those boundaries requires a separate reality contact with its own finite observations and its own mismatch audit.
