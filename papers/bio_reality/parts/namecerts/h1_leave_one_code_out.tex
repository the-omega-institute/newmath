\subsection{Leave-One-Code-Out Readback on the Window-Six Codon Cube}
\label{subsec:h1-leave-one-code-out}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and observed code layer.}
The finite object used here is the six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$, read as a binary display of the three nucleotide positions of a codon. The relevant distinctions are coordinate position, local tile face, singleton label corner, and three-point label class. The observed biological contact is only the curated standard-code assignment from codons to amino-acid or stop labels. Thus the row is a code-layer readback test: it asks whether a finite local geometry in $Q_6$ organizes codon-label differences in a way that remains visible when one code table is withheld. It does not assert a biochemical mechanism for translation, a folded protein property, a physical feasibility condition, or a universal law of organisms.

\paragraph{Boundary set.}
The finite boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$. In codon notation the verified list is
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row verifies that this is the punctured-cube boundary pattern with face-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
The boundary therefore is not a free verbal category: it is a concrete thirteen-codon subset of the codon cube with a recorded finite face count. The module decomposition of this same set is complete: $\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\}$ is the $\mathrm{AGR}$ module, $\{\mathrm{AAA}\}$ is the arginine--lysine module, $\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\}$ is the $\mathrm{CUN}$ module, $\{\mathrm{AUA}\}$ is the isoleucine--methionine module, $\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\}$ is the serine/stop-neighbor module, and $\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}$ is the stop--tryptophan module. The accounting row gives $R\_\mathrm{size}=13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no missing codons, and no unassigned codons.

\paragraph{Spectral and null-model checks.}
The row also records numerical structure beyond set membership. The spectral data are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128,
\end{aligned}
$$
with radial cubic residual $-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12}$, the same value reported as the cubic-polynomial residual. This places the measured pattern within numerical tolerance of its finite spectral equation. Against the sampled shape model with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, there was $1$ exact shape match, giving $p=0.0005$, and $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving $p=0.0125$. The sampling log records $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries. Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the exact shape count was $0$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, and the lower-tail count at or below that observed size was $7$, giving lower-tail probability $0.0035$. These figures support the finite claim that the observed local shape is not typical under the two recorded null procedures, while still leaving the result at the codon-label layer.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out row.}
The primary leave-one-code-out run evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ held-out targets. Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The measured hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$; the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$; and the hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ together with the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. In compressed form,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{baseline}&=0.19821739130434785,\\
\mathrm{hit}(>\mathrm{baseline})&=0.9841269841269841,\\
\mathrm{hit}(M\setminus T)&=0.9365079365079365,\\
\mathrm{hit}(M\setminus T\cup R)&=0.9841269841269841.
\end{aligned}
$$
The family-level version of the same row separates five families. The hit rates above baseline are $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial/plastid codes, $1.0$ for candidate-other codes, $1.0$ for mitochondrial codes, and $1.0$ for nuclear-ciliate codes. The weaker alternative-yeast value is still far above the global baseline row, but it is not rewritten as a biological necessity; it remains a finite empirical split in the curated code-table data.

\paragraph{Leave-one-codon-out companion check.}
A companion codon-level row gives recovery rate $1.0$, predictable count $8$, closure-coverage-high count $8$, boundary count $5$, and anchor count $0$. This matters for interpretation: the recovery comes from predictable or boundary placement, not from a separate anchor class. The module-classification row states that every module has predictable or boundary support, and the complete module accounting uses exactly the thirteen codons in $R$. The code-layer reading is therefore internally consistent across the set, module, spectral, null-model, leave-one-code-out, and leave-one-codon-out rows.

\paragraph{Scientific boundary of the result.}
The curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-label observations. It does not observe translation dynamics, ribosomal realization, folded structure, physical admissibility of a peptide, biological function, or a mechanism common to all organisms. The finite result may therefore be stated as follows: window-six coordinates expose a local boundary-tile organization of codon labels, and the recorded leave-one-code-out procedure recovers held-out label behavior at a rate far above the recorded baseline. No stronger biological layer follows from this row alone. Any claim about translation, folding, physical possibility, function, or universality would require a separate contact that directly measures that layer rather than merely reusing codon-label readback.
