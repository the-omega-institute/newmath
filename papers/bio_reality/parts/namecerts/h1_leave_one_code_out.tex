\subsection{Leave-One-Code-Out Readback in a Window-Six Codon Tile}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-code-out}

\paragraph{Finite carrier and external contact.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read through a curated standard-code label table. The carrier supplies codon coordinates, local closure, spectrum, and relation data; the external contact supplies only the codon-to-label readback. This chapter therefore concerns the code-read layer. It does not identify a translation process, a folded protein structure, a physical admissibility condition, a biological function, a universal mechanism, or a global biological law. Those claims require separate contacts not present in this finite row.

\paragraph{Boundary row.}
The distinguished boundary set $R$ has cardinality $13$. In codon notation the finite row reports
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row identifies this set as a punctured-cube object with $f$-vector $(13,16,4)$. Thus the observed local object is not merely a list of exceptional codons: it has thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional incidences, and four two-dimensional faces in the induced finite geometry. The chapter uses this as a local tile boundary, not as a global constraint on biology.

\paragraph{Shape significance.}

$$
\begin{aligned}

p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\geq 0.9} &= 0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second null row preserving amino-acid class labels reports observed $M$-size $20$, exact-match count $0$, and lower-tail count $7$ out of $2000$, giving $p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}}=0.0$ and $p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}}=0.0035$. These numbers support a finite local enrichment claim. They do not convert the tile into a biological necessity.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out row.}
The leave-one-code-out experiment evaluated $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets. The baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$. The row reports hit rate above baseline $0.9841269841269841$, hit rate in $M\setminus T$ equal to $0.9365079365079365$, and hit rate in $M\setminus T$ plus boundary equal to $0.9841269841269841$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{tables} &= 23,\\
\mathrm{targets} &= 63,\\
h_{\mathrm{baseline}} &= 0.19821739130434785,\\
h_{M\setminus T} &= 0.9365079365079365,\\
h_{M\setminus T\cup \partial} &= 0.9841269841269841.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus most held-out code-level targets are recovered by the local coordinate tile together with its boundary correction. The contrast with the baseline is the main finite-row content of this chapter.

The module classification is complete on the same thirteen-codon boundary set. The row assigns all $13$ boundary codons, records no missing codons, no unassigned codons, and no extra module codons. The modules are

Every listed module has a predictable-or-boundary explanation in the finite row. The leave-one-codon-out row separately reports boundary count $5$, predictable count $8$, closure-coverage-high count $8$, anchor count $0$, and recovery rate $1.0$. These data make the local readback robust at the codon level as well as at the code-table level.

\paragraph{Local verdict.}
The ready claim is therefore narrow: window-six codon coordinates expose a local boundary-tile structure whose leave-one-code-out readback is much higher than the finite-row baseline, with $63$ targets across $23$ evaluated tables and a boundary set of $13$ codons. The curated standard-code table is the reality bridge for labels, and the BEDC coordinate construction is the source of the local tile. No statement about translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, function realization, universal mechanism, or biological law follows from this row. Crossing any of those boundaries requires an additional contact that is not part of the present evidence.
