\paragraph{Finite carrier and contact discipline.}
The finite carrier for this record is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read through a standard-code label table.  The distinctions used by the record are the six-bit codon display, a local tile face, a singleton label corner, and a three-point label class.  The internal BEDC structure retained here is coordinate, closure, spectrum, and relation data; the external contact is only the curated standard-code assignment of codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  Thus the chapter is a code-read record: it tests whether a window-six coordinate presentation exposes a local boundary-tile split in the finite label table.  It does not assert that this geometry is translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, a mechanism, or a universal law.

\begin{aligned}

$$
Thus the finite row says that, when one code table is held out, the local coordinate-and-boundary presentation recovers nearly all of the relevant held-out code-read targets relative to the recorded baseline.  The row is deliberately stated at the code-label layer: the targets are label-table targets, not folded structures, functions, translation dynamics, or physical admissibility witnesses.

\paragraph{Boundary verdict.}
The record is ready as a finite code-read claim.  It supports the statement that window-six codon coordinates expose a local boundary-tile structure against the curated standard-code label contact, with $23$ tables, $63$ targets, a $0.19821739130434785$ baseline hit rate, and a $0.9841269841269841$ boundary-augmented hit rate.  It also records shape rarity under two finite null regimes and a complete $13$-codon module classification.  The cannot-claim boundary is part of the result: from this chapter alone one may not infer translation realization, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, universality, or that DNA obeys BEDC geometry as biological necessity.  Any such higher-layer statement would require a separate contact that observes that higher layer directly.
