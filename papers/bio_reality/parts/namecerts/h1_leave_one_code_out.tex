\paragraph{Finite object and readout.}
The packet studies the standard-code codon table through the finite six-coordinate codon cube $Q_6$.  A point of $Q_6$ is a six-bit display of a codon, and the external table supplies the amino-acid or stop label assigned to that codon.  The computation does not treat the table as a mechanism of translation.  It treats the table as a curated label contact: codon strings are read at the code layer, and the only permitted observations are label splits, local tile membership, and finite recovery rates inside the displayed codon geometry.

\paragraph{Scope of the empirical contact.}
The only external contact used here is the curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact supports code-layer readback and local split tests.  It does not observe translation realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a biochemical mechanism, or a universal principle.  An amino-acid label sequence obtained from codon readback is still a label sequence, not a folded protein structure.  Any claim about structure, function, physical viability, organism-specific translation, or universal biological necessity would require a separate contact designed for that higher layer.

\paragraph{Verdict.}
The finite evidence supports a narrow statement: in the six-coordinate codon display, the standard-code labels expose a $13$-codon residual tile with punctured-cube face vector $(13,16,4)$, stable spectral coordinates, significant shape behavior under the tested null rows, and strong leave-one-code-out recovery across $23$ evaluated tables and $63$ targets.  The row is scientifically meaningful as a code-read pattern with explicit boundary controls.  It is not, by itself, a claim about translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality.
