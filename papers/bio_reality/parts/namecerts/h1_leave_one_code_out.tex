\subsection{Leave-one-code-out recovery in the window-six codon tile}
\label{sec:h1-leave-one-code-out}

\paragraph{Question.}
The experiment asks whether the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$ contains a local boundary pattern that remains visible when whole genetic-code tables are withheld.  The object under test is not protein synthesis as a physical process.  It is the codon-label layer: a curated standard-code table supplies codon-to-amino-acid and codon-to-stop labels, and the window-six coordinate system supplies a finite cube geometry in which local faces, singleton label corners, three-point label classes, closures, spectra, and relations can be inspected.  The claim is therefore deliberately narrow.  Window-six geometry may expose a stable local tile around code-label changes, but the labels themselves remain empirical contacts supplied by the curated table.

\paragraph{Finite exceptional set.}
The distinguished codon set $R$ has cardinality $13$.  In the recorded run, the set is exactly
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The same row reports that $R$ is a punctured-cube pattern with face vector $(13,16,4)$.  This is the finite combinatorial object that the later code-holdout tests probe.  It is not asserted to be a law of molecular biology; it is a compact boundary tile in the codon-coordinate display.

\paragraph{Shape and spectrum checks.}
The shape test compares the observed local set against finite random alternatives.  For $N=2000$ sampled alternatives, with seed $42$, the exact shape recurs once, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$.  High-overlap alternatives at Jaccard threshold $0.9$ occur $25$ times, giving $p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125$.  The sampling process required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries, so the table should be read as a finite-row Monte Carlo comparison rather than an asymptotic theorem.  A separate amino-acid-class-preserving null model, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, gives observed $M$-size $20$, exact-match count $0$, lower-tail count $7$, $p_{\mathrm{class,exact}}=0.0$, and $p_{\mathrm{class,lower}}=0.0035$.  Thus the local window-six shape is not reproduced by the tested class-preserving nulls at the recorded finite resolution.

The spectral row supplies a numerical consistency check for the same finite geometry.  The recorded values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128,\\
P(\lambda_M,\lambda_R,\mu)&=-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The residual is near zero at numerical tolerance, so the spectral summary is coherent with the finite row.  This check strengthens the local-coordinate reading of the tile, but it still remains a code-label statement.

\paragraph{Leave-one-code-out recovery.}
The central holdout row evaluates $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate is $0.19821739130434785$.  Against that baseline, the hit rate above baseline is $0.9841269841269841$.  The stricter interior score, using $M\setminus T$, is $0.9365079365079365$, and the score after adjoining the boundary is again $0.9841269841269841$.  In finite-row terms, almost all withheld code-label targets are recovered by the local window-six rule once the boundary part of the tile is allowed.  The result is strongest as a label-recovery statement: it says that the local tile predicts code-table label changes far better than the recorded baseline over the $63$ held-out targets.

\paragraph{Family split.}
The by-family row separates the code-table holdout into five families.  The family-level rates above baseline are $1.0$ for bacterial/plastid, $1.0$ for candidate-other, $1.0$ for mitochondrial, $1.0$ for nuclear-ciliate, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative-yeast.  The non-unity alternative-yeast row matters: it prevents the result from being phrased as perfect recovery in every biological grouping.  The correct reading is that the boundary tile is broadly stable across the tested families, with one recorded family below perfect finite recovery.

\paragraph{Codon-level module accounting.}
The leave-one-codon-out row reports recovery rate $1.0$ and threshold score $1.0$, with $8$ predictable codons, $5$ boundary codons, and $0$ anchor codons.  The module classification accounts for all $13$ codons of $R$: assigned count $13$, no missing module codons, no extra module codons, and no unassigned codons.  The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Every module has either predictable or boundary status in the recorded classification.  This gives a finite audit trail for the claim: the recovery behavior is not carried by an unnamed residue of codons outside the module table.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
The combined rows support the following local conclusion.  In the six-bit codon coordinate display, the set $R$ forms a small punctured-cube boundary object; its shape is rare under the sampled nulls; its spectral row is numerically consistent; and the corresponding boundary tile recovers withheld code-label targets at $0.9841269841269841$ against a baseline of $0.19821739130434785$ over $63$ targets from $23$ code tables.  The result is therefore a finite code-read regularity tied to the curated codon-label table.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
No statement in this chapter promotes the local tile to a claim about translation realization, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.  The curated table observes codon-to-label assignments only.  Amino-acid label strings are not folded structures, and a local CUN/UUR, AGR, stop/tryptophan, isoleucine/methionine, or lysine module split is not evidence for a protein fold or for a universal biological rule.  Any such higher-layer assertion would require a separate experimental or curated contact at that layer.  Until that contact is supplied, the supported content stops at finite codon-label readback and local boundary-tile recovery.
