\subsection{Curated interval contact for open-reading-frame eligibility}
\label{subsec:h2-orf-eligibility-curated-interval-contact}

\paragraph{Layer and local object.}
This record concerns open-reading-frame eligibility as a finite contact problem, not as an assertion that a nucleotide interval is translated, folded, chemically admissible, functional, mechanistic, or universal.  The biological object is the eligibility of intervals under start/stop codon enumeration after an external curated interval source has been attached.  The layer is therefore $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$ only.  A BEDC coordinate or codon-window pattern can organize the test, but it does not by itself supply an observed biological interval.  In this row the required contact is present: the dataset is a curated interval manifest derived from NCBI RefSeq GenBank XML CDS intervals for \emph{Escherichia coli} K-12 MG1655, accession $\mathrm{NC\_000913.3}$, with snapshot date $2026$-$05$-$30$.

\paragraph{Acceptance, rejection, and controls.}
The positive interval statistic reports $72$ accepted positives out of $72$, hence acceptance rate $1.0$, with exact one-sided binomial upper-tail value $2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}$.  The matched non-ORF decoy statistic reports $72$ rejected decoys out of $72$, hence rejection rate $1.0$, with the same upper-tail value $2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}$.  The predeclared paired comparison records $72$ wins in $72$ positive--decoy pairs, again with upper-tail value $2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}$:
$$\begin{aligned}
\widehat p_{+}&=72/72=1.0,\\
\widehat p_{\mathrm{decoy\ reject}}&=72/72=1.0,\\
\widehat p_{\mathrm{pair\ win}}&=72/72=1.0.
\end{aligned}$$
The boundary control is also explicit: $24$ boundary cases were examined and $0$ were promoted, so the promoted fraction is $0.0$.  This is an important negative result, because it shows that the contact row did not turn ambiguous or out-of-scope windows into positive interval claims.

\paragraph{Local conclusion.}
Within this finite row, the curated RefSeq interval contact supports an $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$ claim for the scoped external interval test: $168$ rows were loaded, $72$ positives were accepted, $72$ matched decoys were rejected, $24$ boundary cases stayed unpromoted, and all three held-out splits passed.  The conclusion remains deliberately narrow.  It says that the fixed start/stop-window classifier separates the curated positive intervals from matched decoys in this dataset under the stated controls.  It does not say that the intervals are translated in vivo, that any protein folds, that a molecule is physically admissible, that a biological function exists, that a mechanism has been identified, or that a general biological law has been proved.  Any such stronger claim would require a separate contact appropriate to that higher layer.
