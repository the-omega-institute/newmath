\subsection{Curated interval contact for ORF eligibility}
\label{subsec:h2-orf-eligibility-curated-interval-contact}

\paragraph{Question fixed by the row data.}
The record concerns the layer $\mathsf{orf\_eligibility}$ and asks only whether a frozen start--stop-window classifier can separate curated coding-sequence intervals from matched non-ORF controls after an independently supplied interval annotation has been attached. The biological object is open-reading-frame eligibility as witnessed by start and stop codon enumeration. The initial seed, without the annotation contact, carries no observed interval: its carrier, distinction data, and readback are all recorded as awaiting contact, and its internal structure is empty. Consequently the claim tested here is not that codon geometry alone discovers genes. It is the narrower statement that, once a curated interval table is supplied, a predeclared classifier restricted to start--stop-window information accepts the positive interval rows and rejects the matched decoys in this finite assay.

\paragraph{External interval table and frozen protocol.}
The contact used here is a curated finite interval dataset loaded from \texttt{tools/bio\_reality/data/curated\_orf\_interval\_contact\_dataset.json}. It contains $168$ rows and is dated by the snapshot $2026$-$05$-$30$. The stated source is NCBI RefSeq GenBank XML CDS intervals from \emph{Escherichia coli} K-12 MG1655 complete genome accession NC\_000913.3. The required input classes are present: boundary cases, matched non-ORF decoys, and positive curated ORFs. The protocol was frozen at $2026$-$05$-$29\,19{:}11{:}13+00{:}00$, before the external evidence time $2026$-$05$-$30\,10{:}29{:}31+00{:}00$. The record also states that baselines, classifier rules, held-out splits, and input classes were fixed before observation, and that the analysis used no translation or structure input. The classifier identifier is \texttt{orf\_start\_stop\_window\_eligibility}; its rules were not modified during this contact.

The contact table gives $72$ positive curated intervals, $72$ matched non-ORF decoys, and $24$ boundary cases. The positive rows were all accepted, so the recorded accept rate is $72/72=1.0$. The matched decoys were all rejected, so the rejection rate is $72/72=1.0$. The exact one-sided binomial upper-tail value attached to the positive interval statistic is $2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}$, and the same value is recorded for the matched-decoy rejection statistic. The predeclared paired baseline comparison has $72$ wins in $72$ pairs, hence win rate $1.0$, again with one-sided binomial upper-tail value $2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}$. \paragraph{Local interpretation.}
This is a successful finite contact at the ORF-eligibility layer, not a biological law and not a translation experiment. The result says that, for the supplied RefSeq-derived CDS interval table and its matched controls, the frozen start--stop-window classifier has perfect separation on the recorded rows and stable behavior across the three held-out locus folds. The earlier one-window start--stop enumeration check also reports one externally supplied window, one classified window, scope \texttt{external\_window\_start\_stop\_enumeration\_only}, and no promotion from geometry to translation. The self-check likewise records three checked gates and states that no codon geometry is promoted across biological layers.

\paragraph{Boundary of the claim.}
No translation event, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biochemical mechanism, cellular function, organismal role, or universal rule follows from this contact. The NCBI RefSeq annotation remains a curated biological input rather than kernel content of the finite codon geometry. The matched decoy and boundary controls do not classify windows outside the $168$-row dataset. Any stronger assertion would require a further contact of the appropriate kind: for example transcript evidence for expression, peptide evidence for translation, structural evidence for folding, physical evidence for admissibility, or functional evidence for biological role. Within this chapter the only supported conclusion is the finite, externally annotated ORF-eligibility separation described above.
