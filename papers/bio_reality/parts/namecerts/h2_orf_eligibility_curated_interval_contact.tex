\paragraph{Object of the contact.}

The biological object is not translation, folding, activity, or organismal function. It is the narrower statement that an externally supplied interval can be tested against an already fixed start--stop boundary classifier. The contact used here is the NCBI RefSeq GenBank XML CDS interval annotation for \emph{Escherichia coli} K--12 MG1655, accession $\mathrm{NC\_000913.3}$, with snapshot date $2026$-$05$-$30$. \paragraph{Scope of the conclusion.}
The supported conclusion is exactly an external-interval ORF eligibility contact for this finite dataset. The earlier start--stop-window enumeration had only one external window and recorded the scope $\mathrm{external\_window\_start\_stop\_enumeration\_only}$; this contact supplies a larger curated interval table, but the layer remains $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$ and the scope remains $\mathrm{external\_orf\_interval\_contact\_only}$. The self-check records three checked gates and explicitly keeps codon geometry from crossing biological layers by itself. Consequently, no translation event, folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biochemical function, molecular mechanism, or universal biological law follows from these rows. Any such stronger claim would require a different contact designed to observe that stronger object. The present record says only that, for the stated RefSeq-derived interval table and its matched controls, the predeclared start--stop classifier separates positives from decoys perfectly while leaving boundary cases unpromoted.
