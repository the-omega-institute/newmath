\subsection{Curated Interval Contact for ORF Eligibility}
\label{subsec:bioreality-orf-eligibility-curated-interval-contact}

\paragraph{Claim scope.}
This record concerns open-reading-frame eligibility as tested by a finite start--stop interval protocol.  The biological object is not translation, not a folded protein, not a cellular mechanism, and not a functional assignment.  It is the narrower question whether a predeclared start--stop classifier, already fixed before looking at the curated biological intervals, separates curated coding intervals from matched non-ORF controls and leaves boundary cases unpromoted.  The admissible conclusion is therefore an interval-level eligibility contact only: a curated nucleotide interval may pass the frozen start--stop enumeration test, but no claim about translation realization, protein structure, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality follows from that fact without a further biological contact designed for that higher layer.

The dataset snapshot is dated 2026-05-30 and contains $168$ rows.  The required classes are present: boundary cases, matched non-ORF decoys, and positive curated ORFs.  The protocol was frozen at 2026-05-29T19:11:13+00:00, while the outside curated evidence was observed at 2026-05-30T10:29:31+00:00. \paragraph{Boundary behavior.}
The boundary controls are essential to the interpretation.  The audit reports $24$ boundary cases and $0$ promotions, so the promoted fraction is $0.0$.  This prevents the interval classifier from being read as a generator of claims outside the tested row classes.  The earlier start--stop window contact had only $1$ input window and $1$ classified window, with scope restricted to external window start--stop enumeration and with an explicit note that no geometry-to-translation promotion was made.  The present curated interval contact strengthens that row-level test by adding $168$ curated rows, matched decoys, boundary cases, three held-out folds, and predeclared pairwise baselines, but its layer remains the same: ORF eligibility as an interval-contact statement.

\paragraph{Admissible conclusion.}
The finite evidence supports the following local conclusion.  For this frozen classifier, this RefSeq-derived curated interval table, and these three input classes, the observed finite rows show perfect separation of curated positive intervals from matched non-ORF decoys, perfect held-out split stability across the three locus folds, and zero promotion of boundary cases.  The result is an externally contacted ORF-eligibility record, not a derivation of translation, folding, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or a universal genomic rule.  The NCBI RefSeq annotation remains biological input to the test, while the finite codon geometry supplies the predeclared coordinate language in which the classifier is evaluated.
