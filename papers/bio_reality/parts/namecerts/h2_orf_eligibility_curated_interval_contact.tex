\subsection{Curated interval contact for ORF eligibility}\label{sec:h2-orf-eligibility-curated-interval-contact}

\paragraph{Question isolated by the packet.}
This record concerns only open-reading-frame eligibility as a start--stop interval classification problem. The biological object is not a translated peptide, a folded protein, a physical molecule, a regulatory role, or an organism-level function. The finite claim is narrower: after a protocol had fixed the input classes, the classifier, the baselines, and the held-out folds, a curated RefSeq interval contact was used to test whether positive coding-sequence intervals are accepted while matched non-ORF decoys and boundary cases remain unpromoted. The contact is external biological annotation, not a consequence of the codon-geometry calculation itself. Accordingly, the result reported here is an interval-contact result for $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$ and nothing stronger.

\paragraph{External interval contact.}
The contact used here is the curated finite nucleotide-window manifest loaded from \texttt{tools/bio\_reality/data/curated\_orf\_interval\_contact\_dataset.json}. It contains $168$ rows and is tied to NCBI RefSeq GenBank XML CDS intervals from \textit{Escherichia coli} K-12 MG1655 complete genome accession NC\_000913.3, with snapshot date $2026$-$05$-$30$. The required input classes are all present: boundary cases, matched non-ORF decoys, and positive curated ORFs. The protocol records that classifier rules, input classes, held-out splits, and baselines were fixed at $2026$-$05$-$29\,19{:}11{:}13+00{:}00$, before the external evidence timestamp $2026$-$05$-$30\,10{:}29{:}31+00{:}00$. The classifier is \texttt{orf\_start\_stop\_window\_eligibility}; its rules were not modified after the frozen protocol, and the analysis used no translation or structure input.

\paragraph{Finite outcome.}
The positive curated intervals and matched decoys were each counted over $72$ tested items. All $72$ positive intervals were accepted, giving acceptance rate $1.0$ and exact one-sided binomial upper-tail value
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{pos}}=2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}.
\end{aligned}
$$
All $72$ matched non-ORF decoys were rejected, giving rejection rate $1.0$ and the same one-sided value
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{decoy}}=2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The predeclared paired comparison also has $72$ wins in $72$ pairs, win rate $1.0$, and one-sided value
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{pair}}=2.117582368135751\times 10^{-22}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The boundary-control class contains $24$ cases, of which $0$ were promoted, so the promoted fraction is $0.0$. Held-out stability was checked over three frozen folds. In the left-locus fold, $24$ positives were accepted at rate $1.0$ and $24$ decoys were rejected at rate $1.0$. The same $24/24$ positive acceptance and $24/24$ decoy rejection occurred in the middle-locus fold and in the right-locus fold. Thus the reported finite outcome is not a single aggregate count hiding a failed fold: each of the three folds passed with identical positive and decoy rates.

\paragraph{Local interpretation.}
The verified finite statement is the following. Within the $168$-row curated interval dataset, using the frozen geometry-only classifier and the frozen split plan, the contact separates the $72$ positive curated ORF intervals from the $72$ matched non-ORF decoys, leaves $24$ boundary cases unpromoted, and preserves that behavior across three held-out locus folds. The self-check row records that the packet enforces three gates and does not move codon geometry across biological layers. Therefore the chapter supports an $\mathrm{orf\_eligibility}$ interval-contact claim for this dataset and this protocol.
