\subsection{Window-Six Codon Boundary Row}\label{subsec:h0-r-cardinality-13}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite codon carrier.}
This record concerns the standard-code codon table after the codon positions are represented as a six-coordinate cube display.  The measured row is the boundary set $R$ selected by the local window-six construction.  The finite computation gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\},
\end{aligned}
$$
and the counted size is
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| = 13.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the claim is not that the whole genetic code has thirteen relevant codons, but that this particular local boundary row extracted from the six-bit codon display contains exactly thirteen codon labels.  The same run identifies this set with the expected codon list for the row, so there is no residual disagreement between the selected finite row and the named codon labels reported by the curated standard-code table.

\paragraph{Punctured-cube profile.}
The row $R$ is also reported as a punctured-cube object with the same codon membership.  Its recorded face-count vector is
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
In this local reading, the thirteen vertices are the row codons, the sixteen one-dimensional adjacencies are the retained boundary edges, and the four two-dimensional faces are the retained local tile faces.  This gives the row more structure than a bare list: the codons sit in a small cubical incidence pattern, but the pattern is still only a code-layer object.  The row does not by itself express translation chemistry, ribosomal realization, molecular structure, admissible physical dynamics, biological function, a general mechanism, or a universal law.

The associated spectral run reports $\lambda_M=0.6752479308830213$, $\lambda_R=0.4758352843197002$, and $\mu=3.051487585298128$.  The radial cubic check is numerically stable at

with the same value reported as the cubic-polynomial residual.  At the numerical precision of the run, this supports the interpretation that the row is not merely a hand-picked codon list: it has a reproducible spectral profile inside the window-six geometry.  The spectral radius value attached to $M$ is $0.6752479308830213$, while the row value $0.4758352843197002$ is smaller, consistent with $R$ functioning as a restricted boundary component rather than the full measured local shape.

\paragraph{Shape rarity under shuffled labels.}
The finite null comparison for the measured shape used $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$.  It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and encountered $55377$ duplicate retries.  Among those samples, exactly one exact shape match was observed, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap comparison found $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second null model preserving amino-acid classes reported $N=2000$, zero exact matches, and seven samples in the lower tail at or below the observed measured-shape size $|M|=20$.  Its recorded values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These figures are evidence that the observed local codon shape is not typical under the tested finite randomizations.  They do not upgrade the row into a biological necessity claim; they only say that, relative to these explicit finite nulls, the observed code-layer tile is unusual.

\paragraph{Code-read recovery tests.}
The leave-one-code-out test evaluated $23$ tables and $63$ targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$.  The row-supported rates were much higher: $0.9365079365079365$ inside $M-T$, $0.9841269841269841$ inside $M-T$ together with the boundary, and $0.9841269841269841$ above baseline.  The family split keeps the same pattern across five families: alternative yeast has hit rate above baseline $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial plastid, candidate other, mitochondrial, and nuclear ciliate each report $1.0$.  These numbers support the code-read role of the local boundary row: when one table entry is withheld, the finite geometry often places the held-out code label in the predicted or boundary-supported region.  The test still remains a codon-label test, not an assay of translation, folding, material feasibility, function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Single-codon deletion test.}
The leave-one-codon-out run reports no anchors, five boundary cases, eight high-coverage closure cases, eight predictable cases, and recovery rate $1.0$.  In scalar form,
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{anchor\ count}&=0,\\
\mathrm{boundary\ count}&=5,\\
\mathrm{closure\ coverage\ high}&=8,\\
\mathrm{predictable\ count}&=8,\\
\mathrm{recovery\ rate}&=1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the row has a strong internal deletion response at the code-label level.  The result should be read narrowly: it says that the finite local construction can recover the withheld codon position in the reported cases, not that a cellular process reconstructs missing codons by this geometry.

Every listed module has either predictable or boundary-supported status.  This partition explains the thirteen codons as a structured local label class rather than as an isolated cardinality coincidence.

\paragraph{Local scope of the claim.}
The reality contact used here is the curated standard-code table: it assigns codons to amino-acid or stop labels and therefore supports codon-label readback.  It does not observe ribosomal translation, protein folding, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, a universal mechanism, or any law stating that DNA must instantiate this cubical geometry.  Consequently the row may be cited as a finite, code-level boundary-tile finding: $R$ has thirteen codons, a punctured-cube profile $(13,16,4)$, a stable spectral signature, rare shape behavior under the reported null models, strong leave-one-code and leave-one-codon recovery statistics, and a complete six-module classification.  Any assertion beyond that layer requires a distinct empirical or formal contact aimed at the higher layer being claimed.
