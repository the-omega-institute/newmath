\paragraph{Finite carrier and observed boundary row.}
The finite carrier is the six-bit codon cube $Q_6$, read only as a coordinate display for the standard-code codon table.  The row singled out by the boundary construction is the thirteen-codon set
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The finite audit records $|R|=13$, with the same thirteen codons returned by the expected-codon readback.  As a cubical subcomplex, the same row is recorded as a punctured-cube tile with $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the object certified here is not the full biology of coding, but a local finite-row fact: a thirteen-vertex boundary pattern inside a six-coordinate codon display, together with the curated label table needed to read those vertices as amino-acid or stop labels.

\paragraph{Shape significance and null rows.}
Against the reported shape null with $N=2000$ samples and seed $42$, there was $1$ exact shape match after $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries.  The resulting exact-shape probability was $p=0.0005$.  The high-overlap threshold also remained small: $25$ samples had Jaccard overlap at least $0.9$, giving $p=0.0125$.  A stricter amino-acid-class-preserving null again did not reproduce the exact tile: with $N=2000$, the exact-match count was $0$, the observed $M$-size was $20$, and the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$-size was $7$, giving lower-tail probability $0.0035$.  These numbers support the statement that the local tile is not a common artifact of the tested finite shuffles.  They do not convert the tile into a global law of genetic coding.

\paragraph{Leave-one readback tests.}
The code-layer readback tests measure how the finite row behaves when labels or codons are withheld.  In the leave-one-code-out experiment, $23$ tables and $63$ targets were evaluated.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary was again $0.9841269841269841$.  In the leave-one-codon-out experiment, the anchor count was $0$, the boundary count was $5$, the high-coverage closure count was $8$, the predictable count was $8$, and the recovery rate was $1.0$.  These figures are read as code-layer recoverability statistics for curated labels.  They do not assert translation realization, protein formation, physical admissibility, biological function, mechanism, or universality.

\paragraph{Reality contact and cannot-claim boundary.}
The only reality contact used in this chapter is the curated standard-code table, whose observed fact is that codons are assigned to amino-acid or stop labels.  That contact can test code readback and local codon-tile label splits.  It cannot test translation realization, folded structure, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, universal mechanism, or the claim that DNA must obey this geometry as biological necessity.  Amino-acid label-sequence readback remains a code-layer observation; it is not folded protein structure.  Any promotion beyond the local code layer requires a separate contact of the appropriate kind.  Within that boundary, the certified statement is ready: the six-bit codon display contains a thirteen-codon local boundary tile with punctured-cube face data, stable spectral coordinates, significant finite null comparisons, complete module coverage, and strong leave-one readback behavior against the curated standard-code label table.
