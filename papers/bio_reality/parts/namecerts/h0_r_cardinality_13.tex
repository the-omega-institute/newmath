\subsection{Window-six codon tile with a thirteen-codon boundary set}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-cardinality-13}

\paragraph{Claim.}
The record concerns the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon-coordinate display. The tested object is not a biochemical mechanism. It is a finite code-layer readback: a codon cube is equipped with curated codon labels, and the local coordinate calculation isolates a boundary class $R$ of size $13$. The verified row reports
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| &= 13,\\
R &= \{\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the finite assertion is exact: the boundary set contains the eight listed $A$- or $U/C$-anchored codons outside the CUN block together with the full CUN quartet, and no additional codon is included in this row.

\paragraph{Finite contact.}
The empirical contact used here is a curated standard-code table assigning each codon to an amino-acid or stop label. That contact can check code-label readback and the local split induced by the coordinate window. It cannot, by itself, observe translation in a cell, a folded structure, physical admissibility, biological function, a universal generative mechanism, or an organism-independent law. The distinction is important: the coordinate geometry supplies a reproducible finite partition, while the labels are supplied by the curated code table.
