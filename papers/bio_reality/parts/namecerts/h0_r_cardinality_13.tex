\subsection{Window-six boundary row for the standard codon table}\label{subsec:h0-r-cardinality-13}\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Finite object.} The record concerns the standard-code codon table after it is viewed through a six-coordinate codon display. The carrier is the finite codon cube $Q_6$, with vertices read as codons over $\{\mathrm{U},\mathrm{C},\mathrm{A},\mathrm{G}\}$ after fixing the usual two-bit nucleotide display at each of the three codon positions. The row singled out by the computation is the boundary set $R$. Its verified cardinality is
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| &= 13.
\end{aligned}
$$
The thirteen codons are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&\mathrm{AAA},\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG},\mathrm{AUA},\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU},\\
&\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UGA},\mathrm{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is not a reconstructed biochemical mechanism. It is a finite code-layer row: a small distinguished set in the codon cube whose labels are supplied by the curated standard-code table.

\paragraph{Cubical boundary shape.} The same row was independently checked as a punctured-cube pattern. The finite face data are
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)&=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the observed row is not merely a list of thirteen labels; it carries a local tile geometry with $13$ vertices, $16$ one-dimensional adjacencies, and $4$ two-dimensional faces. The word local is essential. The computation identifies a boundary tile inside the codon-coordinate display, not a global law of biological coding.

\paragraph{Spectral row.} The row also has a verified spectral signature. The recorded values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M&=0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R&=0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu&=3.051487585298128.

$$
The cubic relation was evaluated with residual $-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12}$, the same value reported for the radial relation. At the stated numerical tolerance, the spectral-radius entry is $0.6752479308830213$. These numbers make the row a reproducible finite object: a boundary set, a face vector, and a spectral signature are all attached to the same codon subset.

\paragraph{Null comparison.} Two null checks constrain the interpretation. In the shape-significance run with seed $42$, $N=2000$ samples required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and $55377$ duplicate retries. There was $1$ exact shape match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving $p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125$. In the amino-acid-class-preserving null run, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed comparison size was $|M|=20$, the exact-match count was $0$, and $7$ samples landed in the lower tail at or below the observed $M$ size. At the sampling resolution this gives
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ exact}}&=0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{aa\ class\ lower\ tail}}&=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
The row therefore survives two finite randomization checks as a non-generic local arrangement relative to the tested null models. The conclusion is statistical and finite: it says that this codon-coordinate tile is unusual under the specified sampling procedures.

\paragraph{Leave-out behavior.} The code-level recovery tests also support the row as a usable boundary descriptor. In the leave-one-code-out run, $23$ code tables and $63$ total targets were evaluated. The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$. The hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$; the hit rate in $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$; and the hit rate in $M-T$ together with the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$. In the leave-one-codon-out run, the recovery rate was $1.0$, the threshold comparison was also $1.0$, and the finite counts were $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-coverage closures, and $8$ predictable codons. Across families, $5$ families were recorded: alternative yeast had hit rate $0.8333333333333334$, while bacterial/plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families each had hit rate $1.0$ above baseline.

\paragraph{Module partition.} The thirteen codons in $R$ are completely assigned to six modules. The module assignment is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\mathrm{AGA},\mathrm{AGG}\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\mathrm{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\mathrm{CUA},\mathrm{CUC},\mathrm{CUG},\mathrm{CUU}\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\mathrm{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\mathrm{UCA},\mathrm{UUA}\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\mathrm{UAA},\mathrm{UAG},\mathrm{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The classification report gives $R$ size $13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no missing codons, and no unassigned codons. Every module has a predictable or boundary status. This matters because the row is not only cardinally correct; it is exhausted by named local modules without residue.

\paragraph{Reality contact and limit of the claim.} The empirical contact used here is the curated standard genetic-code table: it assigns codons to amino-acid or stop labels. That contact is enough for code-layer readback and for checking local codon-label splits. It is not a contact with translation dynamics, folded protein geometry, energetic admissibility, biological function, a universal biological mechanism, or any organism-independent necessity claim. An amino-acid label sequence remains a label sequence unless another observation supplies a structure or function contact. Consequently, the row supports the following bounded statement: the standard-code labels, when placed on the six-coordinate codon cube, exhibit a verified thirteen-codon local boundary tile with the listed face data, spectrum, null-model behavior, leave-out behavior, and complete module classification. Nothing in this chapter promotes that tile into translation, folding, physical admissibility, function, mechanism, or universality.
