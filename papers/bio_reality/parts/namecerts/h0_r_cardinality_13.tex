\subsection{A window-six codon boundary row in the standard code}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-cardinality-13}

\paragraph{Coordinate object.}
The finite object in this record is the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon display. The coordinate layer is the codon cube $Q_6$, while the readback layer is the curated assignment of each codon to an amino-acid or stop label. The claim isolated here is not that the cube geometry causes the biological code, but that the standard table contains a distinguished local row $R$ whose membership, boundary shape, and label behavior can be checked as finite data.

The row is the following $13$-element subset of the codon table:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
     &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The verified finite count is therefore
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| = 13.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is the cardinality assertion of the row: the computation returns $R\_cardinality\_equals\_13=13$ and $size=13$, with no extra codons in the displayed row.

\paragraph{Punctured-cube shape.}
The same row was checked as a punctured local cube with face-vector data
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the row is not merely a list of codon names; it carries a finite adjacency-and-face profile inside the six-bit display. The phrase ``punctured'' is used here only for this finite combinatorial profile. It does not assert that DNA, ribosomes, tRNA pools, molecular kinetics, or organism-level constraints are governed by this local cube.

\paragraph{Spectral finite check.}
A second computation attaches scalar spectral data to the same local split. The recorded values are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial cubic residual was
$$
\begin{aligned}
-6.224354365258478\cdot 10^{-12},
\end{aligned}
$$
which is numerically at zero for the reported tolerance. This supplies a finite numerical consistency check for the row geometry and its complementary label split. It is not a biochemical eigenmode claim; it is a coordinate-level statistic computed from the displayed codon object.

\paragraph{Shape significance against randomized label tables.}
The finite null experiment sampled $N=2000$ randomized tables with seed $42$. It required $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and therefore incurred $55377$ duplicate retries. In that run, the exact local shape occurred once, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{exact}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
The high-overlap condition $J\geq 0.9$ occurred $25$ times, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A stricter amino-acid-class-preserving null also used $N=2000$ samples and seed $42$. It observed $0$ exact shape matches. The observed module-side size was $20$, and the lower-tail count at or below that observed size was $7$, yielding
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{class\ exact}=0.0,\qquad p_{class\ lower\ tail}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These values support the claim that the displayed row is a rare finite pattern under the tested randomizations. They do not convert the pattern into a law of translation, a folding principle, a physical admissibility theorem, or a functional mechanism.

\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
$$
while the hit rate above baseline was
$$
\begin{aligned}

\end{aligned}
$$
The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ together with the boundary was again $0.9841269841269841$. These are code-label recovery statistics: they measure how often the finite coordinate split recovers held-out code-label behavior under the stated evaluation. They do not observe translation events or protein products.

The leave-one-codon-out computation found $anchor\_count=0$, $boundary\_count=5$, $closure\_coverage\_high=8$, and $predictable\_count=8$. The reported recovery rate was
$$
\begin{aligned}
1.0,
\end{aligned}
$$
with the threshold comparison also returning $1.0$. This says that the tested held-out codons were recovered by the finite code-layer procedure in the recorded run. It does not say that a cellular system must recover them, nor that the resulting amino-acid label sequence has any folded structure.

\paragraph{Family and module decomposition.}
The code-family evaluation separated $5$ families. The hit rates above baseline were $1.0$ for bacterial-plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families, and $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative-yeast. The module classification assigned all $13$ row codons with no missing, extra, or unassigned codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R\_size &= 13,\\
assigned\_count &= 13,\\
missing\_from\_modules &= 0,\\
extra\_module\_codons &= 0,\\
unassigned\_codons &= 0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
agr &= \{AGA, AGG\},\\
ar\_lys &= \{AAA\},\\
cun &= \{CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU\},\\
ile\_met &= \{AUA\},\\
ssn &= \{UCA, UUA\},\\
stop\_trp &= \{UAA, UAG, UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Each module has either predictable behavior or boundary status in the recorded classification. This gives a complete finite row decomposition for the standard-code readback problem.

\paragraph{Local boundary of the claim.}
The empirical contact used here is a curated standard genetic-code table. It supplies codon-to-label readback and permits local checks of codon labels against the six-bit coordinate display. It does not supply a separate observation of translation realization, protein folding, structural order, physical admissibility, biological function, or a universal biological mechanism. Consequently, the conclusion of this record is deliberately narrow: the standard-code table contains a $13$-codon local row with the displayed punctured-cube profile, spectral scalars, randomized-shape rarity, held-out readback performance, and complete module assignment. Any assertion about translation, folded protein structure, physical feasibility, function, or universality would require an additional contact of the appropriate kind.
