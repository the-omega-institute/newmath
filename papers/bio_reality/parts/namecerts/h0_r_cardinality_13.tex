\subsection{Window-six codon boundary row}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-cardinality-13}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Coordinate record.}
The finite object in this row is the standard-code codon table viewed through a window-six codon cube $Q_6$.  The display distinguishes six binary coordinate positions, local tile faces, singleton label corners, and three-point label classes, but the biological contact is only the curated codon-to-label table.  The row therefore records a code-layer pattern: a finite subset $R$ of codons is selected by the local boundary test, and its readback is compared with standard-code amino-acid or stop labels.  The verified cardinality is
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R| &= 13.
\end{aligned}
$$
The actual row is not represented only by its size.  The verified members are
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
&UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is the finite content of claim $h0.R.cardinality.13$: thirteen named codons, read against the standard-code label table, with no promotion beyond code readback.

\paragraph{Cell-complex shape.}
The same row is also checked as a punctured-cube pattern.  The finite cell count attached to $R$ has $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the record is not merely a list of codons; it carries a local face structure with thirteen vertices, sixteen edges, and four two-dimensional faces.  The word ``local'' is essential here.  The table contact can say which codons carry which labels, and the coordinate system can expose a boundary tile inside $Q_6$, but this does not assert that the genetic code is forced by that geometry.  It says that this finite set, with this small cell structure, is the observed code-level boundary row under the chosen display.

\begin{aligned}

\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\

$$
The radial cubic residual is reported as
$$
\begin{aligned}

$$
which is numerically at zero for the recorded computation.  The spectral-radius value in tolerance is again $0.6752479308830213$.  These numbers do not make the row into a biochemical mechanism; they show that the finite coordinate construction has a reproducible numerical profile tied to the selected codon subset and its surrounding label geometry.

\paragraph{Null-model comparison.}
The shape was tested against finite random controls.  In the shape-significance run with $N=2000$ samples and seed $42$, the pipeline made $57377$ distinct-sample attempts and recorded $55377$ duplicate retries.  Exactly one sample matched the exact shape, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard score at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{Jaccard}\ge 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A stricter amino-acid-class-preserving null, also with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, found no exact shape matches.  In that run the observed $M$ size was $20$, the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, and the lower-tail probability was
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{lower\ tail}}=0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These comparisons support the statement that the recorded local tile is not a routine draw from the tested finite null families.  They do not identify a causal path, a translational apparatus, or a physical admissibility condition.

\paragraph{Readback and recovery probes.}
The leave-one-code-out probe evaluated $23$ tables and $63$ targets.  Its baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  A separate leave-one-codon-out probe reported anchor count $0$, boundary count $5$, predictable count $8$, high closure coverage $8$, and recovery rate
$$
\begin{aligned}
1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
At family level, five families were reported.  The hit rates above baseline were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, and $1.0$ for bacterial/plastid, candidate-other, mitochondrial, and nuclear-ciliate families.  These figures show that the local row is useful as a code-read classifier under the tested finite omissions; they still remain label-table measurements.

\paragraph{Module assignment.}
The thirteen codons of $R$ are fully assigned by the module classification.  The reported modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The module audit gives $R$ size $13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no codons missing from modules, and no unassigned codons.  Hence the boundary row is not an anonymous thirteen-element residue: it decomposes into biologically named codon modules at the code layer, including arginine-related, lysine-related, leucine-box, isoleucine/methionine, serine/leucine, and stop/tryptophan neighborhoods.

\paragraph{Local interpretation.}
The scientific content of the row is a constrained one.  A six-position codon display, together with a curated standard-code label table, exposes a thirteen-codon boundary subset whose finite topology, spectrum, random-control behavior, leave-one-out behavior, and module assignment are all explicitly recorded.  The contact supports codon-label readback and local split checks.  It does not observe translation in a cell, folded protein structure, thermodynamic or kinetic admissibility, biological function, an evolutionary mechanism, or a universal biological law.  Any such higher-layer assertion would require a separate contact with its own measured object.  In this chapter the result stays at the code-read layer: $R$ is a reproducible finite local boundary row in the standard-code table, not a claim that codon geometry alone explains biological realization.
