\paragraph{Finite coordinate setting.}
This record concerns the standard-code codon table viewed through a six-bit codon display. The object under inspection is not translation itself, and it is not a claim about folded proteins. It is a finite code-layer record: codons are placed in a window-six coordinate cube, local faces and corners are inspected, and the resulting boundary set is compared with curated codon labels. The biological contact is the standard-code table assigning each codon to an amino-acid label or to stop. The tested layer is therefore code readback only.

The verified boundary row for the claim $h0.R.cardinality.13$ gives the set
$$
\begin{aligned}
R=\{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
   &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Its recorded size is
$$
\begin{aligned}
|R|=13.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is the central finite observation for the chapter. The list is not inferred from a biochemical mechanism; it is the codon-window output whose labels are then checked against the curated standard code.

It does not assert that biological DNA is governed by this geometry, and it does not promote a codon-label split into a universal rule.

\paragraph{Scope boundary.}
The local tile is aligned with the curated standard-code label table, but that table supplies only codon-to-label assignments. It does not observe ribosomal realization, folded structure, physical admissibility, biochemical function, or a universal biological law. Amino-acid label sequences are not protein structures, and a CUN, UUR, AGR, or stop--tryptophan module name is not a structural measurement. Any claim about translation, folding, physical viability, function, mechanism, or universality requires a separate contact with that layer. Within the data supplied here, the supported conclusion is narrower and finite: the window-six codon coordinate exposes a thirteen-codon local boundary set with punctured-cube shape, stable spectral scalars, significant null-model separation, and strong code-layer recovery behavior against the recorded leave-one-out tests.
