\subsection{The thirteen-codon boundary row in the window-six codon cube}
\label{sec:bioreality-h0-r-cardinality-13}
\origin{ai}

\paragraph{Object and finite row.}
The row considered here is a code-read record for the standard-code codon table, viewed through a six-coordinate codon display.  The relevant carrier is the finite codon cube with one coordinate for each displayed nucleotide bit, together with the curated table assigning each codon to an amino-acid or stop label.  The finite boundary set extracted by the row has cardinality thirteen:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&AAA, AGA, AGG, AUA, CUA, CUC, CUG, CUU,\\
      &UAA, UAG, UCA, UGA, UUA\},\qquad |R|=13.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is not a biological-mechanism assertion.  It is a finite statement about which codons lie in the row's boundary class after the window-six coordinate display and label readback have been fixed.  The same thirteen codons also appear as the punctured-cube row, with face-vector data
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the row is not merely a list of labels: it carries a local tile profile with thirteen vertices, sixteen one-dimensional incidences, and four two-dimensional faces inside the codon-coordinate complex.

\paragraph{Spectral measurement.}
The associated spectral row separates the measured boundary class from a larger codon-label module by three scalar quantities.  The run reports
$$
\begin{aligned}
\lambda_M &= 0.6752479308830213,\\
\lambda_R &= 0.4758352843197002,\\
\mu &= 3.051487585298128.
\end{aligned}
$$
The radial polynomial residual was $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, so the recorded cubic relation is numerically satisfied to the displayed tolerance.  In this chapter the spectral data are used only as finite-row diagnostics: they say that the thirteen-codon boundary row sits in a measured local geometry whose spectral radius is $0.6752479308830213$ for the surrounding module and $0.4758352843197002$ for the boundary row.  They do not by themselves assert anything about ribosomal translation, molecular conformation, biochemical admissibility, organismal function, or a universal biological rule.

\paragraph{Randomization checks.}
Two finite null checks were run against the same code-read object.  In the shape-significance check, $N=2000$ sampled alternatives were produced from $57377$ distinct-sample attempts, with $55377$ duplicate retries.  There was $1$ exact shape match, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005.
\end{aligned}
$$
There were $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{J\geq 0.9}=0.0125.
\end{aligned}
$$
A second amino-acid-class-preserving null check used $N=2000$ samples.  It observed $0$ exact shape matches, $7$ lower-tail cases at or below the observed module size, and an observed module size of $20$.  The corresponding row values were
$$
\begin{aligned}
p_{\mathrm{class,exact}} &= 0.0,\\
p_{\mathrm{class,lower}} &= 0.0035.
\end{aligned}
$$
These numbers make the row a concrete finite finding rather than a visual anecdote: the thirteen-codon boundary is rare under the reported randomizations, including the class-preserving one.  The data still remain at the code-label layer, because the sampled objects and the observed object are codon-table label arrangements, not physical translation events or folded structures.

\paragraph{Leave-one tests.}
The row also appears in two predictive stress tests.  In the leave-one-code-out evaluation, $23$ code tables were evaluated over $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M\setminus T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the rate inside $M\setminus T$ together with the boundary class was $0.9841269841269841$.  In the leave-one-codon-out evaluation, the row had $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary codons, $8$ high-coverage closure cases, and $8$ predictable cases, with recovery rate
$$
\begin{aligned}
\rho=1.0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The family-level leave-one-code-out summary grouped $5$ code families.  Their above-baseline rates were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, and $1.0$ for bacterial plastid, candidate other, mitochondrial, and nuclear ciliate families.  These values support the claim that the row is not an isolated artifact of one deletion protocol; it persists across several finite removals and family groupings at the label-table level.

\paragraph{Module accounting.}
The thirteen codons are completely assigned to six row modules.  The module accounting is
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr} &= \{AGA,AGG\},\\
\mathrm{ar\_lys} &= \{AAA\},\\
\mathrm{cun} &= \{CUA,CUC,CUG,CUU\},\\
\mathrm{ile\_met} &= \{AUA\},\\
\mathrm{ssn} &= \{UCA,UUA\},\\
\mathrm{stop\_trp} &= \{UAA,UAG,UGA\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The classification record reports $R$-size $13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no codons missing from the modules, and no unassigned codons.  This closes the finite bookkeeping for the row: every codon in $R$ is accounted for by exactly the reported module table, and the module table does not claim any codon outside $R$.

\paragraph{Interpretive boundary.}
The curated standard-code table is the contact used here.  It supplies codon-to-label readback and permits local split checks in the window-six display.  It does not observe translation being realized in a cell, a folded protein structure, physical admissibility, biological function, organism-level fitness, or any universal mechanism.  Consequently the chapter's conclusion is deliberately narrow: the standard-code label table contains a thirteen-codon boundary row with the listed codons, punctured-cube face-vector $(13,16,4)$, the displayed spectral scalars, rare-shape randomization values, strong leave-one recovery values, and complete six-module accounting.  Any claim above code-read geometry requires a separate empirical contact of the corresponding kind.
