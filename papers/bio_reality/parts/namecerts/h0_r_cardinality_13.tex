\subsection{Window-six boundary row for the standard code}\label{sec:h0-r-cardinality-13}

\paragraph{Finite row.}
The row concerns the six-coordinate codon display in which each codon is read as a point of a binary window of length six, while its amino-acid or stop label is supplied by the curated standard-code table.  The distinguished boundary set $R$ has exactly thirteen codons:
$$
\begin{aligned}
R = \{&\texttt{AAA},\texttt{AGA},\texttt{AGG},\texttt{AUA},\texttt{CUA},\texttt{CUC},\texttt{CUG},\texttt{CUU},\\
&\texttt{UAA},\texttt{UAG},\texttt{UCA},\texttt{UGA},\texttt{UUA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
Thus the checked finite count is $|R|=13$.  The same row identifies this set with a punctured-cube pattern whose face data have $f$-vector
$$
\begin{aligned}
(f_0,f_1,f_2)=(13,16,4).
\end{aligned}
$$
This is a statement about the finite coordinate-and-label presentation of the standard code.  It does not assert that the coordinate picture is a causal principle for translation or a general biological law.

\paragraph{Spectral and shape evidence.}
The associated spectral row gives $\lambda_M=0.6752479308830213$, $\lambda_R=0.4758352843197002$, and $\mu=3.051487585298128$.  The radial cubic check has residual $-6.224354365258478\times 10^{-12}$, recorded equivalently as the cubic-polynomial residual and the radial-polynomial value.  The spectral-radius value in tolerance is again $0.6752479308830213$.  These numbers support the internal consistency of the finite window calculation: the same row carries a geometric set, a boundary count, and a numerical spectrum that satisfy the recorded polynomial test to numerical tolerance.

The shape comparison used $N=2000$ sampled alternatives with seed $42$.  Among $57377$ distinct-sample attempts, $55377$ were duplicate retries.  There was $1$ exact shape match, giving $p_{\mathrm{exact}}=0.0005$, and $25$ samples with Jaccard similarity at least $0.9$, giving $p_{J\ge 0.9}=0.0125$.  Under the amino-acid-class-preserving null model, again with $N=2000$ and seed $42$, the observed $M$ size was $20$, the exact-match count was $0$, the lower-tail count at or below the observed $M$ size was $7$, the exact-shape $p$-value was $0.0$, and the lower-tail $p$-value was $0.0035$.  These rows make the boundary pattern nontrivial relative to the sampled finite nulls, while still leaving all mechanistic interpretation outside the row.

\paragraph{Readback and recovery.}
The label readback is supplied by a curated standard-code table assigning codons to amino-acid or stop labels.  In the leave-one-code-out row, $23$ tables were evaluated over $63$ total targets.  The baseline hit rate was $0.19821739130434785$, while the hit rate above baseline was $0.9841269841269841$.  The hit rate inside $M-T$ was $0.9365079365079365$, and the hit rate inside $M-T$ plus the boundary was $0.9841269841269841$.  The leave-one-codon-out row recorded $0$ anchors, $5$ boundary cases, $8$ high-coverage closure cases, $8$ predictable cases, and recovery rate $1.0$, with the threshold comparison also equal to $1.0$.

The family-level row separated $5$ families.  The hit-rate-above-baseline values were $0.8333333333333334$ for alternative yeast, $1.0$ for bacterial and plastid codes, $1.0$ for the candidate-other family, $1.0$ for mitochondrial codes, and $1.0$ for nuclear ciliate codes.  At module level, all thirteen $R$ codons are assigned with no missing, extra, or unassigned codons.  The modules are
$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{agr}&=\{\texttt{AGA},\texttt{AGG}\}, &
\mathrm{ar\_lys}&=\{\texttt{AAA}\},\\
\mathrm{cun}&=\{\texttt{CUA},\texttt{CUC},\texttt{CUG},\texttt{CUU}\}, &
\mathrm{ile\_met}&=\{\texttt{AUA}\},\\
\mathrm{ssn}&=\{\texttt{UCA},\texttt{UUA}\}, &
\mathrm{stop\_trp}&=\{\texttt{UAA},\texttt{UAG},\texttt{UGA}\}.
\end{aligned}
$$
The module-classification row reports $R$ size $13$, assigned count $13$, no extra module codons, no missing codons from the modules, and no unassigned codons.

\paragraph{Local interpretation.}
The finite record therefore supports a narrow code-layer statement: in the standard-code label table, the window-six coordinates isolate a thirteen-codon boundary row with punctured-cube face data, nontrivial sampled shape statistics, a compatible spectral calculation, strong leave-one-code and leave-one-codon recovery rows, and a complete module assignment.  The biological contact used here is only the codon-to-label table.  It can check whether the coordinate construction reads back to standard-code labels and whether local split patterns match the curated row.

It cannot by itself test whether translation is realized in a cell, whether an amino-acid string folds, whether a molecule is physically admissible, whether a protein has function, whether a mechanism explains the code, or whether the local tile is universal across biology.  Those stronger statements require separate contacts at the appropriate layer.  In this chapter the boundary remains local: codon labels are observed, the thirteen-row geometry is computed, and every claim beyond code-layer readback is held back until another experiment supplies the missing contact.
