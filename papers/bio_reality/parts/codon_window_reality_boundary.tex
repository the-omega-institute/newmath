\paragraph{Residual survival statistic.}
For a residual coordinate $q \in B^{*}_{Q_6}$ and a translation-layer readout $r$, the intended survival comparison is
$$
\begin{aligned}
  S_Q(r \mid Z) = J_0(Z) - J_Q(Z,q),
\end{aligned}
$$
where $Z$ includes the amino-acid sequence $y$, local sequence context, and simpler codon-feature baselines.  The sign convention is that $S_Q(r \mid Z)>0$ means that adding the residual coordinate $q$ improves the readout model relative to the baseline loss $J_0$.  In the sharper test against an $M$-density model, the comparison is made conditionally on $\Delta_M$:
$$
\begin{aligned}
  S_Q(r \mid Z,\Delta_M) > 0.
\end{aligned}
$$
The testable claim is not that every residual coordinate matters.  It is the weaker existence claim that at least one coordinate in the nine-element basis survives on at least one bounded readout such as tAI, stAI, ribosome occupancy, or protein abundance, after amino-acid composition has been controlled.

\paragraph{Why the amino-acid quotient matters.}
A codon model that only tracks amino-acid sequence cannot distinguish synonymous codons.  Conversely, an $M$-density summary can record a local optimum over a restricted codon feature, but it cannot by itself test contrasts such as $CUN$ versus $UUR$ for leucine or $AAA$ versus $AAG$ for lysine.  The residual basis is introduced exactly to keep those contrasts visible after the amino-acid projection has been applied.  Thus the question is not whether codon usage differs across organisms in the ordinary compositional sense; the finite run already shows nonzero residual variation in $7$ of $9$ coordinates.  The sharper biological question is whether any such residual variation predicts a translation-layer readout once the amino-acid word and simpler baselines have already been accounted for.

\paragraph{Available contacts.}
Three contact classes are relevant.  Per-organism codon-usage tables give the sequence-composition side of the carrier.  Organism-level tRNA gene-copy spectra give an annotation-level proxy for decoding resources, including anticodon-family structure.  tAI or stAI tables give bounded codon-adaptation readouts that can be used as translation-layer response variables if they are kept separate from the residual coordinate construction.  These contacts can support a translation-realization test only when they are organism-matched and jointly curated.  Separate bridges are not enough: a codon-count table, a tRNA-copy table, and a tAI or stAI table must be assembled into the same controlled statistic before a positive survival score can be reported.
